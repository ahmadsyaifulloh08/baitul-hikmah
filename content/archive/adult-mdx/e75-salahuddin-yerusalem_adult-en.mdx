# Saladin Recaptures Jerusalem (1187 CE)

## Historical Context

### 88 Years of Crusader Occupation

Since the First Crusade captured Jerusalem in 1099 CE, Islam's third holiest city had been under Crusader control for 88 years. The 1099 conquest was marked by the mass slaughter of Muslim and Jewish inhabitants — a tragedy that created a deep wound in the collective memory of the Muslim world.

### The Rise of Resistance: Zangi and Nur ad-Din

The turning point began with **Imad ad-Din Zangi** recapturing Edessa in 1144, continued by his son **Nur ad-Din Mahmud** who unified Syria and Egypt. Nur ad-Din was the strategic architect behind Muslim resistance, and Saladin was his protégé and successor.

### Saladin: From Young Commander to Sultan

**Salah ad-Din Yusuf ibn Ayyub** (1137-1193 CE) was born in Tikrit (modern Iraq) to a Kurdish family. He built his military career under his uncle Shirkuh, a general of Nur ad-Din. After Nur ad-Din's death in 1174, Saladin gradually unified Egypt, Syria, and Mesopotamia under his leadership — creating the united front needed to face the Crusaders.

## The Battle of Hattin (4 July 1187 CE)

### Saladin's Strategy

Hattin was the key to Jerusalem. Saladin lured King Guy de Lusignan to abandon his water sources at Sepphoris and march across the arid plain toward Tiberias. The thirsty, exhausted Crusader army was trapped near the Horns of Hattin — twin volcanic hills.

Saladin cut off access to the Sea of Galilee, the only nearby water source, and burned dry brush around the Crusader positions, adding choking smoke to the July heat.

### Decisive Victory

The Crusader army was utterly destroyed. King Guy was captured along with nearly all his nobles, including the **True Cross** — the most sacred Crusader relic. Saladin treated King Guy with honor, offering him water with his own hand.

However, when **Raynald de Châtillon** — infamous for violating truces, raiding pilgrim caravans, and threatening to attack Makkah and Madinah — was brought before him, Saladin executed him personally. This demonstrated Saladin's principle of justice: mercy for the deserving, punishment for the transgressor.

## The Liberation of Jerusalem (2 October 1187 CE / 27 Rajab 583 AH)

### The Contrast with 1099

What followed became one of the most celebrated moments in the history of chivalry. In **stark contrast** to the Crusader massacre of 1099:

1. **No massacre** — not a single civilian was killed
2. **Humane ransom** — each man paid 10 dinars, women 5, children 1; those unable to pay were given time
3. **Saladin freed thousands** from his personal treasury — his brother al-Adil even requested 1,000 prisoners to free for nothing
4. **Churches and Christian holy sites** were protected — the Church of the Holy Sepulchre remained in Christian hands
5. **Eastern Orthodox Christians**, oppressed under Latin Crusader rule, were welcomed
6. **The golden cross** atop the Dome of the Rock was peacefully removed and replaced with a crescent

Even European chroniclers, though Saladin's enemies, recorded his noble conduct. During the European Enlightenment, Saladin became a symbol of the ideal knight — Dante placed him in Limbo (not Hell) in the *Divine Comedy*.

## Analysis of Significance

### 1. Emulating the Prophet's Model

Saladin consciously emulated the **Fath Makkah** of Prophet Muhammad ﷺ — victory followed by amnesty, not vengeance. The liberation on 27 Rajab — coinciding with the anniversary of al-Isra' wa al-Mi'raj — added profound spiritual dimension.

### 2. Standards of Chivalry and Laws of War

Saladin's conduct in Jerusalem set standards surpassing his era and even many modern conflicts. Protection of civilians, respect for enemy holy sites, and humane treatment of prisoners — all aligned with Islamic laws of war rooted in the Qur'an and Sunnah.

### 3. Unifier of the Muslim World

Saladin proved that only unity could defeat an organized enemy. He unified Egypt, Syria, and other territories not through force alone, but through **moral legitimacy, justice, and personal example**.

## Civilizational Impact

- Jerusalem returned to being an open city for all three Abrahamic faiths
- Humanitarian standards in warfare that became references in Islamic jurisprudence
- Saladin became a universal symbol of chivalry, justice, and nobility
- The European response (Third Crusade) paradoxically deepened East-West cultural exchange
