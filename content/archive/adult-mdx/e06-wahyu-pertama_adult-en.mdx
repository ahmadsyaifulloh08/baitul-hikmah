# The First Revelation (Iqra') at Cave Hira (610 CE)

## Historical and Geographical Context

### Arabia on the Eve of Prophecy

At the dawn of the 7th century CE, the Arabian Peninsula was steeped in the darkness of jahiliyah (pre-Islamic ignorance). Idol worship pervaded every aspect of life — over 360 idols surrounded the Ka'bah. The burial of infant girls alive (wa'd al-banat), slavery, usury, and social injustice were accepted norms. Yet amid this darkness, a small group known as the Hunafa (sing. Hanif) — adherents to the monotheism of Prophet Ibrahim (Abraham) — refused idol worship despite social isolation.

Muhammad ibn Abdullah, at the age of 40, had earned the titles al-Amin (the Trustworthy) and al-Sadiq (the Truthful). His reputation as an honest merchant and wise mediator garnered respect throughout Makkah. Yet his soul was troubled by the state of his society. He began withdrawing from social life to practice tahannuts (contemplation) in Cave Hira, a small cave on Jabal Nur (the Mountain of Light), approximately 3 km from Makkah.

### Cave Hira: The Place of Contemplation

Cave Hira (غار حراء) sits atop Jabal Nur at an elevation of approximately 634 meters. The cave is small — barely large enough for one person to sit and recline. From its mouth, one can see the Ka'bah in the distance. Prophet Muhammad ﷺ spent nights upon nights in this cave, particularly during the month of Ramadan, carrying provisions of food and water prepared by his wife, Khadijah bint Khuwaylid.

## The Descent of the First Revelation

### The Night of 17th Ramadan (~6 August 610 CE)

In the narration recorded by Imam al-Bukhari and Muslim from Aisha (RA), the first form of revelation came through true dreams (al-ru'ya al-sadiqah). Every dream the Prophet ﷺ saw would come true like the breaking of dawn.

Then, on a night in Ramadan, the Angel Jibril (Gabriel) came to him in his true form. Jibril said: **"Iqra'!"** (Read!). The Prophet replied: *"Ma ana bi qari'"* (I cannot read). Jibril then embraced him tightly until he could barely endure it, then released him and said again: **"Iqra'!"** This occurred three times, until finally Jibril recited the first verses ever revealed:

> **اقْرَأْ بِاسْمِ رَبِّكَ الَّذِي خَلَقَ ۝ خَلَقَ الْإِنسَانَ مِنْ عَلَقٍ ۝ اقْرَأْ وَرَبُّكَ الْأَكْرَمُ ۝ الَّذِي عَلَّمَ بِالْقَلَمِ ۝ عَلَّمَ الْإِنسَانَ مَا لَمْ يَعْلَمْ**
>
> *"Read in the name of your Lord who created. Created man from a clinging substance. Read, and your Lord is the Most Generous. Who taught by the pen. Taught man that which he knew not."*
> — Qur'an, Al-'Alaq [96]: 1-5

### The Prophet's Reaction

Prophet Muhammad ﷺ descended from Cave Hira trembling violently. He returned to Khadijah saying: *"Zammiluuni, zammiluuni!"* (Cover me, cover me!). After his fear subsided, he recounted what had happened and said: *"Laqad khasyitu 'ala nafsi"* (I fear for myself).

Khadijah — with extraordinary wisdom and faith — consoled him with words etched in history:

> *"Never! By Allah, Allah will never disgrace you. You maintain the ties of kinship, bear the burden of the weak, earn for the destitute, honor your guests, and assist those stricken by calamity."*
> — Sahih al-Bukhari no. 3, Sahih Muslim no. 160

### Waraqah ibn Nawfal: Confirmation of Prophethood

Khadijah then took the Prophet ﷺ to her cousin, Waraqah ibn Nawfal — an elderly Christian scholar who had mastered previous scriptures and wrote the Gospel in Hebrew. After hearing the Prophet's account, Waraqah declared:

> *"This is the Namus (Jibril) whom Allah sent to Musa (Moses)."*

Waraqah then wished he were young enough to support the Prophet when his people would drive him out. The Prophet asked: *"Will they really drive me out?"* Waraqah replied: *"No one has ever brought what you bring except that he was treated with hostility."*

## Analysis of Significance

### 1. The First Command: "Read" — An Intellectual Revolution

It is profoundly significant that the first revelation was not about ritual worship, law, or theology — but a command to **read and learn**. The word "Iqra'" derives from the root *qa-ra-a*, meaning to read, gather, and comprehend. This command descended upon an unlettered man (ummi), in a largely illiterate society — making it an extraordinary intellectual revolution.

These verses affirm that Islam is fundamentally a religion of knowledge. The mention of "al-qalam" (the pen) as an instrument of teaching demonstrates Islam's reverence for the written culture and documentation — the foundation for the civilization of learning that would flourish in subsequent centuries.

### 2. The Beginning of 23 Years of Revelation

The event at Cave Hira marked the beginning of the Qur'anic revelation process spanning approximately 23 years (610-632 CE). The Qur'an was revealed gradually (munajjaman), not all at once — a wisdom explained in Qur'an 25:32, to strengthen the Prophet's heart and to address the community's issues contextually.

### 3. The Role of Khadijah: Mother of Islamic Civilization

Khadijah's response to the first revelation made her the first person to believe (the first mu'minah). Without her emotional, financial, and spiritual support, Prophet Muhammad's mission would have been far more difficult. Khadijah exemplifies how a woman can be the central pillar of the greatest civilizational project in history.

### 4. A New Paradigm: Revelation as the Source of Knowledge

The first revelation established Islam's epistemological hierarchy: true knowledge originates from Allah (revelation), transmitted through a trustworthy intermediary (Jibril → Prophet), and intended for all humanity. This differs fundamentally from the Greek philosophical tradition relying solely on reason, and from mystical traditions depending on unverified inner experience.

## Civilizational Impact

The first revelation became the cornerstone of all Islamic civilization. The command "Iqra'" birthed a culture of literacy that ultimately built:
- **The hadith documentation tradition** — systematic recording of Prophetic teachings
- **The science of tafsir** — methodology for understanding the Qur'an
- **The translation movement** — translating Greek, Persian, and Indian works into Arabic (8th-10th centuries)
- **Bayt al-Hikmah** — the world's greatest research center and library (9th century)
- **The world's first universities** — Al-Qarawiyyin (859 CE) and Al-Azhar (970 CE)

Without the event at Cave Hira, there would be no Islamic civilization that gave birth to algebra, optics, modern medicine, and hundreds of other scientific contributions.
