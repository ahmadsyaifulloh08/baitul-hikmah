# The Fourth Crusade - Deviation to Constantinople (1202-1204 CE)

## Background and Origins

The Fourth Crusade was born from the same spirit as previous expeditions: to retake the Holy Land from Muslim hands.^1 However, the political context of the late 12th century was very different from the time of the First Crusade. **Saladin al-Ayyubi** had captured Jerusalem in 1187 CE, and the Third Crusade (1189-1193 CE), while successfully reclaiming some coastal territories, failed to recapture the holy city.^1

**Pope Innocent III**, who ascended to the papal throne in January 1198 CE, made a new crusade the primary goal of his pontificate.^2 In his bull *Post miserabile*, he called for an expedition to recapture Jerusalem by first defeating the powerful Ayyubid Sultanate in Egypt.^2 This strategy followed the thinking that Egypt was the heart of Muslim power in the region.

However, the Pope's call was largely ignored by European monarchs.^2 The German Emperor was struggling against Papal power, while England and France were still engaged in warfare against each other.^2 It was only in November 1199 CE, at a knightly tournament at **Thibaut III of Champagne's** castle in Écry-sur-Aisne, that momentum changed.^2 The event, tinged with mourning for the recent death of **King Richard I of England**, became a turning point when Thibaut vowed to abandon martial games and dedicate his weapons to God's service.^2

## Contract with Venice and Financial Problems

In early 1200 CE, a council of leading barons - including **Thibaut of Champagne**, **Louis of Blois**, and **Baldwin of Flanders** - convened at Soissons to organize the expedition.^2 They agreed to avoid the overland route through the Byzantine Empire and chose to sail to Egypt, mirroring Richard I's strategy.^2 To secure ships, the lords appointed a six-member committee, among them **Geoffrey of Villehardouin** (later the crusade's chronicler), to negotiate with maritime powers.^2

Thibaut's sudden death in 1201 CE led to **Boniface of Montferrat** - brother of **Conrad of Montferrat** who had died defending Tyre - assuming command.^2 The committee ultimately secured a fleet through Venice after Genoa refused to participate.^3

In March 1201 CE, negotiations were opened with the **Venetian Doge Enrico Dandolo**, a 90-year-old leader who had become blind.^3 Dandolo agreed to transport 33,500 crusaders - a very ambitious number.^3 The planned fleet included 4,500 knights (along with 4,500 horses), 9,000 squires, and 20,000 foot soldiers.^3 This agreement required a full year of preparation on the part of the Venetians to build numerous ships and train the sailors who would man them, all while curtailing the city's commercial activities.^3

The total cost of the contract was 85,000 silver marks - a fantastic sum for that time.^1 However, when departure time arrived in June 1202 CE, only about 12,000 people (4,000-5,000 knights and 8,000 foot soldiers) were present in Venice, far from the expected 33,500.^3 Many crusaders had chosen to sail from other ports such as Flanders, Marseille, and Genoa.^3

## Debt Crisis and Dandolo's Proposal

This situation created a severe financial crisis.^3 The Venetians had fulfilled their part of the agreement: they had prepared 50 war galleys and 450 transports - enough for three times the assembled army.^3 However, the crusaders could only pay 35,000 silver marks of the promised total of 85,000 marks.^3 Although they managed to collect an additional 14,000 marks by reducing the crusaders to extreme poverty, there was still a large shortfall.^3

This situation was disastrous for Venice, which had halted its commerce for a great length of time to prepare this expedition.^3 About 14,000 to 20,000-30,000 people (out of Venice's population of 60,000-100,000) were needed to man the entire fleet, placing further strain on the Venetian economy.^3

**Doge Dandolo**, who joined the crusade during a public ceremony in the church of San Marco, proposed a solution that would benefit Venice: the crusaders could pay their debts by helping Venice intimidate many local ports and towns down the Adriatic, culminating in an attack on the port of **Zara** in Dalmatia.^3 This city had been dominated economically by Venice throughout the 12th century but had rebelled in 1181 CE and allied itself with **King Emeric of Hungary and Croatia**.^3

## Deviation to Zara: First Attack on Fellow Christians

**King Emeric** was a Catholic who had even taken the cross in 1195 or 1196 CE.^3 Many crusaders opposed attacking Zara, and some, including a force led by **Simon V de Montfort**, refused to participate altogether and returned home or went to the Holy Land on their own.^3 While the Papal legate to the Crusade, **Cardinal Peter of Capua**, endorsed the move as necessary to prevent the crusade's complete failure, the Pope was alarmed at this development and wrote a letter to the crusading leadership threatening excommunication.^3

In 1202 CE, **Pope Innocent III**, despite wanting to secure papal authority over the Roman Orthodox Church, forbade the crusaders of Western Christendom from committing any atrocious acts against their Christian neighbors.^3 However, this letter, delivered by **Peter of Lucedio**, may not have reached the army in time.^3

The bulk of the army arrived at Zara on 10-11 November 1202 CE and the attack proceeded.^3 The citizens of Zara made reference to the fact that they were fellow Catholics by hanging banners marked with crosses from their windows and walls of the city, but nevertheless the city fell on 24 November 1202 CE after a brief siege.^3 There was extensive pillaging, and the Venetians and other crusaders came to blows over the division of the spoils.^3 The fortifications of Zara were demolished by the Venetians.^3

When **Innocent III** heard of the sack, he sent a letter to the crusaders excommunicating them and ordering them to return to their holy vows and head for Jerusalem.^3 Out of fear that this would dissolve the army, the leaders of the crusade decided not to inform their followers of this.^3 Regarding the Crusaders as having been coerced by the Venetians, in February 1203 CE he rescinded the excommunications against all non-Venetians in the expedition.^3

## Byzantine Intervention and Alexius's Offer

The situation became more complicated when **Prince Alexius IV Angelos** of Byzantium requested Crusader help.^3 This young prince was the son of **Emperor Isaac II Angelos**, who had been deposed and blinded by his brother **Alexius III Angelos** in a palace coup in 1195 CE. Alexius IV had fled to **Philip of Swabia**, brother-in-law of **Emperor Henry VI of the Holy Roman Empire**.^3

**Boniface of Montferrat** had left the fleet before it sailed from Venice to visit his cousin Philip of Swabia.^3 The reasons for his visit are a matter of debate; he may have realized the Venetians' plans and left to avoid excommunication, or he may have wanted to meet with Prince Alexius IV.^3

There, **Alexius IV** offered tempting rewards: to pay the entire debt owed to the Venetians, give 200,000 silver marks to the crusaders, 10,000 Byzantine professional troops for the Crusade, maintenance of 500 knights in the Holy Land, the service of the Byzantine navy to transport the Crusader Army to Egypt, and the placement of the Eastern Orthodox Church under the authority of the Pope, if they would sail to Constantinople and topple the reigning **Emperor Alexius III Angelos**.^3

This offer, tempting for an enterprise that was short on funds, reached the leaders of the Crusade on 1 January 1203 CE as they wintered at Zara.^3 **Doge Dandolo** was a fierce supporter of the plan; however, in his earlier capacity as an ambassador to the Byzantine Empire and someone who knew the finer details of how the empire's politics worked, it is likely he knew the promises were false and there was no hope of any Byzantine emperor raising the money promised, let alone raising the troops and giving the church to the Holy See.^3

## First Attack on Constantinople

In 1203 CE, the combined Venetian-Crusader fleet sailed toward Constantinople with 60 war galleys, 100 horse transports, and 50 large transports (the entire fleet was manned by 10,000 Venetian oarsmen and marines).^3 They also brought along 300 siege engines.^3

When the **Fourth Crusade** arrived at Constantinople on 23 June 1203 CE, the city had a population of approximately 500,000 people, a garrison of 15,000 men (including 5,000 Varangians), and a fleet of 20 galleys. Constantinople had been in existence for 874 years at the time of the Fourth Crusade and was the largest and most sophisticated city in Christendom. Almost alone amongst major medieval urban centers, it had retained the civic structures, public baths, forums, monuments, and aqueducts of classical Rome in working form.

The crusaders first attacked and were repulsed from the cities of **Chalcedon** and **Chrysopolis**, suburbs of the great city. They won a cavalry skirmish in which they were outnumbered, defeating 500 Byzantines with just 80 Frankish knights.

To take the city by force, the crusaders first needed to cross the **Bosphorus**. About 200 ships, horse transports, and galleys delivered the crusading army across the narrow strait, where **Alexius III** had lined up the Byzantine army in battle formation along the shore, north of the suburb of Galata. The Crusader knights charged straight out of the horse transports, and the Byzantine army fled south.

The **Tower of Galata**, which held the northern end of the massive chain that blocked access to the **Golden Horn**, was held by a garrison of mercenary troops of English, Danish, and Italian origin. On 6 July, the largest ship in the crusaders' fleet, the *Aquila* (Eagle), broke the chain. A section of it was then sent to Acre to boost the defenses in the Holy Land.

## Restoration of Isaac II and Alexius IV

The first attack successfully placed **Alexius IV** and his father **Isaac II** back on the throne on 1 August 1203 CE. However, these two newly restored emperors proved unable to fulfill the grand promises that Alexius had made to the Crusaders.

**Alexius IV** realized that his promises were hard to keep. **Alexius III** had managed to flee with 1,000 pounds of gold and some priceless jewels, leaving the imperial treasury short on funds. At that point the young emperor ordered the destruction and melting of valuable Roman icons in order to extract their gold and silver, but even then he could only raise 100,000 silver marks.

In the eyes of all Greeks who knew of this decision, it was a shocking sign of desperation and weak leadership, which deserved to be punished by God. The Byzantine historian **Niketas Choniates** characterized it as "the turning point towards the decline of the Roman state".

## Rebellion and Murder of Alexius IV

Forcing the populace to destroy their icons at the behest of an army of foreign schismatics did not endear **Alexius IV** to the citizens of Constantinople. In fear of his life, the co-emperor asked the crusaders to renew their contract for another six months, to end by April 1204 CE.

In January 1204 CE, the blinded and incapacitated **Isaac II** died, probably of natural causes. Opposition to his son and co-emperor **Alexius IV** had grown during the preceding months of tension and spasmodic violence in and around Constantinople. The **Byzantine Senate** elected a young noble **Nicolas Canabus** as emperor, in what was to be one of the last known acts of this ancient institution. However he declined the appointment and sought church sanctuary.

A nobleman **Alexius Doukas** (nicknamed Mourtzouphlos) became the leader of the anti-crusader faction within the Byzantine leadership. While holding the court rank of protovestilarios, Doukas had led Byzantine forces during the initial clashes with the crusaders, winning respect from both military and populace. He was accordingly well-placed to move against the increasingly isolated **Alexius IV**, whom he overthrew, imprisoned, and had strangled in early February. Doukas then was crowned as **Emperor Alexius V Doukas Mourtzouphlos**.

## Second Attack on Constantinople and Systematic Looting

The crusaders and Venetians, incensed at the murder of their supposed patron, demanded that **Mourtzouphlos** honor the contract that **Alexius IV** had promised. When the emperor refused, the Crusaders assaulted the city once again. On 8 April, **Alexius V's** army put up a strong resistance, which did much to discourage the Crusaders. The Byzantines hurled large projectiles onto the enemy siege engines, shattering many of them.

On 12 April 1204 CE, the weather conditions finally favored the crusaders. A strong northern wind aided the Venetian ships in coming close to the walls and after a short battle approximately seventy crusaders managed to enter the city. Some were able to knock holes in the walls, large enough for only a few knights at a time to crawl through; the Venetians were also successful at scaling the walls from the sea, though there was fighting with the Byzantine infantry. **The crusaders completely took the city on 13 April**.

What happened next was one of the greatest tragedies in Christian history: **systematic looting of a city that had been the center of Eastern Christian civilization for centuries**. The crusaders sacked Constantinople for three days, during which many ancient and medieval Greco-Roman works of art were stolen or ruined. Many of the civilian population of the city were killed and their property looted. Despite the threat of excommunication, the crusaders destroyed, defiled and looted the city's churches and monasteries.

**Speros Vryonis** in *Byzantium and Europe* gives a vivid account of the sack:

> "The Latin soldiery subjected the greatest city in Europe to an indescribable sack. For three days they murdered, raped, looted and destroyed on a scale which even the ancient Vandals and Goths would have found unbelievable. Constantinople had become a veritable museum of ancient and Byzantine art, an emporium of such incredible wealth that the Latins were astounded at the riches they found."

It was said that the total amount looted from Constantinople was about 900,000 silver marks. The Venetians received 150,000 silver marks that were their due, while the crusaders received 50,000 silver marks. A further 100,000 silver marks were divided evenly between the crusaders and Venetians. The remaining 500,000 silver marks were secretly kept back by many crusader knights.

The Crusaders vented their hatred for the Greeks most spectacularly in the desecration of the greatest Church in Christendom. They smashed the silver iconostasis, the icons and the holy books of **Hagia Sophia**, and seated upon the patriarchal throne a whore who sang coarse songs as they drank wine from the Church's holy vessels.

## Division of the Empire and Frankokratia

After the looting, the victors divided the Byzantine Empire among themselves in what became known as the **Partitio terrarum imperii Romaniae**. **Baldwin of Flanders** was crowned as the first Latin Emperor of Constantinople. Venice gained control over trade in the Aegean Sea and several strategic islands, including Crete. The crusaders then founded several new Crusader states, known as **Frankokratia** ("Rule of the Franks" in Greek), in former Roman territory.

The Byzantine remnants survived in three successor states: the **Empire of Nicaea** in northwestern Anatolia, the **Empire of Trebizond** on the northeastern coast of the Black Sea, and the **Despotate of Epirus** in northwestern Greece. The presence of the Latin Crusader states almost immediately led to war with the Byzantine successor states and with the **Bulgarian Empire**.

## Long-term Consequences and Impact

The **Empire of Nicaea** eventually succeeded in **recapturing Constantinople** and restoring the Byzantine Empire in July 1261 CE. However, Byzantium never fully recovered from this destruction. The Fourth Crusade is considered to have solidified the **East-West Schism**. The crusade dealt an irrevocable blow to the Byzantine Empire, contributing to its decline and fall.

All the unstable governments in the region, the Sack of Constantinople, and the thousands of deaths had left the region depleted of soldiers, resources, people and money, leaving it vulnerable to attack. Additionally, the empire had badly shrunk as it lost control of most of the Balkans, Anatolia, and Aegean islands. This made the restored empire both territorially diminished and vulnerable to **invasions from the expanding Ottomans** in the following centuries, to which the Byzantines ultimately succumbed in 1453 CE.

## Analysis and Wisdom

The Fourth Crusade shows how **political and economic interests can divert noble religious purposes**.^1 ^3 Venice successfully exploited religious fervor to achieve its commercial goals, while nobles saw opportunities to gain wealth and land.^1 ^3 **Doge Dandolo**, with his diplomatic experience in Byzantium, likely knew that Alexius IV's promises were unrealistic, yet continued to push the plan because it benefited Venice.^3

This tragedy also teaches the importance of **unity within the same religion**.^2 Internal divisions are always more destructive than external threats.^2 When fellow believers fight each other, outsiders benefit.^2 Ironically, an expedition intended to strengthen Christianity actually weakened it from within.^2

> لَا يُقَاتِلُونَكُمْ جَمِيعًا إِلَّا فِي قُرًى مُّحَصَّنَةٍ أَوْ مِن وَرَاءِ جُدُرٍ ۚ بَأْسُهُم بَيْنَهُمْ شَدِيدٌ ﴿١٤﴾
>
> *"They will not fight you all except within fortified cities or from behind walls. Their enmity among themselves is severe."* ﴾14﴿
> — QS. Al-Hasyr (59): 14

This verse reflects the condition of the Crusaders who should have been united against a common enemy, yet instead attacked fellow Christians in Constantinople. This internal division proved the truth of Allah's words about severe enmity among themselves, which ultimately weakened their overall position.^1

The Fourth Crusade also demonstrates how **individual and group ambitions** can override collective missions. **Enrico Dandolo** and Venice exploited the financial dependence of the crusaders to achieve their own geopolitical goals. This reminds us of the importance of integrity in leadership and the dangers of excessive dependence on one party.

The long-term impact of this tragedy was felt for centuries to come. The weakened post-1204 CE Constantinople became easy prey for the expanding **Ottoman** power. Thus, the Fourth Crusade paradoxically paved the way for Constantinople's conquest by **Sultan Mehmet II** in 1453 CE, ending more than a thousand years of Eastern Roman Empire history.^2

Finally, this event underscores the importance of maintaining **original objectives in any noble endeavor**. When financial and political motivations begin to dominate spiritual missions, the results are often disasters greater than the problems they sought to solve.^2.^3
