# The Fourth Crusade - Deviation to Constantinople (1202-1204 CE)

## Background

The Fourth Crusade began with the same spirit as previous expeditions: to retake the Holy Land from Muslim hands.^1 **Pope Innocent III** called for a new crusade in 1198 CE, hoping to repeat the success of the First Crusade.^1

However, from the beginning, this expedition faced serious financial problems.^1 The European nobles who joined did not have enough funds to hire a fleet from Venice.^2 The Republic of Venice, controlled by **Doge Enrico Dandolo** who was already blind and 90 years old, saw this as an opportunity for his own political interests.^1 ^2

## Agreement with Venice

Doge Dandolo proposed an alternative that would benefit Venice: instead of paying cash, the Crusaders could help Venice recapture the city of Zara on the Dalmatian coast that had rebelled.^1 This city was formerly an important Venetian colony that had fallen to Hungary.^1

Although **Pope Innocent III** forbade attacks on fellow Christians, economic pressure forced Crusader leaders to agree to the plan.^2 In 1202 CE, they attacked Zara and successfully captured it, which led the Pope to excommunicate the entire expedition.^1 ^2

## Byzantine Intervention

The situation became more complicated when **Prince Alexius Angelos** of Byzantium asked for Crusader help.^3 This young prince claimed that his uncle, Emperor Alexius III, had illegally seized the throne by blinding and imprisoning his father, Emperor Isaac II.^3

Alexius offered tempting rewards: 200,000 silver marks, 10,000 soldiers for the expedition to the Holy Land, and reunification of the Byzantine Orthodox Church with Rome.^3 This offer was too tempting to refuse, especially given the financial problems that continued to haunt the expedition.^1 ^3

## First Attack on Constantinople

In 1203 CE, the combined Venetian-Crusader fleet sailed toward Constantinople, capital of Byzantium.^1 The city that had stood for almost 900 years as a fortress of Orthodox Christianity now faced threats from fellow Christians.^2

The first attack successfully placed Alexius IV and his father Isaac II back on the throne.^2 However, these two newly restored emperors proved unable to fulfill the grand promises that Alexius had made to the Crusaders.^2 ^3 Tensions began to rise within the city.^2

## Massacre and Looting

When the people of Constantinople rebelled and killed Alexius IV in January 1204 CE, the Crusaders had reason to launch a second attack.^3 This time they were determined to capture the city completely.^3

On April 13, 1204 CE, the walls of Constantinople finally fell.^1 What happened next was one of the greatest tragedies in Christian history: systematic looting of a city that had been the center of Eastern Christian civilization for centuries.^2

The Crusaders looted Hagia Sophia, smashed golden altars, and carried precious artifacts back to Western Europe.^2 Many ancient manuscripts and priceless artworks were destroyed or lost forever.^2 It is estimated that hundreds of thousands of Constantinople residents were killed or enslaved.^2

## Division of the Byzantine Empire

After the looting, the victors divided the Byzantine Empire among themselves.^3 **Baldwin of Flanders** was crowned as the first Latin Emperor of Constantinople.^3 Venice gained control over trade in the Aegean Sea and several strategic islands.^1 ^3

The Byzantine remnants survived in three successor states: the Empire of Nicaea, the Empire of Trebizond, and the Despotate of Epirus.^1 Although the Empire of Nicaea eventually succeeded in retaking Constantinople in 1261 CE, Byzantium never fully recovered from this destruction.^1 ^2

## Long-term Consequences

The Fourth Crusade became a turning point in Christian history.^2 The split between the Western (Catholic) and Eastern (Orthodox) Churches became deeper and permanent.^2 Trust between these two branches of Christianity was completely destroyed.^2

Ironically, an expedition intended to strengthen Christianity actually weakened it from within.^2 The weakened post-Latin conquest Constantinople became more vulnerable to Ottoman expansion centuries later.^1 ^2 Thus, the Fourth Crusade indirectly paved the way for Constantinople's conquest by **Sultan Mehmet II** in 1453 CE.^2

## Analysis and Wisdom

The Fourth Crusade shows how **political and economic interests** can divert noble religious purposes.^1 ^3 Venice successfully exploited religious fervor to achieve its commercial goals, while nobles saw opportunities to gain wealth and land.^1 ^3

This tragedy also teaches the importance of **unity within the same religion**.^2 Internal divisions are always more destructive than external threats.^2 When fellow believers fight each other, outsiders benefit.^2
