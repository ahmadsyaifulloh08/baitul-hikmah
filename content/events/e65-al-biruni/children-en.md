## 🧑 Who was he?

**Al-Biruni** was a genius scientist born in 973 CE in Khwarazm (now Uzbekistan). His name comes from the word "birun" meaning "suburb" because he was born outside the city center. From childhood, Al-Biruni showed extraordinary intelligence and could speak 6 languages: Arabic, Persian, Sanskrit, Greek, Hebrew, and Syriac!

Al-Biruni lived for 75 years and wrote more than 140 books about various sciences. He wasn't just good in one field, but was an expert in many fields at once—astronomy, mathematics, geography, history, pharmacology, and anthropology. Scientists call him a "polymath" (expert in many sciences).

## ⭐ What did he do?

Al-Biruni did extraordinary things in the world of science! He successfully measured the Earth's circumference with very high accuracy—his result was only 200 km different from modern calculations. His method was clever too: using trigonometry and observation from mountaintops, much more practical than previous methods.

He also wrote "*Kitab al-Hind*" after spending years learning Sanskrit and associating with Indian philosophers. This book is like a complete encyclopedia about Indian culture, religion, and science. Al-Biruni was very objective—he didn't mix personal prejudices into his research.

In pharmacology, he documented more than 700 types of medicines with detailed explanations about their properties and usage. He also designed various sophisticated scientific instruments to make his measurements more accurate.

## 💡 Why was he extraordinary?

Al-Biruni was a pioneer of modern scientific method! He didn't immediately believe old theories, but always tested them with observation and experiments. When he found sea fossils on land, he concluded that the place was once the sea floor—geological understanding very advanced for his time.

What made him special was his ability to combine various traditions of knowledge. He learned from Greek, Indian, Persian, and Islamic traditions, then created a more complete synthesis. This interdisciplinary approach was only commonly done by modern scientists.

His intellectual dialogue with Ibn Sina (Avicenna) about Aristotelian philosophy showed that Muslim scientists weren't afraid to argue and test ideas. The empirical Al-Biruni versus the rationalist Ibn Sina—healthy discussion that advanced science.

## 📖 Evidence

The Qur'an teaches that humans are born without knowledge, but given the ability to learn:

> وَاللَّهُ أَخْرَجَكُم مِّن بُطُونِ أُمَّهَاتِكُمْ لَا تَعْلَمُونَ شَيْئًا وَجَعَلَ لَكُمُ السَّمْعَ وَالْأَبْصَارَ وَالْأَفْئِدَةَ لَعَلَّكُمْ تَشْكُرُونَ ﴿٧٨﴾
>
> *"And Allah brought you forth from the wombs of your mothers knowing nothing, and He gave you hearing and sight and hearts that you might give thanks." ﴾78﴿*
> — QS. An-Nahl (16): 78

Al-Biruni maximally embodied this verse's teaching. He used his eyes for observation, ears to hear various traditions of knowledge, and mind to analyze all the information he received. His linguistic abilities allowed him to "hear" wisdom from various civilizations.

Al-Biruni's humility in learning from other traditions, including Hinduism, showed that seeking scientific truth is a universal task that transcends religious and cultural boundaries. In Islam, seeking knowledge is worship, wherever its source.
