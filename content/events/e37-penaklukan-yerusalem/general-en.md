# The Conquest of Jerusalem / Al-Quds (637 CE)

## Context: The Holy City of Three Faiths

Jerusalem — known in the Islamic tradition as *al-Quds* (The Holy) or *Iliya'* (from the Roman name *Aelia Capitolina*) — was no ordinary city. It was sacred to three great religions: Judaism, Christianity, and Islam. For Muslims, Jerusalem held a special station as the first *qiblah* before the direction of prayer was changed to the Ka'bah, and as the place from which the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ ascended to the heavens during the *Isra' wa al-Mi'raj*.^1 ^2

The Quran immortalizes this sacred bond^4:

> سُبْحَانَ الَّذِي أَسْرَىٰ بِعَبْدِهِ لَيْلًا مِنَ الْمَسْجِدِ الْحَرَامِ إِلَى الْمَسْجِدِ الْأَقْصَى الَّذِي بَارَكْنَا حَوْلَهُ ﴿١﴾
>
> *"Glory be to the One Who took His servant on a night journey from the Sacred Mosque to the Farthest Mosque, whose surroundings We have blessed. ﴾1﴿"*
> — QS. Al-Isra (17): 1

In 637 CE, Jerusalem was under Byzantine (Eastern Roman) rule. The city had been held by Byzantium for centuries, though it briefly fell to Sasanian Persia in 614 CE before being recaptured by Emperor Heraclius in 629 CE. Its population was predominantly Christian, with a Jewish community that had been expelled by Rome and only partially permitted to return.^1 ^4

After the Muslim victory at the Battle of Yarmuk (636 CE), which shattered the main Byzantine force in Syria, cities fell one by one to the Islamic armies. **Abu Ubaidah ibn al-Jarrah**, supreme commander of Muslim forces in Syria, besieged Jerusalem. Al-Tabari and al-Baladhuri record that the siege lasted several months without major violence — the Muslim army did not storm the city but waited patiently.^1 ^2

## Events: The Caliph's Arrival That Changed History

### Patriarch Sophronius: "I Will Surrender Only to the Caliph"

**Patriarch Sophronius**, the highest authority of the Orthodox Church in Jerusalem, recognized that military defense was no longer viable. Yet he set an unusual condition: he would surrender the city only to the caliph of the Muslims in person — not to any general.^1 ^2

Abu Ubaidah sent word to Caliph **Umar ibn al-Khattab** in Madinah, relaying the Patriarch's condition. Al-Tabari records that Umar consulted the companions. **Ali ibn Abi Talib** advised Umar to go himself, given Jerusalem's importance in the Islamic tradition.^1

### A Journey That Astonished the World

Umar set out from Madinah to Jerusalem with a remarkably humble retinue. Al-Baladhuri and Ibn Kathir relate the now-legendary details: Umar brought only **one camel and one servant**. The two took turns riding — when it was the servant's turn to ride, Umar walked on foot, leading the camel.^2 ^3

Ibn Kathir in *Al-Bidayah wa al-Nihayah* records that as the party neared Jerusalem, it happened to be the servant's turn on the camel while Umar walked barefoot, sandals in hand, through a muddy stretch. Abu Ubaidah, who had come out to meet him, felt embarrassed and said: "O Commander of the Faithful, the people of this city will see you like this..." Umar replied:^3

*"O Abu Ubaidah, had anyone other than you said that, I would have punished him. We are a people whom Allah honored through Islam. If we seek honor from anything else, Allah will humiliate us."*

### The Covenant of Aelia — *al-'Uhdah al-'Umariyyah*

When Umar arrived in Jerusalem, Patriarch Sophronius received him and handed over the keys of the city. Al-Tabari records that Umar then issued a written guarantee to the people of Jerusalem known as the **Covenant of Aelia** (*al-'Uhdah al-'Umariyyah*), one of the most important documents in the history of religious tolerance:^1

*"In the name of Allah, the Most Merciful, the Most Compassionate. This is the guarantee of safety given by the servant of Allah, Umar, Commander of the Faithful, to the people of Iliya' (Jerusalem). He grants them safety for their lives, their property, their churches, their crosses, their sick and their healthy, and for all their co-religionists. Their churches shall not be occupied, nor destroyed, nor diminished — neither the churches themselves, their grounds, their crosses, nor their property. They shall not be compelled to abandon their religion, and none of them shall be harmed."*

Al-Baladhuri adds that the covenant also required the people of Jerusalem to pay the *jizyah* (protection tax) as in other cities of Syria, and to harbor no Roman spies.^2

### Umar at Al-Aqsa Mosque

Al-Tabari and Ibn Kathir recount that after the covenant was signed, Sophronius took Umar on a tour of the holy city. When prayer time arrived and they were inside the Church of the Holy Sepulchre, Sophronius invited Umar to pray inside the church.^1 ^3

Umar gently declined. He said: *"Were I to pray here, the Muslims after me would seize this place and say: Umar prayed here. Then they would turn it into a mosque."* Umar instead prayed outside the church, a few steps from its entrance.^1 ^3 This decision — refusing to pray inside a church in order to protect Christian rights — became a symbol of tolerance remembered to this day.

Umar then searched for the site of **Al-Aqsa Mosque** — the sacred place mentioned in the Quran. He found that the area (now known as the Haram al-Sharif / Temple Mount) had been turned into a rubbish dump by the Roman authorities as a deliberate insult to Jewish tradition.^1 ^3 Umar himself began clearing the site with his own hands, and the companions followed. It was here that a simple mosque was later built — the precursor to Al-Aqsa Mosque.

## Key Sayings and Scriptural Evidence

Beyond the dialogues already mentioned, several other significant moments stand out:

When Umar beheld the splendor of Jerusalem and compared it with the simplicity of Madinah, he remembered Allah's promise and said, as recorded by al-Tabari:^1

*"All praise belongs to Allah, Who has honored Islam and the Muslims."*

Ibn Kathir also records that when Umar entered the neglected, filthy precinct of Al-Aqsa, he proclaimed the *takbir* and said: *"By Allah, this is the mosque that the Messenger of Allah ﷺ described to me."*^3

The Prophet ﷺ himself affirmed the special status of Al-Aqsa Mosque:

> لَا تُشَدُّ الرِّحَالُ إِلَّا إِلَى ثَلَاثَةِ مَسَاجِدَ: الْمَسْجِدِ الْحَرَامِ، وَمَسْجِدِي هَذَا، وَالْمَسْجِدِ الْأَقْصَى
>
> *"No journey should be undertaken (for worship) except to three mosques: the Sacred Mosque, this mosque of mine (the Prophet's Mosque), and Al-Aqsa Mosque."*
> — Bukhari, no. 1189; Muslim, no. 1397

## Analysis and Lessons: A Model of Tolerance for All Time

### A Conquest Without Bloodshed

Umar's conquest of Jerusalem was one of the rare instances in history when a holy city changed hands **without bloodshed**. There was no massacre, no looting, no forced conversion. Compare this with the Crusader capture of Jerusalem in 1099 CE — marked by mass slaughter of Muslim, Jewish, and even Eastern Christian inhabitants.^1 ^2

### The Covenant of Aelia as a Charter of Rights

The Covenant of Aelia anticipated modern concepts of religious freedom and minority rights by over a millennium. It guaranteed:
- Safety of life and property for non-Muslim residents
- Preservation of churches and places of worship
- Freedom of religion without coercion
- Equal legal protection

Western historians, including Karen Armstrong in *Jerusalem: One City, Three Faiths*, acknowledge that the Islamic conquest of Jerusalem ushered in an era of religious tolerance far surpassing that of previous rulers.

### Simplicity as Strength

Umar's arrival on foot, in a patched robe, without a royal entourage, created a dramatic contrast with Byzantine splendor. Yet it was precisely this simplicity that impressed the people of Jerusalem — long accustomed to the opulence and oppression of Roman rulers — and convinced them that their new sovereign was a just man.^3

### Protecting Christian Rights

Umar's refusal to pray inside the Church of the Holy Sepulchre was more than a courtesy — it was legal precedent. By declining to pray there, Umar prevented any future Muslim claim to the church. This demonstrates the long-term vision of a leader who considered not only the present moment but the consequences of his decisions for generations to come.^1 ^3

### Restoring Jewish Rights

Al-Tabari records that after the conquest, Umar permitted Jews to return and reside in Jerusalem — a right that had been denied by Roman and Byzantine rulers for centuries.^1 This made the Islamic conquest a moment of liberation not only for Muslims, but also for the Jewish community long exiled from their holy city.
