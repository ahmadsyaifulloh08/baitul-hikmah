# Spanish Inquisition - Book Burning and Destruction of Islamic Heritage

## Historical Context

In 1478 CE, the **Spanish Inquisition** was officially established by King Ferdinand and Queen Isabella with the approval of Pope Sixtus IV. This institution became one of the darkest episodes in the history of civilization, particularly for Muslim communities in the Iberian Peninsula. The Inquisition not only targeted individuals, but also systematically destroyed the Islamic intellectual heritage that had been built over nearly eight centuries in Al-Andalus.^1

This period marked the end of the golden age of Islam in Europe. Granada, the last Muslim kingdom in Iberia, fell in 1492 CE. After the conquest, Spanish Christians began implementing policies of forced conversion and cultural annihilation against the remaining Muslim (Mudéjar) and Jewish populations.^2

## Cardinal Cisneros and Book Burning

### Systematic Destruction

**Francisco Jiménez de Cisneros**, Cardinal and Confessor to Queen Isabella, led a systematic campaign to destroy Islamic manuscripts and books. In 1499 CE, he ordered the mass burning of more than one million Islamic manuscripts in Plaza Bib-Rambla, Granada. This event was one of the greatest acts of cultural vandalism in human history.^3

This burning was not merely an act of religious fanaticism, but a strategic effort to eliminate Islamic intellectual tradition from Europe. The manuscripts burned included:

**Scientific works**: Mathematical, astronomical, medical, and philosophical works. **Religious texts**: Copies of the Quran, commentaries, hadith collections, and fiqh. **Literary works**: Arabic-Andalusi poetry, history, and literature. **Translation works**: Translations of Greek classics into Arabic that were later translated into Latin.

### Impact on Knowledge Preservation

This destruction had devastating consequences for knowledge preservation. Many scientific works that existed only in Arabic manuscripts were lost forever. Works by Islamic Andalusi scholars like **Ibn Rushd**, **Ibn Arabi**, and **Ibn Hazm** - who had influenced Europe's intellectual development - began to disappear from libraries.^4

Ironically, many of these Islamic manuscripts contained translations and commentaries on Greek philosophical and scientific works that later became the foundation of the European Renaissance. This destruction effectively severed a crucial link in the intellectual chain of transmission.

## Forced Conversion and Moriscos

### Policy of Religious Coercion

After the conquest of Granada, Muslims were given an ultimatum: convert to Christianity, exile, or face death. Many chose to convert outwardly for survival while secretly maintaining Islamic practices. These converted Muslims were known as **Moriscos** (little Moors).^1

However, even after conversion, Moriscos continued facing suspicion and persecution. The Inquisition systematically investigated them for signs of "relapse" into Islam. Practices such as:

**Ritual cleanliness**: Frequent bathing or washing before prayer. **Dietary habits**: Avoiding pork or wine. **Calendar observance**: Fasting during Ramadan or celebrating Islamic holidays. **Language use**: Speaking Arabic or reading Arabic texts.

### Underground Islamic Culture

Despite persecution, a significant portion of the Morisco population successfully maintained Islamic identity in secret. They developed sophisticated networks to:

**Preserve religious texts**: Hiding Qurans and Islamic books in false walls or buried locations. **Maintain religious practices**: Conducting secret prayers, fasting, and Islamic ceremonies. **Transmit knowledge**: Teaching Arabic literacy and Islamic theology to children. **Economic cooperation**: Supporting each other through Islamic commercial networks.^2

## The Aljamiado Literature

### Unique Cultural Response

Faced with the prohibition of Arabic texts, Morisco communities developed a unique literary tradition called **Aljamiado** - Islamic texts written in Spanish or Aragonese using Arabic script. This was a remarkable example of cultural resistance and adaptation.^3

Aljamiado works included:

**Religious guidance**: Islamic theology and jurisprudence written for communities losing access to Arabic texts. **Historical accounts**: Narratives about Islamic history and prophecies. **Literary works**: Poetry and stories maintaining Islamic themes. **Practical manuals**: Instructions for Islamic rituals and practices.

The development of Aljamiado literature showed the incredible resilience of Islamic culture even under extreme oppression. These texts were often hidden in walls, buried underground, or concealed within other books.

## Economic and Social Impact

### Disruption of Islamic Economy

Islamic commercial networks that had been the backbone of Andalusi economy were systematically dismantled. Muslim merchants, artisans, and professionals were forced to abandon established businesses or convert. Many chose exile, leading to massive brain drain.^4

Industries traditionally dominated by Muslims - such as silk production, metalwork, leather crafts, and agriculture - experienced severe decline. Knowledge and techniques that had been passed down through generations were lost or suppressed.

### Demographic Transformation

Forced conversions and mass emigration drastically changed the demographic composition of the Iberian Peninsula. Communities that had existed for centuries were dispersed or destroyed. The social fabric that had evolved during the period of convivencia (coexistence) among Christians, Muslims, and Jews was torn apart.

## International Ramifications

### Refugee Crisis

Persecution in Spain triggered massive refugee movements. Andalusi Muslims fled to:

**North Africa**: Morocco, Tunisia, and Algeria received hundreds of thousands of refugees. **Ottoman Empire**: Many found sanctuary in Ottoman territories in the Balkans and Eastern Mediterranean. **Other European territories**: Some sought refuge in more tolerant regions.^1

These refugees brought with them valuable skills, knowledge, and cultural traditions that enriched their new homelands. Paradoxically, Spain's loss became a gain for other regions.

### Diplomatic Consequences

Inquisition policies created tensions with the Ottoman Empire, which saw itself as the protector of Muslims worldwide. Ottoman Sultan **Bayezid II** sent ships to evacuate Spanish Muslims and offered them settlements in Ottoman territories.^2

## Resistance and Revolts

### Alpujarras Rebellion

Escalating persecution finally triggered open rebellion. The **War of the Alpujarras** (1568-1571 CE) was the last major attempt by Moriscos to assert their rights and maintain their cultural identity. Led by figures like **Aben Humeya** (Fernando de Córdoba y Valor), the rebellion showed the desperate courage of an oppressed community.^3

Although ultimately unsuccessful, the rebellion demonstrated that Islamic identity remained strong among Morisco populations despite decades of persecution. The savage suppression of the rebellion led to even more severe restrictions and eventual expulsion.

### Final Expulsion (1609-1614)

Under **Philip III**, Spain implemented the final solution: mass expulsion of all Moriscos from the peninsula. Approximately 300,000-400,000 people were expelled, effectively ending eight centuries of Islamic presence in the Iberian Peninsula.^4

## Legacy and Lessons

### Loss for Humanity

The destruction of Islamic civilization in Spain represents one of history's greatest cultural catastrophes. The loss of manuscripts, architectural monuments, and intellectual traditions impoverished not only the Islamic world but all humanity. Many scientific and philosophical works that could have advanced human knowledge were lost forever.

### Patterns of Intolerance

The Spanish Inquisition established templates for religious persecution that would be repeated throughout history. The methods used - systematic discrimination, forced conversion, cultural destruction, and mass deportation - became models for later episodes of ethnic cleansing.

### Lessons about Coexistence

The tragedy of the Spanish Inquisition reminds us of the fragility of multicultural societies and the importance of protecting minority rights. The previous period of convivencia in Al-Andalus had shown that different religious communities could coexist and mutually enrich each other.
