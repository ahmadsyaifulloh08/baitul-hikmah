# Al-Azhar University Founded (970 CE)

## Background: Cairo Under the Fatimid Dynasty

In the 10th century CE, Egypt was under the rule of the **Fatimid Dynasty**, which followed the Ismaili Shia school and claimed descent from **Fatimah az-Zahra**, daughter of Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, through the lineage of **Imam Ismail ibn Ja'far as-Sadiq**.^1 This genealogical claim became the foundation of their political and spiritual legitimacy in building a rival caliphate to challenge Abbasid hegemony in Baghdad.^2

After conquering Egypt from the Ikhshidid Dynasty in 969 CE, **Jauhar al-Siqilli** (Jauhar from Sicily), a brilliant general from Sicily assigned by **Caliph Al-Mu'izz li-Din Allah**, immediately began building a new city north of Fustat (Old Cairo).^3 This city was named "Al-Qahirah al-Mu'izziyah" (The Victorious Cairo of Al-Mu'izz), which became known as Cairo.^4

## Jauhar al-Siqilli: Architect of Fatimid Civilization

**Jauhar al-Siqilli** (d. 992 CE) was one of the most influential figures in Fatimid Dynasty history.^5 Born in Sicily as a slave (*mamluk*), he demonstrated extraordinary talents in military strategy and administration that elevated him to the highest commander position in the Fatimid hierarchy.^1 His abilities extended beyond military affairs to include urbanization vision and religious institution development.^6

In the midst of building this new capital, Jauhar al-Siqilli planned the construction of a grand mosque that would become a symbol of Fatimid power and a center for spreading their teachings.^4 The mosque's location was strategically chosen: at the heart of the new city, easily accessible from the caliph's palace, and properly oriented toward Makkah.^5

## Construction of Al-Azhar Mosque and Institution

### Construction Phase (970-972 CE)

Construction of Al-Azhar Mosque began in Jumada al-Ula 359 AH (April 970 CE) and was completed in Ramadan 361 AH (June 972 CE).^4 The project was executed by teams of the best architects and craftsmen from various regions of the Fatimid caliphate, including artisans from Ifriqiya (Tunisia), Sicily, and Andalusia.^7

The name "Al-Azhar" derives from "Az-Zahra" (The Radiant One), referring to **Fatimah az-Zahra**, daughter of Prophet Muhammad ﷺ.^2 This naming choice was not coincidental but a theological and political statement affirming Fatimid claims as legitimate descendants of the Prophet through Fatimah and Ali ibn Abi Talib.^3

The mosque was built with architecture combining Fatimid style with local Egyptian elements.^5 With an initial area of about 1,500 square meters, it was designed not only as a place of worship but also as a center for learning and religious discussion.^8

### Inauguration and Early Functions

The first Friday prayer at Al-Azhar Mosque was held on 7 Ramadan 361 AH (June 22, 972 CE).^4 This ceremony was led directly by **Caliph Al-Mu'izz li-Din Allah** who had moved from Mahdiyyah (Tunisia) to Cairo in 973 CE.^9

However, Al-Azhar's educational function only began to develop formally during **Caliph Al-Aziz Billah's** reign (975-996 CE).^10 Fatimid *qadis* (judges) and scholars began holding regular *halaqah* (study circles), teaching Quranic interpretation, hadith, jurisprudence, and specific Ismaili doctrines.^5

## Fatimid Era: Center of Shia Learning

### Reign of Caliph Al-Aziz Billah (975-996 CE)

**Caliph Al-Aziz Billah** was the Fatimid ruler most instrumental in developing Al-Azhar as an educational institution.^2 With a strong intellectual background, he understood that Fatimid supremacy depended not only on military strength but also on intellectual and spiritual superiority.^11

Under his leadership, Al-Azhar began attracting prominent scholars from various regions of the Islamic world.^3 A scholarship (*stipend*) system was introduced to support students from outside Egypt. Professors (*ustadz*) received regular salaries from the state treasury, enabling them to focus on academic activities without economic concerns.^6

### Reign of Caliph Al-Hakim bi Amr Allah (996-1021 CE)

During **Caliph Al-Hakim bi Amr Allah's** reign, Al-Azhar received even greater support despite this caliph's controversial reputation.^7 Al-Hakim established **"Dar al-Hikmah"** (House of Wisdom) in 1005 CE as a library and research center complementing Al-Azhar's functions.^8 Dar al-Hikmah housed over 600,000 manuscripts in various fields of knowledge and was open to the public, including non-Muslims.^1

## Transition Era: From Fatimid to Ayyubid

### Fall of the Fatimid Dynasty (1171 CE)

When **Salah al-Din al-Ayyubi** (Saladin) overthrew the Fatimid Dynasty in 1171 CE, a fundamental sectarian change occurred at Al-Azhar.^8 Saladin applied a strategy of "gradual deradicalization" by not forcibly closing Al-Azhar but slowly changing its orientation and curriculum.^11

This process took nearly two decades (1171-1193 CE) and was only completed after Saladin's death.^2 The strategy proved effective as it successfully changed Al-Azhar's orientation without triggering rebellion or social division.^9

## Mamluk Era: Consolidation and Expansion

### Mamluk Bahri Dynasty (1250-1382 CE)

During the **Mamluk Bahri Dynasty**, Al-Azhar experienced massive revitalization that made it the leading Sunni Islamic learning center in the world.^2 Sultan **Baibars** (1260-1277 CE) and his successors provided consistent financial support not only for daily operations but also for expansion and modernization programs.^12

### Mamluk Burji Dynasty (1382-1517 CE)

The Mamluk Burji era marked the peak of Al-Azhar's glory as an Islamic educational institution.^9 **Sultan al-Zahir Barquq** (1382-1399 CE) conducted major renovations and expanded Al-Azhar's buildings from 1,500 square meters to nearly 12,000 square meters.^4 This expansion included additional student dormitories (*riwaq*), a larger library, and specialized seminar rooms.^7

## Ijazah System: Academic Credential Innovation

The **ijazah** (certificate/teaching license) system developed by Al-Azhar became a model for other Islamic institutions and even provided inspiration for European university systems.^6 *Ijazah* was not merely a graduation certificate but an intellectual *chain of transmission* (*sanad*) connecting a student to a long scholarly tradition back to its original source.^8

Al-Azhar developed various types of ijazah according to level and specialization, including *Ijazah al-Sama'* (certificate of hearing and memorizing), *Ijazah al-Qira'ah* (certificate of reading and understanding), *Ijazah al-Riwayah* (license to narrate hadith), *Ijazah al-Ifta'* (license to issue fatwas), and *Ijazah al-Tadris* (license to teach).^2

## Al-Azhar's Distinctive Methodology

### Halaqah System and Knowledge Transmission

Al-Azhar developed a unique learning system through **halaqah** — study circles where a shaykh would sit leaning against one of the mosque's pillars, surrounded by students in concentric formation.^12 This system allowed interactive discussions and more personal knowledge transmission compared to one-way lecture systems.^2

### Integration of Knowledge and Spirituality

>
> *"Allah will raise those who have believed among you and those who were given knowledge, by degrees. And Allah is Acquainted with what you do."* ﴾11﴿

Al-Azhar became a pioneer in integrating various branches of Islamic knowledge, embodying Allah's promise to elevate the status of those endowed with knowledge. Students had to master "instrumental sciences" (*nahw*, *sarf*, *mantiq*) as foundation for understanding higher sciences like *tafsir*, *hadith*, and *fiqh*.^13

## Global Influence and Alumni Networks

### Center of Islamic World Learning

From the 13th century, Al-Azhar began attracting students from throughout the Islamic world and became the primary reference for other Islamic educational institutions. Students came not only for formal education but also for spiritual experience and networking that would benefit them throughout their lives.^2

Al-Azhar developed a **riwaq** (dormitory) system based on regional origin to accommodate international students. Each riwaq had unique characteristics reflecting the cultural and intellectual traditions of its region of origin, including *Riwaq al-Atrak* (for Turkish and Central Asian students), *Riwaq al-Magharibah* (for North African students), and *Riwaq al-Jawiyin* (for Southeast Asian students).^10

### Great Alumni Figures

**Imam al-Suyuti** (1445-1505 CE), one of Al-Azhar's greatest scholars, wrote more than 500 works in various fields of Islamic knowledge. His works such as *"Tafsir al-Jalalayn"* (with al-Mahalli), *"Al-Itqan fi Ulum al-Quran"*, and *"Husn al-Muhadarah fi Ta'rikh Misr wa al-Qahirah"* remain primary references today and are studied in Islamic schools worldwide.^1

**Ibn Hajar al-Asqalani** (1372-1449 CE), though born in Damascus, completed his education at Al-Azhar and later taught there.^4 His work *"Fath al-Bari Sharh Sahih al-Bukhari"* is considered the best commentary on Sahih al-Bukhari and is still widely studied.^5

## Role in Modern Islamic Reform Movements

### Era of Muhammad Abduh and Rashid Rida

In the late 19th and early 20th centuries, Al-Azhar became the center of Islamic reform movements (*islah*) through figures like **Muhammad Abduh** (1849-1905 CE) and **Rashid Rida** (1865-1935 CE). They advocated for reform in Al-Azhar's teaching methods and curriculum to make them more relevant to modern challenges without losing traditional roots.^4

**Muhammad Abduh**, who became Mufti of Egypt and later Rector of Al-Azhar, introduced several important reforms including curriculum modernization (adding mathematics, natural sciences, and foreign languages), teaching methods (introducing class systems and structured examinations), management (administrative reorganization with more modern systems), and library improvements (cataloging and digitizing ancient manuscripts).^11

## Contemporary Legacy and Continuity

### Al-Azhar as Global Institution

In the contemporary era, Al-Azhar not only survived for over a thousand years but continues to develop and adapt to modern challenges.^10 From an institution originally following Ismaili Shia teachings, it transformed into a bastion of Sunni Islam, and now a modern university that maintains its classical scholarly traditions.^1

Currently, Al-Azhar has over 450,000 students from 110 countries, making it one of the world's largest universities.^12 Al-Azhar campuses are spread across various cities in Egypt, and its distance education programs reach students worldwide.^4

### Authority in Fatwas and Islamic Law

Al-Azhar remains the primary reference for Islamic law, especially in the Shafi'i school dominant in Egypt and Southeast Asia. **Dar al-Ifta** (Fatwa Institution) of Al-Azhar issues thousands of fatwas annually that are read and followed by Muslims worldwide.^2

### Preservation of Islamic Intellectual Heritage

Al-Azhar's library houses over 350,000 ancient manuscripts and millions of books in various languages, making it one of the world's largest Islamic libraries.^9 Many classical works are preserved only at Al-Azhar, including rare manuscripts from the Fatimid, Ayyubid, and Mamluk eras.^6

In recent decades, Al-Azhar launched a massive digitization program to convert ancient manuscripts to digital format, not only for preservation but also to make Islamic intellectual heritage accessible to researchers and students worldwide.^10
