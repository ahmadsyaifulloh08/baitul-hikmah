# Al-Azhar University Founded

## Background: Cairo Under the Fatimid Dynasty

In the 10th century CE, Egypt was under the rule of the **Fatimid Dynasty**, which followed the Ismaili Shia school.^1 After conquering Egypt from the Ikhshidid Dynasty in 969 CE, **Jauhar al-Siqilli**, a general from the Maghreb assigned by Caliph Al-Mu'izz, immediately began building a new city north of Fustat (Old Cairo) named "Al-Qahirah al-Mu'izziyah" (The Victorious Cairo of Al-Mu'izz).^2

In the midst of building this new capital, Jauhar al-Siqilli also planned the construction of a grand mosque that would become a symbol of Fatimid power and a center for spreading their teachings.^3 This mosque would later develop into one of the most influential Islamic educational institutions in history.

## Construction of Al-Azhar Mosque and University

### Construction Phase (970-972 CE)

Construction of Al-Azhar Mosque began in Jumada al-Ula 359 AH (April 970 CE) and was completed in Ramadan 361 AH (June 972 CE).^4 The name "Al-Azhar" derives from "Az-Zahra" (The Radiant One), referring to **Fatimah az-Zahra**, the daughter of Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, in accordance with the Fatimid Dynasty's claim of descent from Fatimah.

The mosque was built with architecture combining Fatimid style with local Egyptian elements.^5 With an initial area of about 1,500 square meters, the mosque was designed not only as a place of worship but also as a center for learning and religious discussion.

### Transformation into Educational Institution

The first Friday prayer at Al-Azhar Mosque was held on 7 Ramadan 361 AH (June 22, 972 CE).^6 However, its educational function only began to develop formally during the reign of **Caliph Al-Aziz Billah** (975-996 CE). Fatimid qadis (judges) and scholars began holding regular halaqah (study circles).

During **Caliph Al-Hakim bi Amr Allah's** reign (996-1021 CE), Al-Azhar received greater support.^7 This controversial caliph, despite being known for his extreme policies, gave serious attention to developing educational institutions. He established "Dar al-Hikmah" (House of Wisdom) as a library and research center complementing Al-Azhar's functions.

## Sectarian Change and Consolidation

### The Ayyubid Era

When **Salah al-Din al-Ayyubi** (Saladin) overthrew the Fatimid Dynasty in 1171 CE, a significant sectarian change occurred.^8 Al-Azhar, which had been a center for spreading Ismaili Shia teachings, was gradually transformed into a Sunni institution. This process did not happen immediately, but through the gradual replacement of teachers and curriculum.

Saladin temporarily suspended formal educational activities at Al-Azhar and returned its primary function to that of a mosque.^9 However, informal learning traditions continued through scholars loyal to the new government.

### The Mamluk Era

During the **Mamluk Dynasty** (1250-1517 CE), Al-Azhar experienced massive revitalization.^10 Sultan **Baibars** (1260-1277 CE) and his successors provided consistent financial support to develop Al-Azhar as a leading Sunni Islamic learning center.

**Sultan al-Zahir Barquq** (1382-1399 CE) conducted major renovations and expanded Al-Azhar's buildings.^11 During this period, a formal madrasah system was implemented with structured curriculum covering Quranic sciences, Hadith, Fiqh, Arabic language, and Mantiq (logic).

## Al-Azhar's Distinctive Methodology and Curriculum

### Halaqah and Ijazah Systems

Al-Azhar developed a unique learning system through "halaqah" - study circles where a shaykh would sit leaning against one of the mosque's pillars, surrounded by students.^12 This system allowed interactive discussions and more personal knowledge transmission.

The "ijazah" (certificate) system developed by Al-Azhar became a model for other Islamic institutions. A student had to master entire texts in a particular field and receive direct approval from their shaykh to be able to teach or issue fatwas.

### Integration of Islamic Sciences

> يَرْفَعِ اللَّهُ الَّذِينَ آمَنُوا مِنكُمْ وَالَّذِينَ أُوتُوا الْعِلْمَ دَرَجَاتٍ ﴿١١﴾
>
> *"Allah will raise those who have believed among you and those who were given knowledge, by degrees."* ﴾11﴿
> — QS. Al-Mujadalah (58): 11^13

Al-Azhar became a pioneer in integrating various branches of Islamic knowledge, embodying Allah's promise to elevate the status of those endowed with knowledge. Students not only studied one field but had to master the "instrumental sciences" (nahw, sarf, mantiq) as a foundation for understanding higher sciences like tafsir, hadith, and fiqh.

## Global Influence and Scholarly Networks

### Center of Islamic World Learning

From the 13th century, Al-Azhar began attracting students from throughout the Islamic world.^14 Students came from Andalusia, West Africa, Central Asia, and the Archipelago to seek knowledge. Al-Azhar graduates then spread the knowledge they acquired to their regions of origin, creating extensive scholarly networks.

**Imam al-Suyuti** (1445-1505 CE), one of Al-Azhar's greatest scholars, wrote more than 500 works in various fields of Islamic knowledge.^15 His works such as "Tafsir al-Jalalayn" (with al-Mahalli) and "Al-Itqan fi Ulum al-Quran" remain primary references today.

### Role in Islamic Reform Movements

In the 19th and 20th centuries, Al-Azhar became the center of Islamic reform movements through figures like **Muhammad Abduh** and **Rashid Rida**.^16 They advocated for reform in Al-Azhar's teaching methods and curriculum to make them more relevant to modern challenges.

## Legacy and Continuity

Al-Azhar not only survived for more than a thousand years but continued to develop and adapt. From an institution originally following Ismaili Shia teachings, it transformed into a bastion of Sunni Islam, and now a modern university that maintains its classical scholarly traditions.

### Contribution to Islamic Law

Al-Azhar became the primary reference for Islamic law, especially in the Shafi'i school dominant in Egypt. Fatwas issued by Al-Azhar scholars have recognized authority throughout the Islamic world.^17

### Preservation of Islamic Heritage

Al-Azhar's library houses thousands of ancient manuscripts that constitute Islam's intellectual heritage. Many classical works are preserved only at Al-Azhar, making it an invaluable repository of Islamic knowledge.
