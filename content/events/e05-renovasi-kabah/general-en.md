# The Renovation of the Ka'bah and the Resolution of the Black Stone Dispute (605 CE)

## Context

In the year 605 CE — approximately five years before the descent of the first revelation — an event occurred that showcased the extraordinary wisdom of **Prophet Muhammad SAW** before the entire Quraysh tribe. This event concerned the major renovation of the Ka'bah and a dispute that nearly erupted into civil war among the Quraysh clans over who would have the honor of placing the Black Stone back in its position.^1

The Ka'bah, which according to Islamic tradition was first built by Prophet Ibrahim (Abraham) and his son Prophet Ismail (Ishmael), had suffered severe damage from a flash flood that struck Mecca. Floodwaters flowing from the surrounding hills entered the Sacred Mosque and damaged the aging walls of the Ka'bah. The structure had become fragile and cracked, prompting the Quraysh to decide that a major renovation was urgently needed.^2

Al-Azraqi in *Akhbar Makkah* records in detail that at that time, the Ka'bah was still a low structure without a roof, its walls made of uncemented stones. It stood only about nine cubits high (roughly four meters), and the flooding had seriously weakened its foundations.^3

An additional important factor was a Roman merchant ship that had run aground at the port of Jeddah. The wood and materials from this vessel were utilized by the Quraysh for the Ka'bah renovation. A Roman carpenter named Baqum — who was also a passenger on the ship — assisted in the construction process.^2

## The Events

### Demolition and Reconstruction

The decision to renovate the Ka'bah did not come easily. The Quraysh felt fearful and reluctant to demolish a building they believed to be sacred, built by Ibrahim. No one dared to be the first to begin the demolition. Finally, **al-Walid ibn al-Mughirah** — one of the Quraysh leaders — took an axe and began dismantling part of the wall while saying: "O Allah, we intend nothing but good."^1

The people of Quraysh waited overnight to see whether al-Walid would be struck by misfortune. When the next morning found him unharmed, they joined in collectively demolishing the Ka'bah walls down to the foundations laid by Ibrahim.^2

The reconstruction was carried out collectively. Each Quraysh clan was responsible for one section of wall. They built the Ka'bah taller than before and this time added a roof — which previously had not existed. However, due to limited funds from lawful sources (they had agreed to use only wealth untainted by usury or illicit earnings), the Ka'bah structure did not encompass the entirety of Ibrahim's original foundation. The uncovered portion became known as *Hijr Ismail*.^2

### The Black Stone Dispute

Construction proceeded smoothly until the moment came to replace the Black Stone — the sacred stone occupying the southeastern corner of the Ka'bah. Every Quraysh clan considered placing the Black Stone to be the supreme honor, and they all wanted it for themselves.^1

The rivalry became extremely serious. Ibn Hisham narrates that the clan of Banu Abd al-Dar had even formed a blood pact with the clan of Banu Adi — they dipped their hands into a vessel of blood as a symbol of their willingness to fight for this right. Tensions escalated and bloodshed seemed imminent.^1

The dispute continued for four or five days without resolution. Then Abu Umayyah ibn al-Mughirah — the eldest among the Quraysh leaders — proposed a solution: "O people of Quraysh, let the first person to enter through the gate of the Sacred Mosque be the judge among you."^1

All parties agreed to this proposal. They waited in suspense. And the first person to step through the gate of the Sacred Mosque was Muhammad ibn Abdullah.^1

### Muhammad's Brilliant Solution

When they saw Muhammad approaching, the people of Quraysh exclaimed: "It is *al-Amin*! It is *al-Amin*! We are pleased to accept him as judge among us!"^1

This spontaneous exclamation reveals just how high Muhammad's reputation stood among the people of Mecca — a young man of approximately thirty-five years known throughout the entire city as the most honest and most trustworthy person.^2

After hearing the dispute, Muhammad asked for a cloth to be brought. He placed the Black Stone in the center of the cloth, then asked a representative from each clan to hold an edge of the cloth and lift it together. When the cloth had been raised to the correct height, Muhammad took the Black Stone with his own hands and placed it in its position in the Ka'bah wall.^1

The solution was accepted by all parties with open hearts. Every clan felt honored because they had participated in lifting the Black Stone. No one felt aggrieved. A dispute that had nearly erupted into civil war was resolved without a single drop of blood being spilled.^2

## Dialogue and Sacred Texts

The Black Stone event does not have a direct proof-text from the Quran or a hadith that specifically discusses it, but it is recorded in great detail in the *sirah* literature and is regarded as one of the most powerful pieces of evidence concerning Muhammad's character before prophethood.

Ibn Hisham places this event as the climax of the pre-prophetic narrative — a vivid demonstration of the qualities that would later form the core of the prophetic message: justice, wisdom, and the ability to resolve conflict without violence.^1

Al-Mubarakfuri in *Al-Rahiq al-Makhtum*, in the chapter on Muhammad's life before prophethood, analyzes that Muhammad's solution reflected several important principles. First, he did not claim the honor for himself even though he was entitled to it. Second, he involved all parties so that no one felt defeated. Third, he retained the most important role — placing the stone with his own hands — without provoking jealousy.^2

Al-Bukhari in his *Sahih* narrates hadiths about the sacredness of the Black Stone itself in the context of the Hajj pilgrimage, which demonstrates the special status of this stone in Islamic tradition.^4

## Analysis and Lessons

### A Leader Before Becoming a Prophet

This event occurred five years before Muhammad received the first revelation, yet it already displayed extraordinary leadership qualities. Muhammad held no formal position, possessed no great wealth, and led no particular clan. Yet the entire Quraysh trusted his judgment without objection. This demonstrates that true leadership springs from character and integrity, not from power or wealth.^2

### The Art of Conflict Resolution

The cloth solution employed by Muhammad is a textbook example of win-win conflict resolution. In the Arab world, where tribal honor was paramount, any solution that made one clan feel superior to another would certainly have been rejected. Muhammad understood these social dynamics expertly and devised a solution that satisfied all parties.^1

### Reputation as Social Capital

The title *al-Amin* borne by Muhammad was not a ceremonial honor bestowed in a formal ceremony — it was an organic recognition from society based on character proven over many years. It was this reputation that made his solution accepted without opposition. The lesson is profoundly relevant: trust is built slowly through consistency of character, not through verbal claims.^2

### The Ka'bah as a Symbol of Unity

The renovation of the Ka'bah, which nearly caused division, instead became a moment of unity thanks to Muhammad's wisdom. Ironically, the Ka'bah — which was fundamentally a symbol of monotheism and unity, having been built by Ibrahim for the worship of the One God — had nearly become the cause of civil war due to tribal egos. Muhammad restored the spirit of unity to its rightful place.^1

### Pre-Prophetic Legitimacy

This event also served a legitimizing function in the *sirah* narrative. When Muhammad later claimed to be a prophet and messenger, the people of Mecca could not deny his personal character. They themselves had chosen him as their judge. They themselves had called him *al-Amin*. Their rejection of the Islamic message was not because they doubted Muhammad's honesty, but because that message challenged the social, economic, and power structures they had long maintained.^2
