# The Building of Masjid Nabawi and the Brotherhood of Muhajirin and Ansar

## Context: Madinah Needed a Centre

When Prophet Muhammad ﷺ arrived in Madinah after the eventful hijrah journey, the very first step he took was to build a mosque.¹ ² This was no accident. In the Islamic conception, a mosque is not merely a place of prayer — it is the centre of community life: a place of worship, education, consultation, adjudication, and even shelter for the homeless.

Prophet Muhammad's camel, al-Qaswa', was allowed to walk freely after entering Madinah. Al-Bukhari narrates that the camel passed through several places, then stopped and knelt on a plot of land belonging to two orphan boys of the Banu al-Najjar — **Sahl and Suhayl**, sons of **Rafi'**.¹ ³ The Prophet said: *"This is our place, God willing."*

The land had previously been used as a date-drying yard. Prophet Muhammad ﷺ wished to purchase it, but the two orphans wanted to donate it. He refused to accept the land for free and insisted on paying — it was **Abu Bakr** who ultimately paid the price.¹ ² This demonstrated a crucial principle: even for the noblest of purposes, the property rights of orphans must not be violated.

## The Event: Building the Mosque with Their Own Hands

The construction of Masjid Nabawi was a communal effort. Prophet Muhammad ﷺ himself **joined the labour** — carrying clay bricks (*libn*), hauling stones, and laying walls.¹ ² ³ Ibn Hisham narrates that the companions were so spirited they sang poetry while working. One famous verse went:

*"Allahumma la 'aysha illa 'aysh al-akhirah / Faghfir lil-Ansar wa al-Muhajirah..."*
("O Allah, there is no life but the life of the hereafter / So forgive the Ansar and the Muhajirin...")¹

The Prophet repeated this chant along with them as they worked.

The design of the first mosque was remarkably simple:¹ ²
- **Dimensions**: approximately 60 × 70 cubits (roughly 30 × 35 metres)
- **Walls**: clay bricks (*libn*)
- **Pillars**: date palm trunks
- **Roof**: palm fronds and leaves — covering only part of the area, the rest left open
- **Floor**: bare earth, later covered with pebbles
- **Initial qiblah**: facing **Bayt al-Maqdis** (Jerusalem), later redirected toward the Ka'bah when the command to change the qiblah was revealed approximately 16–17 months later

On the eastern side of the mosque, the Prophet built simple chambers (*hujurat*) for his wives — small rooms of clay brick with doors made of woven palm fronds.¹ ²

### The *Suffah*: A Hostel for Seekers of Knowledge

At the rear of the mosque was a covered area called the ***Suffah***.¹ ² Here lived the companions who had no homes or family in Madinah — mostly muhajirin who had arrived with nothing. They were known as ***Ahl al-Suffah*** (the People of the Bench).

The Ahl al-Suffah devoted their time to studying the Quran and religious knowledge directly from Prophet Muhammad ﷺ. Among them were towering figures such as **Abu Hurayrah**, who would become one of the most prolific narrators of hadith.³ The Suffah functioned as **the first educational institution in Islam** — a modest university on the veranda of a mosque.

## The Event: *Muakhah* — The Brotherhood of Muhajirin and Ansar

Alongside the construction of the mosque, Prophet Muhammad ﷺ took an extraordinarily bold socio-economic step: he **paired** the Muhajirin (immigrants from Makkah) with the Ansar (native Muslims of Madinah) as brothers.¹ ² ³ This system was called *muakhah* (brotherhood).

Ibn Hisham records that the Prophet paired approximately **90 individuals** — 45 pairs of Muhajirin and Ansar.¹ This brotherhood was far from symbolic. Initially, the bond even included **inheritance rights** — a Muhajir could inherit from his Ansari brother if one of them died, and vice versa.² This inheritance provision was later abrogated by the Quranic verses on inheritance, which established inheritance rights based on blood kinship.

Some well-known brotherhood pairings:¹ ²
- **Abd al-Rahman ibn Awf** (Muhajir) was paired with **Sa'd ibn al-Rabi'** (Ansari)
- **Abu Bakr al-Siddiq** was paired with **Kharijah ibn Zuhayr**
- **Umar ibn al-Khattab** was paired with **Itban ibn Malik**
- **Hamzah ibn Abd al-Muttalib** was paired with **Zayd ibn Harithah**

### The Story of Abd al-Rahman ibn Awf and Sa'd ibn al-Rabi'

The most celebrated story from the *muakhah* is that of **Abd al-Rahman ibn Awf** and **Sa'd ibn al-Rabi'**. Al-Bukhari narrates that Sa'd said to his new brother: *"I am the wealthiest of the Ansar. Look at my wealth and take half of it. And I have two wives — see which one pleases you, and I shall divorce her so you may marry her."*³

Abd al-Rahman's reply revealed the noble character of a Muhajir: *"May Allah bless you in your family and your wealth. Just show me where your market is."*³ Abd al-Rahman went to the market, began trading, and in a short time built his own wealth through sheer hard work. He did not take a single coin from his brother's fortune.

## Analysis: Two Foundations of Madinan Civilisation

The building of Masjid Nabawi and the *muakhah* were the first two steps Prophet Muhammad ﷺ took in Madinah, and together they represent the twin foundations of Islamic civilisation:

**First, the mosque as a civilisational centre.** By building a mosque before building a palace, a market, or a fortress, Prophet Muhammad ﷺ declared the priorities of Islamic civilisation: spirituality and knowledge over power and wealth. The mosque functioned simultaneously as a house of worship, a school, a parliament, and a court — a multi-purpose institution that became the heart of the community.

**Second, solidarity beyond tribalism.** The *muakhah* shattered the tribal barriers that formed the bedrock of pre-Islamic Arab society. A Qurayshi from Makkah and a Khazraji from Madinah — who under normal circumstances had no connection whatsoever — suddenly became brothers who inherited from each other. It was a radically innovative social experiment, and it succeeded.

**Third, economic self-reliance.** The story of Abd al-Rahman ibn Awf demonstrates that *muakhah* was not a system of dependency. Its purpose was not to make the Muhajirin a burden on the Ansar but to give them a starting foothold toward independence. The generosity of the Ansar opened the door; the hard work of the Muhajirin determined their future.

**Fourth, leadership that works.** Prophet Muhammad ﷺ did not merely order the construction of the mosque — he carried bricks and hauled stones alongside everyone else. This set the standard for leadership in Islam: a leader is not someone who commands from a throne but one who works alongside their people.

## Lessons: Enduring Wisdom

1. **Institutions before individuals.** The Prophet's first act in Madinah was not to build a house for himself but a mosque for his community. Communal interests precede personal ones.

2. **Knowledge is foundational.** The Suffah demonstrates that education was a priority from the very first day of the Islamic state. Even amid scarcity, the pursuit of knowledge must never cease.

3. **Active, not passive, solidarity.** The Ansar did not merely say "welcome" — they shared their wealth, their homes, and their land. True solidarity demands real sacrifice.

4. **Self-reliance is honourable.** Abd al-Rahman ibn Awf teaches us that accepting help with the aim of becoming self-sufficient is nobler than depending on another's generosity.
