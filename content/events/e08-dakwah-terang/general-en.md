# The Open Da'wah — The Proclamation from Mount Safa (613 CE)

## Context

After three years of secret da'wah, the number of Muslims in Mecca had reached approximately forty. The foundation of faith had been laid, the core cadre had been formed, and now the time had come for Allah's command to expand the reach of the mission. The verse descended:^1 ^3

> وَأَنذِرْ عَشِيرَتَكَ ٱلْأَقْرَبِينَ ﴿٢١٤﴾
>
> *"And warn your closest kindred. ﴾214﴿"*
> — QS. Al-Shu'ara' (26): 214

This verse marked the transition from *sirriyyah* (secret) da'wah to *jahriyyah* (open) da'wah. Mecca at this time was still entirely dominated by the Quraysh elite whose livelihood depended on the idol-worship system. An open call to monotheism meant challenging the entire social, economic, and religious order that had been established for centuries. ^3

## The Events

### The Banquet for Banu Hashim

Ibn Hisham narrates that the Prophet's first step after receiving this command was to invite the extended family of Banu Hashim and Banu Abd al-Muttalib to a banquet.^2 He prepared food and invited approximately forty people from his closest relatives.

At this gathering, Prophet Muhammad SAW presented his message openly before his family for the first time. He offered the teachings of monotheism and asked for their support. However, before he could finish speaking, **Abu Lahab** — the Prophet's own uncle — interrupted and dispersed the gathering with harsh words.^2

The Prophet did not give up. He held a second banquet and delivered his message again. This time, he addressed his relatives firmly, as narrated by Ibn Hisham:

"O Banu Abd al-Muttalib, by Allah, I do not know of any young man among the Arabs who has brought to his people something better than what I have brought to you. I have brought you the good of this world and the Hereafter."^2

### The Proclamation from Mount Safa

After addressing his family, the next step was far more dramatic. Prophet Muhammad SAW ascended **Mount Safa** — a small hill in the center of Mecca traditionally used for making important announcements — and called out in a loud voice:

"Yaa Shabaahah!" ("Danger at dawn!")^1

This was the traditional Arab alarm used to warn of enemy attack. Every Quraysh clan — Banu Hashim, Banu Abd al-Muttalib, Banu Zuhrah, Banu Taym, Banu Makhzum, and others — rushed to Mount Safa. Those who could not come sent representatives.^1

When all had gathered, Prophet Muhammad SAW asked:

"What would you think if I told you there was a cavalry behind this valley about to attack you — would you believe me?"^1

They responded spontaneously: "Yes! We have never known you to lie."^1

Then the Prophet declared:

"Indeed, I am a warner to you before a severe punishment."^1

In the narration recorded by al-Bukhari, he called out to each clan individually:

> *"O Banu Abd al-Muttalib, O Banu Abd Manaf, O Banu Zuhrah... Save yourselves from the Fire, for I have no power to protect you from Allah in any way."*
> — Sahih al-Bukhari, no. 2753, Kitab al-Wasaya

### Abu Lahab's Reaction and the Revelation of Surah al-Lahab

In the tense silence following the Prophet's proclamation, **Abu Lahab** stood up and said with fury:

"May you perish! Is this what you gathered us for?"^1

Abu Lahab — whose real name was Abd al-'Uzza ibn Abd al-Muttalib — was the Prophet's uncle most fiercely opposed to Islam. His reaction at Mount Safa became the occasion for the revelation of an entire surah:

> تَبَّتْ يَدَآ أَبِى لَهَبٍ وَتَبَّ ﴿١﴾ مَآ أَغْنَىٰ عَنْهُ مَالُهُۥ وَمَا كَسَبَ ﴿٢﴾ سَيَصْلَىٰ نَارًا ذَاتَ لَهَبٍ ﴿٣﴾ وَٱمْرَأَتُهُۥ حَمَّالَةَ ٱلْحَطَبِ ﴿٤﴾ فِى جِيدِهَا حَبْلٌ مِّن مَّسَدٍ ﴿٥﴾
>
> *"May the hands of Abu Lahab perish, and may he perish. ﴾1﴿ His wealth and what he earned will not benefit him. ﴾2﴿ He will burn in a Fire of blazing flame. ﴾3﴿ And his wife — the carrier of firewood (spreader of slander). ﴾4﴿ Around her neck is a rope of palm fiber. ﴾5﴿"*
> — QS. Al-Lahab (111): 1–5

Ibn Kathir explains in his tafsir that this surah was a direct response to Abu Lahab's rejection and to the role of his wife, **Umm Jamil bint Harb** (Abu Sufyan's sister), who frequently scattered thorns on the Prophet's path and spread malicious rumors about him among the Quraysh.^3

## Quotations and Evidence

The event at Mount Safa contains several extraordinary lessons in the rhetoric of da'wah:

**First**, the Prophet established credibility before delivering his message. He asked: "Would you believe me?" The positive response from the Quraysh — "We have never known you to lie" — created a moral foundation that made it difficult for them to reject what followed without contradiction.^1

**Second**, he used an analogy his audience deeply understood. The threat of cavalry behind a valley was a real danger in Arabian tribal life. Through this analogy, he translated the concept of divine punishment into something that felt tangible and urgent.^1

**Third**, he addressed each clan individually — ensuring no one felt the message was not for them. Even his closest family received no exemption:

> *"O Fatimah bint Muhammad, ask me whatever you wish from my wealth, but I cannot avail you anything against Allah."*
> — Sahih al-Bukhari, no. 2753

## Analysis and Lessons

### A Historical Turning Point

The event at Mount Safa marked an irreversible turning point. Before it, Islam was an underground movement that could be ignored. After Mount Safa, Islam became an open challenge to the status quo of Mecca. From this point, there was no turning back — neither for the Prophet nor for the Quraysh.^2

### Justice Without Exception

One of the most striking aspects of the Mount Safa proclamation was the Prophet's insistence that family ties confer no privilege before Allah. He explicitly named his own daughter, Fatimah, to emphasize that salvation comes only through faith and deeds, not through lineage or proximity to the Prophet.^1

### Response to Rejection

Abu Lahab's reaction and the revelation of Surah al-Lahab demonstrate that rejection of truth carries eternal consequences. Yet the Prophet's response to this rejection also serves as a model: he did not answer insult with insult, did not resort to violence, and continued his mission with unwavering resolve.^2
