# The Tombstone of Fatimah binti Maimun

## 🌍 What Happened?

In 1887, a Dutch researcher named Dr. J.P. Moquette discovered an ancient tombstone in Leran, Gresik, East Java. This tombstone was very special because it had the name "Fatimah binti Maimun" written in Arabic with very beautiful writing.

What made this discovery extraordinary was the death year written: 475 Hijri or 1082 CE. This meant Islam had existed in Java almost 1000 years ago! Before this discovery, scholars thought Islam only entered Java in the 13th century.

The tombstone was made of high-quality marble with very fine Arabic calligraphy carvings. This luxurious material showed that Fatimah came from an important family in her time.

## 📖 The Story

Fatimah binti Maimun lived in Leran around 1000-1082 CE. Leran was an important port at the mouth of the Bengawan Solo River that connected Java's interior with the outside world. Many traders from Arabia, Persia, and India stopped at this port.

The name "Fatimah" is Arabic meaning "the one who weans," while "Maimun" means "blessed." These names showed that her family had embraced Islam or at least been influenced by Islamic culture.

The use of expensive marble and high-quality calligraphy showed that Fatimah came from a wealthy family, possibly merchants or local nobles who had become Muslims. At that time, only rich people could afford such expensive tombstones.

Muslim traders brought not only trade goods but also the religion of Islam. They married local women and gradually spread Islamic teachings in peaceful ways.

## 🌟 The Lesson

The discovery of Fatimah's tombstone taught that Islamic history in Indonesia began much earlier than ever imagined. Islam had been part of Javanese society life for almost 1000 years.

The spread of Islam in the Archipelago happened in peaceful and harmonious ways. There was no force or warfare. Muslim traders came bringing peace and mutual respect for local culture.

This tombstone shows how important it is to preserve historical traces. Without researchers like Dr. Moquette, we might never have known that Islam existed in Java since the 11th century.

Fatimah's story also reminds us that Muslim women have been an important part of Indonesian Islamic history from the beginning. They helped build Islamic civilization in the homeland.

>
> *"There is no compulsion in religion. The right course has become clear from the wrong. ﴾256﴿"*
> — QS. Al-Baqarah (2): 256

This was how Islam entered Indonesia since Fatimah binti Maimun's time. Islamic spreaders came peacefully, traded, married, and gradually taught the goodness of Islam to local communities.

Fatimah's story teaches us to appreciate history and always be grateful for the civilizational heritage passed down by our ancestors. Every trace of the past is a valuable lesson for the future.
