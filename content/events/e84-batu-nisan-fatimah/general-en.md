# The Tombstone of Fatimah binti Maimun — Earliest Islamic Trace in the Archipelago

## Context of Early Islamic Spread

The spread of Islam to the Archipelago was a gradual process through trade routes, marriage, and peaceful da'wah. Unlike military conquests that occurred in other regions, the Islamization of the Archipelago took place through harmonious acculturation with local cultures.^1

The 11th century CE became a crucial period in the history of Islam's entry into the Archipelago. Muslim traders from Arabia, Persia, and India had long sailed across the Indian Ocean to trade spices. Ports on Sumatra's west coast became important stopping points in this international trade route.^2

The arrival of Islam in the Archipelago cannot be separated from the well-established maritime trade system. Muslim merchants brought not only trade commodities but also Islamic spiritual values and civilization that later acculturated with local cultures.^3

## Discovery of Fatimah binti Maimun's Tombstone

In 1887, a Dutch researcher named Dr. J.P. Moquette discovered an ancient tombstone in Leran, Gresik, East Java. This tombstone was later identified as the grave of **Fatimah binti Maimun**, a Muslim woman who died in 475 Hijri or 1082 CE.^1

This discovery greatly surprised the academic world as it showed that Islam's presence in Java had occurred much earlier than previously estimated. Before this discovery, scholars generally estimated Islam only entered Java in the 13th or 14th centuries CE.^2

The tombstone was made of marble with beautiful Arabic calligraphy carvings. The inscription used Arabic language with Kufic script showing high standards of calligraphic art. This indicated that the Muslim community in the area already had an advanced level of civilization and artistic capability.^3

## Epigraphic and Archaeological Analysis

The inscription on Fatimah binti Maimun's tombstone provides valuable information about the condition of early Muslim communities in Java. The inscription text reads: "Haza qabr Fatimah binti Maimun, tuwuffiyat sanat khams wa sab'in wa arba' mi'ah" (This is the grave of Fatimah binti Maimun, who died in the year 475 Hijri).^1

Paleographic analysis shows that the writing style and ornamentation on the tombstone have characteristics similar to inscriptions from West Asia and Central Asia. This indicates strong cultural and trade relations between the Archipelago and other Muslim regions.^2

The marble material used also attracted archaeologists' attention. High-quality marble like this was most likely imported from Gujarat, India, or West Asia. The use of such luxurious material indicates the high social status of the deceased and the economic capability of the local Muslim community.^3

## Historical and Social Context

The existence of Fatimah binti Maimun in Leran in the 11th century shows that this region had become part of the international trade network. Leran, located at the mouth of the Bengawan Solo River, was an important port connecting Java's interior with the outside world.^1

The name "Fatimah," which is Arabic, suggests that the deceased's parents were likely Muslims or at least influenced by Islamic culture. Meanwhile, the name "Maimun" means "blessed" in Arabic, reflecting the parents' hopes for their child.^2

Social context analysis indicates that Fatimah binti Maimun likely came from a merchant or local noble family that had embraced Islam. Her high social status is reflected in the use of luxurious marble tombstones and high-quality calligraphy.^3

## Significance in Archipelago Islamic History

The discovery of Fatimah binti Maimun's tombstone brought revolutionary impact to understanding Islamic history in the Archipelago. This finding proved that Islam had taken root in Java since the 11th century, long before the era of famous Islamic sultanates.^1

This archaeological evidence strengthens the theory that Islam's spread in the Archipelago occurred gradually and peacefully. There is no indication of conflict or coercion in the early Islamization process. Instead, Islam appears to have been voluntarily accepted by part of the local community.^2

The Qur'an affirms the principle of religious freedom that may have been applied in the early da'wah process in the Archipelago:

## Implications for Islamic Historiography^4

The discovery of Fatimah binti Maimun's tombstone changed the paradigm of Islamic historiography in Indonesia. Historians now must revise the timeline of Islam's entry into Java that had long been believed to occur in the 13th or 14th centuries.^1

This epigraphic evidence also shows that early Muslim communities in Java already possessed Arabic literacy and established inscription writing traditions. This indicates the existence of educational networks and Islamic knowledge transmission that functioned well.^2

Furthermore, this discovery opens the possibility of other Islamic traces still hidden in various regions of the Archipelago. More intensive archaeological and epigraphic research is needed to fully uncover the early history of Islam in Indonesia.^3

## Legacy and Learning

Fatimah binti Maimun's tombstone has now become one of the most valuable artifacts in the National Museum of Indonesia's collection. As the oldest evidence of Islam's presence in Java, this artifact has very high historical value and becomes an important learning source for future generations.^1

The story of Fatimah binti Maimun teaches that Islamic history in the Archipelago began much earlier than previously known. This shows that Islam has been an integral part of Indonesia's historical journey for nearly a thousand years.^2

The spiritual legacy left through this tombstone reminds us of the importance of documenting and preserving Islamic historical traces in the Archipelago. Every artifact has a valuable story to be studied and passed on to future generations.^3
