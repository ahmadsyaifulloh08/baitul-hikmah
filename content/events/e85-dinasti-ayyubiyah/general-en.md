# Ayyubid Dynasty — Saladin's Legacy (~1171-1260 CE)

## Origins and Formation of the Dynasty

The **Ayyubid Dynasty** was founded by **Salahuddin Yusuf ibn Ayyub** (Saladin) in 1171 CE, replacing the Fatimid Dynasty in Egypt. Salahuddin came from a Kurdish family that served **Nur al-Din Mahmud**, the Zangid ruler of Damascus. His father, **Najm al-Din Ayyub**, and his uncle, **Shirkuh**, were trusted commanders of Nur al-Din.^1

Salahuddin was born in 1137 CE at the fortress of Tikrit, Iraq, into a family that had dedicated itself to jihad against the Crusaders. His youth was spent at Nur al-Din's court in Damascus, where he received comprehensive military, political, and religious education. This education made him not only a skilled commander, but also a wise statesman.^2

The name "Ayyubid" was taken from Salahuddin's father's name. Despite being ethnically Kurdish, this dynasty adopted Arab culture and language as their primary identity. This reflected the universal character of Islam that transcends tribal and national boundaries.

**Salahuddin's spiritual leadership** was not limited to political and military aspects. He was known as a highly religious, just, and generous figure. Salahuddin consistently performed the five daily prayers, often fasted voluntary fasts, and greatly respected scholars. These qualities gave him legitimacy not only as a political ruler, but also as a spiritual leader of the ummah.^3

Salahuddin's rise to power was not simple. After Shirkuh's death in 1169 CE, Salahuddin was appointed as wazir (prime minister) by the Fatimid Caliph **al-Adid**. However, in 1171 CE, he ended the Fatimid caliphate and restored legitimacy to the Abbasids in Baghdad, while establishing his own dynasty.^4

**The process of consolidating power** in Egypt required extraordinary political wisdom from Salahuddin. He had to face resistance from Fatimid amirs, Sudanese troops loyal to the old dynasty, and external threats from the Kingdom of Jerusalem. Salahuddin successfully overcame all these challenges through a combination of military force, diplomacy, and systematic administrative reforms.^5

## Unification of Levant and Egypt

Salahuddin's greatest achievement was uniting Egypt and the Levant under one government. After Nur al-Din's death in 1174 CE, he claimed the Zangid inheritance and successfully controlled Damascus, Aleppo, and other regions. This unification process took more than ten years and involved both diplomacy and warfare.^3

This unification was strategic in facing the Crusader threat. Previously, Crusader kingdoms exploited Muslim political fragmentation by playing one power against another. With the unity of Egypt-Syria, the Crusaders lost this strategic advantage.

## Ayyubid Government System

Salahuddin built a government system that combined Arab-Islamic administrative traditions with practical experience from various regions under his control. **The *dīwān* system** (government departments) was efficiently organized, consisting of *dīwān al-jaysh* (military department), *dīwān al-kharāj* (finance department), *dīwān al-rasā'il* (correspondence department), and *dīwān al-qadā'* (judiciary department).^6

**The *iqtā'* system** (land grants) was used as the economic backbone of the state. Amirs and commanders received rights to manage certain territories with obligations to provide troops and pay taxes to the central government. However, unlike European feudalism, the Ayyubid *iqtā'* system remained under strict sultanate control, and these rights could not be automatically inherited.^7

**Multiethnic bureaucracy** became a hallmark of Ayyubid government. Salahuddin appointed officials based on competence, not ethnic or tribal origin. Arab, Kurdish, Turkish, and even some Coptic Christian administrators worked together in one government system. This policy reflected Islam's inclusive vision that prioritized justice and competence.^8

**The judicial system** was managed very strictly. Salahuddin appointed competent qadhi (judges) with high integrity to implement Islamic law. He also formed *mahkamah al-mazālim* (grievance courts) that specifically handled public complaints against government officials, ensuring no one was immune from the law, including the sultan's own family.^9

## Crusades and Military Achievements

The most famous period of the Ayyubid Dynasty was the **Third Crusade** (1189-1192 CE) triggered by Salahuddin's brilliant victory at **Hattin** (1187 CE). This victory enabled Muslims to recapture Jerusalem after 88 years under Crusader control.^5

However, the war against **Richard Lionheart** of England, **Philip Augustus** of France, and **Frederick Barbarossa** of the Holy Roman Empire was no easy matter. Salahuddin faced the strongest coalition Europe had ever sent to the East.

Salahuddin's strategy was guerrilla warfare and avoiding open battle when disadvantageous. He utilized terrain knowledge, desert climate, and supply routes. Scorched earth tactics were used to cut enemy supplies.

The war ended with the **Treaty of Ramla** (1192 CE) which allowed the Crusaders to maintain coastal territories while Jerusalem and the interior remained in Muslim hands. Although not expelling all Crusaders, this was a compromise that benefited Muslims in the long term.^5

## Successors and Dynastic Continuity

After Salahuddin's death in 1193 CE, the Ayyubid Dynasty was led by his sons: **al-Afdal** in Damascus, **al-Aziz Uthman** in Egypt, and **al-Zahir** in Aleppo. This division of power initially created internal tensions, but overall the dynasty managed to survive.^6

**Al-Kamil** (1218-1238 CE), Salahuddin's grandson, was the most capable Ayyubid ruler after the dynasty's founder. He faced the **Fifth Crusade** (1217-1221 CE) in Egypt with brilliant strategy. When Crusader forces captured Damietta, al-Kamil allowed them to become trapped in the flooding Nile delta, forcing their surrender.

Al-Kamil also engaged in diplomacy with **Frederick II**, Holy Roman Emperor, resulting in the **Treaty of Jaffa** (1229 CE). Through negotiation, not warfare, Frederick gained access to Jerusalem for Christian pilgrimage purposes, while Muslims maintained political control over the city.

## Contributions to Civilization and Knowledge

The Ayyubid Dynasty was not merely a military dynasty, but also patrons of knowledge and civilization. **Bayt al-Hikmah** in Cairo was established to translate and preserve scientific works. Scholars from various schools of thought received protection and support.^8

Salahuddin founded various madrasas in Damascus and Cairo. **Madrasah Salahiyyah** in Jerusalem became a famous center of learning. This educational policy aimed to strengthen Sunni Islamic identity after centuries of Shia Fatimid dominance in Egypt.

## Architectural Heritage and Monuments

The Ayyubid Dynasty left remarkable architectural traces throughout their territories. **Saladin's Citadel** (*Qal'at Salah al-Din*) in the Syrian mountains represents a masterpiece of medieval military architecture. This fortress was built with revolutionary techniques, using an artificial moat carved directly from limestone with depths reaching 28 meters and width of 14 meters.^10

**Madrasah al-Salahiyyah** in Jerusalem, built on the former Church of Saint Anne, demonstrates Salahuddin's wisdom in converting Crusader buildings into Islamic educational centers. The architecture of this madrasah combines European Gothic style with Islamic ornamentation, creating a unique cultural synthesis.^11

In Cairo, Salahuddin built **Cairo Citadel** (*Qal'at al-Jabal*) which became a symbol of Ayyubid power in Egypt. This fortress functioned not only as a palace, but also as an administrative, military, and religious complex. The famous Muhammad Ali Mosque today was built on the foundation of the original Ayyubid mosque.^12

**Ayyubid civil architecture** also included the construction of *bimaristan* (hospitals), *khan* (caravanserai), and *hammam* (public baths) in various cities. Bimaristan al-Nuri in Damascus and Bimaristan al-Mu'ayyadi in Cairo became models of modern hospitals with pavilion systems, pharmacies, and medical libraries.^13

## Dynasty Descendants and Branches

After Salahuddin's death in 1193 CE, the Ayyubid Dynasty split into several branches, each controlling certain territories. **Al-Afdal Ali** inherited Damascus and most of Syria, **al-Aziz Uthman** controlled Egypt, **al-Zahir Ghazi** received Aleppo, and **al-Adil Sayf al-Din** (Salahuddin's brother) controlled Mesopotamian territories.^1

**The Egyptian branch** under al-Aziz Uthman and later al-Kamil became the strongest and most influential. Al-Kamil (ruled 1218-1238 CE) successfully faced the Fifth Crusade brilliantly and engaged in clever diplomacy with Frederick II. His descendants ruled Egypt until the rise of the Mamluks in 1250 CE.^2

**Minor Ayyubid branches** lasted longer in remote areas. The Hisn Kayfa branch in northern Mesopotamia survived until 1334 CE, while the Ayyubids of Hamah in central Syria lasted until 1341 CE. These small branches maintained Ayyubid scientific and cultural traditions despite losing major political power.^3

## Decline and Mamluk Succession

The Ayyubid Dynasty began to weaken in the mid-13th century CE due to several factors. First, the division of power among family members created internal fragmentation. Second, the **Mongol** threat from the east forced them to shift focus from Crusaders to a greater menace.^9

**Al-Salih Ayyub** (1240-1249 CE), the last significant Ayyubid ruler, faced the **Seventh Crusade** led by **Louis IX** of France. Although successfully defeating and capturing Louis IX at Mansourah (1250 CE), al-Salih died in the midst of the campaign.

After his death, al-Salih's widow, **Shajar al-Durr**, briefly ruled as sultana. However, real power shifted to the **Mamluks** (slave soldiers) who had become the backbone of Ayyubid military. In 1260 CE, the Mamluks officially took over power.

**Lessons and Legacy**

The Ayyubid Dynasty proved that Muslim political unity could face great external challenges. Their success was not only due to military prowess, but also clear political vision and diplomatic ability.

Their main legacy was the restoration of Jerusalem as an Islamic holy city and the strengthening of Sunni identity in regions once controlled by Shias. Their governance model—combining religious legitimacy with administrative efficiency—became inspiration for subsequent Muslim dynasties.

In broader context, the Ayyubids demonstrated that Islam as a civilization could integrate various ethnicities (Arab, Kurdish, Turkic) within one shared vision. This was a valuable lesson about unity in diversity.
