# Letters of Invitation to Kings: Rome, Persia, Egypt, and Abyssinia

## Context: From a Local Movement to a Universal Call

The Treaty of Hudaybiyyah at the end of the 6th year of Hijrah (628 CE) fundamentally altered the political landscape of the Arabian Peninsula. The ceasefire with the Quraysh gave Prophet Muhammad ﷺ an unprecedented freedom of action. Without the threat of war, he could turn his attention to a far broader horizon.^1 ^2

In early 7th Hijrah, Prophet Muhammad ﷺ took an unprecedented step: sending official letters to the great rulers of the world, inviting them to Islam. This was no ordinary correspondence — it was a declaration that Muhammad's message was universal, not confined to the Arab nation.^1 ^3

For this purpose, the Prophet ordered the crafting of a silver signet ring inscribed with three lines: *"Muhammad / Rasul / Allah"* — read from bottom to top. This ring became the official seal of his state correspondence.^1

## The Event: Six Envoys to Six Corners of the World

### Letter to Heraclius — Emperor of the Eastern Roman Empire

Prophet Muhammad ﷺ dispatched **Dihyah ibn Khalifah al-Kalbi** to deliver a letter to Heraclius, the Byzantine emperor who had recently achieved a great victory over Persia. The letter read:^1

*"In the Name of Allah, the Most Gracious, the Most Merciful. From Muhammad, the servant of Allah and His Messenger, to Heraclius, the great one of Rome. Peace be upon those who follow guidance. To proceed: I invite you with the call of Islam. Embrace Islam and you shall be safe, and Allah will give you a double reward. If you turn away, you bear the sin of your subjects... "*

The letter then quoted a Quranic verse^4:

> قُلْ يَا أَهْلَ الْكِتَابِ تَعَالَوْا إِلَىٰ كَلِمَةٍ سَوَاءٍ بَيْنَنَا وَبَيْنَكُمْ أَلَّا نَعْبُدَ إِلَّا اللَّهَ وَلَا نُشْرِكَ بِهِ شَيْئًا ﴿٦٤﴾
>
> *"Say, 'O People of the Book, come to a word that is equitable between us and you — that we will not worship except Allah and not associate anything with Him.' ﴾64﴿"*
> — QS. Ali Imran (3): 64

Heraclius's reaction was remarkable and documented in detail in Sahih al-Bukhari through an extended narration from Abu Sufyan. At that time, Abu Sufyan — still a polytheist — happened to be in al-Sham on a trading mission. Heraclius summoned him and posed a series of questions about Muhammad: his lineage, his followers, his teachings, whether he had ever lied, whether his followers were increasing or decreasing. ^4

After hearing all of Abu Sufyan's answers, Heraclius concluded: *"If what you say is true, he will come to rule the ground beneath my feet."* Abu Sufyan later said: *"I left there convinced that the affair of Ibn Abi Kabshah [a derogatory name for Muhammad] would become great."*^1

Heraclius appeared interested and even inclined toward accepting Islam, but pressure from his court officials forced him to retreat. He did not embrace Islam, but neither did he tear up the Prophet's letter — a gesture that indicated respect.^1 ^2

### Letter to Chosroes Parviz — King of Persia

**Abdullah ibn Hudhayfah al-Sahmi** was dispatched to deliver a letter to Chosroes Parviz (Khosrau II), ruler of the Sasanid Empire that stretched from Mesopotamia to Central Asia. The Prophet's letter employed the same tone: a respectful invitation to Islam.^1 ^2

Chosroes's reaction was starkly different from that of Heraclius. When the letter was read to him and he heard that Muhammad's name was written before his own, he flew into a rage and tore the letter to pieces. When this news reached Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, he said:^1

*"May Allah tear apart his kingdom."*

This supplication proved historically prescient. Only a few years later, Chosroes was assassinated by his own son, Shiruya, and the Sasanid Empire entered a phase of collapse that ended with the Muslim conquest during the caliphate of Umar ibn al-Khattab.

Chosroes even sent orders to Badhan, the Persian governor in Yemen, to arrest Muhammad. Badhan dispatched two envoys to Madinah. Prophet Muhammad ﷺ received them and said: *"Tell your master that my religion and my dominion will reach what the kingdom of Chosroes has not reached."* Shortly after, news arrived that Chosroes had been killed. Badhan and the people of Yemen embraced Islam.^2 ^3

### Letter to Muqawqis — Ruler of Egypt

**Hatib ibn Abi Balta'ah** carried a letter to Muqawqis (the Arabic name for Cyrus, the Coptic patriarch who also served as the Roman governor of Egypt). Muqawqis received the letter with respect, read it carefully, then placed it in an ivory casket.^2 ^3

He did not embrace Islam, but gave a highly diplomatic response. He said: *"I knew that there was still a prophet to come, but I thought he would appear from al-Sham."* He sent gifts to the Prophet: two slave women — **Mariyah al-Qibtiyyah** (who later became the Prophet's wife and bore his son Ibrahim) and her sister Sirin — as well as a white mule named Duldul, a donkey named Ya'fur, and quantities of clothing and honey.^1 ^2

### Letter to the Negus — King of Abyssinia

**Amr ibn Umayyah al-Damri** carried a letter to the Negus (Ashama), king of Abyssinia (Aksum/Ethiopia). The Negus was no stranger to the Muslims — he had sheltered the first emigrants who migrated to Abyssinia, long before the migration to Madinah.^2 ^3

The Negus's response was the most positive of all the kings. He accepted Islam — according to several narrations, he had secretly embraced Islam since his encounter with Ja'far ibn Abi Talib years earlier. He placed the Prophet's letter upon his eyes as a mark of highest respect, descended from his throne, and sat upon the ground.^2

When the Negus died, Prophet Muhammad ﷺ performed the funeral prayer in absentia for him in Madinah — the only time he did so for a king. This shows the special status the Negus holds in Islamic history.

### Other Envoys

Beyond the four great kings, the Prophet also sent letters to:

- **Mundhir ibn Sawa**, ruler of Bahrain — he accepted Islam along with most of his people.^2
- **Hawdhah ibn Ali**, ruler of Yamamah — he demanded a share of power as a condition, which the Prophet refused. He died shortly afterward.^2
- **Al-Harith ibn Abi Shamir al-Ghassani**, ruler of Ghassan (a Roman buffer state in al-Sham) — he was furious and planned to attack Madinah, but Heraclius forbade him.^2

## Analysis: Strategic and Theological Dimensions

### Theological Dimension

The sending of these letters was a direct implementation of the universality of Muhammad's message. The Quran affirms:

> وَمَا أَرْسَلْنَاكَ إِلَّا رَحْمَةً لِّلْعَالَمِينَ ﴿١٠٧﴾
>
> *"And We have not sent you except as a mercy to all the worlds. ﴾107﴿"*
> — QS. Al-Anbiya (21): 107

By sending letters to Rome, Persia, Egypt, and Abyssinia, Prophet Muhammad ﷺ literally conveyed this message to the four corners of the known world. This confirmed that Islam was not a tribal or racial religion, but a call for all of humanity.^4

### Diplomatic Dimension

These letters demonstrate extraordinary diplomatic intelligence. The Prophet employed the formal protocols of the time: a signet ring, formal language, proper use of rulers' titles, and dispatch through trusted envoys. He did not send ultimatums of war — he sent respectful invitations with gentle warnings.

The choice of envoys was also deliberate: Dihyah was known for his striking appearance (suitable for the image-conscious Roman court), Hatib was a skilled diplomat, and Amr ibn Umayyah had prior experience with Abyssinia.^2

### Historical Dimension

From a historical perspective, these letters marked the transformation of Islam from a local religious movement into an international geopolitical force. In less than a decade after these letters were sent, Muslim forces had conquered Persia and taken over Roman provinces in al-Sham and Egypt — precisely as the Prophet had intimated to Heraclius and Chosroes.

## Lessons: The Courage to Speak Truth to Power

These letters of invitation contain several important lessons:

**Intellectual courage.** A leader from a small desert city wrote to the two most powerful emperors in the world — Rome and Persia — on equal terms, not as a subordinate. This required extraordinary conviction in the truth of his message.

**Diplomacy before confrontation.** Before any battle with Rome or Persia, the Prophet had extended a peaceful invitation. This established an important precedent in Islamic law of war: war is the last resort, after invitation and dialogue have been offered.

**Universality of the message.** The Prophet made no distinctions — the same letter was sent to Christian Rome, Zoroastrian Persia, Coptic Egypt, and Christian Abyssinia. Islam's message transcends ethnic, linguistic, and cultural boundaries.

**Diverse responses, consistent wisdom.** Heraclius respected but did not convert, Chosroes tore the letter, Muqawqis used diplomacy, and the Negus embraced Islam. This diversity of responses teaches that the duty of the caller is to convey, not to compel.
