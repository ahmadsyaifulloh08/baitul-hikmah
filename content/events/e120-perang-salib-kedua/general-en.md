# The Second Crusade — Failure at Damascus (1147-1149 CE)

## Context After the First Crusade

Following the success of the First Crusade (1096-1099 CE), four Crusader states were established in the Levant: the Kingdom of Jerusalem, the Principality of Antioch, the County of Edessa, and the County of Tripoli.^1 However, their position was far from secure. In 1144 CE, **Imad al-Din Zangi**, the Muslim ruler of Aleppo, successfully recaptured Edessa, the first Crusader county to fall.^1

The fall of Edessa shocked the Christian world of Europe.^2 Pope Eugenius III immediately issued the bull *Quantum praedecessores* in 1145 CE, calling for a new crusade.^2 **Bernard of Clairvaux**, an influential Cistercian monk, played a key role in campaigning for this Christian *jihad* throughout Europe.^2

## European Kings March to the Levant

The papal call received response from the two most powerful rulers of Europe: **Louis VII of France** and **Conrad III of the Holy Roman Empire**.^3 This was the first time European kings personally led a crusade, demonstrating how seriously the Christian world perceived the threat.^3

Louis VII departed from France in the summer of 1147 CE with an army of approximately 70,000 men, including his wife **Eleanor of Aquitaine**.^3 Meanwhile, Conrad III led an equally large German force.^3 Both kings took the overland route through Constantinople, as participants in the First Crusade had done fifty years earlier.^3

The journey to the Levant proved more difficult than expected.^4 Conrad's army was attacked and destroyed by Seljuk forces at Doryleum in October 1147 CE.^4 The remnants were forced to join the French army. However, they met a similar fate at the Cadmus Mountains in January 1148 CE, where thousands of soldiers died in a surprise attack by Seljuk forces.^4

## The Failure at Damascus

After arriving in Antioch and Jerusalem with greatly diminished forces, Louis VII met with **Baldwin III of Jerusalem** and other Crusader leaders.^5 At a council in Acre in June 1148 CE, they decided to attack Damascus, which was then ruled by **Mu'in al-Din Unur**.^5

This decision was actually strategically counterproductive.^5 Damascus under Unur's leadership was a traditional ally of Jerusalem against the threat from Aleppo under the Zangid dynasty.^5 By attacking Damascus, the Crusaders would actually push for broader Muslim unity against them.^5

In July 1148 CE, the combined Crusader forces led by Louis VII, Conrad III, and Baldwin III besieged Damascus.^4 The city had strong defenses with high walls and a strategic position at the edge of the desert.^4 **Nur al-Din**, son and successor of Imad al-Din Zangi, immediately sent reinforcements to Damascus.^6

The siege lasted only four days. The Crusaders initially succeeded in breaching the outer defenses and occupying the gardens around Damascus. However, their decision to change their attack position from the west side (which was more open) to the east side (which was more heavily fortified) proved fatal. Muslim forces managed to cut their supply lines while reinforcements from Aleppo drew closer. ^7

Facing an increasingly disadvantageous situation, the Crusader leaders decided to retreat.^4 In the hasty withdrawal, they lost much equipment and supplies.^4 This failure at Damascus was a severe blow to Crusader prestige while simultaneously strengthening the Muslim position in the region.^6

## Impact and Consequences

The failure of the Second Crusade brought significant long-term impacts.^1 First, it showed that the Crusaders were not an invincible force.^1 Second, this failure accelerated the process of Muslim unification under Zangid leadership.^1

**Nur al-Din** exploited this momentum to consolidate his power.^6 He successfully took control of Damascus in 1154 CE, uniting Aleppo and Damascus under one ruler for the first time since the death of **Saladin al-Ayyubi** would later inherit this legacy.^6 This unity would become key to Muslim victory in recapturing Jerusalem in 1187 CE.^6

For Europe, this failure created disillusionment and doubt about the concept of holy war.^2 Relations between the Byzantine Empire and the West deteriorated further due to mutual accusations of betrayal during this expedition.^3 Eleanor of Aquitaine even demanded divorce from Louis VII after returning to France, which subsequently changed the political map of Europe.^3

**Lessons from Failure**

The Second Crusade taught several important lessons.^1 ^6 For Muslims, it demonstrated the importance of unity in facing external enemies.^6 The political fragmentation that had weakened the Islamic world since the collapse of Abbasid power began to be addressed through the strategic vision of leaders like Imad al-Din Zangi and Nur al-Din.^1 ^6

For the Crusaders, this failure made them realize that military strength alone was insufficient without wise political strategy.^2 ^3 The decision to attack Damascus, which was actually a natural ally against Aleppo, showed their lack of understanding of local political dynamics.^5

In broader historical perspective, the Second Crusade marked a turning point in the Crusader-Muslim conflict in the Levant.^1 Strategic initiative began to shift to Muslim hands, which would reach its peak with the brilliant victory at Hattin forty years later.^1
