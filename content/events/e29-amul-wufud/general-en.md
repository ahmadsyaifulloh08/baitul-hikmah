# 'Am al-Wufud: The Year of Delegations

## Historical Context: Arabia After the Conquest of Makkah

The Conquest of Makkah in 8 AH (630 CE) fundamentally altered the political landscape of the Arabian Peninsula. Makkah — the spiritual, economic, and social center of the Arabs — now stood under the banner of Islam. For the tribes that had been watching from a distance, the fall of Makkah was a turning point that could no longer be ignored.^1

Ibn Hisham in his *Sirah Nabawiyyah* explains the reasoning that developed among the Arab tribes: they had said, *"Leave Muhammad and his people (the Quraysh). If he prevails over them, then he is indeed a true prophet."*^1 When Makkah was finally conquered with virtually no bloodshed, and the Quraysh — the most prestigious tribe in all of Arabia — accepted Islam, the other tribes lost their primary reason for resistance.

Al-Mubarakfuri in *al-Rahiq al-Makhtum* notes that the Conquest of Makkah was followed by victory at the Battle of Hunayn and the siege of Ta'if, which further consolidated Islamic authority in the Hijaz region.^2 No significant military force remained capable of challenging Madinah. It was this context that gave rise to the phenomenon of *'Am al-Wufud* — the year in which delegation after delegation traveled to Madinah to declare their acceptance of Islam.

The Quran itself immortalized this phenomenon in Surah An-Nasr:

> إِذَا جَاءَ نَصْرُ اللَّهِ وَالْفَتْحُ ﴿١﴾ وَرَأَيْتَ النَّاسَ يَدْخُلُونَ فِي دِينِ اللَّهِ أَفْوَاجًا ﴿٢﴾ فَسَبِّحْ بِحَمْدِ رَبِّكَ وَاسْتَغْفِرْهُ ۚ إِنَّهُ كَانَ تَوَّابًا ﴿٣﴾
>
> *"When the help of Allah comes and the conquest, ﴾1﴿ and you see the people entering the religion of Allah in multitudes, ﴾2﴿ then glorify the praises of your Lord and seek His forgiveness. Indeed, He is ever Accepting of repentance. ﴾3﴿"*
> — QS. An-Nasr (110): 1-3

## The Events: Waves of Delegations to Madinah

The year 9 AH (631 CE) became known as *'Am al-Wufud* (the Year of Delegations). Ibn Sa'd in *Tabaqat al-Kubra* records that the number of delegations arriving in Madinah was immense, involving dozens of tribes from every corner of the Arabian Peninsula.^3 Among the most significant:

### The Delegation of Banu Tamim

Banu Tamim was one of the largest tribes in Arabia, residing in the Najd region. They sent a delegation led by several of their prominent figures. Ibn Hisham relates that this delegation arrived with an arrogant disposition — they called out to the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ from behind his private chambers in loud voices.^1

This incident was recorded in the Quran:

> إِنَّ الَّذِينَ يُنَادُونَكَ مِن وَرَاءِ الْحُجُرَاتِ أَكْثَرُهُمْ لَا يَعْقِلُونَ ﴿٤﴾
>
> *"Indeed, those who call you from behind the chambers — most of them do not understand. ﴾4﴿"*
> — QS. Al-Hujurat (49): 4

Yet after dialogue with the Prophet ﷺ and hearing the Quran, they accepted Islam and returned to their people as Muslims.

### The Delegation of Banu 'Amir ibn Sa'sa'ah

This tribe sent its delegation to learn about Islam firsthand. Among them was **'Amir ibn al-Tufayl**, a leader with political ambitions — he wished to be granted authority after the Prophet ﷺ. When the Prophet declined this demand, 'Amir refused to accept Islam. However, the majority of the delegation's other members embraced the faith.^1 ^2

### The Delegation of Thaqif from Ta'if

The Thaqif delegation carried deep emotional significance. Ta'if was the city that had once expelled and injured the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ during the early period of his mission — its people pelted him with stones until he bled. Now, after the siege of Ta'if following the Battle of Hunayn and after witnessing all of Arabia gravitating toward Islam, the Thaqif finally dispatched a delegation to Madinah.^1 ^3

Al-Mubarakfuri records that the Thaqif delegation initially proposed several conditions — including that their idol, al-Lat, not be destroyed for three years, and that they be exempted from the obligation of prayer. The Prophet ﷺ rejected every condition that contradicted the principle of *tawhid* (monotheism), though he agreed that they would not have to destroy the idol with their own hands — he sent Abu Sufyan and al-Mughirah ibn Shu'bah to accomplish the task.^2

### The Delegation from Najran (Christians)

One of the most remarkable delegations was that from Najran, an Arab Christian community. Ibn Hisham records that the delegation consisted of approximately sixty people, led by their bishop, **Abu Harithah**.^1

They debated with the Prophet ﷺ regarding the nature of Jesus Christ. When no theological agreement was reached, the verse of *mubahalah* (mutual invocation of divine curse) was revealed:

> فَمَنْ حَاجَّكَ فِيهِ مِن بَعْدِ مَا جَاءَكَ مِنَ الْعِلْمِ فَقُلْ تَعَالَوْا نَدْعُ أَبْنَاءَنَا وَأَبْنَاءَكُمْ وَنِسَاءَنَا وَنِسَاءَكُمْ وَأَنفُسَنَا وَأَنفُسَكُمْ ثُمَّ نَبْتَهِلْ فَنَجْعَل لَّعْنَتَ اللَّهِ عَلَى الْكَاذِبِينَ ﴿٦١﴾
>
> *"Then whoever argues with you about it after knowledge has come to you — say, 'Come, let us call our sons and your sons, our women and your women, ourselves and yourselves, then supplicate earnestly and invoke the curse of Allah upon the liars.' ﴾61﴿"*
> — QS. Ali Imran (3): 61

The Najran delegation ultimately declined the *mubahalah* and chose instead to establish a peace treaty with the Prophet ﷺ — they maintained their faith while paying the *jizyah* and living under the protection of the Islamic state.^1 This event set a crucial precedent for interfaith relations in Islam.

### Other Delegations

Beyond those mentioned above, Ibn Sa'd in *Tabaqat al-Kubra* records dozens of additional delegations, including:^3

- **Banu Hanifah** from Yamamah, among whose members was Musaylimah, who would later claim prophethood
- **Banu Asad**, who sent a delegation announcing their conversion
- **Yemeni tribes** — including delegations from Himyar, Kindah, and Hadhramaut
- **A delegation from Oman** — led by the two brothers Jayfar and 'Abbad, sons of al-Julanda

Each delegation was received by the Prophet ﷺ with full hospitality. He provided them lodging, listened to their concerns, answered their questions about Islam, and dispatched teachers to accompany them home to instruct their people in the Quran and Islamic law.

## Diplomacy of the Prophet: Letters to Rulers

This wave of delegations did not occur purely spontaneously. Prior to *'Am al-Wufud*, the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ had dispatched diplomatic letters to rulers and tribal leaders across the Arabian Peninsula and beyond. Ibn Sa'd records that he sent letters to the Roman Emperor (Heraclius), the Persian King (Khosrow), the ruler of Egypt (Muqawqis), the King of Abyssinia (Negus), as well as numerous Arab tribal chiefs.^3

These letters demonstrated that the Islamic call was not merely a local spiritual movement, but a universal mission. The Prophet ﷺ invited all of humanity to *tawhid* — regardless of race, tribe, or social standing.

## Analysis: Why Did the Tribes Accept Islam?

Historians identify several factors that drove the mass acceptance of Islam in 9 AH:

**First, the theological factor.** Many Arabs had long harbored doubts about idol worship. They were called *hunafa'* — seekers of truth who rejected polytheism but had not yet found a clear path. Islam offered a lucid and logical monotheism that resonated with the natural human disposition*fitrah*.

**Second, the political-military factor.** After the Conquest of Makkah and the victory at Hunayn, no military force in Arabia could challenge Madinah. Accepting Islam meant joining a rising power — not submitting to an oppressive conqueror, for Islam offered equality and brotherhood.

**Third, the socio-economic factor.** Islam abolished rigid tribal hierarchies. A believing former slave was nobler than an unbelieving nobleman. This message was profoundly revolutionary for a society that had been locked in rigid tribal stratification for centuries.

**Fourth, the moral factor.** The personal example of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ — his honesty, justice, gentleness, even toward his enemies — proved irresistibly compelling. Al-Mubarakfuri notes that many delegations accepted Islam not out of coercion or political calculation, but because they were moved by the Prophet's character.^2

## Lessons from 'Am al-Wufud

### Diplomacy and Dialogue, Not Coercion

*'Am al-Wufud* demonstrates that the spread of Islam fundamentally proceeded through diplomacy, dialogue, and personal example — not through the sword. The vast majority of Arab tribes accepted Islam voluntarily after sending delegations, engaging in dialogue with the Prophet ﷺ, and becoming convinced of his message's truth. This aligns with Allah's declaration:

> لَا إِكْرَاهَ فِي الدِّينِ ۖ قَد تَّبَيَّنَ الرُّشْدُ مِنَ الْغَيِّ ﴿٢٥٦﴾
>
> *"There shall be no compulsion in religion. The right course has become clear from the wrong. ﴾256﴿"*
> — QS. Al-Baqarah (2): 256

### Respecting Diversity

The Prophet's treatment of the Najran delegation demonstrates that Islam acknowledges the reality of religious diversity. Those who declined to accept Islam were not coerced — they were granted protection and civil rights under a clear treaty. This was a remarkably advanced model of coexistence for its era.

### Unity Amid Tribal Diversity

Before Islam, the Arabian Peninsula was fragmented by ceaseless inter-tribal rivalries. *'Am al-Wufud* witnessed tribes from every direction — from Najd to Yemen, from Oman to Bahrain — uniting under a single faith. The brotherhood of belief replaced tribal fanaticism*'asabiyyah*. The Prophet ﷺ said:

> لَيْسَ مِنَّا مَنْ دَعَا إِلَى عَصَبِيَّةٍ
>
> *"He is not one of us who calls to tribal fanaticism."*
> — Narrated by Abu Dawud, no. 5121

### Servant Leadership

In receiving dozens of delegations from across Arabia, the Prophet ﷺ exemplified servant leadership — listening, understanding, respecting, and guiding. He did not compel, insult, or demean. Even toward delegations that arrived with arrogance, such as Banu Tamim, he remained patient and welcoming.
