# Admiral Zheng He & the Spread of Islam in the Archipelago

## Historical Context

Between 1405-1433 CE, **Admiral Zheng He** led seven massive maritime expeditions under orders from Emperor Yongle of the Ming Dynasty.^2 These expeditions were not merely diplomatic or trade missions, but also important moments in the spread of Islam in the Archipelago. Zheng He, a Hui Muslim from Yunnan whose original name was **Ma He**, used his position to strengthen existing Muslim communities and facilitate the spread of Islam in the Indian Ocean region.^1

At this time, the Archipelago had already begun to know Islam through Arab, Persian, and Gujarati traders since the 8th century CE.^3 However, the arrival of Zheng He's fleet with hundreds of ships and tens of thousands of crew members accelerated the Islamization process, especially in strategic ports such as Palembang, Malacca, and Java.^2

## Zheng He: A Muslim Admiral in the Chinese Court

### Personal Background

Ma He was born around 1371 CE in Yunnan province into a Hui Muslim family.^4 His father, **Ma Hajji**, had performed the pilgrimage to Mecca, demonstrating the family's strong religious commitment. When Yunnan was conquered by the Ming Dynasty, Ma He, still a child, was captured and castrated to be made a eunuch in the palace.^3

Despite experiencing heavy personal tragedy, Ma He showed extraordinary abilities in military and diplomatic affairs.^1 He gained the trust of Emperor Yongle and was appointed admiral with the new name Zheng He. Remarkably, he never abandoned his Muslim identity despite being at the peak of the Chinese imperial hierarchy.^2

### Commitment to Islam

Evidence shows that Zheng He continued to practice Islam throughout his life.^3 In his expeditions, he always brought imams and built temporary mosques at various ports. Inscriptions found in Galle, Sri Lanka, show that he gave offerings at Buddhist temples, Hindu temples, and Islamic shrines simultaneously - sophisticated religious diplomacy.^4

## Maritime Expeditions and Their Impact in the Archipelago

### Scale and Magnitude

Zheng He's armada was known as the "treasure fleets" consisting of 200-300 ships with a total crew of 27,000-30,000 people.^4 His flagship, the "treasure ship," was estimated to be 120-150 meters long - much larger than Columbus's ships that would sail 60 years later. This armada carried diplomats, traders, technicians, scholars, and of course many Muslims.^1

Importantly, the crew composition was very diverse but Muslims formed a significant proportion, especially in key positions.^1 Zheng He deliberately recruited Muslims for navigation, trade, and diplomacy positions because Muslim communities already had established networks along Indian Ocean trade routes.

### Interaction with Muslim Communities in the Archipelago

When Zheng He's armada arrived at Archipelago ports, they were enthusiastically welcomed by existing local Muslim communities.^2 In **Palembang**, Zheng He met with **Wang Jinming**, a Chinese Muslim who had already become an influential trader there. This interaction strengthened the position of Muslims in Palembang and surrounding areas.^2

In **Semarang**, Zheng He allegedly once met with **Sam Poo Kong** (who was later associated with Zheng He himself in folk legend).^3 Historical records show that he built temporary mosques and conducted prayers together with the local Muslim community.

### Role in the Spread of Islam

Although not a missionary in the formal sense, Zheng He's expeditions contributed to the spread of Islam through several mechanisms:

**Networking**: Strengthening already established Muslim trading networks.^4 **Prestige**: Showing that Muslims could achieve high positions in government. **Facilities**: Building infrastructure supporting Islamic activities.^1 **Intermarriage**: Many Muslim crew members settled and married local women.^3

## Impact in Malacca and Java

### Malacca Sultanate

Zheng He's expeditions had a profound impact on the development of the **Malacca Sultanate**.^2 **Parameswara** (who later converted to Islam with the name Megat Iskandar Shah) received political legitimacy from the Ming through Zheng He. Support from China helped Malacca become a major trading hub and, subsequently, a center for spreading Islam in Southeast Asia.^4

Zheng He was personally involved in diplomatic negotiations that established Malacca as a tributary state of Ming.^3 This arrangement gave Malacca protection from Siam and Majapahit, allowing the Islamic sultanate to flourish.

### Influence in Java

In Java, Zheng He's expeditions coincided with the transition period from Hindu-Buddhist Majapahit to emerging Islamic powers.^4 Muslim communities in coastal cities such as **Surabaya**, **Gresik**, and **Demak** received a significant boost from interaction with Zheng He's Muslim crew members.

Some historical sources suggest that Zheng He's members who settled in Java later played roles in establishing Islamic institutions and supporting local Islamic scholars.^1

## Dialogue with Local Leaders

Historical records show Zheng He's extraordinary diplomatic skills in interacting with diverse religious leaders:

> "The Emperor of China highly values righteousness and harmony. All who submit shall be protected and prosper."

The message conveyed by Zheng He to local rulers reflects a diplomatic approach that was respectful of local traditions while promoting Chinese interests.^2 As a Muslim, he was uniquely positioned to bridge Chinese imperial interests with Islamic communities.^1

## Religious Significance

### Synthesis Model

Zheng He represents a unique model in Islamic history - a Muslim serving a non-Muslim state but using his position to advance Islamic interests subtly.^3 He showed that Muslims could maintain religious identity while contributing positively to diverse societies.

### Facilitator, Not Missionary

Unlike Muslim missionaries who were explicit in spreading Islam, Zheng He served as a facilitator creating a conducive environment for Islamic development.^4 This approach was arguably more effective in the pluralistic context of the Archipelago.

## Legacy and Wisdom

Zheng He's expeditions teach several important lessons:

**Diplomatic wisdom**: Respecting local customs while maintaining one's own principles.^1 **Religious tolerance**: Creating space for multiple faiths to coexist. **Strategic thinking**: Using official positions to advance community interests responsibly.^2 **Cultural bridge-building**: Facilitating interaction between different civilizations.

The story of Zheng He demonstrates that wise diplomacy and mutual respect can serve as bridges connecting different civilizations. His ability to maintain Islamic identity while serving faithfully shows that spiritual diversity need not be a barrier to building unity and shared progress.^3
