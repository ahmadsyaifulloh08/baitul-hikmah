# Jalaluddin Rumi - Masnavi & Mevlevi Order (1207-1273 CE)

## Life Background

**Jalal al-Din Muhammad Balkhi**, known as Rumi, was born in Balkh (now Afghanistan) in 1207 CE from a prominent scholarly family.^1 His father, **Baha al-Din Walad**, was a sufi and preacher nicknamed "Sultan al-Ulama" for his deep knowledge. This family was forced to migrate to Anatolia due to pressure from Mongol invasions threatening Central Asian territories.

The long journey of Rumi's family from Balkh to Konya through Makkah and various cities in the Middle East provided wide exposure to the diversity of Islamic culture and spiritual traditions.^2 Konya, which became Rumi's final home, was then one of the Islamic cultural centers under the Seljuk Sultanate of Rum.

## Meeting with Shams-i Tabrizi

The spiritual turning point in Rumi's life occurred in 1244 CE when he met **Shams al-Din Tabrizi**, a mysterious dervish from Tabriz.^3 This encounter transformed Rumi from a conventional scholar into a sufi poet full of spiritual passion. Shams brought Rumi to a deep experience of divine love (*ishq*).

The intense spiritual relationship between Rumi and Shams aroused jealousy among Rumi's other students. This social pressure eventually forced Shams to disappear mysteriously in 1248 CE.^1 This loss triggered an extraordinary creative outpouring from Rumi, producing thousands of poetry verses expressing spiritual longing.

## Literary Works: Diwan-i Shams and Masnavi

The loss of Shams triggered the greatest creative period in Rumi's life. He wrote the poetry collection *Diwan-i Shams-i Tabrizi* containing more than 3,000 ghazals and 2,000 rubais.^2 This work expresses love and longing for the spiritual teacher who had transformed his life.

Rumi's masterpiece is *Masnavi-ye Ma'navi*, a spiritual epic containing 25,700 lines completed toward the end of his life.^3 This book, nicknamed "The Quran in Persian language," combines esoteric Islamic teachings with profound symbolic stories. Each story in Masnavi has different layers of meaning, from literal to spiritual.

## Spiritual Teaching: Love as the Path

The core of Rumi's teaching is the concept that **love (*ishq*) is the shortest path to Allah**.^1 Unlike the rational theology approach dominant in his time, Rumi emphasized direct experience and spiritual union through burning love.

In Rumi's view, all creatures are reflections of Divine beauty, and humanity's task is to recognize Unity (tawhid) behind phenomenal diversity.^2 He used very rich metaphors: humans as reeds longing for their origins, birds caged in the worldly prison, or drops of water wanting to return to the ocean.

## Mevlevi Order and Sama' Practice

After Rumi's death in 1273 CE, his son **Sultan Walad** organized his father's teachings into a formal order known as **Mevlevi** or "Whirling Dervishes".^3 The distinctive practice of this order is sama' or ritual dance involving ecstatic spinning while remembering Allah.

Mevlevi sama' is not just dance, but moving meditation that is symbolic. The spinning dervish symbolizes universal movement, right hand raised receiving blessings from heaven, left hand bowed sharing blessings to earth.^1 Every element in sama' has deep spiritual meaning.

## Cross-Cultural Influence

Rumi's teachings spread far beyond geographical and even religious boundaries. His poetry was translated into dozens of languages and gained appreciation from great European thinkers like Goethe and Emerson.^2 In the modern Western world, Rumi became one of the most widely read poets.

However, it should be noted that Rumi's popularity in the West is often detached from the Islamic context that forms the basis of his teachings.^3 Many translations remove references to the Quran and Hadith that actually form Rumi's spiritual foundation.

## Metaphysics of Love in Masnavi

Rumi's Masnavi develops a sophisticated metaphysics of love. In "The Story of the Shepherd," Rumi shows that sincere love is more important than ritual perfection.^1 In "The Story of Majnun and Layla," he uses worldly love as metaphor for transformative divine love.

Each story in Masnavi operates at multiple levels: literal (zahir), allegorical (misal), moral (akhlaq), and spiritual (batin).^2 Different readers can take different lessons according to their spiritual level.

## Synthesis of Islamic Traditions

Rumi successfully integrated various traditions within Islam: jurisprudence (fiqh), theology (kalam), philosophy (falsafa), and mysticism (tasawuf).^3 He did not reject sharia, but saw it as the first step toward reality. In his view, sharia is the skin, tariqa is the flesh, haqiqa is the bone, and ma'rifa is the marrow.

This integrative approach made Rumi's teachings acceptable to various groups, from orthodox scholars to radical sufis.^1 He proved that deep spirituality does not have to conflict with solid scholarship.

## Contemporary Legacy

Today, Rumi's poetry is still read by millions of people worldwide as a source of spiritual inspiration.^2 Teachings about tolerance, universal love, and the search for deep meaning in life make him relevant in the modern era that often loses spiritual orientation.

However, Muslim scholars emphasize the importance of understanding Rumi in the correct context: as a devout Muslim, not as a universalist who detaches himself from particular religion.^3 True understanding of Rumi requires appreciation of the Islamic tradition that forms his life background.

## Analysis and Wisdom

Rumi demonstrates that **Islam has a very rich spiritual dimension** that can satisfy souls thirsty for meaning.^1 His teachings prove that rationality and spirituality, sharia and reality, can go hand in hand without negating each other.

The importance of **spiritual teachers (murshid)** in the sufi journey is also clearly seen from Rumi's transformation after meeting Shams.^3 Without proper guidance, spiritual seeking can get lost in ego and illusion.
