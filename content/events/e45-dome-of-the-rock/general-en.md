# The Dome of the Rock Is Built (691 CE)

## Historical Context: Jerusalem in Islamic Civilization

**Jerusalem** (in Arabic: *al-Quds* or *Bayt al-Maqdis*) holds a special position in Islamic theology as the third holiest city after Makkah and Madinah. It was the destination of Prophet Muhammad's ﷺ night journey (*Isra*) and the point of departure for his ascension (*Mi'raj*) to the heavens, as immortalized in the Quran^4:

> سُبْحَانَ الَّذِي أَسْرَىٰ بِعَبْدِهِ لَيْلًا مِّنَ الْمَسْجِدِ الْحَرَامِ إِلَى الْمَسْجِدِ الْأَقْصَى الَّذِي بَارَكْنَا حَوْلَهُ ﴿١﴾
>
> *"Glory be to the One Who took His servant by night from the Sacred Mosque to the Farthest Mosque, whose surroundings We have blessed. ﴾1﴿"*
> — QS. Al-Isra (17): 1

Jerusalem was conquered by Muslim forces under Caliph Umar ibn al-Khattab in 637 CE through the famous peaceful treaty known as the **"Pact of Iliya"**.^1 When Umar personally visited the city, he found that the area of the Haram al-Sharif (Temple Mount) — a sacred site that had once been the location of earlier prophets' places of worship — had been neglected and used as a rubbish dump by the Byzantine rulers as a form of humiliation toward the Jewish heritage.^4^5

Caliph Umar personally cleared the area with his own robe and established a simple prayer space (*mihrab*) facing Makkah.^2 This symbolic act demonstrated Islam's respect for the continuity of prophecy and the sanctity of a place blessed by Allah since the time of Ibrahim.^1

## Abd al-Malik ibn Marwan: Architect of Umayyad Civilization

**Abd al-Malik ibn Marwan** (ruled 685-705 CE) was the fifth ruler of the Umayyad Dynasty who possessed a grand vision to immortalize the glory of Islamic civilization through monumental works.^3 Ascending to the throne at age 45, Abd al-Malik inherited a caliphate facing complex political and theological challenges.^2

This caliph was known as an intellectual who mastered Arabic literature, history, and Islamic theology.^1 Before becoming caliph, Abd al-Malik was active as a *qari* (Quran reciter) and often led scholarly discussions at the Prophet's Mosque in Madinah. This intellectual background greatly influenced his aesthetic and theological approach to architectural works.^4

**Political Context of Abd al-Malik's Era**

The period 680-692 CE was known as the **"Second Fitna"** in Islamic history.^2 Abd al-Malik faced serious rebellion from **Abdullah ibn al-Zubayr** who controlled the Hijaz (including Makkah and Madinah) and declared himself a rival caliph with support from some companions and their followers (*tabi'in*).^3

This situation created theological and practical dilemmas: pilgrims from Umayyad territories had to pass through Ibn al-Zubayr's domain to reach Makkah.^1 Additionally, the rebellion of **Mukhtar al-Thaqafi** in Kufa, who acted in the name of **Muhammad ibn al-Hanafiyyah** (Ali's son by a wife other than Fatimah), added political complexity.^4

**Strategy for Legitimizing Power**

Abd al-Malik understood that Umayyad legitimacy depended not only on military strength, but also on demonstrating commitment to Islam and the welfare of the ummah.^2 The construction of the Dome of the Rock was part of a grand strategy to position the Umayyad dynasty as protectors of Islamic holy places and legitimate inheritors of community leadership.^3

Al-Tabari records that Abd al-Malik once said: *"We want to build something for Allah that will amaze people until the end of time."*^1 This statement shows that the Dome of the Rock project was not only politically motivated, but also had deep spiritual dimensions.^4

## The Event: Construction of Qubbat al-Sakhrah

### Location Selection and Its Theological Meaning

Abd al-Malik's decision to build the Dome of the Rock over the **Sacred Rock** (*al-Sakhrah*) had very deep theological significance.^2 According to Islamic tradition, this rock was the place where Prophet Muhammad ﷺ performed *Mi'raj* to the heavens on the night of *Isra*.^1 This rock, approximately 18 meters in diameter and 1.5 meters high, is also associated with various events in the history of the prophets.^4

Islamic tradition mentions that at this place **Prophet Ibrahim** once prepared to sacrifice his son **Ismail** at Allah's command.^3 **Prophet Dawud** and **Prophet Sulaiman** also had spiritual connections with this place, where Solomon's Temple (*Haikal Sulaiman*) once stood.^2 Thus, the Dome of the Rock became a symbol of prophetic continuity and the unity of the messengers' mission.^1

### Architectural Team and Planning

Al-Tabari and Mujir al-Din record that Abd al-Malik appointed two engineers to oversee this project: **Raja' ibn Haywah** al-Kindi from Palestine and **Yazid ibn Salam**, a *mawla* (Arab client) from Jerusalem.^1 These two men were chosen not only for their technical abilities, but also for their deep knowledge of local conditions and architectural traditions of the Levant region.^4

**Raja' ibn Haywah** (661-738 CE) was a *tabi'in* known as a scholar (*faqih*) and talented architect.^2 He had previously been involved in the construction of the Umayyad Mosque in Damascus and had experience integrating Byzantine architectural elements with Islamic principles. He was also close to scholarly circles and understood theological aspects in architectural design.^3

**Yazid ibn Salam** was a local engineer who mastered Byzantine construction techniques and had networks with the best craftsmen in the Palestine region.^1 The combination of Raja's theoretical expertise and Yazid's practical knowledge created an ideal team for a project that integrated spiritual vision with technical reality.^4

### Financing and Resources

Abd al-Malik allocated tax revenue (*kharaj*) from the entire province of Egypt for seven years to finance the construction of the Dome of the Rock.^1 This extraordinary allocation showed the high priority the caliph gave to this project. Considering Egypt was the wealthiest province in the Umayyad caliphate, this decision reflected very serious financial commitment.^2

Workers and artisans were brought from various parts of the Muslim world.^3 Mosaic teams were recruited from Christian Syrian craftsmen skilled in the Byzantine tradition, while the best Arab calligraphers were employed to create Quranic inscriptions that would adorn the building.^4 Building materials including marble from various Mediterranean regions and gold to gild the dome were brought through international trade networks.^1

### Construction Process (685-691 CE)

Construction began around 685 CE with foundation preparation that had to accommodate the uneven contours of the Sacred Rock.^2 The first phase included building an octagonal platform (*mimbar*) surrounding the rock with sufficient distance to provide space for pilgrims who wanted to visit.^3

The second phase (686-689 CE) was the construction of octagonal walls with eight sides of equal length.^1 Each side measured approximately 20.5 meters, creating spacious yet intimate interior space. These walls were built using the *opus sectile* technique — using pieces of colored marble arranged in complex geometric patterns.^4

The third phase (689-691 CE) was the construction of the wooden dome sheathed in lead, then covered with gold sheets.^2 The dome with an internal diameter of 20.4 meters was supported by a *pendentive* system — a Byzantine technique for distributing the load of a circular dome to the octagonal structure below.^3 The total height of the building from ground level to the dome's peak was approximately 35 meters, making it the tallest structure in Jerusalem at that time.^1

## The Marvel of Octagonal Architecture

### Sacred Geometry and Symbolism

The choice of octagonal (eight-sided) form had deep symbolic meaning in Islamic tradition.^4 The number eight (*thamaniyyah*) in Islamic numerology symbolizes resurrection and eternal life, as the Day of Resurrection will occur after the seven heavens. The octagonal shape is also a geometric transition between square (material world) and circle (divine perfection).^2

The octagonal geometry created unique space where every visitor could see the Sacred Rock from any angle, yet no single point provided dominant view.^3 This reflected the egalitarian principle of Islam where all Muslims have equal access to the sacred.^1

**Mathematical Proportions**

Modern analysis shows that the Dome of the Rock's proportions follow the golden ratio also found in classical Greek and Roman architecture.^4 The ratio between the dome's diameter and the building's height, as well as the octagon's proportions to the circle, demonstrates sophisticated mathematical understanding by its architects.^2

### Innovative Structural System

The Dome of the Rock introduced several structural innovations later adopted in Islamic architecture:^3

**Double Shell Dome**: The dome consists of two layers — an *inner dome* providing beautiful visual proportions from the interior, and an *outer dome* creating dramatic silhouette from the exterior.^1 This system allowed optimization of interior acoustics while providing better weather protection.^4

**Ambulatory System**: The interior space was divided into three concentric zones — central zone around the Sacred Rock, inner ambulatory, and outer ambulatory. This system facilitated *tawaf* (circumambulation) of the Sacred Rock while efficiently managing pilgrim flow.^2

**Window Arrangement**: Windows were strategically positioned to create dramatic lighting that illuminated the Sacred Rock at certain times of day, especially at sunrise and sunset.^3

## Quranic Inscriptions: Invitation through Art

### Strategic Theological Content

The Quranic inscriptions surrounding the Dome of the Rock's interior for 240 meters constitute one of the oldest surviving monumental inscriptions.^1 The verses chosen were not random, but carefully selected to convey specific theological messages to Christian and Jewish audiences.^4

**Affirmation of Tawhid**

The inscriptions begin with verses affirming Allah's oneness and rejecting the concept of Trinity:^2

> قُلْ هُوَ اللَّهُ أَحَدٌ ﴿١﴾ اللَّهُ الصَّمَدُ ﴿٢﴾ لَمْ يَلِدْ وَلَمْ يُولَدْ ﴿٣﴾ وَلَمْ يَكُن لَّهُ كُفُوًا أَحَدٌ ﴿٤﴾
>
> *"Say: He is Allah, the One! ﴾1﴿ Allah, the Eternal, Absolute; ﴾2﴿ He begets not, nor is He begotten; ﴾3﴿ And there is none like unto Him. ﴾4﴿"*
> — QS. Al-Ikhlas (112): 1-4

**Dialogue with People of the Book**^5

The middle section of the inscriptions contains verses specifically addressing the People of the Book (Christians and Jews):^3

> يَا أَهْلَ الْكِتَابِ لَا تَغْلُوا فِي دِينِكُمْ وَلَا تَقُولُوا عَلَى اللَّهِ إِلَّا الْحَقَّ ۚ إِنَّمَا الْمَسِيحُ عِيسَى ابْنُ مَرْيَمَ رَسُولُ اللَّهِ وَكَلِمَتُهُ أَلْقَاهَا إِلَىٰ مَرْيَمَ وَرُوحٌ مِّنْهُ ۖ فَآمِنُوا بِاللَّهِ وَرُسُلِهِ ۖ وَلَا تَقُولُوا ثَلَاثَةٌ ۚ انتَهُوا خَيْرًا لَّكُمْ ﴿١٧١﴾
>
> *"O People of the Book! Commit no excesses in your religion: nor say of Allah aught but the truth. Christ Jesus the son of Mary was (no more than) a messenger of Allah, and His Word, which He bestowed on Mary, and a spirit proceeding from Him: so believe in Allah and His messengers. Say not 'Trinity': desist: it will be better for you. ﴾171﴿"*
> — QS. An-Nisa (4): 171

This verse conveyed the message that Islam honors Isa (*Jesus*) as a noble prophet, while rejecting his divinity.^1 This message was delivered in a gentle yet firm tone (*"desist. it will be better for you"*).^4^5

### Calligraphic Technique and Aesthetics

The Quranic inscriptions were written in early **Kufic script** which was an evolution of the calligraphic style developed in Kufa.^2 This script was chosen for its geometric and monumental character, very suitable for the building's architectural scale.^3

**Aesthetic Innovations:**
- **Height modulation**: Letters had varying heights to create dynamic visual rhythm^1
- **Color contrast**: Golden calligraphy against blue and green mosaic backgrounds created dramatic contrast^4
- **Integration with decoration**: Calligraphy was integrated with geometric motifs and *arabesque* without mutual interference^2

## Umayyad Political Context and Message of Power

### Spiritual vs. Political Legitimacy

The Dome of the Rock must be understood in the context of legitimacy competition between the Umayyad caliphate in Damascus and Ibn al-Zubayr's rival caliphate in Makkah.^3 However, viewing this building merely as a "Ka'bah alternative" is problematic oversimplification.^1

**Mujir al-Din al-Hanbali** in *Al-Uns al-Jalil* firmly rejects the view that Abd al-Malik intended to replace the Ka'bah:^4 *"Those who say that Abd al-Malik built Qubbat al-Sakhrah to divert attention from the Ka'bah are greatly mistaken. Indeed, he built it because he honored the place that Allah had blessed and wanted to demonstrate Islam's grandeur."*^2

### Architectural Diplomacy with Byzantium

The Dome of the Rock was also a form of architectural diplomacy with the Byzantine Empire.^3 By building a structure rivaling **Hagia Sophia** in Constantinople and the **Church of the Holy Sepulchre** in Jerusalem, Abd al-Malik declared that Islamic civilization had matured and could compete in art and architecture.^1

The use of gold in large quantities for the dome demonstrated the wealth and prosperity of the Umayyad caliphate.^4 The gleaming golden dome could be seen from dozens of kilometers away, becoming a landmark asserting Islam's presence in a city also sacred to Christians and Jews.^2

### Message to the Christian World

The Quranic inscriptions facing west (toward Byzantine territories) conveyed theological messages to the Christian world.^3 This message was not a threat, but an invitation to reconsider the Trinity doctrine and recognize Isa in his proper position as Allah's prophet.^1

The aesthetics of the Dome of the Rock — combining beauty with spiritual message — showed that Islam could convey *da'wah* through beauty and art, not only through theological debate or military force.^4

## Architectural Influence and Legacy

### Template for Global Islamic Architecture

The Dome of the Rock became a template for Islamic architectural development in various parts of the world.^2 The *central plan* concept with a dominant dome was later adopted in the construction of great mosques from Cordoba to Delhi.^3

**The Great Mosque of Cordoba** (begun 785 CE) adopted the layered arcade system (*horseshoe arches*) inspired by the Dome of the Rock's ambulatory.^1 **The Taj Mahal** in India (1648 CE) used the central dome concept popularized by the Dome of the Rock, albeit with significant local adaptations.^4

### Development of Calligraphic Art

The monumental inscriptions of the Dome of the Rock became catalysts for the development of calligraphy as an integral element in Islamic architecture.^2 This tradition later developed into one of the most distinctive characteristics of Islamic art, where calligraphy functioned not only as text, but also as beautiful ornamentation.^3

**Calligraphic schools** that developed in Baghdad, Cairo, and Cordoba referred to principles first implemented in the Dome of the Rock.^1 The integration between textual meaning, visual aesthetics, and architectural context became standard in Islamic decorative art.^4

## Restoration and Maintenance Throughout History

### Abbasid and Fatimid Periods

After the fall of the Umayyad Dynasty in 750 CE, Abbasid rulers continued maintaining the Dome of the Rock as heritage of the Muslim ummah.^2 **Caliph Al-Ma'mun** (813-833 CE) conducted the first restoration by replacing some damaged mosaics and improving the dome's drainage system.^3

Under **Fatimid Dynasty** rule (969-1099 CE), the Dome of the Rock underwent major renovation.^1 **Caliph Al-Aziz Billah** (975-996 CE) added new ornaments and reinforced the dome structure showing signs of aging.^4

### Crusader Era and Ayyubid Restoration

During Crusader control (1099-1187 CE), the Dome of the Rock was converted into a Christian church called "Templum Domini".^2 The Crusaders added a cross to the dome's peak and covered some Quranic inscriptions with Christian ornaments.^3

After **Saladin al-Ayyubi** recaptured Jerusalem in 1187 CE, he immediately ordered total restoration to return the building's Islamic identity.^1 The cross was replaced with a golden crescent, Quranic inscriptions were cleaned and repaired, and the interior lighting system was restored to its original condition.^4

### Ottoman Era Maintenance

Under **Ottoman Sultanate** rule (1517-1917 CE), the Dome of the Rock received special attention from sultans.^2 **Sultan Suleiman the Magnificent** (1520-1566 CE) conducted major renovation by adding famous *Iznik* ceramics to the building's exterior.^3

**Sultan Abdul Hamid II** (1876-1909 CE) ordered dome restoration by replacing the old lead covering with pure gold plating that survives today.^1 The total gold used reached 80 kilograms, making the Dome of the Rock's dome one of the world's largest gold-plated structures.^4

## Contemporary Analysis and Wisdom

### Cultural Synthesis in Islam

The Dome of the Rock demonstrates Islamic civilization's extraordinary ability to synthesize various cultural traditions without losing spiritual identity.^2 Byzantine, Persian, and Arab elements were combined in perfect harmony, creating new aesthetics authentically Islamic.^3

This synthesis capability became one of Islamic civilization's main strengths in adapting to various local cultural contexts while maintaining universal principles.^1

### Da'wah through Beauty

Abd al-Malik's strategy of using architectural beauty as a medium for *da'wah* provides important lessons for the contemporary era.^4 Spiritual messages can be conveyed through aesthetics that touch souls, not only through rational argumentation.^2

The Dome of the Rock proved that Islam provides broad space for artistic expression, as long as it remains within the framework of *tawhid* and does not contradict Islamic principles.^3

### Tolerance within Firm Principles

The inscriptions of the Dome of the Rock show brilliant examples of how Islam can convey theological differences with People of the Book without losing respect and desire for dialogue.^1 The tone used was invitation for contemplation, not confrontation.^4

This attitude is very relevant in the context of religious plurality in the modern era, where interfaith dialogue requires firm principles balanced with wisdom in communication.^2

### Legacy Transcending Dynasties

The Dome of the Rock has stood for over 1,300 years, outlasting the Umayyad dynasty that built it.^3 This proves that works built with spiritual vision and high dedication can endure beyond political powers that are temporary.^1

The golden dome that gleams today remains a universally recognized symbol of Jerusalem, reminding the world that Islamic civilization once reached peaks of excellence in art, architecture, and spirituality.^4

> لَقَدْ كَانَ فِي قَصَصِهِمْ عِبْرَةٌ لِّأُولِي الْأَلْبَابِ ۗ مَا كَانَ حَدِيثًا يُفْتَرَىٰ وَلَٰكِن تَصْدِيقَ الَّذِي بَيْنَ يَدَيْهِ وَتَفْصِيلَ كُلِّ شَيْءٍ وَهُدًى وَرَحْمَةً لِّقَوْمٍ يُؤْمِنُونَ ﴿١١١﴾
>
> *"Indeed in their stories, there is a lesson for men of understanding. It is not a narrated tale, but a confirmation of what went before it — a detailed exposition of all things, and a guide and a mercy to any such as believe. ﴾111﴿"*
> — QS. Yusuf (12): 111

This verse reminds us that the history of the Dome of the Rock's construction provides valuable lessons about how faith, vision, and dedication can create lasting heritage for human civilization.^4^5
