# The Conquest of Makkah: A Bloodless Liberation

## Context: The Breach of the Treaty of Hudaybiyyah

In the sixth year of Hijrah (628 CE), the Muslims and Quraysh had signed the Treaty of Hudaybiyyah — a ten-year ceasefire agreement.^1 Though initially appearing disadvantageous to the Muslims, the treaty provided an unprecedented space for *da'wah*. In the two years following Hudaybiyyah, more people embraced Islam than in all the preceding years of the Prophet's mission combined.^2

In the eighth year of Hijrah (630 CE), however, Banu Bakr — allies of Quraysh — attacked Banu Khuza'ah, who were allied with the Muslims. The attack occurred at night, while Banu Khuza'ah were resting near the waters of al-Watir.^1 More critically, several Quraysh leaders actively assisted Banu Bakr by supplying weapons and even joining the fighting under cover of darkness. Ibn Hisham records that among them were Ikrimah ibn Abi Jahl, Safwan ibn Umayyah, and Suhayl ibn Amr — prominent Qurayshi figures.^1

**Amr ibn Salim al-Khuza'i** immediately traveled to Madinah to report the attack to the Prophet ﷺ. Standing before him, he recited poetry invoking the long-standing alliance between Banu Khuza'ah and Banu Hashim.^1 The Prophet ﷺ responded decisively: *"You shall be aided, O Amr ibn Salim."*^1

Quraysh recognized their fatal error. **Abu Sufyan ibn Harb** hastened to Madinah to repair the treaty and extend the truce. His mission failed completely — he approached the Prophet ﷺ, Abu Bakr, Umar, and even his own daughter **Umm Habibah** (the Prophet's wife), but no one would help him.^1 ^2 Umm Habibah even folded the sitting mat when Abu Sufyan attempted to sit on it, refusing to allow her polytheist father to sit on the Prophet's mat.

Abu Sufyan returned to Makkah empty-handed, and Quraysh knew that the consequences of their breach were inevitable.

## The Event: Preparations and the March to Makkah

The Prophet ﷺ ordered the Muslims to prepare, but kept the destination of the expedition secret.^2 Secrecy was the cornerstone of his strategy — he did not want Quraysh to have time to prepare defenses that would result in bloodshed.

A notable incident occurred when **Hatib ibn Abi Balta'ah**, a companion who had fought at Badr, sent a secret letter to Makkah revealing the Prophet's plans. Hatib did this not out of hypocrisy, but because he had family in Makkah without tribal protection. Allah revealed his action, and the Prophet ﷺ dispatched **Ali ibn Abi Talib** and **al-Zubayr ibn al-Awwam** to intercept the woman courier carrying the letter.^3

Imam al-Bukhari records^4:

> يَا رَسُولَ اللَّهِ لَا تَعْجَلْ عَلَيَّ إِنِّي كُنْتُ امْرَأً مُلْصَقًا فِي قُرَيْشٍ وَلَمْ أَكُنْ مِنْ أَنْفُسِهَا
>
> *"O Messenger of Allah, do not be hasty with me. I was a man attached to Quraysh, but not truly one of them."*
> — Sahih al-Bukhari, no. 3007, Kitab al-Jihad

**Umar ibn al-Khattab** asked permission to execute Hatib, but the Prophet ﷺ said: *"Was he not among the people of Badr? Indeed, Allah looked upon the people of Badr and said: Do as you wish, for I have already forgiven you."*^3 This pardon demonstrated a fundamental principle of the Prophet's leadership: judging a person by the totality of their service, not by a single mistake.

On the 10th of Ramadan, 8 AH, the Prophet ﷺ departed from Madinah with approximately 10,000 men — the largest force he had ever led.^1 ^2 The army grew as various tribes joined along the way. At Marr al-Zahran (near Makkah), the Prophet ﷺ ordered every unit to light a campfire, filling the valley with thousands of blazing fires. This spectacle was designed to project overwhelming strength and break Quraysh's will to resist.^2

It was here that **Abu Sufyan** came to survey the situation. **Al-Abbas ibn Abd al-Muttalib**, the Prophet's uncle who had recently emigrated, escorted Abu Sufyan to meet the Prophet ﷺ. When Abu Sufyan saw the sea of fires and the endless ranks of soldiers, he said to al-Abbas: *"Truly, your nephew's power has grown great."* Al-Abbas replied: *"This is prophethood."* Abu Sufyan said: *"Then, yes."*^1

## Key Dialogues and Evidence: Pivotal Moments of Liberation

The Prophet ﷺ entered Makkah on Friday, 20 Ramadan 8 AH (January 11, 630 CE), with his head bowed low upon his camel — so low that his beard nearly touched the saddle — as a sign of gratitude and humility before Allah.^2 He recited Surah al-Fath:

> إِنَّا فَتَحْنَا لَكَ فَتْحًا مُبِينًا ﴿١﴾
>
> *"Indeed, We have given you a clear conquest."*
> — QS. Al-Fath (48): 1

The army was divided into four columns entering Makkah from four different directions. **Khalid ibn al-Walid** led the right flank from the south. His column encountered brief resistance from a small group of Quraysh led by Ikrimah ibn Abi Jahl and Safwan ibn Umayyah, but this was quickly suppressed. The other columns met no resistance at all. Makkah was liberated with virtually no bloodshed — an extraordinary military and moral achievement.^4

After entering Makkah, the Prophet ﷺ proceeded to the Ka'bah. He performed *tawaf* seven times on his camel while destroying the 360 idols surrounding the Ka'bah with a staff in his hand, reciting:^1 ^3

> جَاءَ الْحَقُّ وَزَهَقَ الْبَاطِلُ إِنَّ الْبَاطِلَ كَانَ زَهُوقًا ﴿٨١﴾
>
> *"Truth has come and falsehood has vanished. Indeed, falsehood is ever bound to vanish. ﴾81﴿"*
> — QS. Al-Isra (17): 81

Then came the most historic moment. The Prophet ﷺ stood at the door of the Ka'bah facing the people of Makkah — people who had for twenty years tortured, expelled, boycotted, and attempted to kill him. They stood with bowed heads, awaiting their fate. The Prophet ﷺ asked:^4

*"O people of Quraysh, what do you think I am going to do with you? "*

They answered: *"Good. You are a noble brother, the son of a noble brother."*

The Prophet ﷺ declared:^1 ^2

*"Go, for you are free"* (*al-tulaqa'*).

These words — a mere handful — overturned the entire paradigm of ancient warfare. In an era when conquerors slaughtered, enslaved, or expelled the populations of conquered cities, the Prophet ﷺ set them free without condition.

## Analysis and Lessons: Victory Without the Sword

### A Brilliant Military Strategy

The Conquest of Makkah is a perfect demonstration of a principle that would later be articulated by Sun Tzu: *the supreme art of war is to subdue the enemy without fighting.* The Prophet ﷺ achieved this through:

1. **Total secrecy** — the expedition's destination was concealed until the last moment
2. **Speed of movement** — from Madinah to Makkah in rapid succession, denying Quraysh time to consolidate their defenses
3. **Show of force** — 10,000 campfires at Marr al-Zahran psychologically crushed the enemy's morale
4. **Multi-directional assault** — four columns from four directions made centralized defense impossible
5. **Pardon as a weapon** — the guarantee of safety for anyone who laid down arms eliminated the motivation to resist

### The Moral Dimension: Forgiveness as Strength

The Prophet's mass pardon on the day of Fath Makkah was not weakness — it was the highest form of strength. He pardoned people who had:
- Tortured his companions (Bilal, Ammar, the family of Yasir)
- Killed his uncle Hamzah and mutilated his body
- Expelled him from his homeland
- Boycotted his entire clan for three years until they nearly starved
- Repeatedly attempted to assassinate him

When **Hind bint Utbah** — who had chewed Hamzah's liver at Uhud — came to pledge allegiance and testify to her faith, the Prophet ﷺ accepted her.^1 When **Ikrimah ibn Abi Jahl** fled to Yemen, his wife sought a guarantee of safety from the Prophet and brought her husband back — Ikrimah then embraced Islam and became one of Islam's finest commanders.^1

### Fath Makkah in the Quran

Allah named this event *al-fath al-mubin* (the clear conquest) in Surah al-Fath. Scholars differ on whether Surah al-Fath was revealed concerning Hudaybiyyah or the Conquest of Makkah — the stronger view holds that the surah was revealed after Hudaybiyyah as a promise of the coming liberation, making both events part of a single continuum.^2

> لَقَدْ صَدَقَ اللَّهُ رَسُولَهُ الرُّؤْيَا بِالْحَقِّ ۖ لَتَدْخُلُنَّ الْمَسْجِدَ الْحَرَامَ إِن شَاءَ اللَّهُ آمِنِينَ ﴿٢٧﴾
>
> *"Certainly has Allah shown to His Messenger the vision in truth: you will surely enter al-Masjid al-Haram, if Allah wills, in safety. ﴾27﴿"*
> — QS. Al-Fath (48): 27

### Long-Term Impact^4

The Conquest of Makkah permanently transformed the geopolitical map of the Arabian Peninsula. With the fall of Makkah — the spiritual and political center of the Arab tribes — to Islam, there remained no compelling reason for other tribes to resist the call. The following year was called *'Am al-Wufud* (the Year of Delegations), when tribes from every corner of Arabia came in droves to embrace Islam.^2 This was the realization of Allah's words:

> إِذَا جَاءَ نَصْرُ اللَّهِ وَالْفَتْحُ ﴿١﴾ وَرَأَيْتَ النَّاسَ يَدْخُلُونَ فِي دِينِ اللَّهِ أَفْوَاجًا ﴿٢﴾
>
> *"When the help of Allah and the conquest come, and you see the people entering the religion of Allah in multitudes. ﴾2﴿"*
> — QS. An-Nasr (110): 1-2
