# The Conquest of North Africa (670–710 CE)

## Historical Context: The Byzantine Legacy in Africa

Before the arrival of Islam, North Africa was part of the Byzantine Empire, which had inherited the Roman territories. The region that Arab writers called *Ifriqiya* — encompassing modern Tunisia, eastern Libya, and eastern Algeria — had been a center of Mediterranean civilization for centuries. Meanwhile, the **Berber** (Amazigh) tribes, the indigenous people of the region, had a complex relationship with their Byzantine rulers — sometimes submitting, sometimes rebelling.^1

The earliest wave of Islamic expansion into North Africa began during the caliphate of Umar ibn al-Khattab, when Amr ibn al-As conquered Egypt in 641 CE. From Egypt, Muslim forces began moving westward into *Barqah* (Cyrenaica) and *Tarabulus* (Tripolitania).^1 However, a systematic and comprehensive conquest only began during the Umayyad era.

## The Events: Four Decades of Conquest

### Uqba ibn Nafi and the Founding of Qayrawan (670 CE)

The central figure in the conquest of North Africa was **Uqba ibn Nafi al-Fihri**, a commander appointed by the governor of Egypt to lead the western expedition. Al-Baladhuri in *Futuh al-Buldan* records that Uqba led a large army into Ifriqiya around the year 50 AH (670 CE).^1

Uqba's most monumental achievement was the founding of the city of **Qayrawan** (Kairouan) in modern Tunisia in 670 CE. The city was designed as both a military base and a center for the spread of Islam in North Africa. Ibn al-Athir in *Al-Kamil fi al-Tarikh* describes how Uqba chose a location far from the coast — to avoid surprise attacks from the Byzantine fleet — and ordered the construction of a great mosque that would become one of the oldest and most important mosques in the Islamic world: the **Great Mosque of Qayrawan**.^2

### The Expedition to the Far West (681–683 CE)

In 62 AH (681 CE), Uqba launched his legendary expedition across the entire Maghreb. Al-Tabari recounts that he marched from Qayrawan through Algeria, Morocco, until he reached the shores of the Atlantic Ocean.^3 There, according to the famous tradition, Uqba rode his horse into the waves and cried out:

*"O Allah, were it not for this sea, I would continue onward to the lands beyond it, fighting for Your cause!"*

However, Uqba's military success was not matched by adequate political consolidation. On his return journey from the Atlantic, he was ambushed by a combined Berber-Byzantine force under the command of **Kusayla**, a Berber chieftain of the Awraba tribe, near Biskra (modern Algeria) in 63 AH (683 CE). Uqba fell as a martyr in this battle along with most of his troops.^2 ^3

### Berber Resistance: Kusayla and al-Kahina

Uqba's death marked a temporary setback for Islamic power in the Maghreb. Kusayla recaptured Qayrawan and controlled Ifriqiya for several years.^2

After Kusayla was killed in battle against Muslim forces led by Zuhayr ibn Qais (688 CE), Berber resistance was continued by **al-Kahina** (Dihya) — a charismatic Berber woman leader from the Jarawa tribe. Al-Kahina defeated the Muslim forces under Hasan ibn al-Nu'man and controlled Ifriqiya for approximately five years.^2 She implemented a scorched-earth strategy to impede the Muslim advance — a move that ultimately alienated some of the local population.

### Final Consolidation: Hasan ibn al-Nu'man and Musa ibn Nusayr (698–710 CE)

The definitive conquest of North Africa was achieved by two final commanders. **Hasan ibn al-Nu'man** returned with a larger force, defeated al-Kahina around 698 CE, and most importantly, captured **Carthage** — the Byzantine capital in Africa — permanently ending Byzantine presence in the region.^1 ^2

At its culmination, **Musa ibn Nusayr** was appointed governor of North Africa and completed the consolidation of the entire Maghreb up to the Atlantic coast around 710 CE. Under his leadership, many Berber tribes not only embraced Islam but also became an integral part of the Muslim armies — including **Tariq ibn Ziyad**, a Berber commander who would later conquer Spain.^1 ^3

## Scriptural Motivation

The conquest of North Africa was driven by the spirit of *da'wah* and *jihad* rooted in the Quranic command^4:

> وَمَا لَكُمْ لَا تُقَاتِلُونَ فِي سَبِيلِ اللَّهِ وَالْمُسْتَضْعَفِينَ مِنَ الرِّجَالِ وَالنِّسَاءِ وَالْوِلْدَانِ ﴿٧٥﴾
>
> *"And what is the matter with you that you do not fight in the cause of Allah and for the oppressed among men, women, and children. ﴾75﴿"*
> — QS. An-Nisa (4): 75

The Muslim commanders brought not only military strength but also a message of justice and equality that appealed to many local populations — especially the Berber tribes who had long suffered under Byzantine tax discrimination.^1

## Analysis and Lessons

### A Gradual Strategy

The conquest of North Africa was not a single event but a gradual process spanning four decades that involved victories and defeats, advances and setbacks. This demonstrates that great changes in history rarely happen instantly — they require perseverance, adaptation, and patience.

### Cultural Integration

One of the greatest successes of this conquest was not on the battlefield but in cultural integration. The Berber tribes that initially resisted eventually became the primary pillars of Islamic civilization in the Maghreb. Qayrawan grew into a center of learning that rivaled Baghdad and Cordoba.

### The Role of Women in History

The story of al-Kahina demonstrates that in North African history, women could play significant leadership roles — both among those who resisted and those who eventually supported the spread of Islam.

### The Price of Sacrifice

Uqba ibn Nafi's martyrdom reminds us that every great achievement requires sacrifice. He did not live to see the fruits of what he planted, yet Qayrawan — the city he founded — endured for centuries as a beacon of Islamic civilization in Africa.
