# The Boycott of Banu Hashim at Shi'b Abi Talib (616–619 CE)

## Context

By the sixth year of prophethood (around 616 CE), the preaching of **Prophet Muhammad SAW** in Mecca had gained a momentum that alarmed the Quraysh leadership. The number of followers continued to grow, and the influence of Islam had begun reaching various layers of society — from enslaved people to young nobles.^1 The Quraysh leaders realized that persuasion, intimidation, and even the physical torture of individual Muslims had failed to halt the progress of the message.^2

The key factor frustrating the Quraysh was the firm protection provided by **Abu Talib** ibn Abd al-Muttalib, the Prophet's uncle and head of Banu Hashim. Although Abu Talib did not embrace Islam, he refused to hand over his nephew to the Quraysh. Tribal loyalty (*asabiyyah*) and his deep love for Muhammad formed a fortress that no political pressure could breach.^3

In the socio-political context of pre-Islamic Mecca, the tribe (*qabilah*) was the fundamental unit of protection. Attacking a member of a tribe meant confronting the entire tribe. The Quraysh therefore chose a collective strategy: to isolate all of Banu Hashim and Banu Abd al-Muttalib economically and socially, forcing them to withdraw their protection of the Prophet Muhammad SAW.^4

## The Events

### The Boycott Pact

At the beginning of the seventh year of prophethood (around 616 CE), the Quraysh leaders held a major assembly and agreed upon a boycott charter (*shahifah*). This charter was written by **Mansur ibn Ikrimah** — according to some narrations by **Baghidh ibn Amir ibn Hashim** — and was hung inside the Ka'bah as a symbol of the pact's sanctity and binding force.^5

The boycott charter contained three principal provisions:^6

1. **Marriage ban** — no one was to marry anyone from Banu Hashim or Banu Abd al-Muttalib, nor give their children in marriage to members of these two clans.
2. **Trade ban** — no commercial transactions with Banu Hashim or Banu Abd al-Muttalib; no one was to sell them food or any commodities whatsoever.
3. **Social isolation** — no one was to sit with, visit, or converse with members of the two clans until they handed Muhammad over to be killed.

The charter was signed by leaders of various Quraysh clans and was binding upon all inhabitants of Mecca. Violation of the pact was considered a betrayal of collective honor.^7

### Life in Shi'b Abi Talib

Banu Hashim and Banu Abd al-Muttalib — both Muslim and non-Muslim members — were forced to take refuge in a narrow ravine on the outskirts of Mecca known as *Shi'b Abi Talib* (the valley of Abu Talib). Only **Abu Lahab** ibn Abd al-Muttalib separated himself and joined the Quraysh side, choosing enmity toward Islam over kinship bonds.^8

For nearly three years (616–619 CE), life in the ravine was marked by extraordinary suffering. Food supplies were almost completely cut off. Ibn Hisham narrated that the cries of starving children could be heard beyond the valley walls, yet this served as "entertainment" for Quraysh leaders passing by.^9

**Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas** narrated his personal experience during the boycott:

> "One night I went out to relieve myself, and suddenly my foot stepped on something moist. It turned out to be a piece of dried camel hide. I picked it up, washed it, burned it until it became charcoal, then ground it and mixed it with water. I ate it, and it sustained me for three days."^1 ^0

This account illustrates the severity of the famine they endured. Food could only be obtained secretly during the pilgrimage season, when visitors from outside Mecca were not bound by the boycott agreement.^1 ^1

### Secret Solidarity

Despite the strict enforcement of the boycott, several Quraysh individuals secretly defied it, driven by conscience and kinship ties. **Hakim ibn Hizam**, Khadijah's nephew, smuggled wheat to his aunt on several occasions. **Hisham ibn Amr ibn al-Harith** of Banu Amir ibn Lu'ay regularly loaded his camel with food at night and released it toward the mouth of the ravine so that Banu Hashim could retrieve it.^1 ^2

These individual acts of humanity, though carried out in secrecy, demonstrated that the boycott was not unanimously accepted by all of Meccan society. Many felt that collective punishment of an entire clan — including women, children, and the elderly — was a measure that exceeded all bounds.^1 ^3

### Annulment of the Charter

In the tenth year of prophethood (619 CE), the movement to annul the boycott finally bore fruit. Five Quraysh men of moral courage — **Hisham ibn Amr**, **Zuhayr ibn Abi Umayyah**, **al-Mut'im ibn Adi**, **Abu al-Bakhtari ibn Hisham**, and **Zam'ah ibn al-Aswad** — agreed to openly oppose the charter.^1 ^4

Zuhayr ibn Abi Umayyah initiated the action before the Ka'bah, calling out to the people of Mecca: "O people of Mecca! Shall we eat and dress ourselves while Banu Hashim perish, unable to buy or sell? By Allah, I will not rest until this unjust charter severing ties of kinship is torn up! "^1 ^5

**Abu Jahl** attempted to thwart this effort, but the five men supported one another. When the charter was finally retrieved from inside the Ka'bah, it was discovered that termites had consumed the entire text except for the phrase *"Bismika Allahumma"* (In Your name, O Allah). Prophet Muhammad SAW had previously informed Abu Talib of this through revelation he had received, and when the charter was opened, it was found to be exactly as he had said.^1 ^6

## Dialogue and Sacred Texts

The boycott is connected to several important scriptural references. Regarding the suffering and patience endured, Allah the Almighty says:

> أَمْ حَسِبْتُمْ أَن تَدْخُلُوا الْجَنَّةَ وَلَمَّا يَأْتِكُم مَّثَلُ الَّذِينَ خَلَوْا مِن قَبْلِكُم ۖ مَّسَّتْهُمُ الْبَأْسَاءُ وَالضَّرَّاءُ وَزُلْزِلُوا ﴿٢١٤﴾
>
> *"Do you think that you will enter Paradise without undergoing such trials as were experienced by the believers before you? They were afflicted with suffering and adversity, and were so shaken. ﴾214﴿"*
> — QS. Al-Baqarah (2): 214

Regarding the termites consuming the boycott charter, this event was narrated by Ibn Ishaq through a chain of transmission indicating that Prophet Muhammad SAW received the news through revelation and conveyed it to Abu Talib before the charter was opened.^1 ^7 Abu Talib then went to the Quraysh assembly and said: "My nephew has informed me that Allah has sent termites upon your charter, which consumed everything in it except the name of Allah. If he is truthful, cease what you are doing to us. And if he lies, I will hand him over to you."^1 ^8

## Analysis and Lessons

### Political and Social Dimensions

The boycott of Shi'b Abi Talib represents one of the earliest documented forms of collective punishment (*collective sanctions*) in history. This strategy reflected the limitations of the Quraysh: they were unable to attack Banu Hashim militarily due to tribal norms, so they resorted to economic pressure and social isolation.^1 ^9

From a political science perspective, the boycott demonstrates the mechanisms of social control employed by the Quraysh oligarchy. The boycott charter functioned as "emergency legislation" ratified through the consensus of tribal leaders — a form of collective legislation in a society without a formal state.^2 ^0

### The Resilience of the Muslim Community

Three years of boycott revealed the extraordinary resilience of the early Muslim community. Several important lessons can be drawn:

1. **Cross-faith solidarity** — non-Muslim members of Banu Hashim shared in the suffering alongside the Muslims, demonstrating that bonds of kinship and justice can transcend differences of belief.^2 ^1
2. **Abu Talib's protective leadership** — though not a Muslim himself, Abu Talib sacrificed his comfort and safety to protect his nephew's mission. This stands as an example of how justice and compassion can emerge from anyone.^2 ^2
3. **Humanity prevails over hatred** — the secret aid from Hakim ibn Hizam, Hisham ibn Amr, and others proved that the human conscience cannot be entirely extinguished by social pressure.^2 ^3

### Long-term Impact

The boycott, rather than weakening Islam, actually strengthened the internal bonds of the Muslim community and generated sympathy from segments of Meccan society. Its termination also demonstrated that repressive strategies have limits — when oppression becomes too extreme, even those who initially supported it will turn against it.^2 ^4

However, three years of physical suffering left serious health consequences for many members of Banu Hashim. Several historians have linked the deaths of Abu Talib and **Khadijah bint Khuwaylid** shortly after the boycott ended (in the same year, 619 CE) to their deteriorating physical condition caused by prolonged starvation and pressure.^2 ^5
