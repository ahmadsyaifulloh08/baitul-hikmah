# Al-Ghazali: Ihya Ulum al-Din — Revival of Religious Sciences

## 11th Century Intellectual Context

The 11th century CE marked a crucial period in Islamic intellectual history. During this time, the Islamic world experienced rapid development in various fields of knowledge, from philosophy and theology to Sufism. However, tensions also emerged between different schools of thought.^1

Baghdad as the center of Islamic learning experienced complex intellectual dynamics. Muslim philosophers like al-Farabi and Ibn Sina had developed profound philosophical thought, but this raised concerns among traditional scholars who felt Islamic teachings were becoming mixed with Greek philosophy.^2

Amidst these conditions emerged a great figure who could bridge various streams of Islamic thought. **Abu Hamid Muhammad ibn Muhammad al-Ghazali** (1058-1111 CE) was born in Tus, Khurasan (now Iran), in a simple family with great love for knowledge.^3

## Al-Ghazali's Intellectual Journey

Al-Ghazali began his education in his hometown, then continued to Jurjan to study jurisprudence. However, his most significant academic achievement came when studying under **Imam al-Haramain al-Juwayni** at the Nizamiyyah Madrasah in Nishapur^1.

Al-Ghazali's intellectual brilliance was quickly recognized. At a young age, he had mastered various disciplines, from jurisprudence, legal methodology, theology, logic, to philosophy. His ability to debate and present strong arguments made him known as one of the most brilliant scholars of his time^2.

In 1091 CE, al-Ghazali was appointed professor at the Nizamiyyah Madrasah in Baghdad, the most prestigious educational institution of its time. Here he began writing his monumental works and engaging in intense intellectual debates with various schools of thought^3.

## Spiritual Crisis and Paradigm Shift

Despite reaching the peak of academic success, al-Ghazali experienced a profound spiritual crisis. In his autobiography "al-Munqidz min al-Dalal," he described how doubt began to penetrate his being about the nature of truth and the meaning of life^1.

This crisis culminated in 1095 CE when al-Ghazali suffered a psychological breakdown. He lost his ability to speak and teach, feeling that all his knowledge could not provide true spiritual satisfaction. This condition forced him to leave his prestigious position in Baghdad^2.

Al-Ghazali then began a spiritual journey that totally transformed his life. He wandered to Damascus, Jerusalem, and Mecca to perform the hajj pilgrimage. During this period, he lived a simple Sufi life and devoted himself to seeking deeper spiritual meaning^3.

## Ihya Ulum al-Din: Magnum Opus

After ten years of contemplative and spiritual life, al-Ghazali returned to academic world with a completely changed perspective. His spiritual experience gave birth to the masterpiece that transformed Islamic thought: "Ihya Ulum al-Din" (Revival of Religious Sciences)^1.

This monumental work consists of forty books divided into four major sections: *Ibadat* (worship), *Adat* (social customs), *Muhlikat* (things that destroy the soul), and *Munjiyat* (things that save the soul). Each section contains ten books discussing different aspects of Muslim life^2.

What was revolutionary about "Ihya Ulum al-Din" was al-Ghazali's holistic approach to integrating various dimensions of Islamic life. He successfully integrated jurisprudence, theology, ethics, and Sufism within a coherent framework of thought^3.

In the preface to his work, al-Ghazali explained the purpose of writing:

> وَمَا أَرۡسَلۡنَٰكَ إِلَّا رَحۡمَةٗ لِّلۡعَٰلَمِينَ ﴿١٠٧﴾
>
> *"And We have not sent you except as a mercy to all the worlds. ﴾107﴿"*
> — QS. Al-Anbiya' (21): 107^1

## Methodology and Approach

One of the strengths of "Ihya Ulum al-Din" lies in the methodology used by al-Ghazali. He did not merely present jurisprudential laws dryly, but always explained the wisdom and spiritual dimensions behind every Islamic teaching^2.

Al-Ghazali began each discussion with evidence from the Qur'an and hadith, then explained scholars' opinions, and concluded with spiritual and psychological dimensions. This approach allowed readers to understand not only the outward aspects of Islamic teachings but also their inward dimensions^3.

In the book on prayer, for example, al-Ghazali not only explained the procedures of prayer but also the spiritual meaning of every movement and recitation. He explained how prayer could become a means of spiritual ascension toward Allah^1.

## Integration of Knowledge and Spirituality

Al-Ghazali's most important contribution was his success in integrating knowledge and spirituality in Islam. Previously, there was a dichotomy between *ulama zahir* (scholars of Islamic law) and *ulama batin* (Sufi scholars). Al-Ghazali proved that both were not contradictory but complementary^2.

In "Ihya Ulum al-Din," he showed how every aspect of Muslim life, from ritual worship to social interaction, has profound spiritual dimensions. The concept of *ihsan* he taught instructed the community to worship as if seeing Allah^3.

Al-Ghazali also introduced the concept of soul purification (*tazkiyah al-nafs*) as a fundamental aspect of Muslim life. He explained in detail various diseases of the heart such as *riya* (showing off), *ujub* (arrogance), and *hasad* (envy), along with ways to cure them^1.

## Influence and Criticism

"Ihya Ulum al-Din" received extraordinary reception from Muslims worldwide. This work was translated into various languages and became a primary reference in Islamic education for centuries. Scholars gave high praise to the depth and comprehensiveness of this work^2.

However, al-Ghazali's work also drew criticism, especially from Maghrib scholars like Ibn Taymiyyah and Ibn al-Jawzi. They criticized the use of weak (*dha'if*) hadiths and Israelite stories (*israiliyyat*) in some parts of "Ihya Ulum al-Din"^3.

Nevertheless, the value and contribution of "Ihya Ulum al-Din" remain undeniable. This work successfully restored balance in Islamic thought and provided a middle path between rational and emotional extremism in religion^1.

## Eternal Legacy

The influence of al-Ghazali and "Ihya Ulum al-Din" can be seen in various aspects of Muslim life to this day. In Indonesia, this work has been translated and become required reading in various pesantren and Islamic educational institutions^2.

Al-Ghazali's methodology in integrating outward and inward aspects of Islam provides inspiration for developing holistic Islamic education. The concepts he proposed about ethics, spirituality, and character building remain relevant in modern life contexts^3.

Al-Ghazali's death in 1111 CE in Tus marked the end of one of the most brilliant periods in Islamic intellectual history. However, his intellectual and spiritual legacy continues to live through "Ihya Ulum al-Din" and his other works^1.
