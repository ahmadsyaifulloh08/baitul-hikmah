# Al-Ghazali: Ihya Ulum al-Din — Revival of Religious Sciences

## 11th Century Intellectual Context

The 11th century CE marked a crucial period in Islamic intellectual history. During this time, the Islamic world experienced rapid development in various fields of knowledge, from philosophy and theology to Sufism. However, tensions also emerged between different schools of thought.¹

Baghdad as the center of Islamic learning experienced complex intellectual dynamics. Muslim philosophers like al-Farabi and Ibn Sina had developed profound philosophical thought and translated and commented on the works of Aristotle and Plato. However, this raised concerns among traditional scholars who felt Islamic teachings were becoming mixed with Greek philosophy.²

The Nizamiyyah Madrasah established by Vizier Nizam al-Mulk became an arena for fierce intellectual debates. On one side, philosophers developed rational and logical systems of thought. On the other side, traditional scholars defended the purity of Islamic teachings based on the Qur'an and hadith. This tension required a figure capable of building bridges between both camps.³

Amidst these conditions emerged a great figure who could bridge various streams of Islamic thought. **Abu Hamid Muhammad ibn Muhammad al-Ghazali** (1058-1111 CE) was born in Tus, Khurasan (now Iran), in a simple family with great love for knowledge. His father was a pious wool-spinner who, before his death, entrusted al-Ghazali's education to a Sufi.⁴

## Al-Ghazali's Intellectual Journey

Al-Ghazali began his education in his hometown, then continued to Jurjan to study jurisprudence. In Jurjan, he studied under Abu Nasr al-Ismaili and began to demonstrate his extraordinary intellectual brilliance. His ability to memorize and understand lessons quickly amazed his teachers.¹

His most significant academic achievement came when studying under **Imam al-Haramain al-Juwayni** at the Nizamiyyah Madrasah in Nishapur. Al-Juwayni, who was called "Imam al-Haramain" for having taught in Mecca and Medina, was a great scholar of Ash'arite theology and Shafi'i jurisprudence. Under his guidance, al-Ghazali mastered not only jurisprudence and legal methodology, but also theology, logic, and philosophy.²

Al-Ghazali's intellectual brilliance was quickly recognized. At a young age, he had mastered various disciplines profoundly. Al-Juwayni even called him "bahrun mughriqun" (an overwhelming ocean), indicating the depth of his knowledge. His ability to debate and present strong arguments made him known as one of the most brilliant scholars of his time.³

In 1091 CE, after al-Juwayni's death, al-Ghazali was appointed by Vizier Nizam al-Mulk as professor at the Nizamiyyah Madrasah in Baghdad, the most prestigious educational institution of its time. This appointment was an extraordinary honor, considering Baghdad was the center of Islamic learning and the Nizamiyyah Madrasah was where the best scholars gathered. Here he began writing his monumental works and engaging in intense intellectual debates with various schools of thought.⁴

## Spiritual Crisis at Nizamiyyah Baghdad

Despite reaching the peak of academic success and enjoying high social status, al-Ghazali began to experience a profound spiritual crisis. In his autobiography "al-Munqidz min al-Dalal" (The Deliverer from Error), he described how doubt began to penetrate his being about the nature of truth and the true meaning of life.¹

Al-Ghazali began to question the various systems of knowledge he had mastered. He studied and critiqued four groups of truth seekers: the theologians (*mutakallimin*), the philosophers, the followers of *ta'lim* (the Isma'ili group), and the Sufis. Through deep research, he found weaknesses in each method of seeking truth.²

His critique of the philosophers was expressed in the work "Tahafut al-Falasifah" (The Incoherence of the Philosophers), where he refuted twenty propositions of Muslim philosophers that he considered to deviate from Islamic teachings. Three of these he categorized as disbelief (*kufr*): the eternity of the world, the limitation of God's knowledge, and the denial of bodily resurrection. This work demonstrated his profound mastery of philosophy while showing his commitment to purifying Islamic thought.³

This crisis culminated in 1095 CE when al-Ghazali suffered a severe psychological breakdown. He lost his ability to speak and teach, his tongue felt stiff, and he could not swallow food normally. This condition was not merely a physical disorder but a manifestation of deep inner conflict. He felt that all his knowledge could not provide true spiritual satisfaction or genuine certainty.⁴

This experience forced him to leave his prestigious position in Baghdad. This decision shocked the academic world of his time, as it was very rare for a scholar to abandon such an honorable position. He spread the story that he would go on pilgrimage to hide his real intention of living a Sufi life.¹

## Spiritual Journey and Contemplation

Al-Ghazali then began a spiritual journey that totally transformed his life. He wandered to Damascus and secluded himself in a mosque minaret for dhikr and prayer. In Damascus, he lived a simple Sufi life, far from the luxury of academic Baghdad. The experience in Damascus's minaret gave him spiritual enlightenment that he had never obtained through intellectual learning.²

From Damascus, he continued his journey to Jerusalem and secluded himself in the Dome of the Rock. In this holy place, he deepened his spiritual practice and contemplation. Finally, he went to Mecca to perform the hajj pilgrimage, which became the peak of his spiritual transformation.³

During this ten-year period (1095-1105 CE), al-Ghazali lived a life very different from before. He was no longer involved in intellectual debates or teaching at formal institutions. Instead, he devoted himself to seeking deeper spiritual meaning through dhikr, *muhasabah* (introspection), and *riyadah* (spiritual training).⁴

This experience gave him a new understanding of the nature of knowledge and spirituality. He realized that intellectual knowledge without direct spiritual experience was merely shallow knowledge. The search for true truth required a combination of reason and heart, between knowledge and practice.¹

## Ihya Ulum al-Din: Magnum Opus

After ten years of contemplative and spiritual life, al-Ghazali returned to the academic world with a completely changed perspective. His spiritual experience gave birth to the masterpiece that transformed Islamic thought: "Ihya Ulum al-Din" (Revival of Religious Sciences).²

This monumental work was written after al-Ghazali returned to Tus and began teaching again on a limited scale. "Ihya Ulum al-Din" consists of forty books divided into four major sections (*rubu'*): *Rubu' al-Ibadat* (quarter on worship), *Rubu' al-Adat* (quarter on customs), *Rubu' al-Muhlikat* (quarter on destructive things), and *Rubu' al-Munjiyat* (quarter on saving things). Each *rubu'* contains ten books discussing different aspects of Muslim life.³

*Rubu' al-Ibadat* discusses various ritual worship such as purification, prayer, zakat, fasting, and hajj, but with emphasis on their spiritual dimensions. *Rubu' al-Adat* examines Muslim social life from eating ethics, marriage, seeking livelihood, to social interaction. *Rubu' al-Muhlikat* explains various diseases of the heart to be avoided, while *Rubu' al-Munjiyat* describes praiseworthy qualities to be cultivated.⁴

What was revolutionary about "Ihya Ulum al-Din" was al-Ghazali's holistic approach to integrating various dimensions of Islamic life. He successfully integrated jurisprudence, theology, ethics, and Sufism within a coherent framework of thought. There was no longer a dichotomy between outward scholars and inward scholars, between shari'ah and *tarekat*.¹

In the preface to his work, al-Ghazali explained the purpose of writing by quoting a verse about the Prophet's mission:

> وَمَا أَرۡسَلۡنَٰكَ إِلَّا رَحۡمَةٗ لِّلۡعَٰلَمِينَ ﴿١٠٧﴾
>
> *"And We have not sent you except as a mercy to all the worlds. ﴾107﴿"*
> — QS. Al-Anbiya' (21): 107

This verse inspired al-Ghazali to make his work a mercy for the Muslim community by reviving the true religious sciences, namely knowledge that not only fills the mind but also purifies the heart.²^1

## Innovative Methodology and Approach

One of the strengths of "Ihya Ulum al-Din" lies in the innovative methodology used by al-Ghazali. He did not merely present jurisprudential laws dryly, but always explained the philosophical wisdom and spiritual dimensions behind every Islamic teaching. This approach allowed readers to understand not only the outward aspects of Islamic teachings but also their inward dimensions.³

Al-Ghazali began each discussion with evidence from the Qur'an and authentic hadith, then explained scholars' opinions from various schools, and concluded with spiritual and psychological dimensions. This method reflected his mastery of various disciplines and his ability to synthesize different thoughts.⁴

In the book on prayer, for example, al-Ghazali not only explained prayer procedures according to the Shafi'i school, but also the spiritual meaning of every movement and recitation. He explained how the opening *takbir* symbolizes cutting ties with the mortal world, how *ruku'* and *sujud* manifest humility before Allah, and how *khushu'* could be achieved through perfect presence of heart.¹

Another example is his discussion of zakat. Al-Ghazali not only explained the nisab and rates of zakat according to jurisprudence, but also explored the spiritual meaning of zakat as purification of the soul from miserliness and love of the world. He explained how zakat could become a means of spiritual ascension when accompanied by sincere intention and awareness of Allah's blessings.²

## The Concept of Ihsan and Soul Purification

Al-Ghazali's most important contribution was his success in integrating knowledge and spirituality in Islam. Previously, there was a sharp dichotomy between outward scholars (legal experts) and inward scholars (Sufis). The first group considered Sufism as innovation (*bid'ah*), while the second group considered jurisprudence as low knowledge. Al-Ghazali proved that both were not contradictory but complementary.³

In "Ihya Ulum al-Din," he showed how every aspect of Muslim life, from ritual worship to social interaction, has profound spiritual dimensions. The concept of *ihsan* he taught - "worshipping Allah as if you see Him, and if you cannot see Him, then (know that) He sees you" - became the basic principle in his entire spiritual approach.⁴

Al-Ghazali introduced the concept of soul purification (*tazkiyah al-nafs*) as a fundamental aspect of Muslim life. In *Rubu' al-Muhlikat*, he explained in detail various diseases of the heart such as *riya* (showing off), *ujub* (arrogance), *hasad* (envy), *ghadab* (anger), and *hubb al-dunya* (excessive love of the world). Each disease was analyzed from psychological aspects, its causes, its impact on spiritual life, and ways to cure it.¹

Conversely, in *Rubu' al-Munjiyat*, he described praiseworthy qualities to be cultivated such as *taubah* (repentance), patience, gratitude, *khauf* (fear of Allah), *raja'* (hope), *zuhud* (detachment from the world), and *mahabbah* (love for Allah). This discussion was accompanied by practical methods for developing these qualities.²

## Tahafut al-Falasifah and Critique of Philosophy

Before writing "Ihya Ulum al-Din," al-Ghazali had written "Tahafut al-Falasifah" (The Incoherence of the Philosophers), a work that demonstrated his profound mastery of philosophy and his sharp critique. In this work, he refuted twenty propositions of Muslim philosophers like al-Farabi and Ibn Sina that he considered contrary to Islamic teachings.³

The three propositions he most criticized were: (1) the eternity of the world (*qidam al-alam*), (2) the limitation of Allah's knowledge of particulars (*adam ilmihi ta'ala bi'l-juz'iyyat*), and (3) the denial of bodily resurrection (*inkar al-ba'th al-jismani*). These three propositions were categorized by al-Ghazali as disbelief because they fundamentally contradicted Quranic teachings.⁴

However, al-Ghazali's critique of philosophy did not mean anti-rationality. Instead, he used logic and philosophical argumentation to refute the philosophers. This showed that he fully mastered philosophical methods, but he rejected conclusions that contradicted revelation. This approach later influenced the development of more moderate and balanced Islamic theology.¹

## Influence on Sunni Theology

"Ihya Ulum al-Din" had a profound influence on the development of Sunni theology. Al-Ghazali's work successfully provided a middle path between excessive rationalist approaches and rigid traditionalist approaches. He maintained basic Ash'arite principles while integrating spiritual dimensions previously neglected in theology.²

Al-Ghazali's concept of balance between reason and *naql* (revelation) became a new paradigm in Islamic theology. He showed that reason has certain limits and cannot reach absolute truth without the guidance of revelation. However, reason still has an important role in understanding and implementing revealed teachings.³

This influence can be seen in the works of later Sunni theologians such as Fakhr al-Din al-Razi, al-Shahrastani, and al-Nasafi. They adopted al-Ghazali's balanced approach between rationality and spirituality, between theology and Sufism.⁴

## Comparison with Contemporary Philosophers

Al-Ghazali's position in Islamic intellectual history becomes clearer when compared with contemporary and later philosophers. Ibn Rushd (Averroes) later wrote "Tahafut al-Tahafut" (The Incoherence of Incoherence) to refute al-Ghazali's critique of philosophy. However, Ibn Rushd's work did not receive wide acceptance in the Eastern Islamic world, showing that al-Ghazali's approach was more accepted by mainstream Islam.¹

Unlike Ibn Sina who tended to prioritize reason, al-Ghazali emphasized the balance between reason and spiritual intuition. While Ibn Rushd tried to maintain the authority of reason in all fields, al-Ghazali showed the limitations of reason in reaching spiritual and metaphysical truth.²

Al-Ghazali's approach later influenced great figures like Ibn Arabi and Jalal al-Din Rumi, although they developed deeper mystical aspects. In a broader context, al-Ghazali can be seen as the founder of an Islamic tradition that balances orthodoxy and spirituality.³

## Criticism and Response

Although "Ihya Ulum al-Din" received extraordinary reception from the majority of Muslims, this work also drew criticism from various circles. Maghrib scholars like Ibn Taymiyyah and Ibn al-Jawzi criticized the use of weak (*dha'if*) hadith and Israelite stories (*israiliyyat*) in some parts of this work. They believed that al-Ghazali was too permissive in accepting unauthentic narrations.⁴

Ibn Taymiyyah specifically criticized some Sufi concepts in the "Ihya" that he considered inconsistent with Salafi practice. He rejected the concept of *fana* (annihilation of self-consciousness) and some dhikr practices he considered innovations. However, Ibn Taymiyyah still acknowledged al-Ghazali's excellence in other aspects such as his critique of philosophy and his integration of knowledge and practice.¹

On the other hand, Eastern scholars like al-Nawawi, al-Subki, and al-Suyuti gave very positive assessments of "Ihya Ulum al-Din." They considered this work one of the most important works in Islamic history after the Qur'an and hadith collections.²

To respond to such criticism, some scholars later wrote summaries of the "Ihya" that cleaned out weak hadith, such as "Mukhtasar Ihya Ulum al-Din" by al-Zabidi and "Bidayat al-Hidayah" by al-Ghazali himself, which was a concise and practical version.³

## Eternal Legacy and Contemporary Relevance

The influence of al-Ghazali and "Ihya Ulum al-Din" can be seen in various aspects of Muslim life to this day. In Indonesia, this work has been translated completely and become required reading in various pesantren and Islamic educational institutions. Al-Ghazali's holistic approach in integrating outward and inward aspects of Islam is very suitable for the moderate and inclusive tradition of Indonesian Islam.⁴

Al-Ghazali's methodology in integrating outward and inward aspects of Islam provides inspiration for developing holistic Islamic education that emphasizes not only cognitive aspects but also affective and psychomotor aspects. The concepts he proposed about ethics, spirituality, and character building remain relevant in modern life contexts full of challenges.¹

In the context of modern psychology, al-Ghazali's approach in analyzing various conditions of the human soul and methods of healing shows compatibility with developments in psychology and spiritual therapy. The concepts of soul purification and development of positive qualities that he developed have similarities with contemporary therapeutic approaches.²

Al-Ghazali's death in 1111 CE in Tus marked the end of one of the most brilliant periods in Islamic intellectual history. However, his intellectual and spiritual legacy continues to live through "Ihya Ulum al-Din" and his other works. The title "Hujjat al-Islam" (Proof of Islam) given to him reflects the Muslim community's recognition of his extraordinary contribution to purifying and reviving Islamic teachings.³

Al-Ghazali succeeded in showing that Islam is a balanced religion that accommodates the needs of reason and heart, this world and the hereafter, shari'ah and *tarekat*. This legacy became the foundation for the development of moderate and inclusive Islam in various parts of the world to this day.⁴.^2.^3
