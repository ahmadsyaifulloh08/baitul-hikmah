# Sultan Baibars — Expulsion of the Crusaders

## Background

After the glorious victory at Ain Jalut in 1260 CE, the Islamic world faced a new challenge: expelling the remaining Crusaders who still controlled strategic fortresses in the Levant. **Al-Malik al-Dhahir Rukn al-Din Baibars al-Bunduqdari** became the Mamluk Sultan in 1260 CE after killing Sultan Qutuz in a political dispute.^1

Baibars inherited a complex situation: Mongols still threatened from the east, Crusaders controlled the coastline from Antioch to Acre, and the Mamluk internal politics was full of intrigue. However, this figure dubbed the "Lion of Egypt" would prove himself as one of the greatest Muslim commanders in history.^2

## Character and Strategy of Baibars

Baibars possessed a rare combination of strategic intelligence, combat courage, and cunning diplomacy. Ibn Abd al-Dhahir, his secretary, recorded that Baibars always studied the battlefield in detail before attacking.^3 He developed a sophisticated intelligence system with spy networks reaching into Crusader fortresses.

Baibars' main strategy was "lightning war" (*harb al-barq*) - sudden attacks, destroying targets, and retreating before the enemy could organize assistance. He was also skilled in psychological warfare, often sending threatening letters to Crusader commanders before attacking.^2

## Campaigns Against the Crusaders (1265-1271 CE)

### Antioch - Peak of Crusader Cruelty

In May 1268 CE, Baibars launched a massive assault on Antioch, one of the most important Crusader strongholds. In a letter sent to Bohemond VI, Prince of Antioch, Baibars wrote: "You will see your soldiers torn by swords, your houses plundered, your wealth seized... and you will regret not accepting my protection!"^4

The attack lasted four days. The city that had once been the center of Crusader power fell with enormous casualties. Baibars deliberately did not inform Bohemond VI, who was in Tripoli, so he only learned of his city's destruction after it was all over.^3

### Krak des Chevaliers - The Impregnable Fortress

Krak des Chevaliers, the strongest Crusader fortress built by the Knights Hospitaller, was considered impossible to conquer. This fortress had withstood dozens of attacks for 160 years. However, in April 1271 CE, Baibars used brilliant tactics.

Instead of a frontal siege, Baibars cut off the fortress's supply lines and used the latest siege engines. More cunningly, he forged a letter from the Grand Master of the Hospitallers ordering the fortress's surrender. After 36 days of siege, this "impregnable" fortress finally surrendered.^4

## Diplomacy and Politics

Baibars was not only a war commander but also a skilled diplomat. He established relations with Byzantine Emperor Michael VIII Palaiologos to face the Mongol threat together. With Europe, he even corresponded with kings such as Alfonso X of Castile and James I of Aragon.^1

In facing the Mongols, Baibars used a "two-front war" strategy - attacking Crusaders in the west while defending the eastern border from Persian Ilkhanate attacks. He managed to defeat Mongol forces at Elbistan (1277 CE), showing that the victory at Ain Jalut was not mere luck.^2

## Military and Administrative Reforms

Baibars carried out massive modernization of the Mamluk military. He established a new training system for cavalry (*furusiyya*), strengthened military architecture by building and renovating dozens of fortresses, and developed a rapid communication system with *barid* (express post) that could deliver messages from Cairo to Damascus in 4 days.^3

He also reformed the economic system by increasing trade with India and Central Asia, and strengthened the naval fleet to control Mediterranean trade routes. These policies made the Mamluk Sultanate a powerful economic force.^4

## End of Life and Legacy

Baibars died on July 1, 1277 CE in Damascus under suspicious circumstances. Some sources mention he was poisoned by his personal physician on orders from political enemies, while others cite natural illness.^1

Baibars' legacy was enormous: he successfully expelled Crusaders from most of the Levant, repeatedly defeated Mongols, and built a strong foundation for the continuation of the Mamluk Sultanate. More importantly, he showed that with proper strategy and strong leadership, the Muslim ummah could rise from adversity.

## Wisdom and Lessons

Sultan Baibars taught several important principles:

**Thorough preparation**: Every campaign began with deep research about enemies and battlefields. **Adaptability**: Baibars did not stick to one strategy but adapted tactics to situations. **Perseverance**: He did not despair despite facing fortresses that seemed impossible to conquer.
