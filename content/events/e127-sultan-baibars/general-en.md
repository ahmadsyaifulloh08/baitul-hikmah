# Sultan Baibars — Lion of Egypt, Expeller of the Crusaders (1223-1277 CE)

## Origins: From Kipchak Slave to Sultan

**Al-Malik al-Dhahir Rukn al-Din Baibars al-Bunduqdari** was born around 1223 CE in the Central Asian steppes as a child of the Kipchak Turk tribe. Fate led to his capture and sale as a slave (*mamluk*) while still a teenager, then brought to Egypt to be trained as an elite soldier. The name "al-Bunduqdari" derives from *bunduq* (rifle), referring to his extraordinary skill in using firearms and arrows.^1

His early history as a Kipchak slave gave Baibars a unique perspective on suffering and injustice, which later shaped his character as a leader who was harsh yet fair. Ibn Abd al-Dhahir, his secretary and official biographer, recorded that Baibars had piercing blue eyes (*azraq al-'aynayn*) and a tall, sturdy posture — typical Kipchak characteristics that made him easily recognizable on the battlefield.^2

In the Mamluk system, selected slaves received the best military education in the world at that time. Baibars mastered the art of horsemanship (*furusiyya*), archery, swordsmanship, as well as military and political strategy. What distinguished him from other Mamluks was his intelligence in learning languages — he mastered Arabic, Turkish, Mongol, and even some Frankish dialects used by the Crusaders.^1

## Rise to Power: Murder Amid Victory

On October 23, 1260 CE, only 50 days after the glorious victory at Ain Jalut, a shocking event occurred: **Baibars and a group of senior amirs killed Sultan Qutuz** on the return journey from Syria to Egypt. This murder was triggered by disputes over the distribution of territories liberated from the Mongols.^2

Sultan Qutuz insisted that all of Syria should be directly integrated into the Mamluk Sultanate with governors appointed from Cairo. Baibars, feeling his services in the Ain Jalut victory were not properly rewarded, demanded autonomous governorship over part of Syria. When Qutuz refused, tensions culminated in a confrontation that ended tragically.^2

Ibn Taghribirdi recorded Qutuz's last words when he saw Baibars approaching with a drawn sword: *"Baibars, is this the reward for my kindness to you?"* Baibars replied coldly: *"Sultan, Syria needs a leader who understands it. You are too far from here."* Then he stabbed Qutuz to death on the spot.^2

This murder reflected the brutal nature of Mamluk politics, but also showed Baibars' ambition and political intelligence. He knew that by controlling Syria, he would control the most important trade routes and become a power equal to Egypt.^1

## Strategic Situation: Threats from Three Directions

When he ascended the throne in November 1260 CE, Baibars faced a very complex strategic situation. The Mamluk Sultanate was surrounded by enemies from three directions:

**From the East**: Hulagu Khan and the Mongol Ilkhanate forces still threatening from Persia and Mesopotamia. Despite defeat at Ain Jalut, Mongol strength was still very great and preparing for a counterattack.^3

**From the North**: Crusader States still controlling the strategic coastline from Antioch to Acre, including fortresses considered impregnable like Krak des Chevaliers and Margat. They had access to the Mediterranean and support from Europe.^3

**From Within**: Internal Mamluk political intrigue full of competition among amirs, plus Baibars' legitimacy questioned because of how he rose to power through murder.^2

Making the situation more difficult, Baibars had to build loyalty among troops that were partly still loyal to the late Qutuz. He overcame this with a combination of firmness and generosity — punishing opposing amirs while giving abundant rewards to supporters.^2

## The Baridi Intelligence System: The Best Spy Network

One of Baibars' greatest innovations was the development of an intelligence system called *al-baridi* (express post), which became the backbone of all his military campaigns. This system consisted of a network of spies, fast messengers, and relay stations spread from Cairo to Anatolia.^4

The *baridi* network could deliver information from Damascus to Cairo in four days — extraordinary speed for the 13th century CE. More than just communication, this system also gathered detailed intelligence about enemies: troop numbers, fortress conditions, even personal details about Crusader commanders.^4

Ibn Abd al-Dhahir recorded that Baibars had spies inside Bohemond VI's palace in Antioch, even among the Hospitaller and Templar knights. The information gathered was very detailed: patrol schedules, fortress weak points, even personal habits of commanders. This enabled Baibars to plan attacks with stunning precision.^2

The *baridi* system was also used for psychological warfare. Baibars often sent threatening letters to his enemies before attacking, containing very accurate details about their strengths and weaknesses — showing that he knew everything. The psychological effect was enormous: many fortresses surrendered without resistance due to fear of Baibars' intelligence capabilities.^4

## The Antioch Campaign 1268 CE: Destruction of a Christian Metropolis

**Antioch** was one of the most important cities in Christian history — where Jesus' followers were first called "Christians" and became the center of the Eastern Orthodox Patriarchate. For the Crusaders, Antioch was not just a strategic fortress, but also a very important religious symbol. Therefore, when Baibars decided to attack it in May 1268 CE, this was not just an ordinary military operation.^5

Attack preparations lasted for months. Baibars' spies reported that **Bohemond VI**, Prince of Antioch, was in Tripoli and left the city with minimal guard. This opportunity was too good to miss.^5

In a letter sent to Bohemond VI before attacking, Baibars wrote with a threatening tone that reflected his confidence:^2

*"To Bohemond, the arrogant blonde son: You will see your soldiers torn by swords, your houses plundered, your wealth seized, your women become widows, your children become orphans. You will regret not accepting my protection when there was still time. Now it's too late — Antioch will become ash!"*^2

The attack began on May 14, 1268 CE with bombardment using the latest siege engines brought from Damascus. Baibars used giant *manjaniq* (trebuchet) capable of hurling stones weighing 150 kg with high accuracy. Antioch's walls, though thick and strong, could not withstand the repeated hammering.^5

What made this attack so effective was multi-directional coordination. While *manjaniq* destroyed the main wall, Mamluk forces attacked from three different points simultaneously, preventing defenders from concentrating resistance. In four days, the city that had survived 170 years under Crusader control finally fell.^5

The massacre that followed was horrific. Al-Maqrizi recorded that thousands of Christian residents were killed, while the rest were sold as slaves. Churches were burned and Christian monuments destroyed. Baibars deliberately did not inform Bohemond VI about the city's fall until everything was over — psychological warfare at its most cruel level.^3

## Krak des Chevaliers 1271 CE: Conquering the "Impregnable"

**Krak des Chevaliers** (*Hisn al-Akrad*) was the greatest work of medieval military architecture. Built by the Knights Hospitaller in the 12th century CE, this fortress was considered impossible to conquer. With double walls, concentric towers, and unlimited water supply, Krak had withstood dozens of attacks for 160 years.^6

However, Baibars saw what his predecessors did not: Krak was not just a military problem, but also economic and psychological. This fortress controlled trade routes between Damascus and the coast, giving the Hospitallers very large income. Destroying Krak meant cutting the economic lifeline of Crusaders in the region.^6

The siege began on March 3, 1271 CE with a different strategy from the usual frontal assault. Baibars first cut all supply lines to the fortress, then built a permanent siege system with palisades and trenches surrounding the entire complex. No one could enter or leave.^6

Baibars' secret weapon was the latest siege engines adapted from Chinese technology. The *manjaniq al-firangi* (Frankish-style trebuchet) he developed could hurl larger projectiles with better accuracy than conventional siege equipment. Every day, dozens of giant stones hammered Krak's walls, slowly eroding the seemingly impregnable defenses.^4

More brilliant was Baibars' psychological warfare. On the 30th day of siege, when defender morale began to drop, Baibars forged a letter from the **Grand Master of Hospitallers in Acre** ordering the fortress's surrender to "avoid unnecessary massacre." This fake letter was made very carefully, using seals stolen by spies and authentic Latin language style.^2

After 36 days of siege, on April 8, 1271 CE, **Krak des Chevaliers finally surrendered**. The fortress considered "impregnable" by all of Europe could apparently be conquered with a combination of technology, strategy, and clever psychological warfare.^6

Baibars showed unexpected generosity to the surrendering defenders. The Hospitaller knights were allowed to leave safely to Tripoli, carrying their weapons and personal belongings. This was not just a diplomatic gesture, but also political calculation — Baibars wanted to build a reputation as a leader who honored honorable surrender.^6

## Multi-Track Diplomacy: Playing with All Parties

Baibars' advantage was not only on the battlefield, but also in the complex diplomatic game of the 13th century CE. He understood that military victory had to be supported by strong political alliances, especially in facing the Mongol threat that still lurked from the east.^3

With **Byzantine Emperor Michael VIII Palaiologos**, Baibars forged mutually beneficial strategic relations. Byzantium needed allies against the Mongol threat and Charles of Anjou from Sicily, while the Mamluks required access to the Black Sea for trade with Central Asia. This cooperation resulted in intelligence exchange about Mongol movements and logistical support for military campaigns.^3

More surprisingly, Baibars even corresponded with European kings. With **Alfonso X of Castile**, he discussed the possibility of alliance against the Mongols threatening Eastern Europe. With **James I of Aragon**, he negotiated about limiting European aid to Crusaders in the Levant. These diplomatic letters show that Baibars understood geopolitics on a global scale.^1

In the internal Islamic context, Baibars sought to build religious legitimacy by supporting the restoration of the Abbasid Caliphate in Cairo. After Baghdad fell and al-Musta'sim was killed in 1258 CE, Baibars invited **al-Mustansir**, a relative of the Abbasid caliph, to become symbolic caliph in Egypt. This provided religious legitimacy to Mamluk rule and strengthened Baibars' position as defender of Islam.^3

## War Against the Mongols: Elbistan 1277 CE

Although Baibars' main focus was expelling Crusaders, the Mongol threat never disappeared. **Abaga Khan**, Hulagu's successor as ruler of the Persian Ilkhanate, continued planning counterattacks to avenge the defeat at Ain Jalut. In 1277 CE, this long-postponed conflict finally erupted.^4

In the **Battle of Elbistan (April 11, 1277 CE)**, Baibars faced Mongol forces directly for the first time as sultan. The Mongol army led by **Nogudar** and **Tukhta Timur** attacked from Anatolia with about 30,000 soldiers — a mix of Mongol core, Turkish auxiliaries, and Georgian Christian volunteers.^4

Baibars used the same tactics that proved effective against Crusaders: superior intelligence, high mobility, and coordinated attacks. Mamluk forces moved with surprising speed, intercepting Mongol forces before they could join reinforcements from Anatolia.^4

The victory at Elbistan was symbolically very important. It proved that the Ain Jalut victory was not luck, but the superiority of the Mamluk military system over the Mongols. News of this victory spread throughout the Islamic world, strengthening Baibars' position as champion of Islam against invaders.^4

## Military Reform: Modernization of Furusiyya

One of Baibars' greatest contributions to Islamic civilization was modernizing the Mamluk military system, especially in the art of *furusiyya* (horsemanship). He not only improved individual soldier training, but also developed comprehensive military doctrine.^5

**Hippodromes** (horse training grounds) were built in Cairo, Damascus, and Aleppo with the most complete facilities. Every prospective Mamluk had to master 70 basic horse movements, 30 archery techniques from horseback, and 50 group combat formations. This standardization made Mamluk forces the most disciplined and effective in the world at that time.^5

Baibars also imported military instructors from various countries. From captured Mongols, he learned cavalry charge tactics and more effective stirrup use. From Byzantium, he adopted Greek fire technology modified for sieges. From Arab and Persian engineers, he developed more sophisticated siege engines.^4

Most revolutionary was the development of **written military manuals** that became mandatory curriculum for all Mamluk officers. These manuals covered strategy, tactics, logistics, even diplomacy and intelligence. For the first time in Islamic history, military knowledge was systematically codified and formally taught.^5

## Infrastructure Development: Foundation for Power

Baibars' military success was supported by a very ambitious infrastructure development program. He understood that military strength had to be underpinned by a strong economy and efficient communication system.^6

**Trade routes** from India to Europe through Egypt were revitalized by building caravanserais, improving Alexandria and Damietta ports, and standardizing currency and measurement systems. Income from spice and silk trade provided sufficient revenue to finance military modernization.^6

**The fortress system** along the eastern border was strengthened with the latest technology. Old fortresses were renovated with designs that adopted lessons from the siege of Krak des Chevaliers. Thicker walls, taller towers, and safer water supply systems became the new standard.^6

**Madrasas and libraries** were built as part of the soft power campaign. Baibars understood that military victory had to be followed by intellectual victory. Scholars from Baghdad and Cordoba who were refugees due to Mongol invasion and Reconquista were invited to Damascus and Cairo, creating an intellectual renaissance in Mamluk territories.^3

## Mysterious Death: Poison or Disease?

On **July 1, 1277 CE (19 Muharram 676 AH)**, Baibars died suddenly in Damascus under very suspicious circumstances. Age 54 was still considered young for a sultan, and his health previously seemed fine. This sudden death gave rise to speculation that persists to this day.^2

The official version recorded by Ibn Abd al-Dhahir mentioned that Baibars died from high fever that struck suddenly. However, other sources, including al-Maqrizi and Ibn Taghribirdi, mentioned the possibility of poisoning. Some pointed to **Baibars' Jewish personal physician** as the perpetrator, while others suspected involvement of amirs dissatisfied with the sultan's policies.^2

What added to suspicion was the timing of death that was very disadvantageous to the Mamluks. Baibars was preparing a major campaign to expel the remaining Crusaders from Acre and Tripoli — a mission that would complete his project of expelling Crusaders from the Levant. His death allowed Crusaders to survive 14 years longer until the fall of Acre in 1291 CE.^1

Baibars' tomb in Damascus became a popular pilgrimage site, showing how great the people's respect for him was. Despite the controversial way he rose to power, his achievements as defender of Islam were undeniable.^2

## Legacy and Long-term Impact

**Sultan Baibars** left a very great legacy for the Islamic world and global history as a whole:

**1. Expelling Crusaders**: Baibars' systematic campaigns destroyed 36 Crusader fortresses and cities, reducing their territory to just a few coastal areas. Without Baibars, Crusaders might have lasted longer and received reinforcements from Europe.^1

**2. Stopping Mongol expansion**: Victory at Elbistan proved that Mongols could be defeated consistently, not just momentary luck. This stopped Mongol ambitions to control Egypt and North Africa.^4

**3. Military modernization**: The *furusiyya* system and military doctrine developed by Baibars became the foundation of Mamluk strength for the next 250 years. Many techniques he used were adopted by the Ottoman Sultanate.^5

**4. Global diplomacy**: Baibars proved that Muslim leaders could play sophisticated global politics, not just rely on military strength. His diplomatic approach became a model for subsequent Muslim rulers.^3

## Analysis and Wisdom

Sultan Baibars taught several eternal principles in leadership and strategy:

**Systematic preparation**: Every Baibars campaign began with deep research about enemies, battlefields, and political conditions. He never fought without complete information.^2

**Strategic adaptability**: Baibars was not stuck on one tactic, but adapted his approach to the situation. Against Mongols he used mobility, against Crusaders he focused on sieges, against internal opposition he used a combination of firmness and diplomacy.^1

**Long-term investment**: Military reforms, infrastructure development, and intelligence system development that Baibars undertook took years to show results, but provided a solid foundation for long-term success.^6

**Balance between strength and mercy**: Baibars could be very cruel to enemies who resisted, but generous to those who surrendered. This was not only moral, but also strategy to reduce future resistance.^6

This verse reflects Baibars' philosophy in facing seemingly impossible challenges. Despite facing enemies from various directions and difficult political situations, he remained patient and consistent in his long-term strategy. Sultan Baibars proved that with a combination of courage, intelligence, patience, and piety, a leader can transform a seemingly hopeless situation into history-changing victory. His legacy was not only in the form of military victories, but also in systems and institutions that continued to function even after his death.^1 ^7
