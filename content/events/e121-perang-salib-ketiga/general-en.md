# The Third Crusade - Richard vs Saladin (1189-1192 CE)

## Post-Hattin Context: Shock in Christian Europe

The Third Crusade was born from the tremendous shock that struck the Christian world after the **Battle of Hattin** on July 4, 1187 CE. In this decisive battle, **Saladin al-Ayyubi** completely annihilated the largest Crusader army ever assembled in the Holy Land. King Guy de Lusignan was captured, the True Cross — Christianity's most sacred relic — fell into Muslim hands, and within three months, nearly the entire Kingdom of Jerusalem collapsed like dominoes.^1

Ibn al-Athir described how city after city fell without meaningful resistance: Tiberias, Nazareth, Caesarea, Nablus, Jaffa, and finally on October 2, 1187 CE, Jerusalem itself surrendered after a brief siege. From the entire Latin Kingdom, only three cities remained: Tyre, Tripoli, and Antioch — and even these were isolated without land connections.^2

News of this catastrophe reached Europe in late 1187 CE, triggering what Ibn al-Athir called *"wailing that thundered from the western end of the world to its eastern end."*^1 Pope Urban III reportedly died from shock upon hearing news of Jerusalem's fall. His successor, Pope Gregory VIII, immediately issued the bull *Audita Tremendi* calling for a new crusade with unprecedented urgency.^2

## Mobilization of Three Kings: Ambition and Competition

Unlike previous crusades led by mid-level nobles, this time three of Europe's most powerful kings descended directly to the field: **Frederick Barbarossa** (66 years old) from the Holy Roman Empire, **Philip Augustus** (24 years old) from France, and **Richard I Lionheart** (31 years old) from England. The direct involvement of these kings showed how seriously Christian Europe felt the threat.^2 ^3

Frederick Barbarossa, veteran of the Second Crusade, was viewed as the natural leader of the coalition due to his age and experience. He managed to assemble the largest army — estimated at 100,000 men — and chose the overland route through Anatolia as it was more economical and allowed bringing full cavalry strength.^1 But fate decreed otherwise: on June 10, 1190 CE, the 67-year-old emperor drowned in the Saleph River (now Göksu) in Lesser Armenia. Whether from a heart attack while crossing or swept away by currents — contemporary sources give different versions.^1

Frederick's death caused extraordinary psychological devastation to his troops. His son, Frederick of Swabia, tried to maintain leadership, but without his father's charisma, most German soldiers returned to Europe. Only about 5,000 continued the journey to the Holy Land — an insignificant force compared to the original plan.^1

## Richard-Philip Rivalry: A Fractured Friendship

The relationship between Richard Lionheart and Philip Augustus reflected the complexity of 12th-century feudal politics. Technically, Richard was Philip's vassal for his French territories (Normandy, Anjou, Aquitaine), but as King of England, he was equal in status. Their youthful friendship turned into rivalry when dynastic interests clashed.^3

The first crisis emerged when they stopped in Sicily during the winter of 1190-1191 CE. Richard refused to marry **Alais of Vexin**, Philip's sister, claiming she had become his father Henry II's concubine. Instead, Richard married **Berengaria of Navarre**, which strategically gave England a new ally against France. Philip felt publicly humiliated — an insult that would have long-term consequences.^3

Tensions escalated further when Richard conquered Cyprus on the journey to the Holy Land. This unilateral action was taken without consulting Philip, and the wealth Richard gained from Cyprus made him financially independent — even richer than Philip himself. Ibn al-Athir noted that from this point, the two kings *"walked together but dreamed differently."*^1

## Siege of Acre: Test of Endurance

When Richard and Philip arrived in the Holy Land in summer 1191 CE, they found a situation both encouraging and worrying. Crusader forces under Guy de Lusignan (who had been freed by Saladin after promising not to fight again — a promise he broke) had been besieging **Acre** since August 1189 CE. However, this siege had turned into a deadly war of attrition.^2

Acre, a strategic port city controlling eastern Mediterranean trade routes, was defended by an elite garrison under **Baha al-Din Qaraqush**, one of Saladin's best commanders. Meanwhile, Saladin himself with the main army was "besieging the besiegers" — creating a unique double situation in military history.^2

William of Tyre described the horrific conditions during the siege: famine, plague, and continuously declining morale. An estimated 100,000 Crusaders died during the two-year siege, mainly from disease. This situation changed dramatically with the arrival of Richard and Philip with a large fleet bringing siege engines (*mangonels* and *trebuchets*) and fresh supplies.^2

The arrival of both kings also brought the latest military technology. Richard introduced *"God's Own Catapult"* — a giant trebuchet capable of hurling 300-kg stones. Philip not to be outdone with *"Evil Neighbor"* — his own siege engine. This competition, though seemingly childish, proved effective in destroying Acre's fortifications.^3

On July 12, 1191 CE, after intensive bombardment for a month, **Acre finally surrendered**. Saladin, who tried to help from outside, could not penetrate the reinforced Crusader lines. Qaraqush's garrison surrendered on condition their lives would be spared and Saladin would pay a ransom of 200,000 gold dinars plus free 1,600 Crusader prisoners.^2

## Massacre of Prisoners: A Stain in History

What happened next became one of the most controversial episodes in Crusade history. When Saladin was late paying the first installment of ransom on schedule, Richard — in a decision that surprised even his own allies — ordered the mass execution of 2,700 Muslim prisoners in front of Acre's walls on August 20, 1191 CE.^1 ^3

Ibn al-Athir wrote with fury: *"The Crusader forces brought the prisoners to a hill facing Saladin's camp, then killed them all before the Sultan's eyes. This was a heartbreaking sight — even some Christians wept witnessing it."*^1

Richard's motivation is still debated by historians. Some cite impatience with payment delays, others point to logistical pressure — feeding 2,700 prisoners when supplies were limited. But most likely this was psychological calculation to break Saladin's spirit and show Crusader resolve that could not be shaken.^3

This tactic backfired. Saladin, previously known for his chivalrous treatment of prisoners, began applying similar retaliation. The war that had been relatively "civilized" became more brutal.^1

## Battle of Arsuf: Richard's Tactical Masterpiece

After Acre, Richard and Philip began showing fundamental differences in vision. Philip, feeling his duty completed with Acre's fall, announced his return to France in August 1191 CE claiming illness. This decision caused great controversy — many accused him of abandoning the Holy Land out of jealousy over Richard's achievements at Acre.^3

Imad al-Din al-Isfahani, Saladin's secretary, recorded the Sultan's reaction: *"The French King's departure is Allah's blessing for us. Now we face only one enemy, not two."*^1 However, this assessment proved premature, as Richard as sole leader actually became more dangerous.

With remaining forces (about 50,000 men), Richard began movement south toward Jaffa and eventually Jerusalem. He chose the coastal route that allowed fleet support, while Saladin followed parallel inland, continuously conducting guerrilla attacks to wear down Crusader stamina.^2

The climactic confrontation occurred on **September 7, 1191 CE at Arsuf**. Saladin, with 30,000 troops, decided to give frontal battle to stop Richard's advance. He placed Nubian archers on the flanks, Arab light cavalry in the center, and elite *Mamluk* troops as reserves.^1

Richard responded with a revolutionary formation that showed his tactical genius. He divided his forces into 12 divisions in an elongated formation: infantry with spears and shields outside, crossbowmen in the middle, and heavy cavalry inside as the final blow. Uniquely, each infantry unit had a dual mission: protect themselves and protect cavalry horses from Saracen arrows.^2

Battle began with Saladin's arrow rain trying to provoke Crusader cavalry into premature attack. For hours, Richard restrained himself though his forces suffered heavy casualties. Only when Saladin's cavalry began tiring from chasing the seemingly impenetrable Crusader formation did Richard give the signal: war horns sounded and Crusader heavy cavalry attacked in a devastating *"charge."*^3

Ibn al-Athir admitted: *"We had never witnessed such a terrible Frankish cavalry charge. Like lightning striking, they tore through our lines and in an instant the formation the Sultan had prepared was shattered."*^1 Saladin's forces retreated in chaos, leaving 700 casualties including several senior *emirs*.

## Character of Two Leaders: Lion vs Eagle

What made this conflict historically fascinating was the character contrast between two leaders who respected each other despite being enemies.

**Richard Lionheart** was known for his borderline reckless bravery. In every battle, he led from the front in gleaming mail armor and a helmet crowned with white ostrich feathers — easily recognizable to friend and foe alike. Imad al-Din wrote: *"The King of England is a devil in battle, yet a knight in his treatment of noble prisoners."*^1

Richard's impulsive nature was visible in the Acre incident, but also in his respectful attitude toward brave enemies. When Saladin's horse died in a small battle near Jaffa, Richard sent two of his best Arab horses as replacements with the message: *"A king should not walk on foot."*^3

**Saladin al-Ayyubi**, conversely, was a master of long-term strategy and diplomacy. His 53 years made him wiser than the 32-year-old Richard. He preferred avoiding frontal battles and relying on guerrilla tactics, economic blockade, and diplomacy to break enemy coalitions.^1

Most impressive about Saladin was his ability to unite the previously fragmented Islamic world. He successfully integrated Kurdish, Arab, Turkmen, and Egyptian Mamluk armies under one command — an achievement even previous Muslim leaders found difficult. Ibn al-Athir noted: *"The Sultan not only conquered the Crusaders, but also conquered the hearts of Muslims who had been divided."*^1

## Negotiations: Diplomacy Amid War

What was unique about the Third Crusade was the intensity of diplomacy amid military conflict. Both leaders, though fighting each other, continued communicating through envoys and even met directly on several occasions. This reflected 12th-century chivalric culture that still valued chivalry even toward enemies.

The most surprising proposal came from Richard in spring 1192 CE: he proposed **marriage between his sister, Joan of Sicily, and al-Adil** (Saladin's brother) as a peaceful solution. This couple would rule Jerusalem together, with Joan remaining Christian and al-Adil remaining Muslim.^3

This proposal showed Richard's extraordinary pragmatism, ready to set aside religious fanaticism for political solution. However, this proposal was firmly rejected by Crusader bishops who considered it betrayal of faith, while several Muslim *ulama* also objected to sharing power over Jerusalem.^3

Other negotiations revolved around pilgrimage access. Saladin, in a surprising gesture, offered to open Jerusalem to Christian pilgrims without military conditions — merely with simple tax payment. But Richard insisted that Crusaders must have political control of the city, not just religious access.^2

## Failure at Jerusalem: Controversial Decision

In January 1192 CE, Richard reached **Beit Nuba**, only 20 kilometers from Jerusalem. From this hill, Crusader soldiers could see the dome of Al-Aqsa Mosque and the Dome of the Rock gleaming in sunlight. Many wept with emotion seeing the Holy City that had been their dream for years.^2

However, in a controversial and surprising decision, Richard decided **not to conduct a siege**. This decision was based on several strategic calculations:^2

1. **Logistical limitations**: Besieging Jerusalem required months, while Richard's supplies were limited and supply lines from the coast could be cut by Saladin.

2. **External threats**: Saladin's forces remained intact and could attack from behind during the siege.

3. **Political problems**: Even if Jerusalem were captured, defending it would require a large permanent garrison — while most Crusaders planned to return home after pilgrimage obligations were fulfilled.

4. **Domestic pressure**: News from Europe reported that Philip Augustus and John Lackland (Richard's brother) were conspiring to take over Angevin territories during Richard's absence.

Al-Isfahani recorded the confused reaction of Saladin's forces: *"We had prepared the best defense for the Holy City, yet the enemy who was already at the gates turned back. This was Allah's intervention protecting Jerusalem."*^1

## Treaty of Jaffa: Pragmatic Solution

After several months of military maneuvering without decisive results, both sides realized that prolonged war would only drain resources without final victory. On **September 2, 1192 CE**, Richard and Saladin signed the **Treaty of Jaffa** that ended the Third Crusade.^3

This treaty demonstrated the extraordinary diplomatic abilities of both leaders. Its main clauses were:^3

1. **Jerusalem remained under Saladin's control**, but opened to Christian pilgrims without discrimination.

2. **Coastal territory from Tyre to Jaffa** remained under Crusader control, ensuring sea access to Europe.

3. **Ascalon was destroyed** and could not be rebuilt for three years — a compromise to prevent a military base threatening both sides.

4. **Free trade** between Christian and Muslim territories, restoring the region's economic prosperity.

5. **Truce for three years, three months, three weeks, and three days** — formulation reflecting the symbolic nature of medieval negotiations.

Most touchingly, in personal clauses, Saladin specifically invited Richard to visit Jerusalem as his personal guest. But Richard diplomatically declined: *"I will not enter a city I cannot defend for my people."*^3

## Analysis and Wisdom

The Third Crusade demonstrated that **united leadership** is more effective than divided military strength.^3 Saladin succeeded in defending Jerusalem not only because of his military capabilities, but because he managed to unite various Arab dynasties and tribes under one vision.^3

This conflict also proved the importance of **diplomacy** in resolving disputes.^3 Both sides realized that prolonged war would only exhaust resources without satisfactory results.^3 Ultimately, a middle path through negotiation proved more wise.^3

From a tactical perspective, the Third Crusade marked evolution in medieval warfare arts. Richard introduced coordination between infantry, archers, and cavalry that became a model for European armies for hundreds of years. Meanwhile, Saladin perfected guerrilla tactics and psychological warfare later adopted by many Muslim leaders.

Most importantly, this conflict showed that even amid fundamental religious differences, mutual respect and chivalry were still possible. The Richard-Saladin relationship became a legend inspiring writers and historians for centuries, proving that humanity could transcend political and religious barriers.

## The Exhausting Siege of Acre

After arriving in the Holy Land, the Crusader forces joined the siege of Acre that had been ongoing since 1189 CE.^2 This strategic port city was controlled by Saladin, while Crusader forces besieged it from land and sea.^2

During this siege, relations between Richard and Philip deteriorated further.^3 Philip Augustus finally decided to return to France in July 1191 CE after Acre fell, leaving Richard alone to face Saladin.^1 Philip's decision surprised many and weakened the Crusader position.^1

## Strategic Duel: Richard vs Saladin

What remained was a battle between two charismatic leaders: Richard Lionheart and Saladin al-Ayyubi.^3 Richard was known for his courage in battle and aggressive military tactics.^3 Meanwhile, Saladin excelled in diplomacy and his ability to unite various Muslim factions.^3

Richard succeeded in capturing several coastal cities like Jaffa and Arsuf, but each of his advances was countered by Saladin's guerrilla tactics that avoided frontal battles.^1 ^3 The Ayyubid Sultan preferred attacking Crusader supply lines and conducting surprise raids.^3

## Negotiations and Jerusalem's Failure

Interestingly, amid the warfare, these two leaders respected each other and negotiated repeatedly.^3 Richard even once proposed a marriage between his sister and Saladin's brother to resolve the conflict diplomatically, but it failed due to religious differences.^3

Richard was only 20 kilometers from Jerusalem in early 1192 CE, but decided not to conduct a siege.^2 This controversial decision was based on realistic calculations: although Jerusalem could be captured, it would be difficult to defend without full support from European allies who were diminishing.^2

## The Treaty of Jaffa and Conclusion

In September 1192 CE, Richard and Saladin signed the **Treaty of Jaffa** that ended the Third Crusade.^3 This treaty gave Saladin control over Jerusalem but provided pilgrimage access for Christians.^3 The coastal region from Tyre to Jaffa remained under Crusader control.^3

Richard departed for Europe in October 1192 CE, leaving the Holy Land without successfully recapturing Jerusalem.^1 Nevertheless, his achievement in capturing coastal cities ensured the survival of Crusader kingdoms until the 13th century.^1 ^3
