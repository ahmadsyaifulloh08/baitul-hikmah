# Construction of Al-Aqsa Mosque - Baitul Maqdis (~705 CE)

## Historical Context of Baitul Maqdis

Baitul Maqdis (*Bait al-Maqdis*) or Jerusalem had been a holy city for the three Abrahamic religions for thousands of years before Islam.^2 When **Caliph Umar ibn al-Khattab** conquered this city in 638 CE, he guaranteed protection for existing places of worship and showed extraordinary tolerance.^1

However, the physical condition of the Haram al-Sharif (Noble Sanctuary) area at that time still required better organization.^3 **Caliph Abd al-Malik ibn Marwan** (685-705 CE) began an ambitious project to build the Dome of the Rock (*Qubbat al-Sakhrah*) in 691 CE, which became the foundation for the development of a larger mosque complex.^2

## Caliph al-Walid's Architectural Vision

**Caliph al-Walid ibn Abd al-Malik** (705-715 CE) continued his father's vision to make Baitul Maqdis a magnificent spiritual center.^4 Around 705 CE, he ordered the construction of a larger and more comprehensive mosque structure in the southern part of the Haram al-Sharif complex, which would later be known as **Al-Aqsa Mosque**.^3

This project was not merely physical construction, but also a manifestation of the Umayyad dynasty's commitment to developing magnificent Islamic architecture.^1 Al-Walid, who also built the Umayyad Mosque in Damascus simultaneously, showed extraordinary dedication to building grand mosques throughout the caliphate.^4

## Construction Process and Challenges

The construction of Al-Aqsa Mosque faced various technical and logistical challenges.^2 The construction site was located above ancient foundations that required special reinforcement. Umayyad architects and engineers had to integrate the new structure with the unique topography of the Noble Sanctuary.^5

The construction team consisted of the best architects and builders from various regions of the caliphate, including experts from Syria (*Sham*), Egypt, and Persia.^3 Building materials were brought from various regions: marble from Anatolia, cedar wood from Lebanon, and gold ornaments from mines in the caliphate territories.^6

Construction proceeded in several phases.^4 The first phase included foundation strengthening and construction of the main structure. The second phase involved installing marble columns and dome construction.^1 The final phase was completing interior and exterior ornaments with Arabic calligraphy and geometric motifs typical of Islam.^7

## Architectural Uniqueness

The Al-Aqsa Mosque built during al-Walid's time had unique architectural characteristics, combining Byzantine, Sassanian traditions, and Islamic innovations.^2 The mosque's initial structure had seven *saff* (rows) for congregational prayer, with a *mihrab* facing the direction of the Ka'bah in Mecca.^8

The mosque's acoustic system was carefully designed so that the imam's voice could be heard clearly by congregants throughout the room.^3 The tall marble columns not only served structural functions but also created beautiful visual rhythm. The wooden ceiling with intricate carvings showed the level of Islamic artistic skill during the Umayyad period.^1

## Spiritual and Political Significance

The construction of Al-Aqsa Mosque had deep spiritual meaning for Muslims.^4 This mosque stands at the location mentioned in the Quran as the destination of Prophet Muhammad's ﷺ Night Journey (*Isra'*):

> سُبْحَانَ الَّذِي أَسْرَىٰ بِعَبْدِهِ لَيْلًا مِّنَ الْمَسْجِدِ الْحَرَامِ إِلَى الْمَسْجِدِ الْأَقْصَى الَّذِي بَارَكْنَا حَوْلَهُ ﴿١﴾
>
> *"Exalted is He who took His Servant by night from al-Masjid al-Haram to al-Masjid al-Aqsa, whose surroundings We have blessed."* ﴾1﴿
> — QS. Al-Isra' (17): 1

Politically, the construction of this mosque affirmed the legitimacy of the Umayyad dynasty as successors to Islamic leadership and protectors of holy places.^1 The Haram al-Sharif complex became a symbol of the power and magnificence of the Islamic caliphate at its peak.^2 ^8

## Legacy and Continuity

The Al-Aqsa Mosque built during Caliph al-Walid's time became the foundation for various subsequent developments.^2 Although it underwent various renovations and reconstructions throughout history, the architectural principles and spatial arrangements established during the Umayyad period remain a reference to this day.^3

The Islamic scholarly tradition also developed around this complex.^3 Scholars and *fuqaha* used the mosque area to teach and discuss various religious matters. This made Al-Aqsa Mosque not only a place of worship, but also a center for the development of Islamic knowledge.^4

## Wisdom and Reflection

The construction of Al-Aqsa Mosque teaches us about the importance of preserving and developing the spiritual heritage of Muslims.^4 This project shows how visionary leadership can create monumental works that provide inspiration for future generations.

**Values that can be learned:**
- **Commitment to the glory of Islam** through magnificent architectural works
- **Religious tolerance** by respecting the sanctity of places for other religions
- **Cultural integration** in creating a unique Islamic architectural identity
- **Heritage sustainability** for future generations
