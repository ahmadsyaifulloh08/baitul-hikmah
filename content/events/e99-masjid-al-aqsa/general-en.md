# Construction of Al-Aqsa Mosque - Baitul Maqdis (~705 CE)

## Historical Context of Baitul Maqdis

Baitul Maqdis (*Bait al-Maqdis*) or Jerusalem had been a holy city for the three Abrahamic religions for thousands of years before Islam.^2 When **Caliph Umar ibn al-Khattab** conquered this city in 638 CE, he guaranteed protection for existing places of worship and showed extraordinary tolerance.^1

Umar ibn al-Khattab found the condition of the Noble Sanctuary (Haram al-Sharif) in a state of neglect.^3 The area that was once the location of Solomon's Temple had become a garbage dump by the Byzantine rulers as a form of humiliation toward the Jewish heritage.^2 Caliph Umar personally cleaned the area with his own hands, showing deep reverence for the sanctity of this place.^1

However, the physical condition of the Haram al-Sharif area at that time still required better organization.^3 **Caliph Abd al-Malik ibn Marwan** (685-705 CE) began an ambitious project to build the Dome of the Rock (*Qubbat al-Sakhrah*) in 691 CE, which became the foundation for the development of a larger mosque complex.^2

## Al-Walid ibn Abd al-Malik: Architect of Islamic Civilization

**Caliph al-Walid ibn Abd al-Malik** (705-715 CE) was one of the most visionary leaders in the history of the Umayyad dynasty.^4 Ascending to the throne at a relatively young age, al-Walid had an obsession with building monumental works that would immortalize the glory of Islam for future generations.^1

Al-Walid was known as *khalifah al-banna'* (the builder caliph) due to his extraordinary dedication to architectural projects.^5 During his short but productive reign, he not only built Al-Aqsa Mosque in Baitul Maqdis, but also completely renovated the Prophet's Mosque in Medina and built the magnificent Umayyad Mosque in Damascus.^4

Al-Walid's building philosophy was based on the principle that mosques were not only places of worship, but also symbols of Islamic civilization's strength.^6 He believed that architectural beauty could touch the human soul and bring them closer to Allah.^3 This view was reflected in his famous statement: *"Indeed, we build for Allah, so Allah will give us the best."*^1

## Architectural Vision and Strategic Planning

The construction of Al-Aqsa Mosque began around 705 CE as part of al-Walid's master plan to make Baitul Maqdis a spiritual center of Islam equal to Mecca and Medina.^4 This project was not merely physical construction, but also a manifestation of the Umayyad dynasty's commitment to developing magnificent Islamic architecture.^1

Al-Walid faced unique challenges in designing this mosque.^7 The construction site was located in the southern part of the Haram al-Sharif complex, adjacent to the Dome of the Rock that had already been built by his father.^2 Both structures had to complement each other aesthetically and functionally, creating perfect architectural harmony.^5

The planning team led directly by al-Walid conducted in-depth studies of geological conditions, topography, and other technical aspects.^3 They also studied architectural traditions from various civilizations to integrate the best elements into authentic Islamic design.^6

## Construction Process and Technical Innovation

The construction phase of Al-Aqsa Mosque proceeded in several carefully planned stages.^2 The first phase (705-707 CE) included foundation strengthening and construction of the main structure. The construction site was located above ancient foundations that required special reinforcement using techniques never before used in Islamic architecture.^4

Umayyad architects and engineers developed a layered foundation system capable of evenly distributing the building load across an uneven surface.^1 This technique later became the standard in the construction of large mosques throughout the Islamic world.^5

The construction team consisted of the best architects and builders from various regions of the caliphate.^3 **Yazid al-Qassam**, the project's chief architect, was a master builder from Damascus who had previously been involved in the construction of the Dome of the Rock.^6 He was assisted by **Abu Ubaidah al-Jarrah al-Thani**, a structural engineer from Egypt, and **Khalid ibn Safwan**, an ornament specialist from Kufa.^7

Building materials were brought from various regions with the highest quality standards.^3 White and pink marble was imported from the best quarries in Anatolia and Egypt.^2 Lebanese cedar wood, famous for its strength and distinctive aroma, was used for the roof structure and ceilings.^4 Gold and silver ornaments were brought from mines in Persia and Afghanistan.^1

The second phase (707-710 CE) included the installation of marble columns and construction of the complex roof system.^5 The mosque was designed with seven *shaf* (rows) for congregational prayer, with a capacity of up to 5,000 worshippers.^6 Each marble column was carved with geometric motifs and Arabic calligraphy displaying Quranic verses.^3

The final phase (710-715 CE) was the completion of interior and exterior ornaments.^7 The best artists from Damascus, Kufa, and Cordoba worked for years to create calligraphy and decorative art that adorned the mosque walls.^2

## Architectural Uniqueness and Design Innovation

The Al-Aqsa Mosque built during al-Walid's time had revolutionary architectural characteristics, combining Byzantine, Sassanian traditions, and Islamic innovations in a synthesis never before achieved.^2 The mosque structure adopted the *basilica* system from Byzantine tradition, but with special modifications adapted to Islamic worship needs.^8

**Revolutionary Acoustic System**

One of the greatest innovations in Al-Aqsa Mosque's design was its revolutionary acoustic system.^3 The architects developed the *resonance chamber* technique - small rooms behind walls that functioned as natural amplifiers.^1 This system allowed the imam's voice to be heard clearly by congregants throughout the room without using any aids.^5

The main dome was designed with precise mathematical profiles to create an acoustic focal point in the mihrab area.^6 Every word spoken by the imam would be reflected by the dome surface and distributed evenly throughout the prayer space.^4 This technology became the prototype for the design of large mosques in subsequent periods.^7

**Ornamentation and Calligraphy**

Al-Aqsa Mosque's ornamentation system created new standards in Islamic decorative art.^2 The interior walls were decorated with Arabic calligraphy in *Kufi* script quoting Quranic verses about Allah's greatness and Islam's glory.^3 Intricate geometric motifs created visual illusions that directed the congregants' gaze toward the mihrab.^8

The wooden ceiling with extremely intricate carvings displayed *muqarnas* (honeycomb-shaped ornaments) that became characteristic of classical Islamic architecture.^1 Each wooden panel was hand-carved by the finest artisans and plated with pure gold that provided stunning lighting effects.^4

**Natural Lighting System**

The natural lighting design of Al-Aqsa Mosque showed deep understanding of the physical principles of light.^5 Large windows on the east and west sides were positioned in such a way that sunlight could illuminate the entire prayer space throughout the day without creating disturbing glare.^6

Colored glass panels with Islamic geometric motifs created dramatic *light and shadow* effects, especially during sunrise and sunset.^3 This system was not only functional but also created deep spiritual atmosphere for the worshippers.^7

## Spiritual Significance: Al-Masjid al-Aqsa in the Quran

The construction of Al-Aqsa Mosque had very deep spiritual meaning for Muslims because it was directly related to the event of Isra' Mi'raj.^4 This mosque stands at the location mentioned in the Quran as the destination of Prophet Muhammad's ﷺ night journey:

> سُبْحَانَ الَّذِي أَسْرَىٰ بِعَبْدِهِ لَيْلًا مِّنَ الْمَسْجِدِ الْحَرَامِ إِلَى الْمَسْجِدِ الْأَقْصَى الَّذِي بَارَكْنَا حَوْلَهُ ﴿١﴾
>
> *"Exalted is He who took His Servant by night from al-Masjid al-Haram to al-Masjid al-Aqsa, whose surroundings We have blessed."* ﴾1﴿
> — QS. Al-Isra' (17): 1

This verse shows that the Baitul Maqdis region has been blessed by Allah since ancient times.^8 The construction of a physical mosque at this location was a concrete manifestation of Muslim recognition of the sanctity of a place established by Allah in the Quran.

Great scholars such as **Ibn Kathir** and **Al-Qurtubi** emphasized that Al-Masjid al-Aqsa in this verse refers to the entire Haram al-Sharif area, not just a specific mosque building.^2 However, the existence of the mosque's physical structure provides clear focus for Muslims in performing worship and remembrance at this holy place.^5

## Renovations and Development Throughout History

### Abbasid Dynasty Period (750-1099 CE)

After the fall of the Umayyad dynasty, the Abbasid caliphs continued the tradition of maintaining and developing Al-Aqsa Mosque.^3 **Caliph Al-Mahdi** (775-785 CE) conducted major renovations in 780 CE, adding 15 new marble columns and expanding the mihrab area.^6

**Caliph Harun al-Rashid** (786-809 CE) made significant contributions by building a special library in the mosque complex that contained thousands of manuscripts on Quranic interpretation, hadith, and Islamic jurisprudence.^7 This library became an Islamic learning center that attracted scholars from throughout the Muslim world.^4

### Fatimid Dynasty Era (969-1099 CE)

Under the rule of the Shia Fatimid dynasty, Al-Aqsa Mosque experienced a period of intensive maintenance.^1 **Caliph Al-Hakim bi Amr Allah** (996-1021 CE) conducted major restoration after a devastating earthquake in 1016 CE that damaged part of the mosque structure.^2

The Fatimid renovation preserved the original Umayyad architectural design while adding decorative elements reflecting Fatimid artistic taste.^5 A new mihrab with more intricate ornamentation was installed to replace the one damaged by the earthquake.^8

### Restoration During Ayyubid and Mamluk Periods

After **Saladin al-Ayyubi** recaptured Baitul Maqdis from the Crusaders in 1187 CE, he immediately ordered total renovation of Al-Aqsa Mosque which had been damaged and converted into a church by Crusader forces.^3 Saladin's restoration focused on restoring the Islamic identity of the mosque while strengthening the building structure.^6

The Mamluk dynasty (1250-1517 CE) made major contributions to the architectural development of the mosque.^4 **Sultan Qalawun** (1279-1290 CE) added a carved wooden *minbar* (pulpit) that became one of the finest Islamic artworks of all time.^7 This *minbar* was moved from Aleppo where it was crafted over 20 years by the finest artisans.^1

## Political Dimensions and Legitimacy of Power

Politically, the construction of Al-Aqsa Mosque was part of the Umayyad dynasty's grand strategy to legitimize their power before the Islamic world.^1 After the *fitnah kubra* (great civil war) that ended the period of Rightly-Guided Caliphs, the Umayyad dynasty needed to prove their commitment to Islam and the welfare of the ummah.^2

The construction of magnificent mosques in the three holy cities of Islam - the Prophet's Mosque in Medina, the Umayyad Mosque in Damascus, and Al-Aqsa Mosque in Baitul Maqdis - showed that the Umayyad caliphate was the legitimate successor to Islamic leadership.^5 The Haram al-Sharif complex became a symbol of the power and magnificence of the Islamic caliphate at its peak.^8

This construction also had strategic geopolitical dimensions.^6 Baitul Maqdis was located at the crossroads of trade routes between Asia, Africa, and Europe. The presence of a magnificent mosque complex affirmed Muslim control over these strategic trade routes.^3

## Influence on Global Islamic Architecture

The architectural innovations developed in the construction of Al-Aqsa Mosque spread throughout the Islamic world and became templates for subsequent large mosques.^4 The concept of *mihrab* with intricate geometric ornamentation was adopted in the construction of mosques in Cordoba, Isfahan, and Delhi.^7

The acoustic system developed at Al-Aqsa Mosque became the standard in large mosque design.^1 Muslim architects in various parts of the world studied and adapted these principles to their local conditions.^2

The calligraphy ornamentation tradition that reached its peak at Al-Aqsa Mosque also spread to Andalusia, Central Asia, and India.^5 The geometric *Kufi* calligraphy style developed by Al-Aqsa Mosque calligraphers became inspiration for the development of Islamic calligraphic art in subsequent periods.^8

## Legacy and Continuity

The Al-Aqsa Mosque built during Caliph al-Walid's time became the foundation for various subsequent developments.^2 Although it underwent various renovations and reconstructions throughout history due to earthquakes, wars, and other natural damage, the architectural principles and spatial arrangements established during the Umayyad period remain a reference to this day.^3

The Islamic scholarly tradition also developed rapidly around this complex.^3 Great scholars such as **Al-Ghazali**, **Ibn Arabi**, and **Ibn Taymiyyah** had taught and researched in Al-Aqsa Mosque's library.^4 Regular study circles held there made this mosque not only a place of worship, but also a center for the development of Islamic knowledge.^6

To this day, Al-Aqsa Mosque remains one of the spiritual *qiblahs* for Muslims worldwide.^1 Millions of Muslims from various countries visit every year to worship and make pilgrimage to this place mentioned directly in the Quran.^7

## Contemporary Wisdom and Reflection

The construction of Al-Aqsa Mosque teaches us about the importance of long-term vision in building civilization.^4 Al-Walid ibn Abd al-Malik showed that investment in architecture and art not only creates momentary beauty, but also heritage that inspires future generations.^1

**Values that can be learned from the history of Al-Aqsa Mosque construction:**

- **Commitment to excellence**: Al-Walid was not satisfied with mediocre standards; he deployed the best resources to create high-quality work.^5

- **Harmonious cultural integration**: Al-Aqsa Mosque successfully combined architectural elements from various traditions without losing its authentic Islamic identity.^2

- **Tolerance and respect**: The mosque construction was carried out while still respecting the sanctity of the place for other religions that had been there earlier.^8

- **Sustainability and maintenance**: The history of continuous renovation shows the commitment of generation after generation of Muslims to preserve spiritual and architectural heritage.^3

- **Synergy between spiritual and aesthetic**: Al-Aqsa Mosque proved that architectural beauty can become a medium for drawing closer to Allah.^6

In contemporary context, the legacy of Al-Aqsa Mosque teaches the importance of maintaining balance between innovation and tradition, between worldly magnificence and spiritual devotion.^7 This mosque remains a symbol of steadfast faith and excellence of Islamic civilization that inspires Muslims throughout the world.^4
