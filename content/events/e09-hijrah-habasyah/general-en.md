# The First Hijrah to Abyssinia — Refuge in the Land of the Negus (615 CE)

## Context

By the fifth year of prophethood (around 615 CE), the pressure on Muslims in Mecca had reached an unbearable point. After the open da'wah began, the Quraysh elites launched a systematic campaign of persecution: enslaved Muslims were tortured openly, Muslim merchants were economically boycotted, and even Muslims of noble birth faced severe social pressure from their own families.^1

Prophet Muhammad SAW, who still had the protection of his uncle Abu Talib, recognized that not all his followers enjoyed similar protection. He decided to send a group of companions to **Abyssinia** (modern Ethiopia) — a Christian kingdom ruled by a king known for his justice, **the Negus** (Najashi, Ashama ibn Abjar).^2

This decision was remarkable in several ways. First, the Prophet sent his followers to a Christian land rather than to another Arab tribe — demonstrating that he saw justice as transcending religious and ethnic boundaries. Second, it was the first intercontinental migration in Islamic history — from the Arabian Peninsula to East Africa, crossing the Red Sea. Third, it showed extraordinary strategic pragmatism: protecting core members so the movement would not perish.^1

## The Events

### The First Group

Ibn Hisham records that the first group to emigrate to Abyssinia consisted of twelve men and four women, led by **Uthman ibn Affan** and his wife **Ruqayyah bint Muhammad** — the Prophet's own daughter.^1 Among the group were also **Zubayr ibn al-Awwam**, **Abd al-Rahman ibn Awf**, and **Mus'ab ibn Umayr**.

They departed secretly, leaving Mecca at night toward the port of Shu'aybah on the Red Sea coast, then crossed to Abyssinia.^2 The journey was fraught with danger — beyond the perils of the sea, there was the threat of pursuit by the Quraysh, who did not want news of Islam spreading beyond Mecca.

### The Second Group

Shortly afterward, a larger second group followed. Ibn Sa'd records that it numbered approximately eighty-three men and eighteen women, led by **Ja'far ibn Abi Talib**, the Prophet's cousin.^4

Ja'far was the perfect choice to lead the Muslim community in exile. He was known for his intelligence, eloquence, handsome appearance (said to resemble the Prophet), and charismatic personality. His diplomatic abilities would soon be tested in one of the most decisive moments in early Islamic history.^1

### The Quraysh Delegation and the Trial at the Negus's Court

When news of the Muslims' presence in Abyssinia reached Mecca, the Quraysh leaders grew deeply concerned. They dispatched two of their finest envoys — **Amr ibn al-As** and **Abdullah ibn Abi Rabi'ah** — bearing lavish gifts for the Negus and his advisors, with the mission of demanding the Muslims' return to Mecca.^1

Amr ibn al-As addressed the Negus: "O King, a group of foolish youths from our people have fled to your land. They have abandoned the religion of their people and have not embraced yours either. They have invented a new religion unknown to us and to you. The leaders of their people — their fathers, uncles, and families — beseech you to return them."^1

The bishops and royal advisors, having received the Quraysh gifts, urged the Negus to hand over the Muslims. But the Negus — known for his justice — refused to make a decision without hearing both sides. He summoned the Muslims to his court.^1

### Ja'far's Address

This was the most dramatic moment of the Hijrah to Abyssinia. Before the Negus, the bishops, and the Quraysh delegation, **Ja'far ibn Abi Talib** stood and delivered what became one of the most influential speeches in Islamic history:^3

"O King, we were a people of ignorance (*jahiliyyah*). We worshipped idols, ate carrion, committed shameful acts, severed ties of kinship, mistreated our neighbors, and the strong among us preyed upon the weak. We remained in that state until Allah sent to us a Messenger from among our own — whose lineage, truthfulness, trustworthiness, and purity we knew well."^1

Ja'far continued:

"He called us to worship Allah alone and to abandon what our forefathers worshipped of stones and idols. He commanded us to speak the truth, fulfill trusts, maintain family ties, be good to our neighbors, and refrain from what is forbidden and from bloodshed. He forbade us from shameful deeds, bearing false witness, consuming the wealth of orphans, and slandering chaste women."^1

"So we believed in him, had faith in him, and followed him. Then our people attacked us, tortured us, and oppressed us to make us return to worshipping idols. When they wronged us and prevented us from our religion, we came to your land. We chose you above all others, and we hoped for protection from you, O King."^1

### Surah Maryam and the Negus's Tears

The Negus then asked: "Do you have anything with you from what your Messenger brought from Allah?"

Ja'far replied: "Yes." He then recited the opening of **Surah Maryam** — the surah narrating the stories of Mary, the birth of Jesus, and John the Baptist.^1

Ibn Hisham narrates that when the Negus heard the verses about Mary and Jesus, tears flowed down his face until they soaked his beard. The bishops present also wept until their scriptures were wet.^1

The Negus declared:

**"Indeed, this and what Jesus brought come from the same source of light."**^1

He then turned to the Quraysh delegation and said: **"Go, for by Allah, I will never hand them over to you."**^1

### The Quraysh's Second Attempt

Amr ibn al-As did not give up. The next day, he returned to the Negus with a new strategy: "O King, they say terrible things about Jesus son of Mary."^1

The Negus summoned the Muslims again. The Muslims were deeply anxious — they knew that Islam's view of Jesus differed from Christian belief. Ja'far stood again and spoke firmly:

"We say about Jesus what our Prophet told us: he is the servant of Allah, His messenger, His spirit, and His word which He bestowed upon Mary, the blessed virgin."^1

The Negus picked up a stick from the ground, drew a line, and declared:

**"By Allah, Jesus son of Mary was not more than what you have said by the length of this line."**^1

His generals snorted in disapproval, but the Negus stood firm. He returned the Quraysh's gifts and granted the Muslims full protection.

## Analysis and Lessons^5

### Cross-Civilizational Diplomacy

The Hijrah to Abyssinia is an early example of cross-civilizational diplomacy in Islam. Prophet Muhammad SAW sent his followers to a Christian land — not because of theological similarity, but because of the king's justice. This demonstrates the principle that justice is a universal value transcending religious boundaries.^2

### The Power of Honest Narrative

Ja'far's address at the Negus's court demonstrates the power of honest storytelling. He did not attack the Negus's religion, did not belittle Christianity, and did not dissemble about Islam's view of Jesus. He explained Islam as it was — and it was this honesty that won the Negus's heart.^1

### The Negus: Justice Beyond Difference

The Negus was a Christian king who protected Muslims from persecution despite their not being Christian. He refused bribes, resisted political pressure, and refused to surrender those seeking refuge. In Islamic tradition, the Negus is honored as a model of just governance — and when he died years later, Prophet Muhammad SAW performed the funeral prayer in absentia for him.^3

### Surah Maryam as a Bridge

Ja'far's choice of Surah Maryam demonstrated great wisdom in da'wah. He selected the part of the Qur'an that had common ground with Christian belief — reverence for Mary and Jesus — enabling the Negus to see a shared source of truth. This was not theological compromise but a communication strategy that built bridges of understanding.^1 ^5
