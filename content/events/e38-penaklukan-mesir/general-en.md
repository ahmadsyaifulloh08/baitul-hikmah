# The Conquest of Egypt (639-641 CE)

## Context: Egypt Under the Byzantine Shadow

Seventh-century Egypt was one of the Byzantine Empire's most vital provinces. The fertile Nile Valley made it the *breadbasket* of Constantinople — the capital's food supply depended heavily on Egyptian agriculture. Alexandria, its great port city, was the largest trading hub in the Mediterranean after Constantinople itself, as well as an intellectual center renowned for its library and scholarly traditions.^1 ^3

Yet beneath the economic prosperity, Egypt's native population — overwhelmingly Coptic Christian — lived under oppression. Al-Baladhuri records in *Futuh al-Buldan* that the Byzantine government imposed harsh religious policies on the Copts, who followed the Monophysite doctrine — at odds with Constantinople's official Orthodoxy.^1 Patriarch Benjamin I, head of the Coptic Church, was forced into hiding for years to escape persecution. Heavy taxation compounded the people's suffering.

Ibn Abd al-Hakam in *Futuh Misr wa Akhbaruha* — the most detailed primary source on the conquest of Egypt — describes widespread discontent among the population.^2 Many Copts viewed the Byzantine rulers not as protectors but as foreign oppressors.

It was here that **Amr ibn al-As** entered the stage of history. Amr was a companion of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ renowned for his political acumen and strategic sharpness. He had known Egypt from trading journeys before his conversion to Islam and recognized both the province's potential and its vulnerability.^1 ^2

## Events: A Conquest That Transformed North Africa

### Amr ibn al-As Takes the Initiative (639 CE)

Al-Tabari and al-Baladhuri record that Amr ibn al-As proposed the conquest of Egypt to Caliph **Umar ibn al-Khattab** after the great victories in Syria.^1 ^3 Umar was initially hesitant — Egypt was a large province garrisoned by Byzantine forces, and Muslim resources were already stretched across multiple fronts.

Ibn Abd al-Hakam records a fascinating account of how Amr finally departed: Umar gave him a letter instructing that if he had already entered Egypt when the letter reached him, he could proceed; but if he had not yet crossed the border, he must turn back. Amr, suspecting the letter's contents, **deliberately opened it only after crossing into Egypt** at al-Arish.^2 He read the letter before his troops, then asked: "Brothers, are we in Egypt?" They replied: "Yes." Amr said: "Then we continue as the Commander of the Faithful has ordered."

While the historicity of this account is debated, it captures Amr's initiative and daring — and his cleverness in exploiting a diplomatic opening.

### The Battles of Pelusium and Heliopolis (640 CE)

Amr entered Egypt with a remarkably small force — roughly **4,000 men**.^1 ^2 This was starkly disproportionate to the much larger Byzantine garrison. But Amr relied on speed, surprise, and the support of the local population.

The first battle took place at **Pelusium** (*al-Farama*), the border fortress in northeast Egypt. Al-Baladhuri records that the city fell after a siege of about one month.^1 Amr then advanced south to **Heliopolis** (*'Ayn Shams*), where a major engagement occurred in July 640 CE. Ibn Abd al-Hakam records that the Muslim victory at Heliopolis opened the road to the heart of Egypt.^2

Umar, now convinced of the campaign's viability, dispatched reinforcements of **4,000 additional troops** under the command of al-Zubayr ibn al-Awwam — a renowned companion and legendary warrior.^1 ^3

### The Siege of the Fortress of Babylon (640-641 CE)

The most decisive moment in the conquest was the **siege of the Fortress of Babylon** (*Qasr al-Sham'*) — a great Roman fortress located at what is now part of Old Cairo. Al-Tabari records that the siege lasted approximately seven months and was the most difficult military operation of the entire Egyptian campaign.^3

Al-Baladhuri relates that **al-Muqawqis** (Cyrus of Alexandria), the Byzantine governor and Melkite patriarch, attempted to negotiate with Amr.^1 The negotiations were protracted — al-Muqawqis was willing to surrender, but Emperor Heraclius in Constantinople refused and ordered resistance.

The Fortress of Babylon finally fell in April 641 CE after a final assault led by al-Zubayr ibn al-Awwam, who, according to Ibn Abd al-Hakam, scaled the fortress walls with a ladder and opened the gates from within.^2 ^3

### The Fall of Alexandria (641 CE)

After the Fortress of Babylon fell, Amr marched north toward **Alexandria** — the jewel of the Mediterranean, the city founded by Alexander the Great in 331 BCE, and for centuries the intellectual capital of the ancient world.

Al-Baladhuri records that Alexandria's population — unlike the Coptic inhabitants of the interior — was more cosmopolitan and more attached to Byzantium.^1 The siege lasted several months. However, after the death of Emperor Heraclius in February 641 CE and the political chaos that followed in Constantinople, hopes of reinforcement from the capital evaporated.

Alexandria surrendered by treaty in September 641 CE. Ibn Abd al-Hakam records that the agreement allowed the Byzantine garrison eleven months to evacuate the city peacefully.^2

### The Founding of Fustat

After the conquest, Amr established a new city called **Fustat** near the Fortress of Babylon — rather than making Alexandria his capital. Al-Baladhuri records that this decision was strategic: Fustat was closer to the Arabian Peninsula and was not cut off by the sea, making communication with Madinah easier.^1

Ibn Abd al-Hakam relates a charming story about why Amr chose that particular site: when he was about to depart for Alexandria, he noticed a pair of doves nesting atop his tent. Not wanting to disturb the nest, he left the tent standing — and that tent became the starting point of Fustat.^2 The name *Fustat* itself derives from the Arabic word for "tent."

Fustat eventually grew into a great city and became the precursor of modern Cairo.

## Key Sayings and Scriptural Evidence

Al-Baladhuri records Amr's message to the people of Egypt after the conquest:^1

*"We have come to liberate you. Whoever accepts Islam becomes one of us — with equal rights and equal duties. Whoever does not, pays the jizyah and we protect him."*

Ibn Abd al-Hakam also records Amr's exchange with al-Muqawqis during negotiations. When al-Muqawqis offered gold for the Muslim army to leave, Amr replied:^2

*"We have not come for gold. We come with three choices: Islam, jizyah, or the sword. Choose."*

Umar himself, in his letter to Amr, emphasized the principle of justice:

*"Do not cut down fruit-bearing trees, do not slaughter livestock except for food, and do not wrong the local people. They are your responsibility."*

## Analysis and Lessons: Civilizational Transformation on the Nile

### The Strategic Genius of Amr ibn al-As

The conquest of Egypt showcased the strategic genius of Amr ibn al-As. With an initially tiny force — just 4,000 men — he conquered one of Byzantium's largest provinces. His success rested on several factors: speed of movement, the ability to exploit local discontent, shrewd diplomacy, and impeccable timing — striking while Byzantium was weakened after Yarmuk and the death of Heraclius.^1 ^2

### The Liberation of the Copts

For Egypt's Coptic population, the Islamic conquest was not a catastrophe — it was a **liberation**. Ibn Abd al-Hakam records that Patriarch Benjamin I, who had been in hiding from Byzantine persecution for years, was finally able to emerge and lead his flock openly after Amr guaranteed his safety.^2 Al-Baladhuri adds that the tax imposed by the Islamic administration (*jizyah*) proved lighter than the Byzantine levies that preceded it.^1

### The Birth of Islamic Civilization in Egypt

The founding of Fustat marked the beginning of a new era for Egypt. Within a few generations, Egypt transformed into one of the most important centers of Islamic civilization. From Fustat arose Cairo, which would become the largest city in the Islamic world. From Egypt also emerged Al-Azhar — the world's oldest continuously operating university — along with generations of scholars, scientists, and literary figures who enriched human civilization.

### A Lesson in Leadership

The dynamic between Amr and Umar during the Egyptian campaign illustrates a fascinating tension between field initiative and central authority. Amr demonstrated the courage to take initiative, while Umar showed the magnanimity to support that initiative once convinced of its merit — despite his initial reservations. This is a model of leadership that recognizes the importance of delegation without relinquishing oversight.^1 ^3
