# The First Crusade / Fall of Jerusalem — A Severe Test for Muslims

## Political and Religious Context

The late 11th century marked a crucial period in the history of relations between the Islamic and Christian worlds. The Byzantine Empire centered in Constantinople faced heavy pressure from Seljuk Turkish expansion into Asia Minor. This condition prompted Byzantine Emperor **Alexios I Komnenos** to request help from Pope Urban II.^1

The Islamic world at that time experienced serious political fragmentation. The Abbasid dynasty in Baghdad had lost its real political power, while various local dynasties such as the Fatimids in Egypt, the Seljuks in Iran and Iraq, and small emirates in Sham competed and fought against each other.^2

This internal division weakened the Islamic world's ability to face the approaching external threat. There was no effective coordination between various Muslim rulers, and open conflicts often occurred among them.^3

## The Crusade Call and Mobilization

On November 27, 1095 CE, **Pope Urban II** issued his historic call at the Council of Clermont, France. In his speech, he called upon all European Christians to wage war against Muslims to recapture the Holy Land, especially Jerusalem.^1

This call received an extraordinary response from various levels of European society. The participants' motivations were diverse: some were driven by religious fervor, the search for adventure, desire to acquire land, or even escape from economic problems in Europe.^2

The first wave known as the "People's Crusade" was led by Peter the Hermit and Walter the Penniless. However, this expedition ended in total failure in Asia Minor due to lack of proper preparation and organization.^3

## The Main Expedition and Journey to the East

The actual Crusade expedition began in 1096 CE with four main groups led by European nobles: **Godfrey of Bouillon**, **Bohemond of Taranto**, **Raymond of Toulouse**, and **Baldwin of Boulogne**.^1

The journey to the Holy Land was full of challenges and difficulties. The Crusaders had to cross thousands of kilometers of difficult terrain, face attacks from local rulers, and overcome logistical problems and diseases.^2

The first city successfully captured was Nicaea in June 1097 CE, which was then returned to Byzantium according to agreement. Subsequently, they succeeded in defeating the Seljuk army at the Battle of Dorylaeum, opening the way to the region of Sham.^3

## Siege and Fall of Antioch

Antioch became the next strategic target because of its position controlling the main route to Jerusalem. The siege of this city lasted from October 1097 to June 1098 CE, making it one of the longest and most exhausting sieges in this campaign.^1

During the siege, both besiegers and besieged experienced starvation and disease. This condition forced many Crusaders to abandon the expedition and return to Europe. Even **Stephen of Blois**, one of the leaders, fled and spread news that the expedition had failed.^2

Antioch finally fell through the betrayal of an Armenian fortress guard named Firuz, who opened the city gate for the Crusaders. The fall of Antioch was accompanied by massive massacres of Muslim and Jewish inhabitants.^3

## Journey to Jerusalem

After capturing Antioch, the Crusader leaders experienced internal conflict regarding ownership of the city. **Bohemond of Taranto** ultimately maintained control over Antioch, while the rest continued their journey to Jerusalem.^1

The journey from Antioch to Jerusalem began in early 1099 CE. The Crusaders faced sporadic resistance from local Muslim garrisons, but there was no effective coordination to stop their advance.^2

Throughout the journey, negotiations occurred with various local rulers. Several cities surrendered without resistance after witnessing the fate that befell other cities that resisted.^3

## Siege of Jerusalem

The Crusaders arrived before Jerusalem's walls on June 7, 1099 CE. The city controlled by the Fatimid dynasty was defended by a relatively small garrison under the command of **Iftikhar al-Dawla**.^1

The siege lasted for five weeks under very difficult conditions. The Crusaders lacked water, food, and siege equipment. They were forced to transport wood from great distances to build siege towers.^2

On July 15, 1099 CE, the Crusaders successfully breached the city's defenses through coordinated attacks from various directions. **Godfrey of Bouillon** and **Tancred** led the attack that succeeded in capturing the city walls.^3

## Massacre and Consequences

The fall of Jerusalem was followed by horrific massacres of Muslim and Jewish inhabitants. Western chronicles themselves recorded that blood flowed ankle-deep in the city streets.^1

The Al-Aqsa Mosque, which served as a refuge for hundreds of Muslims, was not spared from the massacre. Jewish inhabitants who took shelter in the synagogue were also massacred after the building was burned.^2

## Formation of Crusader States^4

After capturing Jerusalem, the Crusader leaders formed four states: the Kingdom of Jerusalem, the Kingdom of Antioch, the County of Edessa, and the County of Tripoli. **Godfrey of Bouillon** was chosen as the first ruler of Jerusalem with the title "Defender of the Holy Sepulchre".^1

The formation of these states fundamentally changed the political map of the Middle East. The European feudal system was imported to regions previously controlled by Muslims, creating a very different governmental structure.^2

The Crusaders built fortresses and castles to defend the territories they had captured. European military architecture mixed with local building techniques, creating a unique style.^3

## Islamic World Response

News of Jerusalem's fall caused tremendous shock throughout the Islamic world. Muslim scholars and rulers began to realize the need for unity to face this new threat.^1

In Baghdad, scholars organized massive demonstrations demanding the Caliph and Sultan take action. However, the fragmented political condition made an organized response difficult to realize.^2

The fall of Jerusalem became a catalyst for the emergence of a more organized jihad movement among Muslims. Figures like **Imad ad-Din Zengi** and later **Nur ad-Din** began consolidating power to fight the Crusaders.^3

## Legacy and Learning

The First Crusade provided important lessons about the dangers of internal division for Muslims. Muslim rulers' indifference to common threats became one of the main factors in Crusader success.^1

This tragedy also showed the importance of tolerance and respect for civilian populations in conflict. The massacre in Jerusalem became a historical wound that affects interfaith relations to this day.^2

However, in the long term, the Crusades also encouraged Muslims to reunite and strengthen their religious identity. Lessons from this defeat would become the foundation for the revival that would be led by Saladin al-Ayyubi.^3
