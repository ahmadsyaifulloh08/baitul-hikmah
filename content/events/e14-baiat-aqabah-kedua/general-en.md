# The Second Pledge of Aqabah: 75 Delegates from Madinah and the Birth of an Islamic State

## Context: From the First to the Second Aqabah

A year before the Second Pledge of Aqabah, twelve men from Yathrib (Madinah) had met Prophet Muhammad ﷺ at the hill of Aqabah and declared their Islam in what became known as the First Pledge of Aqabah.¹ They returned to Madinah accompanied by **Mus'ab ibn Umair**, the Prophet's envoy tasked with teaching Islam and the Quran to the city's inhabitants.¹ ²

Mus'ab fulfilled his mission brilliantly. In less than a year, Islam spread rapidly among the Aws and Khazraj — two major Arab tribes in Madinah that had been warring against each other for generations. Ibn Hisham records that hardly a household among the Ansar remained untouched by the Islamic call.¹ Key figures such as **Sa'd ibn Mu'adh**, chief of the Banu Abd al-Ashhal of the Aws, and **Usayd ibn Hudayr** embraced Islam through Mus'ab's efforts — bringing their entire clans into the faith.¹ ²

This success set the stage for a momentous event at the next hajj season. That year, the pilgrimage caravan from Madinah was far larger than usual — among them were 73 men and 2 women who had embraced Islam and were determined to meet Prophet Muhammad ﷺ in person.¹ ² ³

## The Event: A Secret Meeting at Midnight

The meeting was planned with meticulous care. Prophet Muhammad ﷺ specified the time and place: **the middle of the nights of Tashriq** (the days following the standing at Arafah), at the pass of Aqabah in Mina.¹ Absolute secrecy was essential, for the Quraysh would have reacted violently had they learned that the people of another city intended to offer Muhammad protection.

Ibn Hisham narrates in great detail from Ka'b ibn Malik, one of the pledge participants.¹ Ka'b recounts that they slept among their polytheist fellow-pilgrims that night. When the first third of the night had passed, they slipped out one by one, "creeping like *qata* birds," toward the pass of Aqabah. They gathered there and waited for the Messenger of Allah ﷺ.

Prophet Muhammad ﷺ arrived accompanied by his uncle, **al-Abbas ibn Abd al-Muttalib**, who had not yet embraced Islam but wished to ensure his nephew's safety.¹ ² Al-Abbas spoke first. He said:

*"O people of Khazraj" — the Quraysh referred to all Arab inhabitants of Madinah as 'Khazraj' — "Muhammad holds a position among his people as you well know. We have protected him from those who oppose him. He enjoys honour among his people and security in his land. Yet he has chosen to join you. If you are confident that you can fulfil what you have promised him and protect him from his enemies, then that responsibility is yours. But if you think you will surrender him and betray him after he comes to you, then leave him now."*¹

## Dialogue and Covenant: A Binding Pledge

After al-Abbas finished, the delegates from Madinah asked Prophet Muhammad ﷺ himself to speak. He recited verses from the Quran, called upon Allah, and encouraged them toward Islam.² Then he outlined the terms of the pledge:

*"I take your pledge on the basis that you will protect me as you protect your wives and children."*¹

**Al-Bara' ibn Ma'rur** — the eldest leader among the delegation — immediately extended his hand and declared: *"Yes! By the One who sent you with the truth, we shall protect you as we protect ourselves. Accept our pledge, O Messenger of Allah. We are people of war and people of arms — we have inherited this from generation to generation."*¹

While al-Bara' was still speaking, **Abu al-Haytham ibn al-Tayyihan** interjected with a question that revealed his political acumen: *"O Messenger of Allah, there are ties between us and those people — meaning the Jews of Madinah — and we shall sever them. If we do so, and then Allah grants you victory, will you return to your people and leave us?"*¹

Prophet Muhammad ﷺ smiled and answered:

*"No. Your blood is my blood, your honour is my honour. I am part of you and you are part of me. I fight those you fight and make peace with those you make peace with."*¹ ²

Then **al-Abbas ibn Ubadah ibn Nadlah** stood and addressed his own people — ensuring they fully understood the weight of their decision: *"O people of Khazraj, do you know upon what basis you are pledging allegiance to this man? You are pledging to fight all of mankind — red and black alike. If you think that when your wealth is destroyed by calamity and your leaders are killed, you will hand him over, then leave him now. By Allah, if you do that, it will be disgrace in this world and the next. But if you are certain you will fulfil what you have promised even though your wealth is destroyed and your leaders are killed, then take him. By Allah, that is the good of this world and the hereafter."*¹

They answered in unison: *"We accept him even though our wealth is destroyed and our leaders are killed. What do we receive if we fulfil this pledge, O Messenger of Allah?"*

He replied: *"Paradise."*¹

One by one, all seventy-five came forward and clasped the Prophet's hand, declaring their pledge.

## The Appointment of Twelve *Naqib*

After the pledge was complete, Prophet Muhammad ﷺ asked them to choose twelve *naqib* (leaders/guarantors) — nine from the Khazraj and three from the Aws — to serve as his representatives in Madinah.¹ ² ³ These *naqib* were responsible for their respective communities, ensuring the commitments of the pledge were upheld.

Among the *naqib* were:
- **As'ad ibn Zurarah** (Banu al-Najjar, Khazraj)
- **Sa'd ibn al-Rabi'** (Banu al-Harith, Khazraj)
- **Abdullah ibn Rawahah** (Banu al-Harith, Khazraj)
- **Sa'd ibn Ubadah** (Banu Sa'idah, Khazraj)
- **Usayd ibn Hudayr** (Banu Abd al-Ashhal, Aws)
- **Sa'd ibn Khaythamah** (Banu Abd al-Ashhal, Aws)¹ ³

The Prophet told them: *"You are the guarantors for your people, just as the disciples (hawariyyun) were for Isa ibn Maryam. And I am the guarantor for my people."*¹

## The Quraysh Reaction: Suspicion Too Late

The secrecy of the meeting was not entirely maintained. Ibn Hisham narrates that a devil cried out from the summit of Aqabah, warning the people of Mina about "Muhammad and the *shuba'* (those who have abandoned the religion of their forefathers) who have gathered to wage war against you!"¹

Al-Abbas ibn Ubadah immediately offered: *"O Messenger of Allah, by the One who sent you with the truth, if you wish, tomorrow we will fall upon the people of Mina with our swords!"* The Prophet replied firmly: *"We have not been commanded to do that. Return to your places."*¹

The next morning, a Quraysh delegation visited the Madinah pilgrimage camp and asked directly whether they had pledged allegiance to Muhammad. The polytheists from Madinah — who genuinely knew nothing of the night's events — swore that no such thing had happened. The Muslims from Madinah remained silent, and the honest polytheists unwittingly shielded them.¹ ²

## Analysis: Political and Strategic Significance

The Second Pledge of Aqabah was not merely a religious oath of loyalty — it was a **political and military pact** that fundamentally altered the balance of power in the Arabian Peninsula.

**First, a new political legitimacy.** For the first time, Prophet Muhammad ﷺ received a guarantee of political protection from an entity outside Makkah. During thirteen years of preaching in Makkah, he had depended on clan-based protection — first Abu Talib, then the Banu Hashim. This pledge provided protection based on ideology, not tribal kinship. It was a paradigmatic leap in the concept of Arab loyalty.

**Second, a constitutional framework.** The appointment of twelve *naqib* demonstrated that the Prophet was building not only a spiritual community but also a governance structure. The *naqib* functioned as regional and tribal representatives — an early form of political representation in Islam.

**Third, extraordinary courage from Madinah.** By pledging allegiance to Muhammad, the people of Madinah consciously placed themselves in opposition to the entire Quraysh — the most powerful tribe in the Arabian Peninsula, custodians of the Ka'bah, and controllers of the north-south trade route. This was no impulsive decision; the dialogues show they fully understood the consequences.

**Fourth, the opening of the path to hijrah.** Without the Second Pledge of Aqabah, the emigration to Madinah would have been impossible. This pledge was the political prerequisite enabling the mass migration of Muslims from Makkah to Madinah with a guarantee of security.

## Lessons: Enduring Wisdom

The Second Pledge of Aqabah teaches several fundamental principles:

1. **Planning and secrecy.** Prophet Muhammad ﷺ did not act haphazardly. The time, place, and procedure of the meeting were meticulously planned. Faith does not negate strategic planning.

2. **Transparency in covenants.** Both al-Abbas and al-Abbas ibn Ubadah ibn Nadlah ensured that every participant understood the full consequences of the pledge. No one was deceived or caught off-guard.

3. **Courage grounded in principle.** When asked what their reward would be, the Prophet did not promise wealth, power, or land. He answered: *"Paradise."* And they accepted.

4. **Collective leadership.** The appointment of *naqib* shows that Islam does not mandate absolute one-person rule but rather distributed responsibility.
