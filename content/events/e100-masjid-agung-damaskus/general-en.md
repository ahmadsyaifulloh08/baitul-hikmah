# The Great Mosque of Damascus / Umayyad Mosque (706-715 CE)

## Political and Cultural Context of Damascus

Damascus as the capital of the Umayyad Caliphate required an architectural symbol that reflected the magnificence and legitimacy of the ruling dynasty.^1 **Caliph al-Walid ibn Abd al-Malik** (705-715 CE) understood that building a grand mosque was not only a spiritual need, but also a political statement about the power and progress of Islamic civilization.^1

The city of Damascus itself had a long history as a center of civilization.^2 When Muslim forces conquered this city in 636 CE under the command of **Khalid ibn al-Walid**,^1 they found a mature city structure with various monumental buildings, including the Church of Saint John which would become an important part in the history of the Umayyad Mosque.^2

## Negotiations with the Christian Community

One of the most interesting aspects in the construction of the Umayyad Mosque was the negotiation process between Caliph al-Walid and the Christian community of Damascus.^3 Since the conquest, Christians and Muslims shared the Saint John Church complex for worship.^3 This church had magnificent Byzantine architecture and a strategic position in the heart of the city.^3

Al-Walid proposed to purchase the entire church complex to be converted into a mosque.^4 Initially, the Christian community refused because this church had high historical and spiritual value for them.^4 However, the caliph offered very attractive compensation: construction of several new churches in other locations within the city, plus substantial financial compensation.^4

After lengthy negotiations, an agreement was reached in 706 CE.^5 The Christian community agreed to surrender the church complex under the agreed conditions.^5 This showed a high level of tolerance and diplomacy in interfaith relations during the Umayyad period.^5

## Design Process and Architectural Innovation

Al-Walid invited the best architects and artists from various regions of the caliphate and even from outside Islamic territories.^6 The architectural team consisted of Byzantine, Persian, and Arab experts who created a unique architectural style synthesis.^6 They combined traditional elements with new innovations suitable for Islamic worship needs.^6

One of the most important innovations was the *mihrab* design that not only functioned as a *qiblah* direction indicator, but also as an acoustic element that amplified the imam's voice.^7 The ventilation and lighting systems were designed in such a way that cool air could circulate well in the vast space, while natural light entered through specially designed windows.^7

The use of mosaics as the main decorative element was an adaptation of Byzantine tradition, but with motifs appropriate to Islamic aesthetic principles.^8 There were no human or animal images, but geometric motifs, calligraphy, and abstract natural landscapes were used.^8

## Construction Phases

The construction of the Umayyad Mosque lasted almost a decade (706-715 CE) and was divided into several strategic phases.^5 The first phase (706-708 CE) included partial demolition of the old church structure and foundation strengthening.^5 Architects had to be careful to preserve structural elements that could still be used.^5

The second phase (708-712 CE) was the construction of the main mosque structure, including the main prayer hall with dimensions of 136 x 37 meters.^5 ^6 Installation of marble and granite columns brought from various Mediterranean regions.^5 ^6 Each column was carefully selected to ensure structural strength and visual harmony.^6

The final phase (712-715 CE) was the completion of ornaments and decorations.^7 This was the most spectacular phase, where thousands of square meters of mosaics were installed by expert artists.^7 Arabic calligraphy with Quranic verses was placed in strategic positions, creating a deep spiritual atmosphere.^7

## The Marvel of Mosaics and Ornamentation

The mosaics of the Umayyad Mosque are considered one of the finest achievements of Islamic art throughout history.^7 Using millions of pieces of colored glass and gold, these mosaics depict heavenly scenes with rivers, gardens, and beautiful palaces.^7 The manufacturing technique adapted the skills of Byzantine artists but with Islamic aesthetic vision.^7

The calligraphy adorning the mosque walls displays selected verses from the Quran, especially those related to Allah's magnificence and the beauty of His creation.^8 One of the most prominent verses is:

## Social and Cultural Impact

The completion of the Umayyad Mosque in 715 CE created widespread impact in Damascus society and throughout the caliphate territories.^2 This mosque functioned not only as a place of worship, but also as a center for social, educational, and political activities.^2 Scholars used the mosque spaces for teaching, while merchants conducted business transactions in the area around the mosque.^2

Culturally, the Umayyad Mosque became a model for the construction of grand mosques throughout the Islamic world.^6 The architectural style developed here influenced mosque designs in Andalusia, North Africa, and Central Asia.^6 The concept of integration between aesthetic beauty and practical function became a standard in mosque architecture.^6

## Legacy and Continuity

Despite experiencing various damages and renovations throughout history, including earthquakes and fires, the Umayyad Mosque has maintained its basic structure and architectural character.^2 Each renovation period sought to preserve the integrity of the original design while making necessary technical improvements.^2

The scholarly tradition also developed around this mosque.^2 The library built in the mosque complex became one of the most important learning centers in the Middle Ages.^2 Scholars from various disciplines, from *fiqh* to mathematics, gathered and exchanged ideas here.^2

## Wisdom and Reflection

The construction of the Umayyad Mosque shows how Islam can integrate cultural diversity while maintaining strong spiritual identity. The negotiations with the Christian community exemplified high diplomacy and religious tolerance.

**Important values that can be learned:**
- **Interfaith dialogue** can produce win-win solutions
- **Architectural excellence** reflects the level of civilization of a society
- **Cultural integration** enriches the treasury of Islamic arts and architecture
- **Long-term investment** in spiritual infrastructure provides sustainable benefits
