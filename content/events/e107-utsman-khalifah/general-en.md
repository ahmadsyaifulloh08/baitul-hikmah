# Uthman ibn Affan — The Third Caliph of Islam (644-656 CE)

## Context: Succession After Umar ibn al-Khattab

In 644 CE, the Muslim world lost its great leader, **Umar ibn al-Khattab**, who died after being stabbed by Abu Lu'lu'ah al-Majusi while leading the Fajr prayer at the Prophet's Mosque.^1 Before his death, Umar appointed a consultative council (*shura*) of six senior companions to select the next caliph: Uthman ibn Affan, Ali ibn Abi Talib, Abd al-Rahman ibn Awf, Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas, al-Zubayr ibn al-Awwam, and Talhah ibn Ubaydillah.^1 ^2

The *shura* process lasted three days. Al-Tabari in *Tarikh al-Rusul wa al-Muluk* relates that Abd al-Rahman ibn Awf took on the role of mediator, meeting each council member privately and asking whom they would choose other than themselves.^1 The choice eventually narrowed to two candidates: Uthman and Ali. Abd al-Rahman then conducted broad consultations among the people of Madinah — including women and newly arrived merchants — and found that the majority favored Uthman.^1 ^2

On the fourth day after Umar's death, **Uthman ibn Affan** was pledged allegiance as the third caliph at the Prophet's Mosque. Ibn Kathir in *Al-Bidayah wa al-Nihayah* records that Uthman was approximately 68 years old at the time.^2

## The Event: The Caliphate of Uthman ibn Affan

### Profile and Status of Uthman

Uthman ibn Affan belonged to the Banu Umayyah, one of the most influential clans in Quraysh. He was among the *al-Sabiqun al-Awwalun* — the earliest converts to Islam — having embraced the faith through the invitation of Abu Bakr al-Siddiq.^2 ^3 His famous title was *Dhunnurayn* (possessor of two lights), as he married two daughters of Prophet Muhammad ﷺ: Ruqayyah, and after her death, Umm Kulthum.^2

Ibn al-Jawzi in *Sifat al-Safwa* describes Uthman as gentle, exceedingly modest (*haya'*), extraordinarily generous, and deeply devoted to the Quran.^3 Prophet Muhammad ﷺ himself said regarding Uthman's modesty:

> أَلَا أَسْتَحِي مِنْ رَجُلٍ تَسْتَحِي مِنْهُ الْمَلَائِكَةُ
>
> *"Should I not be shy of a man before whom the angels are shy?"*
> — Sahih Muslim, no. 2401

### Achievements of His Caliphate

**Territorial expansion.** Under Uthman's leadership, the caliphate achieved remarkable expansion. Muslim forces conquered Armenia, parts of eastern Persia extending to Khurasan, Cyprus, and North Africa (Ifriqiya).^1 ^2 Al-Tabari records that during Uthman's reign, the first Islamic naval fleet was established on the initiative of Mu'awiyah ibn Abi Sufyan, governor of Syria.^1

**Standardization of the Quran.** Uthman's greatest and most enduring achievement was the standardization project of the Quranic *mushaf* around 650 CE — an event of such significance that it is discussed separately.

**Expansion of the Prophet's Mosque and the Sacred Mosque.** Uthman carried out significant expansions of the two holy mosques. The Prophet's Mosque was enlarged and renovated with carved stone and stone pillars, replacing the earlier simple structure of palm trunks.^2

**Administrative policies.** Uthman appointed a number of his relatives from the Banu Umayyah to key governmental positions. Ibn Kathir records that he appointed Mu'awiyah as governor of Syria (continuing Umar's policy), Abdullah ibn Sa'd ibn Abi Sarh as governor of Egypt, and al-Walid ibn Uqbah as governor of Kufa.^2 This policy would later become one source of discontent among certain groups.

### Legendary Generosity

Uthman's generosity was renowned even before his caliphate. Several widely reported examples:

When Muslims in Madinah struggled with access to clean water, Uthman purchased the well of Rumah from a Jewish owner and endowed it as a *waqf* for all the people of Madinah.^4 During preparations for the Tabuk expedition (*Jaysh al-Usrah*), Uthman donated 300 fully equipped camels and 1,000 gold dinars. Prophet Muhammad ﷺ said:

> مَا ضَرَّ عُثْمَانَ مَا عَمِلَ بَعْدَ الْيَوْمِ
>
> *"Nothing Uthman does after today can harm him."*
> — Al-Tirmidhi, no. 3701

### Turmoil and Fitnah

The second half of Uthman's caliphate was marked by escalating political unrest. Al-Tabari and Ibn Kathir document several factors that became sources of discontent:^1 ^2

1. **Appointment of relatives** to gubernatorial positions, which some viewed as favoritism (*mahsubiyyah*).
2. **Land distribution** — Uthman granted portions of *fay'* land to relatives, which some companions regarded as a departure from Umar's policies.
3. **Incidents involving certain companions** — including the exile of Abu Dharr al-Ghifari to Rabadha and tensions with Abdullah ibn Mas'ud.

Historians note that behind legitimate grievances, there was also provocation from certain groups. Al-Tabari mentions the role of **Abdullah ibn Saba'** as a chief instigator who incited the people of Egypt, Kufa, and Basra to rebel.^1 While the extent of his role is debated among modern scholars, the existence of systematic provocation is widely acknowledged.

### Siege and Martyrdom

In 656 CE, groups of rebels from Egypt, Kufa, and Basra arrived in Madinah under the pretext of performing Hajj. They besieged Uthman's house for approximately 40 days.^1 ^2 Uthman refused to leave Madinah or use force against the rebels, even though several companions, including Ali ibn Abi Talib, offered assistance.

Ibn Kathir relates that Uthman said to his supporters:^2

*"I forbid anyone who acknowledges my right to fight on my behalf. Whoever wishes to obey me, let him restrain his hand. "*

On Friday, 18 Dhul Hijjah 35 AH (17 June 656 CE), the rebels broke into Uthman's house and killed him while he was reading the Quran.^1 ^2 ^3 His blood fell upon the open *mushaf* at the verse:

> فَسَيَكْفِيكَهُمُ اللَّهُ ۚ وَهُوَ السَّمِيعُ الْعَلِيمُ ﴿١٣٧﴾
>
> *"Allah will be sufficient for you against them. And He is the All-Hearing, the All-Knowing. ﴾137﴿"*
> — QS. Al-Baqarah (2): 137

Uthman died at approximately 82 years of age, after serving as caliph for 12 years. He was buried at al-Baqi' cemetery in Madinah.

## Prophetic Testimony and Key Texts

The exalted status of Uthman in the eyes of Prophet Muhammad ﷺ is affirmed in numerous hadiths. Imam al-Bukhari narrates that the Prophet ﷺ was atop Mount Uhud with Abu Bakr, Umar, and Uthman when the mountain trembled. The Prophet ﷺ said:

> اثْبُتْ أُحُدُ فَإِنَّمَا عَلَيْكَ نَبِيٌّ وَصِدِّيقٌ وَشَهِيدَانِ
>
> *"Be steady, O Uhud, for upon you stand a Prophet, a Siddiq, and two martyrs."*
> — Sahih al-Bukhari, no. 3675

This hadith foretold the martyrdom of both Umar and Uthman. The Prophet ﷺ also gave Uthman glad tidings of Paradise, though accompanied by severe trials. Imam Muslim narrates that one day the Prophet ﷺ was sitting at a well, then Abu Bakr asked permission to enter, followed by Umar, then Uthman. When Uthman sought permission, the Prophet ﷺ said:

> ائْذَنْ لَهُ وَبَشِّرْهُ بِالْجَنَّةِ عَلَى بَلْوَى تُصِيبُهُ
>
> *"Let him in and give him glad tidings of Paradise, accompanied by a trial that will befall him."*
> — Sahih Muslim, no. 2403

## Analysis and Lessons

### The Balance Between Gentleness and Firmness

Uthman's caliphate illustrates the complexities of leadership in a rapidly expanding society. On one hand, his gentleness and generosity won the hearts of many. On the other, that same gentleness was exploited by his opponents. Classical Muslim historians generally view Uthman as a just ruler who faced an engineered *fitnah*.^1 ^2 ^3

### Lessons About Fitnah

The assassination of Uthman became a turning point in Islamic history. It was the first time a caliph was killed at the hands of fellow Muslims. Al-Tabari and Ibn Kathir record that this event opened the door to *fitnah* (division) whose effects were felt for decades afterward.^1 ^2

Uthman's choice to refrain from bloodshed — even when his own life was at stake — stands as a profound lesson in prioritizing communal unity over personal survival. He chose to die a victim rather than become the cause of civil war.

### An Enduring Legacy

Despite his tragic end, Uthman's legacy remains monumental: the standardization of the Quranic *mushaf* that has unified Muslim recitation to this day, the expansion of the caliphate that laid the foundations of Islamic civilization, and a model of generosity that has set the standard for all subsequent generations.
