# Umar ibn al-Khattab — The Second Caliph (634-644 CE)

## Context: From Enemy of Islam to Architect of Civilization

When **Abu Bakr al-Siddiq** passed away in Jumada al-Akhirah 13 AH (August 634 CE), the Muslim community needed a leader capable of continuing the expansion already underway and preserving the unity of the *ummah*, still recovering from the *riddah* wars. Abu Bakr, before his death, consulted senior companions — including Abd al-Rahman ibn Awf, Uthman ibn Affan, and Usayd ibn Hudayr — and then nominated **Umar ibn al-Khattab** as his successor.^1 ^2

The nomination was hardly surprising. Al-Tabari records in *Tarikh al-Rusul wa al-Muluk* that Umar had served as Abu Bakr's right hand throughout his caliphate, overseeing judicial affairs and acting as chief advisor on every major decision.^1 Ibn Kathir in *Al-Bidayah wa al-Nihayah* adds that Umar's reputation as a firm yet just leader was well established among the companions.^2

Umar ibn al-Khattab was born approximately forty years before the Hijrah in Makkah, from the Banu Adi clan — a Qurayshi clan traditionally entrusted with arbitration between tribes. Before embracing Islam, he was among the fiercest opponents of the Prophet Muhammad's mission. Yet his conversion — in the sixth year of prophethood — marked a dramatic turning point. The Prophet ﷺ had prayed for Allah to strengthen Islam through one of the two Umars (Umar ibn al-Khattab or Amr ibn Hisham/Abu Jahl), and Allah chose Umar.^2 ^3

Ibn al-Jawzi in *Sifat al-Safwa* describes Umar as tall and powerfully built, with reddish complexion, left-handed, walking with swift, commanding strides. His voice was booming, his hand heavy, yet his heart was tender — he frequently wept when reciting the Quran and when hearing the grievances of his subjects.^3

## Events: A Decade That Redrew the Map of the World

### Inauguration and the First Address (634 CE)

After Abu Bakr was buried, Umar ascended the pulpit of the Prophet's Mosque and delivered his inaugural address. Al-Tabari records his words:^1

*"O people, I have been appointed over your affairs. Know that my harshness has softened. But I shall remain firm against those who oppress and attack the Muslims — I will press their cheeks to the ground until they submit to the truth. And know that despite my firmness against oppression, I will lay my own cheek on the ground for those who are peaceful and self-restrained."*

This speech established the foundational principles of his rule: impartial justice, firmness against tyranny, and gentleness toward ordinary people.

### Military Expansion: Conquering Two Superpowers

Umar's caliphate witnessed a series of military conquests unprecedented in history. Within ten years, Islamic authority expanded from the Arabian Peninsula to encompass all of Iraq, Persia, the Levant, and Egypt.

**The Conquest of the Levant (634-638 CE)**

Umar continued the military campaigns in Syria that Abu Bakr had initiated. Al-Tabari notes that Umar made a bold strategic decision: he replaced Khalid ibn al-Walid as supreme commander with **Abu Ubaidah ibn al-Jarrah** — not due to any failure on Khalid's part, but so that the community would not become dependent on a single individual and would recognize that victory comes from Allah, not from any general alone.^1 ^2

Damascus fell in 635 CE, followed by the decisive **Battle of Yarmuk** (636 CE), which shattered Byzantine power in the Levant. This battle, led by Khalid ibn al-Walid under Abu Ubaidah's overall command, ranks among the most consequential engagements in world history.^1

**The Conquest of Iraq and Persia (636-642 CE)**

On the eastern front, Muslim forces under **Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas** achieved a monumental victory at the **Battle of Qadisiyyah** (636 CE), crushing the Sasanian Persian army. This was followed by the fall of the Persian capital, Ctesiphon (*al-Mada'in*), and a series of battles that brought the total collapse of the Sasanian Empire.^1

**The Conquest of Jerusalem (637 CE)**

Umar himself traveled to Jerusalem to receive the city's surrender from **Patriarch Sophronius**, who refused to hand over the city to anyone but the caliph in person. This event became one of the most symbolic moments in Islamic history.^1 ^2

**The Conquest of Egypt (639-641 CE)**

**Amr ibn al-As** led the conquest of Egypt with a relatively small force. Alexandria, the greatest Mediterranean city after Constantinople, fell in 641 CE.^1

### Administrative Reforms: Building a State

What distinguishes Umar from a mere military conqueror is his extraordinary administrative vision. He did not simply conquer territories — he built a system of governance that served as the foundation of Islamic civilization for centuries.

**The Diwan (Registration and Stipend System)**

Al-Tabari records that in 20 AH (641 CE), Umar established the *diwan* — a comprehensive registry of all Muslim citizens along with their financial entitlements from the *bayt al-mal* (state treasury).^1 Each Muslim was registered according to their proximity to the Prophet ﷺ and their contribution to the Islamic cause. This system was unprecedented in Arab tradition, though it drew inspiration from Persian and Byzantine administrative models adapted within an Islamic framework.

**Bayt al-Mal (State Treasury)**

Umar formalized the *bayt al-mal* as a state financial institution separate from the caliph's personal wealth. Ibn Kathir notes that Umar was scrupulous in separating state funds from private property — he would even extinguish state-funded lamps when attending to personal matters.^2

**Provincial System and Governors**

Umar divided the caliphate into provinces, each headed by a governor (*wali*) directly accountable to him. He imposed strict oversight: every governor upon appointment was required to declare all his assets, and any suspicious increase in wealth was investigated.^1 ^3

**The Hijri Calendar**

Umar established the Prophet's Hijrah from Makkah to Madinah as the starting point of the Islamic calendar. This decision, taken in 17 AH (638 CE), gave Islamic civilization its own temporal identity.^1 ^2

**Garrison Cities**

Umar founded new cities as military bases and administrative centers: **Kufa** and **Basra** in Iraq, and **Fustat** in Egypt. These cities later developed into major centers of civilization and learning.^1

### Treaties with Non-Muslims

One of Umar's most enduring legacies is his approach to non-Muslim populations in conquered territories. He implemented the *jizyah* (protection tax) in lieu of military service, and guaranteed the security of their lives, property, and places of worship. The **Covenant of Aelia** (*al-'Uhdah al-'Umariyyah*), granted to the people of Jerusalem, became a model of religious tolerance recognized to this day.^1 ^2

### The Death of Umar (644 CE)

On 26 Dhul Hijjah 23 AH (3 November 644 CE), Umar was stabbed by **Abu Lu'lu'ah Firuz**, a Persian slave belonging to al-Mughirah ibn Shu'bah, while leading the Fajr prayer at the Prophet's Mosque. Al-Tabari records that Abu Lu'lu'ah harbored a personal grievance over taxes imposed on him.^1

Umar did not die immediately. Gravely wounded, he appointed a consultative council (*shura*) of six men — Uthman ibn Affan, Ali ibn Abi Talib, Abd al-Rahman ibn Awf, Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas, al-Zubayr ibn al-Awwam, and Talhah ibn Ubaydullah — to select his successor.^1 ^2 He passed away three days later and was buried beside the Prophet ﷺ and Abu Bakr in Aisha's chamber.

## Key Sayings and Scriptural Evidence

Umar was renowned for statements that encapsulated his philosophy of leadership. Among the most famous, recorded by Ibn al-Jawzi:^3

*"Were a mule to stumble on the bank of the Euphrates, I would fear that Allah might ask me: why did you not level the road for it?"*

Al-Tabari also records Umar rebuking a governor who built a lavish residence:^1

*"O servant of Allah, you build a palace while the people go hungry? By Allah, I shall not leave you be."*

The Prophet ﷺ himself praised Umar in several traditions, including:

> لَوْ كَانَ بَعْدِي نَبِيٌّ لَكَانَ عُمَرَ بْنَ الْخَطَّابِ
>
> *"Were there to be a prophet after me, it would be Umar ibn al-Khattab."*
> — Tirmidhi, no. 3686, Kitab al-Manaqib

## Analysis and Lessons: The Enduring Legacy of al-Faruq

### Justice as the Foundation of Power

The title *al-Faruq* (the one who distinguishes truth from falsehood) bestowed upon Umar was no mere honorific — it captured the essence of his rule. Umar demonstrated that authority built on justice is more enduring than authority built on fear.

Ibn al-Jawzi in *Sifat al-Safwa* catalogues dozens of episodes illustrating Umar's justice: his nighttime patrols to ensure no subject went hungry, his punishing his own son for intoxication with the same penalty applied to any commoner, and his refusal to eat fine food while his people still suffered want.^3

### Institutional Innovation

Umar was the institutional architect of Islam. The *diwan*, the *bayt al-mal*, the Hijri calendar, the provincial system, and the judicial framework he established became the blueprint for every subsequent Islamic dynasty — from the Umayyads to the Abbasids, from the Fatimids to the Ottomans. Western historians, too, acknowledge that Umar's administrative system was ahead of its time.^1 ^2

### Expansion with Ethics

What distinguished the Islamic conquests under Umar from other conquests in history was their ethical dimension. Muslim forces were strictly forbidden from felling trees, destroying places of worship, killing priests and monks, or harming women and children. The treaties granted to conquered cities — such as the Covenant of Aelia — guaranteed religious freedom and protection of property.^1 ^2

This approach explains why many inhabitants of Syria, Egypt, and Iraq — long suffering under Byzantine and Persian oppression — actually welcomed the arriving Muslim armies. They greeted not conquerors, but liberators.

### A Model of Simplicity

At the height of an empire stretching from Libya to Afghanistan, Umar continued to live simply. He wore a patched robe, ate bread and olive oil, and slept beneath a tree without guards. When Persian envoys came seeking him, they were astonished to find the ruler of half the known world asleep alone on the bare ground beneath a palm tree.^3

Umar's simplicity was not mere personal preference — it was a political statement. It affirmed that authority in Islam is not a privilege for enjoying luxury, but a heavy trust to be accounted for before Allah.
