# The Trade Journey to Syria — The Monk Bahira Recognizes the Signs of Prophethood (583 CE)

## Context

In the year 583 CE, **Prophet Muhammad SAW**, then only about twelve years old, joined a trade caravan heading to Syria (the region now encompassing modern-day Syria and its surroundings) with his uncle, **Abu Talib**. This journey is one of the most significant events of the Prophet's childhood, documented extensively by historians of the *sirah*.^1

Abu Talib, who had been caring for Muhammad since the death of Abd al-Muttalib, originally did not intend to bring his nephew on the journey. The Mecca–Syria trade route was a long and exhausting expedition through barren deserts lasting several weeks — not a typical journey for a child. However, young Muhammad was deeply reluctant to be separated from his uncle.^1

Ibn Hisham narrates in the chapter on Muhammad's childhood that when the caravan was ready to depart, Muhammad held onto Abu Talib's camel reins and pleaded to be allowed to come along. Abu Talib was moved by his nephew's desire and said: "By Allah, I shall take him with me. I shall not be parted from him, nor shall he be parted from me."^1

Trade journeys to Syria were a central tradition for the Quraysh tribe. Mecca, situated in a barren valley without agricultural land, relied on trade as its primary livelihood. Quraysh caravans carried goods from Yemen and the southern regions — such as spices, frankincense, and leather — to be traded in the markets of Syria. In return, they brought back goods from Syria and the Byzantine Empire such as cloth, wheat, and olive oil.^2

The Quran itself references this tradition of trade journeys^4:

> لِإِيلَافِ قُرَيْشٍ ﴿١﴾ إِيلَافِهِمْ رِحْلَةَ الشِّتَاءِ وَالصَّيْفِ ﴿٢﴾ فَلْيَعْبُدُوا رَبَّ هَٰذَا الْبَيْتِ ﴿٣﴾ الَّذِي أَطْعَمَهُمْ مِّن جُوعٍ وَآمَنَهُمْ مِّنْ خَوْفٍ ﴿٤﴾
>
> *"For the accustomed security of the Quraysh ﴾1﴿ — their accustomed security in the caravan of winter and summer. ﴾2﴿ Let them worship the Lord of this House, ﴾3﴿ who has fed them against hunger and made them safe from fear. ﴾4﴿"*
> — QS. Quraysh (106): 1–4

The *rihlat al-shita'* (winter journey) to Yemen and the *rihlat al-sayf* (summer journey) to Syria were the two primary trade routes that established Mecca as a commercial hub of the Arabian Peninsula. ^4

## The Events

### Departure from Mecca

Abu Talib's trade caravan departed Mecca heading north, following the classic trade route that stretched through Medina (then known as Yathrib), continuing northward across Tabuk, until reaching the territory of Syria. The one-way journey took approximately one month.^1

For the young Muhammad, this was his first experience seeing the world beyond Mecca. The vast deserts, green oases, bustling market towns — all of it provided new horizons for an orphan who until then had only known the barren valley of Mecca.^2

### The Stop at Busra and the Encounter with the Monk Bahira

The most significant event of the journey occurred when the caravan stopped at the town of Busra, a small settlement in the southern region of Syria (now located in southern Syria). In this town lived a Christian monk named **Bahira** — also referred to in some sources by his given name, Jirjis (Georgius).^3

Bahira was known as a monk devoted to studying sacred scriptures. He lived in a hermitage (*sawma'ah*) beside the road commonly traversed by trade caravans. For years, the Quraysh caravans had passed by his monastery without Bahira ever showing them any particular interest.^3

But this time, something different happened. Al-Tirmidhi narrates:

> كَانَ لاَ يُكَلِّمُهُمْ وَلاَ يَعْرِضُ لَهُمْ حَتَّى كَانَ ذَلِكَ الْعَامُ فَصَنَعَ لَهُمْ طَعَامًا كَثِيرًا
>
> *"Bahira used to neither speak to them nor pay them attention until that year, when he prepared abundant food for them."*
> — Narrated by al-Tirmidhi, no. 3620, Kitab al-Manaqib

Bahira invited the entire caravan to dine at his monastery — a hospitality he had never shown before. When everyone arrived, Bahira observed them carefully and realized that the person he was looking for was not among them. He asked: "Is there anyone among you who has not come?" They replied that a young boy had been left behind to watch over the caravan's goods. Bahira insisted that the boy be brought.^3

When young Muhammad arrived, Bahira studied him intently. According to al-Tirmidhi's narration, Bahira then asked him about various things — his sleeping habits, his activities, and other matters. Muhammad's answers corresponded with what Bahira had learned from the scriptures he had studied.^3

### The Signs Recognized by Bahira

Bahira then examined Muhammad's back and found the *khatam al-nubuwwah* (seal of prophethood) between his shoulders — a physical mark that, according to the tradition of earlier scriptures, indicated a prophet who was to come.^3

After this examination, Bahira approached Abu Talib and asked: "What is your relationship to this boy?" Abu Talib replied: "He is my son." Bahira said: "He is not your son. This boy's father should not still be alive." Abu Talib then admitted: "He is my nephew." Bahira asked further: "And what happened to his father?" Abu Talib answered: "His father died while his mother was still carrying him." Bahira said: "You have spoken the truth."^1

Bahira then earnestly advised Abu Talib to take Muhammad back to Mecca immediately and to guard him well, particularly from certain people. Bahira said: "If they see what I have seen in this child, they will seek to harm him. Indeed, your nephew will have a great destiny."^1

Abu Talib heeded Bahira's advice seriously. He swiftly concluded his trading business in Busra and hastened to bring Muhammad back to Mecca.^1

## Dialogue and Sacred Texts

The encounter with Bahira holds an important place in *sirah* literature as one of the earliest evidences that the signs of prophethood were already apparent in Muhammad from childhood.

Scholars of the *sirah* view this event from several perspectives. Ibn Hisham places this narration within the context of early signs (*irhasat*) of prophethood — extraordinary events that occurred before the commissioning of a prophet as divine foreshadowing.^1

Al-Mubarakfuri in *Al-Rahiq al-Makhtum*, in the chapter on the Prophet's youth, explains that the Bahira episode was part of a continuous chain of divine guardianship (*ri'ayah ilahiyyah*) over Muhammad from birth. Allah protected him from idol worship, from physical harm, and prepared him for the great task he would later carry.^2

Regarding the authenticity of the hadith about Bahira, hadith scholars differ on its grading. Al-Tirmidhi himself assessed this hadith as *hasan gharib* — meaning its chain of narration is sound but transmitted through a single route. Some scholars accept it as a valid narration for the purposes of *sirah*, while others exercise greater caution.^3

It should be noted that this event does not serve as a foundation for creed or Islamic jurisprudence — it is part of the historical narrative that enriches our understanding of the Prophet's life before the period of prophethood.

### The Syrian Journey and Character Formation

The journey to Syria also held an important dimension in shaping Muhammad's character and worldview. During this trip, he witnessed firsthand the diversity of cultures, religions, and civilizations that existed beyond Mecca. Syria at that time was a territory under the influence of the Byzantine Empire with a strong Christian tradition.^2

The experience of seeing a wider world, combined with Muhammad's innate quality of *tafakkur* (deep contemplation), contributed to shaping the personality that would later be known as *al-Amin* (the Trustworthy) and *al-Sadiq* (the Truthful) among the people of Mecca.^2

## Analysis and Lessons

### Divine Protection from an Early Age

This event demonstrates that Allah the Almighty guarded and prepared His future prophet from a young age. Bahira's recognition of the signs of prophethood in the young Muhammad was part of a greater divine plan. Allah ensured that Muhammad grew up in the right environment, under the care of people who loved him, and protected from harm.^2

### Abu Talib's Role as Guardian

This journey also reveals the depth of Abu Talib's love for his nephew. His decision to bring Muhammad along on a grueling trade journey — simply because he could not bear to be parted from him — reflects an extraordinarily strong emotional bond. Abu Talib was not merely fulfilling a family obligation; he truly loved Muhammad as his own son.^1

It was this love that would later drive Abu Talib to protect Muhammad from the threats of the Quraysh for years on end, even when doing so meant risking his own social standing and personal safety.

### Trade Traditions and Character Building

Muhammad's involvement in trade activities from a young age formed an important part of his character development. In the world of Arab trade, honesty and trustworthiness were the most valuable currency. It was these experiences in commerce that built Muhammad's reputation as *al-Amin* — the most trusted person in Mecca.^2

This reputation would later become critically important social capital when Muhammad received the first revelation. Although many rejected his message, not a single person — not even his enemies — ever questioned his personal honesty.

### Dialogue Between Civilizations

The encounter between Muhammad, an Arab child from Mecca, and Bahira, a Christian monk in Syria, reflects the interaction between civilizations and religions that had been taking place in the Arabian Peninsula long before Islam. Trade routes were not merely pathways for merchandise but also channels for the exchange of ideas, cultures, and religious knowledge.^2
