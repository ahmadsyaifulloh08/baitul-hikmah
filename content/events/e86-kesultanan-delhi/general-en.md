# The Delhi Sultanate Founded (1206 CE)

## Historical Background of North India

North India in the 12th century was a very strategic region but politically fragmented.^2 Various Hindu kingdoms such as Chauhan, Chandela, and Gahadavala fought each other to compete for hegemony over this resource-rich region. This fragmentation created opportunities for external forces to enter and control the territory.^1

The founder of the Delhi Sultanate, **Qutbuddin Aibak**, began his career as a slave (*mamluk*) under **Muhammad of Ghur** or Muhammad bin Sam Ghuri.^3 The mamluk system in Islamic civilization allowed talented individuals to rise to high positions based on ability, not lineage. Aibak proved this by becoming one of Muhammad of Ghur's most trusted generals in a series of expeditions to North India.^1

Muhammad of Ghur's victory in the **Second Battle of Tarain** (1192 CE) against **Prithviraj Chauhan III** opened the way for Muslim expansion into the heart of India.^2 Aibak played a central role in the conquest of Delhi, which was then controlled by the last Rajput king to rule this strategic region.^4

## Qutbuddin Aibak: From Slave to Sultan

**Qutbuddin Aibak** (ruled 1206-1210 CE) was born in Central Asia and sold as a slave when still a child.^1 The name "Aibak" itself means "moon child" in Turkish, indicating his Turkic origins. Despite his slave status, the *ghilman* (military slave) system in Islamic civilization provided excellent military and administrative education to talented mamluks.^3

Muhammad of Ghur recognized Aibak's extraordinary abilities in military strategy and administration.^2 In 1192 CE, after victory at Tarain, Aibak was appointed as governor (*naib*) for newly conquered territories in North India. This position gave him valuable experience in managing a very ethnically and religiously diverse society.^4

Aibak was known by the title "Lakh Bakhsh" (giver of thousands) due to his generosity in giving gifts and assistance to scholars, poets, and the poor.^1 This generous attitude not only reflected his personal character, but was also a political strategy to build legitimacy and popular support.^5

## Power Transition and Formation of the Sultanate

After Muhammad of Ghur was killed in an ambush by the Ghakkar people in Punjab in 1206 CE, Muslim governors in various territories of Ghur's empire had to choose between returning to Afghanistan or building independent power.^1 Aibak chose the second option and cleverly consolidated his power in Delhi.^2

This decision was not rebellion, but a pragmatic reaction to changing political circumstances.^3 Without direct support from the center of Ghur power, Muslim rulers in India had to rely on local strength and legitimacy they built themselves. Aibak understood that long-term success depended on the ability to adapt to local Indian conditions.^6

The process of formalizing Aibak's power as an independent sultan involved support from scholars and other Muslim leaders in India.^4 Although he never officially claimed the title "sultan," Aibak was viewed as the legitimate de facto ruler of Muslim territories in North India.^1

## Administrative System and Iqta

The Delhi Sultanate under Aibak developed an administrative system that combined Islamic traditions with established Indian administrative practices.^3 **The iqta system**, which was an adaptation of the Abbasid *iqta'* system, became the backbone of the sultanate's economy and military.^2

**Iqta** in the Indian context was a system of granting the right to collect taxes from a territory (*iqta'dar*) in return for military or administrative service to the sultan.^5 Unlike the European feudal system, iqta was non-hereditary and could be revoked by the sultan if the iqta holder (*muqti* or *iqta'dar*) did not fulfill their obligations.^1

This system had several strategic advantages:^6
- **Centralized control**: The sultan retained ultimate control over land and resources
- **Military flexibility**: Muqtis were obligated to provide troops when needed
- **Administrative efficiency**: Tax collection was done by parties with direct incentives
- **Social mobility**: Muqti positions could be obtained based on ability, not lineage

The implementation of the iqta system in India was also adapted to local conditions.^3 Muqtis were not only tasked with collecting taxes (*kharaj*), but were also responsible for territorial security, maintenance of irrigation infrastructure, and resolution of local disputes.^4

## Power Consolidation and Early Challenges

Aibak faced heavy challenges in the early years of his rule. He had to fight rebellions from local Hindu kings who wanted to reclaim their territories, while facing threats from fellow Muslim generals who also had power ambitions.^2 **Taj al-Din Yildiz** in Ghazni and **Nasir al-Din Qabacha** in Multan both claimed to be the legitimate successors of Muhammad of Ghur.^1

What distinguished Aibak from his competitors was his ability to build alliances with local leaders and adopt effective administrative systems.^5 He did not rely on oppression alone, but created systems that provided space for various communities to participate in government.^6

Aibak also demonstrated high diplomatic ability in facing threats from **Khwarazm Shah** in Central Asia.^3 Rather than engage in conflicts that would drain resources, he chose to focus energy on consolidating power in India.^4

## Religious Tolerance and Cultural Integration

One of Aibak's greatest successes was his ability to build religious tolerance in a very diverse region.^1 Although Islam was the official state religion, Hindu, Buddhist, and Jain religious practices were not prohibited as long as they did not conflict with state policy. This policy was very different from practices in some other Muslim regions during the same period.^7

Aibak even maintained some traditional Indian festivals and allowed the rebuilding of temples damaged by war.^3 Brahmins and Hindu religious leaders were still respected and involved in some aspects of administration, especially those related to family law and religious rituals of their communities.^2

Cultural integration was also reflected in palace life.^5 Aibak adopted some Indian ceremonial traditions while maintaining Islamic elements. Persian became the official administrative language, but local languages such as Hindi and Sanskrit were still used in certain contexts.^4

## Indo-Islamic Architecture Revolution

The most important physical legacy of Aibak's era was the beginning of construction of the **Qutb Minar** and **Quwwat-ul-Islam Mosque** in Delhi.^2 These architectural projects marked the birth of a unique Indo-Islamic architectural style, combining Indian building techniques with Islamic aesthetics.^1

**Qutb Minar**, begun during Aibak's time and completed by his successor **Iltutmish**, became a symbol of Islam's permanent presence in North India.^6 This 73-meter high tower was not only a spiritual lighthouse for the call to prayer, but also a political statement about the stability and grandeur of Muslim power.^3

**Quwwat-ul-Islam Mosque** (Mosque of the Power of Islam) was built on the ruins of a pre-existing Hindu-Jain temple complex.^4 However, instead of completely destroying the old structure, architects integrated Indian decorative elements into Islamic design. Hindu-carved columns were reused but arranged according to mosque architectural principles.^1

These architectural innovations included:^5
- **Reuse of local materials**: Red stone and white marble abundant around Delhi
- **Adaptation of construction techniques**: Indian *corbelling* (stepped support) systems combined with Islamic arches
- **Integration of decorative motifs**: Arabic calligraphy combined with Indian floral and geometric motifs
- **Spiritual orientation**: Mihrab directed toward Mecca while maintaining proportions harmonious with the surrounding environment

## Iltutmish Era and Further Consolidation

After Aibak's death in a polo accident in 1210 CE, **Shams al-Din Iltutmish** (1211-1236 CE) ascended the throne and continued building the sultanate's foundation.^2 Iltutmish, who also came from a mamluk background, expanded the iqta system and introduced silver currency (*tanka*) which became the economic standard in North India for centuries.^3

Under Iltutmish, the Delhi Sultanate received official recognition from the **Abbasid Caliph in Baghdad** in 1229 CE.^1 This recognition provided important religious legitimacy for Muslim rulers in India and strengthened their position in the eyes of the global Muslim community.^6

Iltutmish also completed construction of Qutb Minar and expanded the mosque complex.^4 He introduced a more structured administrative system by dividing the sultanate into several provinces (*iqta*) managed by governors directly appointed by the sultan.^7

## Razia Sultana: A Visionary Female Leader

One of the most fascinating figures in the history of the Delhi Sultanate was **Razia al-Din Sultan** (1236-1240 CE), daughter of Iltutmish who became the first female ruler in the history of Islam in India.^2 Ascending to the throne by her father's will that ignored his male sons, Razia proved that leadership knows no gender.^1

Iltutmish chose Razia as his successor because he saw her administrative abilities and political vision as superior compared to his other sons.^5 In his will, Iltutmish said: "My sons are too indulged in luxury and games, but Razia has all the qualities of a king except she is not male."^3

**Razia's reign was marked by several important innovations:**^6

**Administrative Reform**: Razia introduced a stricter meritocracy system in official appointments. She reduced tribal influence and emphasized competence more in job assignments.^4

**Inclusion Policy**: Unlike her predecessors, Razia actively involved non-Muslims in high-level administration. Several important positions were held by Hindus and Jains who had special expertise.^1

**Regional Diplomacy**: Razia established diplomatic relations with Hindu kingdoms in the south and east. She understood that the sultanate's long-term strength depended on regional stability, not just military domination.^7

**Military Reform**: She introduced special units in the army consisting of local Indian soldiers. This step not only saved costs but also increased local loyalty to the sultanate.^2

Unfortunately, Razia's rule ended tragically due to rebellion by Turkish nobles who could not accept female leadership.^3 Although her reign was brief, Razia proved that Islam provides space for competent and visionary female leadership.^5

## Social Impact and Societal Transformation

The establishment of the Delhi Sultanate brought profound social changes to North India.^1 **The Islamization process** proceeded gradually through various mechanisms: voluntary conversion, inter-religious marriage, and the appeal of egalitarian Islamic teachings.^4

**Social Mobility**: The mamluk and iqta systems created opportunities for social mobility that had not previously existed in the rigid Hindu caste system.^2 Individuals from low backgrounds could rise to high positions based on ability and loyalty.^6

**Urban Growth**: Delhi developed into a cosmopolitan center that attracted merchants, scholars, artists, and craftsmen from throughout the Muslim world. This growth created a new middle class consisting of Muslim and Hindu traders.^3

**Language Development**: Interaction between Persian, Arabic, and local Indian languages gave birth to *Hindustani* as the lingua franca in North India. This language later developed into modern Hindi and Urdu.^7

**Culinary Transformation**: The introduction of cooking techniques and spices from Central Asia and Persia created the distinctive Mughlai culinary tradition. Dishes such as biryani, kebabs, and various types of curry became integral parts of Indian culture.^5

## Economic System and Trade

The Delhi Sultanate developed an economic system that integrated India into the global Muslim trade network.^2 **The Silk Route** connecting Central Asia with Indian western ports experienced revitalization under sultanate protection.^1

**Monetary Innovation**: The introduction of *tanka* silver currency by Iltutmish stabilized the economy and facilitated long-distance trade. This system was adopted by other kingdoms in India and persisted until British arrival.^6

**Agricultural Policy**: The iqta system provided incentives for muqtis to increase agricultural productivity. Investment in irrigation systems and opening new land significantly increased harvest yields.^3

**Manufacturing Centers**: Delhi became a center for high-quality textile, metal, and ceramic production. These products were exported to Central Asia, the Middle East, and even Europe through Muslim merchant networks.^4

## Intellectual and Religious Heritage

The Delhi Sultanate became an important center of Islamic learning on the Indian subcontinent.^1 **Delhi Madrasa** established during Iltutmish's time attracted scholars from throughout the Muslim world and produced important works in the fields of jurisprudence, Quranic interpretation, and history.^5

**Sufi tradition** also flourished under sultanate protection.^2 Figures such as **Khwaja Moinuddin Chishti** in Ajmer and **Khwaja Qutbuddin Bakhtiar Kaki** in Delhi spread Islamic teachings through gentle and inclusive approaches.^7

**Historiographical Works**: Writers such as **Minhaj al-Siraj Juzjani** in *Tabaqat-i-Nasiri* and **Ziauddin Barani** in *Tarikh-i-Firoz Shahi* documented the sultanate's early history and provided valuable insights into Indo-Muslim society's development.^3

## External Challenges and Mongol Threat

One of the greatest tests for the Delhi Sultanate came from **Mongol invasions** that began in the mid-13th century.^4 Under the leadership of **Genghis Khan** and his successors, Mongol forces destroyed Baghdad (1258 CE) and threatened the entire Muslim world.^1

**Sultan Ghiyas al-Din Balban** (1266-1287 CE) of the Mamluk dynasty successfully defended Delhi from Mongol attacks through brilliant defense strategies.^6 He built layered fortress systems and organized local militias to complement regular troops.^2

The Mongol threat actually strengthened Islamic identity in India because the Delhi Sultanate was seen as the last bastion of Islamic civilization in the east.^5 Muslim refugees from Central Asia and Persia enriched the sultanate's intellectual and cultural treasures.^3

## Contemporary Analysis and Wisdom

The Delhi Sultanate under Qutbuddin Aibak proved that **effective leadership does not depend on lineage background**, but on ability and wisdom in managing diversity.^1 The mamluk system enabled social mobility based on merit, which was very consistent with egalitarian Islamic values.^4

The success of Aibak and his successors also demonstrates the importance of **local adaptation in implementing Islamic systems**.^3 They did not force practices unsuitable to Indian conditions, but found creative synthesis between Islamic teachings and local social realities.^7

**Lessons from Razia Sultana** remind us that Islam provides space for competent female leadership. Her failure was more due to social prejudice than religious prohibition.^2

**The legacy of Indo-Islamic architecture** shows that Islam can adapt to local cultures without losing its identity. The synthesis between Islamic aesthetics and Indian techniques created unique and high-quality artistic traditions.^5
