# The Treaty of Hudaybiyyah: Diplomacy and Peace

## Historical Context: Longing for the Sacred Land

Six years had passed since the Muslims left Makkah. Those who were born and raised in the sacred city now lived in Madinah, building a new society, enduring war after war. Yet their longing for the Ka'bah — the direction of prayer, the sacred house built by Ibrahim and Ismail — never faded.^1

In the month of Dhul-Qa'dah of the 6th year of Hijrah (628 CE), Prophet Muhammad ﷺ had a dream of entering the Sacred Mosque and performing *tawaf* around the Ka'bah. In the prophetic tradition, the dream of a prophet is revelation. He announced to his companions: they would travel to Makkah to perform *umrah*, not to wage war.^1 ^2

Approximately 1,400 Muslims joined the caravan. They wore *ihram* — the seamless white garments of pilgrimage — and brought sacrificial animals. No weapons of war were carried, only sheathed swords as was customary for travelers.^2 ^3 This was a clear declaration of peace: they came as pilgrims, not soldiers.

The Quraysh, however, saw it differently. To them, the arrival of Muhammad and his followers — even in pilgrim garments — was a threat to their pride and political dominance. They dispatched Khalid ibn al-Walid with 200 cavalry toward Kura' al-Ghamim to intercept the Muslim caravan.^2

## The Journey to Hudaybiyyah

When news of the Quraysh blockade reached them, Prophet Muhammad ﷺ made a brilliant strategic decision. Rather than continuing on the main road guarded by Khalid's forces, he chose an alternative route through the difficult rocky terrain of *Thaniyyat al-Murar*, navigating steep cliffs rarely traversed.^2 The caravan of 1,400, complete with sacrificial animals, had to struggle through narrow and precipitous paths.

At this point occurred a sign of divine will: the Prophet's camel, al-Qaswa', knelt down and refused to move further at a place called Hudaybiyyah, a barren valley on the edge of the sacred territory of Makkah. Some companions said: "Al-Qaswa' is being stubborn!" But the Prophet replied:^1

> مَا خَلَأَتِ الْقَصْوَاءُ، وَمَا ذَاكَ لَهَا بِخُلُقٍ، وَلَكِنْ حَبَسَهَا حَابِسُ الْفِيلِ
>
> *"Al-Qaswa' has not become stubborn, for that is not her nature. Rather, she has been stopped by the One who stopped the elephant."*
> — Sahih al-Bukhari, no. 2731, Kitab al-Shurut

The phrase "the One who stopped the elephant" referred to the event of the Prophet's own birth year, when Allah destroyed the army of Abrahah that sought to demolish the Ka'bah. The Prophet's message was clear: a divine will had halted their journey at this point, and they must comply.

The caravan set up camp at Hudaybiyyah. Water was extremely scarce — the existing well was nearly dry. The companions reported the drought to the Prophet. He took an arrow from his quiver and ordered it placed at the bottom of the well. Ibn Hisham and al-Bukhari record that water then gushed forth abundantly, enough for the entire caravan to drink and perform ablution.^1 ^3

### Diplomatic Negotiations

From Hudaybiyyah began a series of long and tense negotiations. Prophet Muhammad ﷺ sent **Kharash ibn Umayyah al-Khuza'i** as his first envoy to Makkah, but the Quraysh slaughtered his camel and nearly killed him.^2 This was a grave violation of the Arab tradition of honoring peace envoys.

Several envoys then came from the Quraysh side. First was **Budayl ibn Warqa'** of the Khuza'ah, who served as an initial mediator. Then came **Urwah ibn Mas'ud al-Thaqafi**, a respected leader from Ta'if. Urwah carefully observed how the companions treated the Prophet — they competed for the water of his ablution, did not let his hair fall to the ground, and lowered their gaze when speaking to him. Urwah returned to the Quraysh and declared:^2

*"O my people, by Allah, I have been an envoy to kings — the Caesar of Rome, the Chosroes of Persia, and the Negus of Abyssinia — and I have never seen a king revered by his people as Muhammad is revered by his companions. "*

Finally, the Prophet sent **Uthman ibn Affan** to Makkah, as Uthman had strong kinship ties with the Banu Umayyah that would better ensure his safety. However, Uthman was detained longer than expected, and rumors spread that he had been killed.^2

### The Pledge of Ridwan

The report of Uthman's death — which proved to be false — triggered the most dramatic moment of the entire Hudaybiyyah episode. Prophet Muhammad ﷺ sat beneath a tree and called upon his companions to pledge allegiance — to vow not to retreat even in the face of death. One by one, all 1,400 companions extended their hands and made their pledge.^1 ^2

This event was so momentous in the sight of Allah that it was immortalized in the Quran^4:

> لَّقَدْ رَضِيَ اللَّهُ عَنِ الْمُؤْمِنِينَ إِذْ يُبَايِعُونَكَ تَحْتَ الشَّجَرَةِ فَعَلِمَ مَا فِي قُلُوبِهِمْ فَأَنزَلَ السَّكِينَةَ عَلَيْهِمْ وَأَثَابَهُمْ فَتْحًا قَرِيبًا ﴿١٨﴾
>
> *"Certainly was Allah pleased with the believers when they pledged allegiance to you under the tree, and He knew what was in their hearts, so He sent down tranquility upon them and rewarded them with an imminent victory. ﴾18﴿"*
> — QS. Al-Fath (48): 18

The resolute determination of the Muslims changed the Quraysh's political calculus. They realized that attacking a group prepared to die was not a wise option. So they sent **Suhayl ibn Amr**, their most skilled negotiator, to deal directly with Prophet Muhammad ﷺ.

## The Terms of the Treaty and Their Controversy

The negotiation between Prophet Muhammad ﷺ and Suhayl ibn Amr was intense. Every clause was fiercely debated. The final agreement appeared, on the surface, heavily disadvantageous to the Muslims:^1 ^2 ^3

**First**, a ceasefire for ten years. Neither side would attack the other, and Arab tribes were free to ally with whichever party they chose.

**Second**, the Muslims must return to Madinah without performing umrah that year. They could return the following year and stay in Makkah for three days.

**Third**, the most controversial clause: if any Makkan converted to Islam and fled to Madinah, Muhammad must return them to the Quraysh. However, if any Madinan defected to Makkah, the Quraysh had no obligation to return them.

The third clause provoked enormous anger among the companions. **Umar ibn al-Khattab**, known for his firmness and courage, approached the Prophet with a reddened face:^1

*"O Messenger of Allah, are you not truly the Prophet of Allah?"* — "Yes." — *"Are we not upon the truth and our enemy upon falsehood?"* — "Yes." — *"Then why do we accept humiliation in our religion?"*

The Prophet answered calmly: *"I am the Messenger of Allah, and I will not disobey Him. He will surely give me victory."*

Even during the writing of the treaty, tensions flared. When Ali ibn Abi Talib wrote *"Bismillahir-Rahmanir-Rahim"*, Suhayl objected and demanded it be written as *"Bismika Allahumma"* (the pre-Islamic formula). When Ali wrote *"Muhammad Rasulullah"*, Suhayl objected again: "If we acknowledged you as a messenger, we would not fight you." The Prophet instructed Ali to erase the phrase and replace it with *"Muhammad ibn Abdullah"*.^1 Ali found it difficult to do so, and the Prophet himself pointed to the words and erased them.

The real test of the treaty came almost immediately. **Abu Jandal ibn Suhayl ibn Amr** — Suhayl's own son — appeared stumbling into the negotiations, still in chains, his body bearing the marks of torture. He had secretly embraced Islam and was being tortured by his father. He pleaded for asylum with the Muslims. Suhayl insisted: "This is the first person you must return to me." With a heavy heart, the Prophet honored the treaty and asked Abu Jandal to be patient. The sight brought many companions to tears.^1 ^2

## Analysis: Why Hudaybiyyah Was a Victory

The Quran called this event a *fath mubin* — a clear victory:

> إِنَّا فَتَحْنَا لَكَ فَتْحًا مُّبِينًا ﴿١﴾
>
> *"Indeed, We have granted you a clear victory."*
> — QS. Al-Fath (48): 1

Many companions were initially bewildered: where was the victory? They returned without performing umrah, accepted apparently one-sided terms, and had to return Muslims who sought asylum. Yet history proved that Hudaybiyyah was the true strategic turning point.^1 ^2 ^3

**First, de facto recognition.** By sitting down to negotiate as equals with Muhammad, the Quraysh implicitly acknowledged the Muslims as a legitimate political entity. For years, the Quraysh had portrayed Muhammad as a rebel and troublemaker. The Treaty of Hudaybiyyah permanently altered that narrative.

**Second, the field of dawah opened.** The ceasefire removed the threat of war, allowing the Prophet to focus on preaching. In the two years between Hudaybiyyah and the Opening of Makkah, the number of Muslims multiplied — some sources suggest more people embraced Islam in those two years than in the entire preceding period.^2 ^3

**Third, the seemingly disadvantageous clause backfired.** Makkan Muslims who could not go to Madinah formed an independent armed group along the Quraysh trade routes, led by **Abu Basir**. They raided Quraysh caravans so effectively that the Quraysh themselves asked the Prophet to annul the return clause and accept these men in Madinah.^2 ^3

**Fourth, freedom of tribal alliance.** The Khuza'ah immediately joined the Prophet's alliance, while the Banu Bakr allied with the Quraysh. When the Banu Bakr attacked the Khuza'ah with tacit Quraysh support, they violated the treaty — and this became the direct cause for the Opening of Makkah two years later.

**Fifth, international diplomacy.** With peace secured, Prophet Muhammad ﷺ could send letters of invitation to the great rulers of the world — Heraclius (Rome), Chosroes (Persia), Muqawqis (Egypt), and the Negus (Abyssinia). This transformed Islam from a regional movement into a universal call.^2

## Lessons: What Hudaybiyyah Teaches

Hudaybiyyah teaches several fundamental principles:

**Strategic patience.** Prophet Muhammad ﷺ demonstrated that a true leader is not enslaved by momentary emotions. When all his companions desired confrontation, he chose a path that was far more advantageous in the long term. This is a lesson about the difference between courage and recklessness.

**The priority of peace.** Islam is often misunderstood as a religion that glorifies war. Hudaybiyyah proves the opposite: Prophet Muhammad ﷺ was willing to accept terms that appeared humiliating in order to avoid bloodshed and pave the way for peace.

**Obedience to leadership.** Although the companions held different opinions — and some expressed them forcefully — they ultimately submitted to the Prophet's decision. Umar ibn al-Khattab himself later regretted his impatience and performed many good deeds in atonement.^3

**Long-term vision.** Hudaybiyyah teaches that what appears to be defeat in the present can become the foundation of victory in the future. Sometimes stepping back one pace is the way to advance ten.
