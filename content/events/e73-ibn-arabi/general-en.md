# Ibn Arabi — Al-Shaykh al-Akbar & Wahdat al-Wujud (1165-1240 CE)

## Early Life in Andalusia

**Muhyiddin Ibn Arabi** was born in Murcia, Andalusia in 560 AH/1165 CE into a devoutly religious family. His father, **Ali ibn Muhammad**, was an official at the court of the Almohad rulers who had direct access to court scholars and literati. His mother came from an Arab noble family that had long settled in Andalusia.^1

From childhood, Ibn Arabi showed extraordinary intelligence and deep interest in Islamic sciences, particularly Sufism. He experienced his first spiritual dreams at a very young age, including visions of **Prophets Ibrahim, Musa, and Isa** giving him bay'ah (oath of allegiance) in the dream world. These experiences convinced him that he had a special spiritual mission.^2

In his teenage years, his family moved to Seville (Ishbiliyah), a center of culture and learning in Andalusia. Here, Ibn Arabi met various great scholars and Sufis of his time. **Abu al-Hasan ibn Jurayj** became his first teacher in exoteric sciences, while **Abu Muhammad Abdullah ibn Qasim al-Tamimi** taught him the foundations of Sufism.^3

His most important spiritual teacher was **Abu Madyan Shu'ayb** (d. 594 AH) from Maghreb, who profoundly influenced the formation of his Sufi thought. Abu Madyan taught the concepts of **"fana' fi Allah"** (extinction in Allah) and **"baqa' billah"** (subsistence with Allah) which later became the foundation of Ibn Arabi's thinking about wahdat al-wujud.^4

Andalusia during Ibn Arabi's era was an age of intellectual golden period. In the same period lived **Ibn Rushd** (Averroes), **Ibn Tufayl**, and other Muslim philosophers. This intellectual atmosphere gave Ibn Arabi access to various scholarly traditions, from fiqh, theology, philosophy, to Sufism. He even met Ibn Rushd directly when still in his teens, an encounter he immortalized in one of his works.^5

## Spiritual Journey: From Kashf to Shuhud

In his twenties, Ibn Arabi began experiencing various **kashf** (spiritual unveiling) and **ru'ya** (meaningful dreams). These mystical experiences became the foundation for developing his unique Sufi thought system. He recorded in *al-Futuhat* that during this period he often experienced **fanā'** (extinction from self-consciousness) and **baqā'** (awakening in divine consciousness).^6

**The most decisive spiritual experience** occurred when he was at Seville's cemetery one night. There, he experienced **shuhūd** (direct witnessing) of spiritual reality that made him understand that the entire universe is a manifestation of Allah's names and attributes. This experience became the initial seed of his wahdat al-wujud concept.^7

During this period, Ibn Arabi also began receiving what he called **"dictées spirituelles"** — direct inspiration received without rational thought processes. This method of receiving knowledge, which he called **"'ilm laduni"** (knowledge directly from Allah), became a main characteristic of his works that were often written in special spiritual conditions.^8

The year 1194 CE became a turning point in his life when he received a spiritual command to undertake the pilgrimage to Mecca. In his vision, **Prophet Muhammad SAW** directly commanded him to leave Andalusia and seek knowledge in the East. This journey was not merely ritual worship, but a spiritual wandering that would last for decades.^9

## Journey to the East: Consolidation of Doctrine

Ibn Arabi began his journey by visiting various Islamic learning centers in Maghreb. In **Tunis**, he met **Abd al-Aziz al-Mahdawi**, a great Sufi who gave him ijazah (teaching license) in various sciences and introduced him to the rich Maghreb Sufi tradition.^10

In **Fez**, he studied under scholars of **Al-Qarawiyyin University** and deepened his understanding of Maliki jurisprudence. More importantly, in Fez he met **Abu Yusuf Harun**, a Sufi who taught him about **martabat tujuh** (seven levels of being) which later became an important part of his cosmology.^11

His next journey brought him to **Cairo**, where he studied under **Al-Azhar** scholars. Here, he deeply studied **al-Ghazali's** works and began integrating philosophical approaches with mystical experience. He also met followers of **Ibn Sina** and **al-Suhrawardi**, who influenced the philosophical dimension of his thinking.^12

In **Mecca**, while performing pilgrimage in 1201 CE, Ibn Arabi experienced the greatest **shuhud** of his life. He witnessed **the Ka'bah as the heart of the cosmos** and understood Mecca as the spiritual center of the universe. This experience inspired the writing of his monumental work *al-Futuhat al-Makkiyah*.^13

> وَمَا خَلَقْتُ الْجِنَّ وَالْإِنسَ إِلَّا لِيَعْبُدُونِ ﴿٥٦﴾
>
> *"And I did not create the jinn and mankind except to worship Me."*
> — QS. al-Dhariyat (51): 56^14

Ibn Arabi understood this verse as the key to understanding the purpose of creation. For him, **"li ya'budun"** (so they worship) means not only formal ritual, but **"li ya'rifun"** (so they know Me), an interpretation he received through kashf. This understanding became the basis for the wahdat al-wujud concept — that all creation is a means for Allah to be known.^14

## Al-Futuhat al-Makkiyah: The Spiritual Encyclopedia

**"Al-Futuhat al-Makkiyah"** (The Meccan Revelations) is Ibn Arabi's greatest work, written over more than 30 years (1203-1238 CE). Consisting of **560 chapters in 4 large volumes**, this work is the most comprehensive Sufi encyclopedia in Islamic history.^15

The structure of *al-Futuhat* follows **cosmic order**: beginning with discussion of Allah's essence, then descending to levels of creation down to the material world. Each chapter was written based on **direct kashf** received by Ibn Arabi, not based on mere rational deduction.^16

**Important chapters in al-Futuhat** include:
- **Chapters 1-73**: About maqamat (spiritual stations) and ahwal (spiritual states)
- **Chapters 74-175**: About various types of spiritual knowledge and ways to obtain them
- **Chapters 176-300**: About Islamic cosmology and levels of being
- **Chapters 301-450**: About batini fiqh (spiritual meanings of sharia)
- **Chapters 451-560**: About eschatology and afterlife^17

What is unique about *al-Futuhat* is its **"autobiographical-spiritual"** writing method. Ibn Arabi not only conveyed theory, but also described how he obtained each knowledge through direct experience. He often wrote: *"Qala li al-Haqq"* (Allah said to me) or *"Kashafalahu li"* (He revealed to me), showing that the book's content was received through inspiration.^18

**A key concept in al-Futuhat** is **"al-Insan al-Kamil"** (the Perfect Human). According to Ibn Arabi, the Perfect Human is the most perfect mirror for Allah's names and attributes. Prophet Muhammad SAW is the prototype of the Perfect Human, while the saints are his manifestations in every age.^19

## Fusus al-Hikam: The Gems of Prophetic Wisdom

**"Fusus al-Hikam"** (The Bezels of Wisdom) is Ibn Arabi's second most important work, consisting of **27 chapters discussing the spiritual wisdom of 27 prophets and messengers**. This work was written in 1229 CE upon direct command of Prophet Muhammad SAW in a dream, who gave Ibn Arabi a book and said: *"This is the book Fusus al-Hikam, bring it out for humanity's benefit."*^20

Each **"fass"** (bezel) in this book reveals the unique spiritual dimension of each prophet:
- **Fass Adamiyyah**: Divine wisdom in the creation of the first human
- **Fass Nuhiyyah**: Wisdom of spiritual salvation through repentance
- **Fass Ibrahimiyyah**: Wisdom of tawhid and total surrender
- **Fass Musawiyyah**: Wisdom of direct dialogue with Allah
- **Fass Isa**: Wisdom about spirit and spiritual birth
- **Fass Muhammadiyyah**: Wisdom about spiritual perfection^21

**Ibn Arabi's hermeneutic method** in *Fusus* is very unique. He read the stories of prophets in the Quran not only as history, but as **"spiritual archetypes"** that repeat in every truth seeker's journey. Each prophet represents a specific **"hadrat"** (divine presence).^22

This work became highly controversial because Ibn Arabi interpreted some prophetic stories in unconventional ways. For example, he interpreted **the Israelites' worship of the golden calf** as also a form of worshipping Allah, because nothing exists except Allah. This interpretation triggered harsh criticism from orthodox circles.^23

## The Doctrine of Wahdat al-Wujud: Unity of Being

Ibn Arabi's main doctrine, which was most influential as well as controversial, is **"Wahdat al-Wujud"** (Unity of Being). According to Ibn Arabi, the only true reality is **"al-Wujud al-Mutlaq"** (Absolute Being), which is Allah. The entire universe is **"tajalli"** (manifestation) or **"mazahir"** (appearances) of His names and attributes.^24

**The basic principles of wahdat al-wujud** can be summarized in three propositions:
1. **"La mawjud illa Allah"** (Nothing exists except Allah)
2. **"Kull mawjud fa huwa mazhar min mazahir al-Haqq"** (Everything that exists is a manifestation of Allah)
3. **"Al-khalq huwiya al-Haqq fi al-a'yan al-thabitah"** (Creation is Allah manifested in fixed entities)^25

Ibn Arabi clearly distinguishes between **"al-Haqq"** (Allah as Absolute Reality) and **"al-khalq"** (world as manifestation). He uses the analogy of a **mirror**: the world is a mirror reflecting Allah's names and attributes, but the mirror itself is not the object reflected in it.^26

**"A'yan al-thabitah"** (fixed entities) is a key concept in Ibn Arabi's cosmology. These are **"eternal prototypes"** of everything in Allah's knowledge before being created in the empirical world. The world we see is the manifestation of these fixed entities in different levels of being.^27

This concept **does not mean pantheism** in the sense that the world is identical with Allah. Ibn Arabi explicitly rejects **hulul** (Allah's incarnation in the world) and **ittihad** (human union with Allah). Instead, he uses the term **"ayniyyah la-ghayriyyah"** (identity without otherness) — the world is "not Allah" while simultaneously "not not-Allah", a mystical paradox that can only be understood through **kashf**.^28

## Life in Damascus and Formation of a School

After completing the pilgrimage, Ibn Arabi did not return to Andalusia. He chose to settle in the Eastern regions, first in **Konya** (Anatolia) under the patronage of **Sultan Ala al-Din Kaykawus I**, then in Baghdad, and finally in **Damascus** in 1223 CE until his death.^29

In Damascus, Ibn Arabi found an intellectual environment conducive to developing and perfecting his thought. The city was a center of Sufism and Islamic learning during the **Ayyubid Dynasty** under **al-Malik al-Nasir Yusuf**. He received support from local scholars and rulers, including **Qadi Muhyiddin ibn al-Zaki**.^30

It was during this Damascus period that Ibn Arabi completed most of his major works. He established a **zawiyah** (Sufi learning center) in **Salihiyyah**, a district on the slopes of Mount Qasiyun. This zawiyah became a center of learning and spiritual training for his students from various parts of the Islamic world.^31

He taught and mentored many students who later spread his ideas throughout the Islamic world. Among his famous students was **Sadr al-Din al-Qunawi** (d. 1274 CE), who became the main successor and systematizer of his teachings. Qunawi later taught **Fakhr al-Din al-Iraqi** and **Ibn Sab'in**, who continued the Akbarian tradition.^32

**Other important students** included **Awḥad al-Din al-Kirmani**, **Sa'id al-Din al-Farghani**, and **Mu'ayyid al-Din al-Jandi**. They not only spread Ibn Arabi's teachings but also developed them further, creating a **"madrasan Akbariyyah"** (Ibn Arabi school of thought) that influenced centuries to come.^33

## Controversy and Criticism: Debates Lasting Centuries

Ibn Arabi's thought was not always well received by all scholarly circles. His **wahdat al-wujud** doctrine drew harsh criticism from some orthodox jurists and theologians who considered it too close to **hulul** (incarnation) or **pantheism**. This criticism began during his lifetime and continues to this day.^34

**Ibn Taymiyyah** (1263-1328 CE) was the most vocal and influential critic of his teachings. In *Majmu' al-Fatawa*, Ibn Taymiyyah accused Ibn Arabi of falling into **kufr** and **zandaqah** because of his teachings that allegedly equated Allah with creation. He specifically criticized Ibn Arabi's interpretation of **"Pharaoh as a believer"** in *Fusus al-Hikam*.^35

**Al-Dhahabi** in *Mizan al-I'tidal* also criticized Ibn Arabi, though in a more moderate tone. He acknowledged Ibn Arabi's vast knowledge but regretted his tendency to use **batini takwil** that was too extreme. Al-Dhahabi considered some of Ibn Arabi's works to contain dangerous **"khurafat"** (superstitions).^36

On the other hand, many great Sufis and scholars defended Ibn Arabi. **Abd al-Wahhab al-Sha'rani** (d. 1565 CE) in *al-Yawaqit wa al-Jawahir* provided a systematic defense of Ibn Arabi's teachings. He argued that criticism of Ibn Arabi was generally based on **misunderstanding** or **partial reading** of his works.^37

**Jalaluddin al-Suyuti** also gave positive assessment of Ibn Arabi in *Tanbih al-Ghabi*. He considered Ibn Arabi a **wali of Allah** and stated that his teachings were consistent with **ahl al-sunnah wa al-jama'ah** when properly understood.^38

## Influence and Legacy: From Andalusia to the Archipelago

Conversely, Ibn Arabi's influence on Sufism and Islamic thought was enormous and widespread. Those who regarded him as **"al-Shaykh al-Akbar"** (The Greatest Master) and **"Muhyi al-Din"** (Reviver of Religion) spread throughout the Islamic world. His influence was particularly strong on the development of Sufism in **Ottoman Turkey, Persia, and India**.^39

Ibn Arabi's thought influenced great Sufi poets like **Jalaluddin Rumi**, **Hafez Shirazi**, and **Abd al-Rahman Jami**. **Rumi**, though not directly quoting Ibn Arabi, showed the influence of wahdat al-wujud concepts in his *Masnawi*. **Hafez** explicitly referred to Akbarian concepts in his ghazals.^40

In the **Indian subcontinent**, Ibn Arabi's influence was very strong in the **Chishtiyah** and **Suhrawardiyah** Sufi traditions. **Shah Waliullah al-Dihlawi** (d. 1762 CE) wrote several treatises integrating Ibn Arabi's thought with orthodox Sunni theology. **Mulla Sadra** (d. 1636 CE) in Persia developed **"hikmah muta'aliyah"** heavily influenced by Ibn Arabi's metaphysics.^41

Even in the Islamic tradition of the **Archipelago**, Ibn Arabi's influence was evident. **Hamzah Fansuri** (16th century) from Aceh wrote poems clearly showing wahdat al-wujud influence. **Syamsuddin Pasai** wrote *Syair Burung Pungguk* containing Akbarian concepts. **Nuruddin al-Raniri**, though criticizing Hamzah Fansuri's interpretation of wahdat al-wujud, still respected Ibn Arabi as **shaykh muhaqqiq**.^42

## Intellectual and Spiritual Legacy

Ibn Arabi died in Damascus on **28 Rabi' al-Thani 638 AH/22 November 1240 CE** and was buried on **the slopes of Mount Qasiyun** at age 75. His tomb remains a pilgrimage site for Sufism devotees from around the world to this day. His legacy includes not only written works, but also an **intellectual and spiritual tradition** that continues to the present.^43

Ibn Arabi's main contribution to Islamic thought is the **systematization of theoretical Sufism**. He successfully built a comprehensive **metaphysical system** based on the Quran and Sunnah, yet articulated in sophisticated philosophical language. His work became a bridge between traditional practical Sufism and Islamic philosophy.^44

**Ibn Arabi's methodology** in combining **kashf** (mystical intuition) with **'aql** (reason) became a model for developing Islamic epistemology. He showed that spiritual knowledge does not contradict rational knowledge, but complements it at a higher level.^45

## Analysis and Lessons

**Spiritual universality**: Ibn Arabi showed that genuine spiritual seeking transcends geographical boundaries and single scholarly traditions. His journey from Andalusia to Damascus reflects a universal search for truth that can be found in various traditions within Islam.

**Integration of exoteric and esoteric knowledge**: Ibn Arabi's thought proved that Sufism is not anti-intellectual. Instead, he showed how spiritual experience can be philosophically articulated without losing its mystical dimension. This synthesis between kashf and 'aql became a model for Islamic intellectual development.

**Tolerance in diversity**: Although controversial, Ibn Arabi's thought teaches the importance of **"the vastness of Allah's mercy"**. His wahdat al-wujud concept, when properly understood, does not lead to relativism, but to a deeper understanding of the wisdom behind the diversity of creation.

**Depth of Quranic meaning**: Ibn Arabi showed that the Quran has unlimited layers of meaning. His **hermeneutic-spiritual** approach to sacred texts provided inspiration for developing deeper and more contextual interpretation.
