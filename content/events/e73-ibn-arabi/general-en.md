# Ibn Arabi — Al-Shaykh al-Akbar & Wahdat al-Wujud (1165-1240 CE)

## Early Life in Andalusia

**Muhyiddin Ibn Arabi** was born in Murcia, Andalusia in 1165 CE into a devoutly religious family. His father, Ali ibn Muhammad, was an official at the court of the Almohad rulers. From childhood, Ibn Arabi showed extraordinary intelligence and deep interest in Islamic sciences, particularly Sufism.^1

In his teenage years, his family moved to Seville (Ishbiliyah), a center of culture and learning in Andalusia. Here, Ibn Arabi met various great scholars and Sufis of his time. One of his most important spiritual teachers was **Abu Madyan Shu'ayb** from Maghreb, who profoundly influenced the formation of his Sufi thought.^2

Andalusia during Ibn Arabi's era was an age of intellectual golden period. In the same period lived **Ibn Rushd** (Averroes), **Ibn Tufayl**, and other Muslim philosophers. This intellectual atmosphere gave Ibn Arabi access to various scholarly traditions, from fiqh, theology, philosophy, to Sufism.

## Spiritual and Scholarly Journey

In his twenties, Ibn Arabi began experiencing various kashf (spiritual unveiling) and ru'ya (meaningful dreams). These mystical experiences became the foundation for developing his unique Sufi thought system. He began writing small works about his spiritual experiences.^3

The year 1194 CE became a turning point in his life when he decided to undertake the pilgrimage to Mecca. However, this journey was not merely a ritual worship, but a spiritual wandering that would last for decades. He visited various Islamic learning centers in Maghreb, Egypt, and Levant.

In Tunis, he met **Abd al-Aziz al-Mahdawi**, a great Sufi who gave him ijazah (teaching license) in various sciences. In Cairo, he studied under Al-Azhar scholars. Each place he visited enriched his intellectual and spiritual treasure.

## Magnum Opus and the Doctrine of Wahdat al-Wujud^9

Ibn Arabi was a highly productive writer with more than 400 works attributed to him. His two most important works are **"Al-Futuhat al-Makkiyah"** (The Meccan Revelations) and **"Fusus al-Hikam"** (The Bezels of Wisdom). The first is an encyclopedia of Sufism consisting of 560 chapters, while the second contains 27 chapters on the wisdom of the prophets.^5

His main doctrine, which was most influential as well as controversial, is **"Wahdat al-Wujud"** (Unity of Being). According to Ibn Arabi, the only true reality is Allah, and the entire universe is a manifestation of His names and attributes. The world is not separate from Allah, but rather a reflection or tajalli (manifestation) of Allah's essence.^6

This concept does not mean that the world is identical with Allah (hulul) or that Allah has need of the world (ittihad). Instead, Ibn Arabi distinguishes between al-Haqq (Allah as Absolute Reality) and al-khalq (world as manifestation). The world is "not Allah" while simultaneously "not not-Allah" — a mystical paradox that can only be understood through direct spiritual experience.

## Journey to the East and Life in Damascus

After completing the pilgrimage in 1201 CE, Ibn Arabi did not return to Andalusia. He chose to settle in the Eastern regions, first in Konya (Anatolia) under the patronage of **Sultan Kaykawus I**, then in Baghdad, and finally in Damascus in 1223 CE until his death.^7

In Damascus, Ibn Arabi found an intellectual environment conducive to developing and perfecting his thought. The city was a center of Sufism and Islamic learning during the **Ayyubid Dynasty**. He received support from local scholars and rulers.

It was during this Damascus period that Ibn Arabi completed most of his major works. He also taught and mentored many students who later spread his ideas throughout the Islamic world. Among his famous students was **Sadr al-Din al-Qunawi** who became the main successor of his teachings.

## Controversy and Reception

Ibn Arabi's thought was not always well received by all scholarly circles. His doctrine of Wahdat al-Wujud drew harsh criticism from some orthodox jurists and theologians who considered it too close to hulul or pantheism. **Ibn Taymiyyah** (1263-1328 CE) was one of the most vocal critics of his teachings.^8

Conversely, many Sufis and other scholars viewed Ibn Arabi as one of the peaks of Islamic Sufism achievement. They regarded him as "al-Shaykh al-Akbar" (The Greatest Master) and "Muhyi al-Din" (Reviver of Religion). His influence was enormous on the development of Sufism in the Islamic world, particularly in the Ottoman Turkish territories, Persia, and India.

Ibn Arabi's thought also influenced great Sufi poets like **Rumi**, **Hafez**, and **Jami**. Even in the Islamic tradition of the Archipelago, his influence can be found in the works of scholars like **Hamzah Fansuri** and **Syamsuddin Pasai**.

## Intellectual and Spiritual Legacy

Ibn Arabi died in Damascus in 1240 CE and was buried on the slopes of Mount Qasiyun. His tomb remains a pilgrimage site for Sufism devotees to this day. His legacy includes not only written works, but also an intellectual and spiritual tradition that continues to the present.^9

Ibn Arabi's main contribution to Islamic thought is the systematization of theoretical Sufism. He successfully built a comprehensive metaphysical system based on the Quran and Sunnah, yet articulated in sophisticated philosophical language. His work became a bridge between traditional practical Sufism and Islamic philosophy.

**Lessons from Ibn Arabi's Life**

Ibn Arabi's life teaches that genuine spiritual seeking requires total dedication and intellectual openness. He was not bound to one place or single scholarly tradition, but continued learning and integrating various sources of wisdom. His physical journey from West (Andalusia) to East (Damascus) reflects a spiritual journey from zahir (outward) to batin (inward).

Although controversial, his thought proved that Islam has a rich and sophisticated mystical tradition. Ibn Arabi showed that Sufism is not merely ascetic practice, but an epistemological and metaphysical system that can dialogue with philosophy and science.
