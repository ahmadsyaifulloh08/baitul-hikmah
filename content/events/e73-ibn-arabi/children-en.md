# Ibn Arabi — Al-Shaykh al-Akbar & Wahdat al-Wujud (1165-1240 CE)

## 🧑 Who Was He?

**Muhyiddin Ibn Arabi** was born in Murcia, Andalusia in 1165 CE. His father was named Ali ibn Muhammad who worked at the royal palace. From childhood, Ibn Arabi showed extraordinary intelligence and was very interested in religious knowledge, especially Sufism.

When still a teenager, his family moved to Seville, a city famous as a center of knowledge in Andalusia. There, Ibn Arabi met many great scholars and Sufi teachers. One of his most influential spiritual teachers was **Abu Madyan Shu'ayb**.

Ibn Arabi lived at the same time as **Ibn Rushd** (Averroes) and other Muslim philosophers. Andalusia at that time was a very advanced place in Islamic knowledge and culture.

## ⭐ What Did He Do?

In his twenties, Ibn Arabi began experiencing extraordinary dreams and spiritual unveilings. These mystical experiences made him decide to undertake the pilgrimage to Mecca in 1194 CE. However, this journey was not just for worship, but also to seek knowledge.

Ibn Arabi visited various major cities in the Islamic world such as Tunis, Cairo, Baghdad, and finally settled in Damascus. In each place, he learned from the best scholars and deepened his knowledge of Sufism.

He was a very productive writer with more than 400 written works. His two most famous works are **"Al-Futuhat al-Makkiyah"** (containing 560 chapters on Sufism) and **"Fusus al-Hikam"** (containing 27 chapters on the wisdom of the prophets).

## 💡 Why Was He Extraordinary?

Ibn Arabi developed a teaching called **"Wahdat al-Wujud"** (Unity of Being). According to his teaching, Allah is the only reality that truly exists. The entire universe is a manifestation or reflection of Allah's names and attributes.

This concept is very profound and difficult to understand. Ibn Arabi explained that the world is not Allah, but the world is also not separate from Allah. This is a mystery that can only be understood through deep spiritual experience.

Scholars gave him the titles **"al-Shaykh al-Akbar"** (The Greatest Master) and **"Muhyi al-Din"** (Reviver of Religion) because of the depth of his knowledge and spirituality. Although his teachings sometimes caused controversy, his influence was enormous on the development of Sufism throughout the Islamic world.

## 📖 Scripture

Ibn Arabi always based his teachings on the Quran and Sunnah. One verse he often contemplated was about the purpose of human creation:

> وَمَا خَلَقْتُ الْجِنَّ وَالْإِنسَ إِلَّا لِيَعْبُدُونِ ﴿٥٦﴾
>
> *"And I did not create the jinn and mankind except to worship Me."* ﴾56﴿

For Ibn Arabi, true worship is knowing Allah with complete understanding. The spiritual journey he took was to achieve ma'rifah (spiritual recognition) that is profound toward Allah.

Ibn Arabi died in Damascus in 1240 CE and was buried on the slopes of Mount Qasiyun. His tomb is still visited by Sufism lovers from around the world today. His legacy consists of written works and spiritual traditions that continue to live until now.
