# Ibn Rushd (Averroes): Tahafut al-Tahafut (1126-1198 CE)

## Life and Intellectual Background

**Abu al-Walid Muhammad ibn Ahmad ibn Rushd**, known in the West as Averroes, was born in Córdoba in 1126 CE into a prominent family of scholars. His grandfather and father were respected qadis (judges) in the Maliki school tradition in Andalusia. The family environment rich in Islamic legal tradition provided a strong foundation for his intellectual development.^1

Córdoba in the 12th century CE was one of the most advanced centers of civilization in the world. The city became a meeting place for Islamic scholarly traditions with ancient Greek heritage that had been translated and developed by Muslim scholars. Ibn Rushd grew up in an intellectual atmosphere highly conducive to the development of philosophy and science.

Ibn Rushd's formal education encompassed various disciplines: fiqh and usul fiqh, theology, medicine, mathematics, astronomy, and philosophy. He learned from the best teachers of his time, including **Ibn Tufayl**, a philosopher and court physician who later introduced him to Caliph **Abu Ya'qub Yusuf**.

## Meeting with the Caliph and Political Support

Ibn Rushd's meeting with Caliph Abu Ya'qub Yusuf in 1169 CE became a turning point in his career. This Almohad caliph was famous as a patron of science and philosophy. In their first meeting, the caliph tested Ibn Rushd's knowledge of Aristotelian philosophy and was impressed by the depth of his understanding and breadth of his insights.^2

The caliph then commissioned Ibn Rushd to write commentaries (sharh) on Aristotle's works. This task was highly strategic because Aristotelian texts in Arabic were often difficult to understand due to the multi-layered translation process from Greek to Syriac, then to Arabic. Ibn Rushd was asked to create clear and easily understandable expositions.

This political support enabled Ibn Rushd not only to work as a qadi in Seville and Córdoba, but also to develop his philosophical works freely. He became a bridge between Islamic philosophical traditions and the crystallization of Aristotelian thought that would greatly influence philosophical development in Europe.

## Confrontation with Al-Ghazali: Tahafut al-Tahafut^9

Ibn Rushd's most influential work is **"Tahafut al-Tahafut"** (The Incoherence of the Incoherence), written as a refutation of **Al-Ghazali's** work titled "Tahafut al-Falasifah" (The Incoherence of the Philosophers). Al-Ghazali, who died in 1111 CE—fifteen years before Ibn Rushd was born—had harshly criticized Muslim philosophers like **Al-Farabi** and **Ibn Sina** in his work.^4

Al-Ghazali in "Tahafut al-Falasifah" raised 20 criticisms against Aristotelian philosophy adopted by Muslim philosophers. Three of them were considered as kufr (infidelity): the eternity of the world (qidam al-alam), Allah's knowledge only of universals not particulars, and denial of bodily resurrection.

Ibn Rushd in "Tahafut al-Tahafut" systematically responded to each of Al-Ghazali's criticisms. He distinguished between philosophical demonstration (burhan) and dialectical and rhetorical arguments. According to him, Al-Ghazali often confused these levels of argument, making his criticisms miss the mark.

Regarding the eternity of the world, Ibn Rushd argued that creation is a continuous process of emanation from Allah, not an event occurring at a specific time. The world is a manifestation of Allah's eternal will, so it cannot be said to have a temporal beginning.^5

## Methodology of Harmonizing Religion and Philosophy

Ibn Rushd's most important contribution lies in his methodology of harmonizing revelation and reason. In his work **"Fasl al-Maqal"** (The Decisive Treatise), he argued that the Sharia actually commands the use of reason to understand truth. Philosophy is not a threat to religion, but a tool for achieving deeper understanding.^6

Ibn Rushd developed a theory of three levels of religious understanding. First, the rhetorical level (*khitabah*) for common masses who understand religion literally. Second, the dialectical level (*jadal*) for the middle class capable of argumentation. Third, the demonstrative level (*burhan*) for intellectual elites capable of philosophical thinking.

According to him, conflict between religion and philosophy arises when these boundaries are violated. Common masses should not be forced to understand philosophical truths that might shake their simple faith. Conversely, philosophers must respect religion's social function in providing moral guidance to broader society.

## Aristotelian Commentaries and Influence in Europe

Ibn Rushd wrote three types of commentaries on Aristotle's works: summaries (*talkhis*), middle commentaries (*sharh*), and great commentaries (*tafsir*). These works were translated into Latin and became the main references for medieval European scholars to understand Aristotelian philosophy.^7

His influence on Western thought was profound, particularly through **Thomas Aquinas** who extensively used Ibn Rushd's arguments in *Summa Theologica*. Even when Aquinas criticized "Averrois" (the Latin name for Ibn Rushd), he still regarded him as the "Commentator" par excellence on Aristotle.

European universities of the 13th century CE such as Paris and Oxford taught Ibn Rushd's works as part of their mandatory curriculum. An intellectual movement known as "Latin Averroism" developed and influenced figures like **Siger of Brabant** and **Boethius of Dacia**.

## Difficult Times and Exile

Despite strong political support, Ibn Rushd faced difficult periods toward the end of his life. After Caliph Abu Ya'qub Yusuf's death in 1184 CE, his son **Ya'qub al-Mansur** initially continued support for Ibn Rushd. However, political and religious pressure from fuqaha and conservative groups grew stronger.^8

In 1195 CE, Ibn Rushd was accused of teaching doctrines contrary to Islam. His philosophical works were publicly burned in Córdoba, and he was exiled to Lucena, a small town with a majority Jewish population. This exile was more political than theological—the caliph needed to appease conservative groups opposing his intellectual policies.

Ironically, at the same time, Ibn Rushd's works were being massively translated into Latin and beginning to influence philosophical development in Europe. While Andalusia lost its great figure, the West began a new phase of intellectual development thanks to Ibn Rushd's legacy.

## Legacy and Long-term Influence

Ibn Rushd died in Marrakech in 1198 CE, one year after returning from exile. His body was brought back to Córdoba to be buried in his birthplace. His death marked the end of the golden age of Aristotelian philosophy in the Western Islamic world.^9

However, his influence on global intellectual tradition was profound and lasting. In the Islamic world, although his philosophical works became less popular after the 13th century CE, his contribution to Islamic law through his work **"Bidayat al-Mujtahid"** continues to be studied today.

In Europe, Ibn Rushd became one of the most influential figures in the history of philosophy. He introduced systematic rational thinking that became the foundation for medieval scholasticism development and later the Renaissance. Dante in *Divine Comedy* placed Ibn Rushd among the great philosophers in Limbo.

**Lessons from Ibn Rushd's Life**

Ibn Rushd's life teaches that the pursuit of truth requires intellectual courage and long-term commitment. Despite facing rejection and exile, he remained consistent with his belief that reason and revelation are two sources of truth that can be harmonized.

His contribution shows that Islamic tradition has the capacity to dialogue with other intellectual traditions without losing identity. The methodology he developed in harmonizing religion and philosophy remains relevant in the context of civilizational dialogue in the modern era.
