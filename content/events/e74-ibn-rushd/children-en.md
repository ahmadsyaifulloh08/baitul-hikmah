# Ibn Rushd (Averroes): Tahafut al-Tahafut (1126-1198 CE)

## 🧑 Who Was He?

**Abu al-Walid Muhammad ibn Ahmad ibn Rushd** was born in Córdoba, Andalusia in 1126 CE. Westerners know him by the name Averroes. His grandfather and father were highly respected qadis (judges) in the Maliki school tradition in Andalusia.

Ibn Rushd lived in the city of Córdoba which at that time was one of the most advanced centers of civilization in the world. In this city, Islamic knowledge met ancient Greek heritage that had been translated into Arabic. This very rich intellectual atmosphere shaped Ibn Rushd into a great thinker.

From childhood, Ibn Rushd studied various sciences: Islamic law (fiqh), theology, medicine, mathematics, astronomy, and philosophy. One of his most influential teachers was **Ibn Tufayl**, a philosopher as well as court physician who later introduced Ibn Rushd to the caliph.

## ⭐ What Did He Do?

In 1169 CE, Ibn Rushd met Caliph **Abu Ya'qub Yusuf** who was famous for greatly loving knowledge and philosophy. The caliph tested Ibn Rushd's knowledge of Aristotelian philosophy and was impressed by the depth of his understanding.

The caliph then gave Ibn Rushd an important task: to create easily understandable commentaries on the works of **Aristotle**. This task was very important because Aristotelian texts in Arabic were difficult to understand due to the multi-layered translation process from Greek.

Ibn Rushd wrote three types of commentaries: summaries for beginners, middle commentaries for those with some understanding, and great commentaries for experts. These works were later translated into Latin and became the main references for European scholars to understand Aristotelian philosophy.

## 💡 Why Was He Extraordinary?

Ibn Rushd's most famous work is **"Tahafut al-Tahafut"** (The Incoherence of the Incoherence). This book was written to answer **Al-Ghazali's** criticism of Muslim philosophers in his book "Tahafut al-Falasifah" (The Incoherence of the Philosophers).

Al-Ghazali had harshly criticized philosophers like **Al-Farabi** and **Ibn Sina**. Ibn Rushd disagreed with these criticisms. He wrote a very systematic and profound refutation. He showed that religion and philosophy do not actually conflict, but complement each other.

Ibn Rushd developed a theory of three levels of religious understanding: for common people who understand religion simply, for middle-class people who can argue, and for experts who can think philosophically. According to him, problems arise when these boundaries are mixed up.

## 📖 Scripture

Ibn Rushd always emphasized that Islam encourages the use of reason to understand truth. He often referred to Quranic verses that show the importance of knowledge:

> وَمَا أُوتِيتُم مِّنَ الْعِلْمِ إِلَّا قَلِيلًا ﴿٨٥﴾
>
> *"And you have not been given of knowledge except a little."* ﴾85﴿

This verse reminds us that humans must continue learning and seeking knowledge. For Ibn Rushd, philosophy was a way to draw closer to Allah through deep understanding of His creation.

Toward the end of his life, Ibn Rushd faced difficult times. In 1195 CE, he was accused of teaching doctrines contrary to Islam. His works were burned and he was exiled. However, a year later, his position was restored. Ibn Rushd died in Marrakech in 1198 CE and was buried in Córdoba.
