# The Battle of Qadisiyyah — The Conquest of Persia (636 CE)

## Context: Two Worlds Collide

By the mid-seventh century CE, the Sasanian Persian Empire had endured for over four centuries. From its magnificent capital, Ctesiphon (*al-Mada'in*), on the banks of the Tigris, the Sasanian dynasty ruled a domain stretching from Mesopotamia to the borders of India. Its army ranked among the world's finest — equipped with heavy cavalry, war elephants, and a military tradition honed over centuries.^1 ^3

Yet beneath the grandeur, the empire was fracturing. Al-Tabari records in *Tarikh al-Rusul wa al-Muluk* that in the two decades preceding the Battle of Qadisiyyah, Persia had endured more than ten changes of sovereign — an unprecedented instability.^1 The protracted war with Byzantium (602-628 CE) had drained resources and sapped morale. The last stable ruler, Khosrau II, was killed in a coup, and the realm descended into a power struggle.

On the other side, a new force was rising from the deserts of Arabia. Muslim armies under Caliph Umar ibn al-Khattab had begun pushing into the borderlands of Iraq. Early clashes — such as the Battle of the Bridge (*Yawm al-Jisr*) in 634 CE, where the Muslims suffered defeat — demonstrated that conquering Persia would be no easy matter.^1 ^2

Caliph Umar decided to dispatch a major force under **Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas** — a senior companion numbered among the *al-'Asharah al-Mubashsharah* (ten companions promised Paradise) and a maternal cousin of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ. Al-Tabari records that Umar chose Sa'd for his combination of faith, military experience, and mature leadership.^1

## Events: Four Days That Shook an Empire

### Preparation and Diplomacy

Before battle commenced, a legendary diplomatic exchange took place. Both al-Tabari and al-Baladhuri recount that Sa'd sent several envoys to the camp of **Rustam Farrukhzad**, the Persian supreme commander.^1 ^2

The first envoy was **Rib'i ibn Amir**, a simple Bedouin. When he entered Rustam's tent — filled with silk carpets and golden cushions — Rib'i showed not the slightest awe. He drove his spear into the carpet, tearing it, then sat on the ground. When Rustam asked the purpose of their coming, Rib'i answered with words now famous in history:^1 ^2

*"Allah has sent us to deliver whomever He wills from the worship of servants to the worship of Allah, from the narrowness of this world to its vastness, and from the tyranny of religions to the justice of Islam."*

Rustam proposed three days of negotiation, hoping to avert battle. When diplomacy failed, both armies prepared for war.

### Day One: Yawm Armath (The Day of Anxiety)

The Battle of Qadisiyyah was fought on the plain of Qadisiyyah, near the city of Hira in Iraq, in Muharram 15 AH (636 CE). Al-Tabari records the Muslim force at approximately 30,000-36,000, while the Persian army under Rustam numbered between 60,000 and over 100,000 — though exact figures remain debated by historians.^1 ^3

The first day was marked by the charge of Persian war elephants, which terrified the Arab horses and sent them fleeing. Al-Tabari notes that the Muslim troops had never faced war elephants before, and the psychological impact was enormous.^1 Nevertheless, stubborn resistance by Muslim infantry held the line.

### Day Two: Yawm Aghwath (The Day of Succor)

On the second day, reinforcements from Syria — dispatched by Abu Ubaidah ibn al-Jarrah on Umar's orders — arrived on the battlefield. Al-Baladhuri records that the arrival of fresh troops greatly boosted Muslim morale.^2 On this day, Muslim fighters also discovered how to counter the elephants — they attacked their eyes and trunks with spears, causing the beasts to stampede and trample their own Persian handlers.^1

### Day Three: Yawm 'Imas (The Day of Resolve)

The third day was the bloodiest. Fighting raged without pause from morning through the night — highly unusual in Arab warfare. Al-Tabari calls that night *Laylat al-Harir* (the Night of Clamor), for the sound of battle resembled the unceasing growl of wild beasts.^1 ^3

Ibn al-Athir in *Al-Kamil fi al-Tarikh* describes how both armies fought in darkness with swords and spears, barely distinguishing friend from foe save for the cries of *takbir* rising from the Muslim ranks.^3

### Day Four: Yawm al-Qadisiyyah (The Day of Victory)

At dawn on the fourth day, a sandstorm blew from the west — striking the Persian forces full in the face. Al-Tabari notes that the wind gave the Muslims a critical advantage.^1 In the decisive final assault, Muslim forces broke through the Persian center.

**Rustam Farrukhzad** himself was killed — according to al-Tabari's account, he was found hiding behind a pack mule when a Muslim soldier named Hilal ibn Ullafah discovered and slew him.^1 The death of their supreme commander shattered Persian morale, and the army fled en masse. Thousands of Persian soldiers drowned while attempting to cross the Atiq Canal in panic.^1 ^3

## Key Sayings and Scriptural Evidence

Beyond the words of Rib'i ibn Amir already mentioned, al-Tabari records several important moments:

Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas — who was suffering from boils at the time and could not ride a horse — commanded the battle from a tower, motivating his troops by reciting the words of Allah:^1^4

> وَلَقَدْ كَتَبْنَا فِي الزَّبُورِ مِنْ بَعْدِ الذِّكْرِ أَنَّ الْأَرْضَ يَرِثُهَا عِبَادِيَ الصَّالِحُونَ ﴿١٠٥﴾
>
> *"And We have already written in the Psalms, after the Reminder, that the earth shall be inherited by My righteous servants. ﴾105﴿"*
> — QS. Al-Anbiya (21): 105

Al-Baladhuri also records Umar's message to Sa'd before his departure:^2

*"O Sa'd, do not be deceived by the fact that you are called the uncle of the Messenger of Allah. Verily, Allah does not erase evil with evil, but erases evil with good. There is no bond between Allah and anyone except through obedience. "*

## Analysis and Lessons: The Fall of a Superpower

### The Victory of Conviction over Material Might

Qadisiyyah stands as one of history's most dramatic illustrations of how conviction and morale can overcome material superiority. The Persian army had everything — superior numbers, better weapons, war elephants, and centuries of military tradition. The Muslim army brought faith, discipline, and a willingness to die in Allah's cause.

Al-Tabari notes that many Muslim fighters arrived at Qadisiyyah poorly equipped — some even had to share swords.^1 Yet what they possessed, and what the Persians lacked, was a purpose higher than plunder or loyalty to a king.

### Diplomacy Before the Sword

The fact that Sa'd sent multiple envoys before battle reflects a key principle of Islamic war ethics: war is a last resort. Every population approached by Muslim forces was offered three choices — embrace Islam, pay the *jizyah* and live in peace, or fight. This principle was far more humane than the prevailing warfare practices of the era.^1 ^2

### The Fall of an Empire — A Lesson in Hubris

The response of Rustam and the Persian nobility to the Muslim envoys — a mixture of contempt and astonishment at "poor Bedouins" daring to challenge the world's greatest empire — reflected the arrogance that planted the seeds of their own destruction. Ibn al-Athir notes that Persian nobles dismissed the Muslim army because of their humble appearance, failing to recognize that the strength of an army lies not in its finery but in its courage and purpose.^3

### Long-Term Historical Impact

Qadisiyyah was more than a military victory — it marked the beginning of a civilizational transformation. After Qadisiyyah, Ctesiphon fell, and all of Mesopotamia came under Islamic rule. Within decades, Islam spread across the entire former Sasanian domain, and Persian culture — far from being destroyed — was transformed and made immense contributions to Islamic civilization. Persian scholars, poets, and thinkers would become essential pillars of the *Islamic Golden Age*.^1 ^3
