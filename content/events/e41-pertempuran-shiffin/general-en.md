# The Battle of Siffin and the Arbitration — The First Fitnah (657 CE)

## Context: The Roots of the Dispute

The Battle of Siffin stands as one of the most painful events in early Islamic history — an armed conflict between two groups of Muslims, each led by companions of Prophet Muhammad ﷺ. Understanding this event requires great care, as the individuals involved are people whose nobility was affirmed by the Prophet ﷺ himself.

The roots of the dispute trace back to the assassination of Caliph Uthman ibn Affan in 656 CE. After **Ali ibn Abi Talib** was pledged allegiance as the fourth caliph, **Mu'awiyah ibn Abi Sufyan** — the governor of Syria appointed during Umar's reign and confirmed by Uthman — refused to give his pledge.^1 ^2

Al-Tabari in *Tarikh al-Rusul wa al-Muluk* records that Mu'awiyah put forward two demands: first, that Uthman's killers be handed over and brought to justice; second, that the selection of a new caliph be conducted through a broader *shura*.^1 From Mu'awiyah's perspective, he had the right to demand justice for Uthman's death, as Uthman was his kinsman from the Banu Umayyah.

From Ali's perspective, he was the legitimate caliph who had received the pledge of allegiance from the majority of companions in Madinah. In Ali's view, punishing Uthman's killers required stability first — something impossible to achieve while Mu'awiyah refused to recognize his authority.^1 ^2 Nasr ibn Muzahim in *Waq'at Siffin* records that Ali sent several envoys to Syria to negotiate, but diplomatic efforts reached a deadlock.^3

It is important to note that classical Muslim historians — including al-Tabari, Ibn al-Athir, and Nasr ibn Muzahim — present the perspectives of both sides and avoid black-and-white simplification. The dispute is understood as a difference of *ijtihad* (independent reasoning) among the companions, not as an absolute conflict between right and wrong.^1 ^2 ^3

## The Event: The Battle of Siffin

### Troop Movements

After diplomatic efforts failed, both armies moved toward the plain of **Siffin**, located on the western bank of the Euphrates River (modern-day Syria). Al-Tabari records that Ali's forces departed from Kufa while Mu'awiyah's forces advanced from Damascus. Both armies arrived at Siffin around Dhul Hijjah 36 AH (May-June 657 CE).^1

Ibn al-Athir in *Al-Kamil fi al-Tarikh* estimates that Ali's army numbered approximately 50,000-70,000, while Mu'awiyah's numbered around 60,000-80,000 — making this one of the largest military deployments in early Islamic history.^2

### The Water Dispute

Nasr ibn Muzahim records that the first incident occurred before the battle proper.^3 Mu'awiyah's forces arrived first and seized control of access to the Euphrates, then barred Ali's forces from taking water. Ali sent an envoy who said:

*"We have not come to fight over water. Let us take water, and then we can discuss what divides us."*^3

When this request was refused, Ali's forces launched a limited attack and successfully secured access to the river. After gaining control of the water source, Ali opened it to both sides — an attitude that historians contrast with Mu'awiyah's camp's earlier action.^1 ^3

### The Negotiation Phase

Both al-Tabari and Nasr ibn Muzahim emphasize that the two sides spent weeks negotiating before actual combat began.^1 ^3 The month of Muharram 37 AH was observed as a sacred month during which fighting was suspended, and both sides exchanged envoys.

Several exchanges of letters between Ali and Mu'awiyah are preserved in historical sources. Ali wrote to Mu'awiyah reminding him of the obligation of allegiance and communal unity.^1 Mu'awiyah replied reaffirming his demand for justice for Uthman.

### The Battle

The Battle of Siffin was fought intermittently over several days in Safar 37 AH (July 657 CE).^1 ^2 ^3 The two sides faced each other in formation, and combat took the form of individual duels and flank skirmishes — not a single mass engagement. Historians note that both sides sought to minimize the shedding of Muslim blood.

Nasr ibn Muzahim describes the emotional atmosphere in both camps — soldiers reluctant to raise arms against fellow Muslims, calls to return to negotiations, and prayers offered by both sides seeking God's guidance.^3

The most intense fighting occurred on the night known as ***Laylat al-Harir*** (the Night of Clamor), when battle raged throughout the entire night. Al-Tabari records that on this night, both armies fought so fiercely that the sounds of battle cries and clashing swords echoed without cease.^1

### The Raising of the Mushafs

At the moment Ali's forces began gaining a tactical advantage, **Amr ibn al-As** — Mu'awiyah's chief advisor — proposed a strategy that would alter the course of history: the Syrian soldiers raised copies of the Quran on the tips of their spears, calling out:

*"The Book of God between us and you!"*^1 ^2 ^3

This call was an invitation to settle the dispute according to Quranic law rather than the sword. The action caused a split within Ali's own forces. The majority of Ali's soldiers refused to continue fighting against the Quran raised high. They pressured Ali to accept the call.

Al-Tabari relates that Ali was initially reluctant to accept, judging this to be a tactic rather than a sincere gesture.^1 However, the pressure from his own troops was so intense that he ultimately agreed to a ceasefire and acceptance of the principle of arbitration (*tahkim*).

## The Event: The Arbitration (Tahkim)

### The Arbitration Agreement

Both sides agreed to appoint one arbiter each to resolve the dispute based on the Quran and the Sunnah:^1 ^2 ^3

- Ali's side was represented by **Abu Musa al-Ash'ari**, chosen at the insistence of some of Ali's troops — though Ali himself preferred Abdullah ibn Abbas or Malik al-Ashtar.
- Mu'awiyah's side was represented by **Amr ibn al-As**, a shrewd statesman and diplomat.

A written agreement (*sahifah*) was signed, stipulating that both arbiters would meet at **Adhruh** (or according to other accounts, **Dumat al-Jandal**) within six months to a year to announce their decision.

### The Outcome

The details of the arbitration's outcome vary across historical sources, and there is controversy about what actually happened. In general, historians record:^1 ^2 ^3

According to one popular account in classical sources, Abu Musa and Amr agreed to depose both leaders (Ali and Mu'awiyah) from their claims and refer the matter to a *shura*. Abu Musa announced this decision, but Amr then declared that he had only agreed to depose Ali and confirmed Mu'awiyah — an act viewed by Ali's side as a betrayal of the agreement.

However, this version is debated by some modern historians who consider the details may have been embellished by partisan narratives. What is more certain is that the arbitration failed to produce a resolution accepted by both sides, and the conflict continued.

### The Consequence: The Birth of the Khawarij

The most immediate consequence of the arbitration was a schism within Ali's own camp. The group that came to be known as the **Khawarij** broke away, considering that accepting human arbitration in a matter that should be decided by God was a grave sin. Their slogan: *"La hukma illa lillah"* (There is no judgment except God's).^1 ^2

Thus, from one conflict emerged three factions: supporters of Ali, supporters of Mu'awiyah, and the Khawarij — a fracture whose effects were felt for generations to come.

## Key Texts and Evidence

The events at Siffin involved companions of the Prophet ﷺ on both sides. Scholars of Ahl al-Sunnah wa al-Jama'ah approach this dispute with an important principle:

Imam Ahmad ibn Hanbal and the majority of Sunni scholars uphold the principle of **refraining from criticizing the companions of the Prophet** (*al-imsak 'amma shajara bayn al-sahabah*). They hold that Ali was on the side of right (*muhiqq*) as the legitimate caliph, while Mu'awiyah exercised an erroneous *ijtihad* (*mukhti'*) — but as a *mujtahid*, he receives one reward for his effort even though the result was wrong.

The Prophet Muhammad ﷺ himself mentioned both Ali and Mu'awiyah in contexts that indicate the standing of each. Regarding Ali, he said:

> مَنْ كُنْتُ مَوْلَاهُ فَعَلِيٌّ مَوْلَاهُ
>
> *"Whoever takes me as his protector, then Ali is also his protector."*
> — Musnad Ahmad; al-Tirmidhi, no. 3713

Regarding Mu'awiyah, there are narrations that the Prophet ﷺ prayed for him. Scholars affirm that both parties were Muslims exercising *ijtihad*, and Muslims should pray for the good of them all.

## Analysis and Lessons

### Historical Complexity

The Battle of Siffin reminds us that history is rarely black and white. Both sides held their own arguments and motivations. Al-Tabari, Ibn al-Athir, and Nasr ibn Muzahim — despite their respective inclinations — all present the voices of both sides and allow readers to draw their own conclusions.^1 ^2 ^3

### The Danger of Fitnah

Siffin stands as living proof of Prophet Muhammad's ﷺ warnings about the danger of *fitnah*. Once *fitnah* begins, even the best of people can become trapped in a spiral of conflict that is difficult to halt. The primary lesson: preventing *fitnah* is far easier than stopping it once it has ignited.

### The Principle of Consultation and Peace

Although it ended with unsatisfactory results, the attempt at *tahkim* demonstrates that the principle of peaceful conflict resolution was embedded in Islamic tradition. Both sides, in principle, preferred arbitration over prolonged bloodshed — a principle that remains relevant in modern conflict resolution.

### Honoring the Companions

Muslim scholars throughout history have emphasized the importance of honoring all companions involved in this event. Their disagreement is understood as a humanly fallible difference of *ijtihad*, not a basis for hating or condemning either side. This attitude is part of the creed of Ahl al-Sunnah wa al-Jama'ah.
