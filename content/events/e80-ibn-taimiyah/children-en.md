# Ibn Taimiyah — Islamic Reformer in the Era of Crisis

## 🧑 Who Was He?

Ibn Taimiyah (1263-1328 CE) was a great scholar who lived when the Islamic world was facing a major crisis. He was born in Harran, Anatolia, when the Mongols destroyed Baghdad and ended the Abbasid Caliphate. His family was forced to flee to Damascus when the Mongols attacked their city.

He grew up in a learned Hanbali scholar family. His father was a famous preacher and teacher in Damascus. What made Ibn Taimiyah special was the breadth of his knowledge - he not only mastered religious sciences but also Greek philosophy, logic, and even Christian and Jewish theology to be able to criticize them accurately.

## ⭐ What Did He Do?

Ibn Taimiyah developed an Islamic reform movement that returned to the Quran and Sunnah according to the understanding of early generations (*salaf al-salih*). He criticized various practices he considered *bid'ah* (innovation in religion) that had spread widely in society, such as excessive maulid celebrations, grave visitations in deviant ways, and extreme Sufi practices.

He was also active in jihad against Mongols and Crusaders. When Mongols attacked Syria, Ibn Taimiyah issued a controversial fatwa: Mongols who had embraced Islam but still used Mongol law (not shariah) must still be fought. He even directly participated in military expeditions against Crusaders on Arwad Island.

Remarkably, Ibn Taimiyah wrote more than 500 works in various fields: exegesis, hadith, jurisprudence, theology, philosophy, and politics. His work "*Majmu' al-Fatawa*" consists of 37 volumes and became a comprehensive encyclopedia of Islamic thought.

## 💡 Why Was He Extraordinary?

Ibn Taimiyah was extraordinary because of his courage in conveying truth even when unpopular. He criticized practices already widely accepted by society but not according to shariah, such as excessive grave visitation including to Prophet Muhammad's ﷺ grave for special purposes. This put him in conflict with rulers and establishment scholars.

Throughout his life, he was imprisoned several times in Damascus, Cairo, and Alexandria for his controversial views. However, even in prison he continued writing and teaching. His students secretly visited him to study and copy his writings.

What made him different was his methodology that combined textual evidence (*naql*) with reason (*aql*), while still placing the Quran and Sunnah as the highest authority. He showed that scholars should not be passive but must actively face contemporary challenges. His influence was enormous on Islamic reform movements to this day.

## 📖 Verses

Allah SWT says:

> يَا أَيُّهَا الَّذِينَ آمَنُوا كُونُوا قَوَّامِينَ بِالْقِسْطِ شُهَدَاءَ لِلَّهِ ﴿١٣٥﴾
>
> *"O you who believe! Stand out firmly for justice, as witnesses to Allah."* ﴾135﴿
> — QS. An-Nisa' (4): 135

Ibn Taimiyah exemplified the meaning of this verse through consistency in upholding truth despite facing various pressures. He died on September 26, 1328 CE in prison, but his funeral was attended by tens of thousands from all over Syria - proof that despite being controversial, he was still respected as a great scholar.

The story of Ibn Taimiyah teaches us the importance of returning to the original sources of Islam (Quran and Sunnah), balance between revelation and reason, intellectual activism that does not isolate from community problems, and courage in conveying truth even when unpopular. Reform in Islam must be continuous with the correct methodological framework.
