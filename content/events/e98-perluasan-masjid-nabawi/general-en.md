# Expansion of Prophet's Mosque & Madinah as Center of Knowledge

## Historical Context

After the death of **Prophet Muhammad** ﷺ in 632 CE, the Prophet's Mosque functioned not only as a place of worship, but had evolved into a center of learning and administration for the *Khilafah Rashidun*^1. Under the leadership of **Abu Bakr As-Siddiq** (632-634 CE) and especially **Umar ibn al-Khattab** (634-644 CE), the need for physical expansion of the mosque became urgent due to rapid growth of the Muslim community and increasingly intensive scholarly activities^2.

Madinah in this era had become a destination for knowledge seekers from various corners of *Dar al-Islam*. The *tabi'in* (generation after the companions) and delegations from newly conquered territories came to study the Quran, hadith, *fiqh*, and various developing disciplines^3. **Umar ibn al-Khattab**, with his strategic vision, realized that the physical infrastructure of the Prophet's Mosque had to be adjusted to its new function as the first *Baitul Hikmah* in Islamic civilization.

In 634 CE, right after **Umar** became the second caliph, he decided to undertake a major expansion of the Prophet's Mosque^1. This decision was not merely to accommodate the increasing number of worshippers, but more for transforming the mosque into a more organized educational complex and center of *khilafah* administration.

## The Umar Era Expansion Event

### Motivation and Planning

**Umar ibn al-Khattab** had a clear vision about the role of the Prophet's Mosque in the Islamic knowledge ecosystem^2. He observed that senior companions like **Ibn Abbas**, **Abu Hurairah**, **Anas ibn Malik**, and **Abdullah ibn Umar** began to have permanent *halaqah* (study circles) with steadily increasing numbers of students^3.

Additionally, the complexity of the increasingly extensive *khilafah* administration required special spaces for receiving delegations, storing state documents, and conducting *majlis syura*^1. **Umar** also anticipated that Madinah would become the primary reference for standardizing religious practices throughout the continuously expanding Islamic territories.

The planning process involved intensive consultation with senior companions, local architects, and construction experts from Persia and Byzantium who had embraced Islam^2. **Umar** emphasized that the expansion must maintain the simplicity exemplified by the Prophet ﷺ while accommodating evolving functional needs.

### Construction Process and Innovation

The expansion began with purchasing land around the mosque from its owners at fair prices, following the precedent set by the Prophet ﷺ when building Quba Mosque^3. The mosque area was expanded from approximately 2,500 square meters to nearly 3,575 square meters — an increase of about 40%.

**Architectural innovations** introduced in this expansion included:
- ***Maqsurah***: A special area for the caliph and high officials, which also functioned as a *majlis syura* room^1
- ***Baitul Mal***: A special storage room for *baitul mal* (state treasury) connected to the mosque^2
- ***Maktabah***: A primitive library room for storing Quranic manuscripts and hadith records^3
- **Multi-level porticos**: The *Suffah* was expanded with multi-level structures to accommodate more *halaqah* simultaneously^1

The materials used showed technological advancement: besides traditional stone and palm wood, marble from local quarries and construction techniques adopted from Persian and Byzantine architecture were also used^2.

### Organization of Learning Spaces

The most revolutionary aspect of this expansion was the systematization of space based on academic disciplines^3. **Umar ibn al-Khattab** designated specific areas:
- **Quran Zone**: On the eastern side of the mosque, led by **Ubay ibn Ka'ab** and **Zaid ibn Thabit**^1
- **Hadith Zone**: On the western side, led by **Abu Hurairah** and **Anas ibn Malik**^2
- **Fiqh Zone**: In the main portico, led by **Ibn Abbas** and **Abdullah ibn Umar**^3
- **Language Zone**: In the southern corner, for Arabic language learning for *mualaf* from Persia and Byzantium^1

Each zone was equipped with simple blackboards (wooden tablets) and shelves for storing learning materials. This system became the prototype for madrasas that developed in later periods^2.

## Quranic Dialogue and Evidence

**Umar ibn al-Khattab's** vision of the mosque as a center of knowledge aligned with Quranic teachings:^4

> وَقُل رَّبِّ زِدْنِي عِلْمًا ﴿١١٤﴾
>
> *"And say: 'My Lord, increase me in knowledge.'"*
> — QS. Thaha (20): 114

This verse became the motto displayed above the entrance of each learning zone in the Prophet's Mosque expansion^3^4.

**Ibn Abbas** narrated that **Umar ibn al-Khattab** once said:

> تَعَلَّمُوا الْعِلْمَ وَعَلِّمُوهُ النَّاسَ، وَتَعَلَّمُوا لِلْعِلْمِ السَّكِينَةَ وَالْوَقَارَ، وَتَوَاضَعُوا لِمَنْ تَعَلَّمُونَ مِنْهُ
>
> *"Learn knowledge and teach it to people. Learn for knowledge tranquility and dignity, and be humble to those from whom you learn."*
> — HR. Ath-Thabrani, no. 8792, Al-Mu'jam al-Kabir

This statement was delivered by **Umar** when inaugurating the learning zones in the Prophet's Mosque, emphasizing that the mosque's function was not only for pure worship but also for intellectual transformation of the *ummah*^1.

**Abu Hurairah** recorded in his narration:

> كَانَ عُمَرُ يَقُولُ: إِنَّ هَذَا الْمَسْجِدَ لَمْ يُبْنَ لِلصَّلاةِ فَقَطْ، بَلْ لِيَكُونَ مَنَارَةَ الْعِلْمِ لِلْعَالَمِينَ
>
> *"Umar used to say: 'This mosque was not built for prayer only, but to be a beacon of knowledge for all worlds.'"*
> — HR. Ibn Sa'd, Tabaqat al-Kubra, Vol. 3

This statement reflected **Umar's** global vision about Madinah's role in Islamic civilization^2.

## Analysis and Wisdom

### Institutional Transformation

The expansion of the Prophet's Mosque during **Umar's** era marked a fundamental transformation from a place of worship to an integrated educational institution^3. This model was later adopted in various Islamic cities: Kufah, Basrah, Fustat, and Damascus. Every congregational mosque in these cities was equipped with learning zones following the pattern established in Madinah^1.

The systematization by academic disciplines introduced by **Umar** became the precursor to the faculty system in later Islamic universities^2. The separation of Quran, hadith, fiqh, and language zones reflected mature epistemological awareness about specialization in knowledge development.

### Learning Methodology

The structured *halaqah* based on these zones enabled the creation of more systematic learning methodologies^3. Each *sheikh* was responsible for curriculum, student progression, and standardization of materials in their field. This system produced a generation of *tabi'in* who were competent and consistent in transmitting knowledge^1.

The presence of *maktabah* (library) within the mosque complex was also revolutionary. This was the first time in Islamic history that written texts were systematically stored and accessible to learners^2. **Zaid ibn Thabit** was appointed as *amin maktabah* (head librarian) responsible for preservation and cataloging of manuscripts and hadith records.

### Geopolitical Implications

**Umar's** decision to make Madinah a center of knowledge had strategic geopolitical implications^3. Amid the rapid expansion of *Dar al-Islam* covering Persia, Syria, and parts of Byzantium, the need for standardizing religious practices became crucial for maintaining *ummah* unity^1.

Every regional governor (*wali*) was required to send annual delegations to Madinah for "updating" religious knowledge and practices. This system ensured that innovations and *ijtihad* developing at the center could be disseminated throughout territories, while gathering feedback from the regions^2.

### Intellectual Legacy

The model of the Prophet's Mosque post-expansion became a template for Islamic educational institutions in later periods: Dar al-Hikmah in Baghdad, Al-Azhar in Cairo, and Bait al-Hikmah in Cordoba^3. The concept of integration between worship, learning, and administration introduced by **Umar** proved effective and sustainable.

Most importantly, this expansion affirmed that mosques in Islamic civilization were not merely places of ritual, but centers of continuous socio-intellectual transformation^1. This principle later became the DNA of every authentic Islamic educational institution: combining spiritual, intellectual, and social dimensions in one integrated ecosystem^2.
