# Amir Timur / Tamerlane — The Last Great Conqueror of Central Asia

## 🧑 Who Was He?

Timur ibn Taraghay Barlas (1336-1405 CE), known in the West as Tamerlane, was the last great conqueror from Central Asia and one of the most controversial figures in Islamic history. He was born in Shahrisabz near Samarkand from the Barlas tribe, one of the Mongol-Turkic tribes that had embraced Islam but still maintained nomadic traditions and Central Asian steppe militarism.

Timur had a disability in his right leg that made him lame, earning him the nickname "Timur-i Lang" (Timur the Lame). But rather than weakening him, this disability actually strengthened his determination to prove himself as an undefeated warrior. He grew up in a bilingual environment (Turkic and Persian), exposed to classical Persian literature but also trained in nomadic horsemanship and warfare.

## ⭐ What Did He Do?

Timur consolidated power in Transoxiana in 1370 CE after defeating his last rival. Uniquely, he didn't claim the Khan title because he wasn't a descendant of Genghis Khan. Instead, he used the title "Amir" and made puppet khans serve as formal legitimacy while holding real power.

Timur conducted major military campaigns across Eurasia: conquering Persia (1381-1387 CE), invading India and destroying Delhi (1398 CE), even defeating Ottoman Sultan Bayezid I at the Battle of Ankara (1402 CE). His strategy was brilliant - combining terror and mercy as psychological warfare. Cities that surrendered were treated well, those that resisted were completely destroyed as examples.

Paradoxically, despite being known for destruction, Timur was also a great patron of arts and learning. Samarkand under his rule became one of the most beautiful cities in the world with monumental architecture combining Mongol grandeur with Islamic aesthetics. The Registan complex that still stands today is proof of his architectural vision.

## 💡 Why Was He Extraordinary?

Timur was extraordinary because he succeeded in building the last greatest empire from Central Asian nomadic tradition within an Islamic framework. He combined Mongol mobility with Islamic discipline, creating a military system unmatched in his time. His army consisted of a core of personally loyal Turko-Mongol cavalry, plus infantry and specialists from various ethnicities.

What made him unique was his ability to combine destruction with construction. Besides conquering, he also built - Isfahan which he destroyed was later rebuilt as a center of learning. He gathered scholars, artists, and craftsmen from all territories, making Samarkand a repository for manuscripts from Baghdad, Damascus, and other centers of learning.

His legacy is proven through his descendants: Ulugh Beg (grandson) became the greatest astronomer in history, and Babur founded the Mughal Empire in India that lasted until British colonization. Timur showed that even destructive conquests could lead to positive cultural developments in the long term.

## 📖 Scripture

Allah SWT says:

> وَتِلْكَ الْأَيَّامُ نُدَاوِلُهَا بَيْنَ النَّاسِ ﴿١٤٠﴾
>
> *"And these days [of varying conditions] We alternate among the people."* ﴾140﴿
> — QS. Ali Imran (3): 140

This verse reflects the cyclical nature of power exemplified by the rise and fall of the Timurid Empire. Timur showed that worldly power is temporary, rotating from one hand to another according to Allah's will.

The story of Timur teaches us the complexity of historical figures - no historical figure is purely good or evil. He also proved the impact of individual leadership that can dramatically change the course of history. Most importantly, even in destruction there can be constructive wisdom if intended to build a better civilization.
