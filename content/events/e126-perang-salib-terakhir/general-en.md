# The Eighth & Ninth Crusades — The Final Expeditions

## Background

By the mid-13th century CE, Crusader power in the Middle East had entered its terminal phase. Sultan Baibars' systematic attacks had eroded almost all Crusader territories in the Levant. However, the spirit of holy war in Europe had not completely died out. **King Louis IX of France**, who had failed in the Seventh Crusade (1248-1254 CE), was determined to launch a final expedition to save the remnants of Christian presence in the Holy Land.^1

The geopolitical situation had changed drastically since the First Crusade. Mamluk power under Sultan Baibars' leadership was growing stronger, while support from Europe was weakening. Pope Clement IV, who called for the Eighth Crusade in 1267 CE, faced a cold response from European kings who were beginning to focus on domestic politics.^2

## The Eighth Crusade - Expedition to Tunis (1270 CE)

### Planning and Motivation

Louis IX had a personal obsession to redeem his failure in Egypt two decades earlier. However, instead of attacking the Holy Land directly, he decided on a controversial strategy: attacking Tunis first in hopes of converting Emir al-Mustansir and gaining an operational base in North Africa.^3

This decision faced strong opposition from his brother, **Charles of Anjou** who became King of Sicily, but had economic interests in Tunisia. Some historians argue that Charles manipulated Louis to protect his trading interests in the Mediterranean.^1

### A Tragically Ended Expedition

In July 1270 CE, the French fleet consisting of about 10,000 soldiers landed at Carthage, near Tunis. However, instead of facing military resistance, they faced a far deadlier enemy: dysentery and typhus plagues that spread rapidly among the troops due to poor sanitation and hot African weather.^4

Within weeks, the Crusader army lay sick. Louis IX himself fell ill and died on August 25, 1270 CE, just one month after landing. The death of the king dubbed "Saint Louis" marked the end of the Eighth Crusade before serious fighting even began.^2

## The Ninth Crusade - Prince Edward's Expedition (1271-1272 CE)

### Prince Edward's Arrival

After Louis IX's death, **Prince Edward of England** (later Edward I) decided to continue the expedition by bringing about 1,000 soldiers to Acre in May 1271 CE. Edward, who was only 32 years old, had different motivations: he wanted to prove himself as a brave Christian leader while strengthening his political position in England.^3

Edward received a warm welcome from the remaining Crusaders in Acre, but he quickly realized the situation was nearly hopeless. Mamluk forces under Sultan Baibars had controlled almost the entire interior, while the Crusaders only controlled a few coastal cities.

### Limited Campaign

Edward launched several small attacks against Mamluk outposts, including attacks on Qaqun and several villages around Nazareth. However, these operations were more symbolic than strategic. Baibars wisely did not respond to these minor provocations, knowing that Edward lacked the strength to seriously threaten Mamluk positions.^4

Most dramatic was the assassination attempt against Edward by an assassin in June 1272 CE. Edward survived the attack, but his wounds were quite serious. Edward's wife, Eleanor of Castile, allegedly sucked poison from her husband's wound - a story that later became a romantic legend in Europe though its truth is questioned by historians.^1

## Diplomacy and Truce

Realizing his military incapacity, Edward turned to diplomacy. He negotiated directly with Sultan Baibars and successfully achieved a truce for 10 years, 10 months, 10 days, and 10 hours in September 1272 CE - a strange formula reflecting medieval diplomatic traditions.^2

This agreement essentially maintained the status quo: Crusaders continued to control several coastal cities (mainly Acre, Tyre, and Tripoli) while Mamluks controlled the interior. However, all parties knew this was merely a postponed execution, not a permanent solution.

## Analysis of Failure

The Eighth and Ninth Crusades failed due to several structural factors:

**Geopolitical changes**: Europe no longer saw the Holy Land as a top priority. Internal crises, inter-kingdom conflicts, and domestic economic growth became the main focus.^3 **Strengthening Muslim power**: Mamluks had developed into a superior military force with solid organization and competent leadership. **Logistics and health**: Long-distance expeditions without adequate medical preparation proved fatal, as experienced by Louis IX.

More fundamentally, the religious spirit that motivated the First Crusade had been replaced by political and economic pragmatism. European kings were more interested in maintaining domestic power than adventuring in the Middle East.^4

## Impact and Legacy

The failure of the Eighth and Ninth Crusades marked the end of the era of large-scale holy wars between Christians and Muslims. The Edward-Baibars truce gave breathing room to the remaining Crusaders, but also gave time for Mamluks to prepare their final assault.

Louis IX's death in Tunis led to his canonization by the Catholic Church in 1297 CE, making him the only French king to become a saint. Ironically, his military failure actually enhanced his spiritual status.^1

## Wisdom and Lessons

These final expeditions taught several important lessons:

**Sincere motivation vs political interests**: Louis IX was driven by sincere faith, but his strategic decisions were influenced by his brother's political interests. **Realism vs idealism**: Edward showed realism by negotiating when he realized his limitations, while Louis was trapped in unrealistic idealism.

The Eighth and Ninth Crusades marked the end of the Crusading spirit in Europe. Repeated failures proved that violence is never a permanent solution to civilizational conflicts — a lesson that should have been learned from the very beginning.
