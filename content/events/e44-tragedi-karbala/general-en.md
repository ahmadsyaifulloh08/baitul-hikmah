# The Tragedy of Karbala — The Martyrdom of Husayn ibn Ali (680 CE)

## Historical Context: Yazid's Succession and Opposition in the Hijaz

In the year 60 AH (680 CE), **Yazid ibn Muawiyah** ascended to the throne as the second caliph of the Umayyad Dynasty following the death of his father, Muawiyah ibn Abu Sufyan. Yazid's appointment as crown prince had already provoked opposition from several prominent figures, and after Muawiyah's death, this opposition intensified.¹

Among those who refused to pledge allegiance to Yazid were **Husayn ibn Ali** — the grandson of Prophet Muhammad ﷺ through his daughter Fatimah — and **Abdullah ibn al-Zubayr**. Husayn left Madinah for Makkah to avoid pressure from the governor of Madinah, who demanded his pledge.¹ ²

Meanwhile, the people of **Kufa** in Iraq — who had been a base of support for Ali ibn Abi Talib — sent letters to Husayn, inviting him to come to Kufa and promising to support him as caliph. Al-Tabari records that the number of letters Husayn received reached into the thousands.¹ Husayn sent his cousin, **Muslim ibn Aqil**, to Kufa as an envoy to verify these claims of support.

Muslim ibn Aqil initially received a warm reception in Kufa, with thousands pledging allegiance. However, when Yazid appointed **Ubaydullah ibn Ziyad** as the new governor of Kufa, the situation changed drastically. Ibn Ziyad employed intimidation and threats that caused the people of Kufa to withdraw their support. Muslim ibn Aqil was eventually captured and executed.¹ ³

## The Events: The Journey to Karbala

### Departure from Makkah

Husayn departed from Makkah toward Kufa on 8 Dhul Hijjah 60 AH with a caravan of approximately 70 family members and loyal supporters — including women and children.¹ Several senior companions tried to dissuade him. Ibn Kathir records that **Abdullah ibn Abbas** advised Husayn not to go to Kufa, warning that its people could not be trusted given their track record of having betrayed his father, Ali.²

Nevertheless, Husayn remained resolute. Historians note various motivations behind his decision: a moral obligation to uphold truth, the promise of support from the people of Kufa, and the conviction that silence would mean accepting injustice.

### The Siege at Karbala (2 Muharram 61 AH)

En route, Husayn received news of Muslim ibn Aqil's death and the changed situation in Kufa. Before he could decide his next course, his caravan was intercepted by a force of one thousand soldiers under **al-Hurr ibn Yazid al-Tamimi**, dispatched by Ibn Ziyad. Al-Hurr was not ordered to attack but to prevent Husayn from continuing to Kufa or turning back.¹

Husayn's caravan was ultimately directed to an arid plain called **Karbala**, near the banks of the Euphrates River. On 2 Muharram 61 AH, they pitched camp there. Soon after, a far larger force under **Umar ibn Sa'd** — numbering around 4,000 according to some sources, and more according to others — arrived to besiege Husayn's party.¹ ³

Ibn Ziyad issued an ultimatum through Umar ibn Sa'd: Husayn must pledge allegiance to Yazid or face the consequences. Husayn offered several options: to be allowed to return to where he came from, to go to a frontier to fight in jihad, or to be taken directly to Yazid in Damascus. Sources differ on the details of these negotiations and which options were proposed.¹ ² Ibn Ziyad rejected all options except unconditional allegiance.

### The Cutting Off of Water

One of the most grievous aspects of the siege was the cutting off of Husayn's camp from the Euphrates River. Al-Tabari and Abu Mikhnaf recount that although Husayn's camp was near the river, Umayyad forces blocked their access to water — including for women and children.¹ ³ This condition persisted for several days leading up to the final battle.

### The Day of Ashura (10 Muharram 61 AH / 10 October 680 CE)

On the morning of 10 Muharram, after failed negotiations, a profoundly unequal battle commenced. Husayn's party of approximately 72 faced an army of thousands.¹

Al-Tabari provides a detailed account of the battle: Husayn's supporters advanced one by one or in small groups, fighting valiantly despite knowing they would fall. Among the martyred were Husayn's brothers, nephews, and his own sons.¹ ³

In the final phase of the battle, Husayn ibn Ali — the grandson of Prophet Muhammad ﷺ — himself entered the field. Severely wounded, parched with thirst, and surrounded by enemies, he ultimately fell as a martyr. Ibn Kathir in *Al-Bidayah wa al-Nihayah* records that Husayn sustained dozens of wounds before finally falling.²

After the battle, Husayn's head was severed and sent to Ibn Ziyad in Kufa, then to Yazid in Damascus. The women and children of Husayn's caravan — including his sister **Zaynab bint Ali** — were taken captive and brought first to Kufa and then to Damascus.¹ ²

## Reactions and Impact

### Reactions to the Tragedy

The events at Karbala sent shockwaves of grief and anger across the Muslim world. Al-Tabari records that even Yazid himself, when Husayn's head was brought before him, expressed regret and said: *"May Allah curse Ibn Ziyad! Had Husayn come to me, I would have pardoned him."* However, the authenticity and sincerity of this statement is debated by historians.¹ ²

This wave of outrage contributed to several major uprisings against the Umayyad Dynasty, including the *Tawwabun* (Penitents) movement in 684 CE and the revolt of al-Mukhtar in 685–687 CE.

### Long-Term Impact

The Tragedy of Karbala became one of the most consequential events in Islamic history:

- **Sectarian division** — The event deepened the rift between the groups later known as Sunni and Shia, though the roots of the division predated it.
- **Symbol of resistance** — Husayn became a universal symbol of resistance against tyranny in Islamic tradition.
- **The Ashura commemoration** — The 10th of Muharram is observed in different ways by various Muslim communities to this day.

## Scriptural Context

Prophet Muhammad ﷺ expressed the special status of Husayn in several hadiths:

> حُسَيْنٌ مِنِّي وَأَنَا مِنْ حُسَيْنٍ، أَحَبَّ اللَّهُ مَنْ أَحَبَّ حُسَيْنًا
>
> *"Husayn is from me and I am from Husayn. Allah loves whoever loves Husayn."*
> — Narrated by al-Tirmidhi, no. 3775

## Analysis and Lessons

### Methodological Note

The events of Karbala are among the most sensitive topics in Islamic history. Different Islamic traditions have varying perspectives and emphases in narrating these events. In this article, we have endeavored to present the facts agreed upon by the majority of classical primary sources — especially al-Tabari, Ibn Kathir, and Abu Mikhnaf — while noting differences in accounts where relevant.

### Moral Courage

Husayn's decision to uphold his principles despite facing overwhelmingly superior forces has inspired Muslims across all schools of thought for centuries. Regardless of differing theological interpretations, nearly all Islamic traditions agree that Husayn was on the side of truth.

### The Danger of Political Absolutism

The Tragedy of Karbala demonstrates what can happen when political power lacks adequate mechanisms of accountability. Ibn Ziyad's decision to resort to violence against the Prophet's own family reflects the erosion of moral values in politics.

### Lessons on Loyalty

This event also teaches about the price of loyalty. Husayn's supporters who remained with him until the end — knowing they would fall — demonstrated extraordinary devotion. Conversely, the retreat of the people of Kufa from their promises illustrates the danger of political opportunism.
