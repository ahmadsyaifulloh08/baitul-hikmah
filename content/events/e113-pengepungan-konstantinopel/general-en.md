# The Second Siege of Constantinople (717-718 CE)

## Strategic Context of the Umayyad Caliphate

In the early 8th century CE, the Umayyad Caliphate under **Caliph Sulayman ibn Abd al-Malik** (715-717 CE) was at the height of its power. Islamic expansion had reached Andalusia in the west and Central Asia in the east. Constantinople, the capital of the Byzantine Empire, became a very important strategic target due to its position connecting Europe and Asia.^1

This city had experienced the first siege by Muslim forces in 674-678 CE under **Muawiyah ibn Abi Sufyan**, but managed to survive. That failure provided valuable lessons for Umayyad leadership about the complexity of conquering the "City Protected by God" (*al-Madinah al-Mahrusah*) as Byzantium called it.^2

## Comprehensive Military Preparation

**Caliph Umar ibn Abd al-Aziz** (717-720 CE) who replaced Sulayman inherited the grand plan to conquer Constantinople. Military preparations began with mobilizing the largest naval fleet in Islamic history up to that time. More than 1,800 warships were prepared with logistical support from throughout the caliphate territories.^3

Command of the expedition was given to **Maslama ibn Abd al-Malik**, brother of Caliph Umar ibn Abd al-Aziz, who was an experienced general with an excellent strategic reputation. He mobilized land forces of about 120,000 soldiers consisting of Arab, Berber troops, and contingents from various caliphate regions.^4

The strategy developed was a double siege: naval blockade to cut off Byzantine supply routes and land siege to prevent assistance from mainland Europe. The Islamic fleet under the command of **Sulayman ibn Mu'adh** was positioned to control the Bosphorus Strait and Golden Horn.^5

## Political Conditions of Byzantium

The Byzantine Empire at that time was in an unstable political situation. **Emperor Theodosius III** had just experienced a coup in 715 CE and was replaced by **Leo III the Isaurian** (717-741 CE). This leadership transition created uncertainty in the coordination of Constantinople's defense.^6

However, Leo III proved to be a tough leader with military experience. He immediately consolidated his forces and prepared the city's defense with a comprehensive strategy. Constantinople's famous defense system with the "Theodosian Walls" was strengthened and repaired to face the siege.^7

## The Course of the Siege

Muslim forces began the siege in summer 717 CE by landing on the Anatolian peninsula and building siege lines around Constantinople. Maslama ibn Abd al-Malik successfully cut off land supply routes from Europe and built strong siege fortresses.^8

The Islamic fleet successfully entered the Golden Horn and blockaded Constantinople's main harbor. However, Byzantium had an advantage in the form of "Greek Fire," a secret oil-based weapon that could burn on water and was very effective against wooden ships.^1

The siege lasted through the harsh winter of 717-718 CE. Muslim forces faced heavy logistical difficulties due to long supply routes and extreme weather. Many soldiers died not from battle, but from cold and food shortages.^2

## Factors of Failure

Several factors contributed to the failure of this siege. First, Byzantine military technological superiority, especially Greek fire which destroyed most of the Muslim fleet. Second, Leo III's diplomacy which successfully secured assistance from the Bulgarian Kingdom that attacked Muslim forces from behind.^3

Third, complex logistical problems. The distance between Constantinople and the main supply bases in Syria and Egypt was very far, while the Mediterranean Sea often experienced storms in winter. Fourth, disease outbreaks that struck Muslim camps due to poor sanitary conditions during the siege.^4

## Strategic and Political Impact

The failure of the second siege of Constantinople had significant impact on the Umayyad Caliphate. Militarily, this marked the limit of Islamic expansion northward and confirmed that Byzantium still had the strength to defend its core territory.^5

Politically, this failure strengthened Emperor Leo III's position domestically and increased the prestige of the Byzantine Empire in the eyes of the Christian world. This also influenced the internal political dynamics of the Umayyads, where various factions began to question overly ambitious expansion policies.^6

## Strategic Learning

The failure of this siege provided important lessons for Islamic civilization about the importance of mature strategic calculations. The Quran reminds about the principle of caution in planning:

This siege also showed that military strength alone is not enough without being accompanied by diplomatic strategy, adequate logistics, and deep understanding of geographical and climatic conditions.^7 ^8

## Long-Term Impact

Despite failing, the second siege of Constantinople had long-term impact on Islam-Byzantine relations. Both powers began to develop more diplomatic relationship patterns, although still within the framework of strategic competition.^8

This failure also contributed to changes in Umayyad expansion focus. Attention began to shift to internal consolidation and civilizational development, which later produced extraordinary advances in arts, architecture, and sciences as seen in the construction of grand mosques in Damascus and Cordoba.^1

## Wisdom and Reflection

The event of the second siege of Constantinople teaches important values about wisdom in leadership and strategic planning. Not all objectives can be achieved through force alone, but require mature consideration of various factors.

**Lessons that can be learned:**
- **Comprehensive strategic planning** must consider all aspects: military, logistical, diplomatic, and geographical
- **Technology and innovation** can be determining factors in conflict
- **Failure can become learning** to focus on internal development
- **Diplomacy** is as important as military strength in achieving strategic objectives
