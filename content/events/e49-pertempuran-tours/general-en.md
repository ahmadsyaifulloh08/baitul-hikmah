# The Battle of Tours/Poitiers (732 CE)

## Political Context in Western Europe

In the early 8th century CE, Western Europe was in a fragmented condition. The Frankish Kingdom under the leadership of **Charles Martel** controlled most of modern-day France, while in the south, Islamic forces had conquered the entire Iberian Peninsula since 711 CE.^1

The conquest of Andalusia by Muslim forces under **Tariq ibn Ziyad** and **Musa ibn Nusayr** opened the path for further expansion northward. The governor of Andalusia, **Abdul Rahman al-Ghafiqi**, saw an opportunity to extend Islamic territory into the heart of Europe. The relatively weak and fragmented political condition of the Franks provided the perfect momentum for expansion.^2

## Preparation and Troop Mobilization

In 732 CE, Abdul Rahman al-Ghafiqi mobilized a large army consisting of Arab, Berber, and *muallaf* (European converts to Islam) troops from Andalusia. This force was estimated to number between 60,000 to 80,000 soldiers, making it one of the largest military expeditions in the history of Islamic conquests in Europe.^3

Meanwhile, Charles Martel, known as "The Hammer" (*Martellus* in Latin meaning hammer), successfully united various Frankish tribes to face the threat from the south. He built a coalition consisting of Frankish, Aquitaine, and Burgundy forces totaling approximately 30,000 soldiers. Although smaller in number, the Frankish forces had advantages in unified command and knowledge of the terrain.^4

## The Course of Battle

The two armies met on the plains between the cities of Tours and Poitiers in October 732 CE. For seven days, both sides faced each other without initiating major combat, each waiting for the right moment and seeking their opponent's weaknesses.^5

The Muslim forces relied on cavalry strategies that had proven effective in various previous conquests. They had advantages in mobility and combat experience. Meanwhile, Charles Martel implemented a strong defensive formation by placing heavy infantry as the backbone, forming a "shield wall" that was difficult to penetrate.^6

On the eighth day, Abdul Rahman decided to launch a massive attack. The Muslim cavalry attacked fiercely but encountered difficulties penetrating the tight and disciplined Frankish infantry formation. At the height of the battle, Abdul Rahman al-Ghafiqi fell on the battlefield, causing a decline in Muslim troop morale.^7

## Impact and Strategic Analysis

The death of the Muslim army leader became the turning point in the battle. Without clear command, the Muslim forces began to retreat and eventually decided to return to Andalusia. Charles Martel's victory at Tours is considered an important moment that halted further Islamic expansion into Western Europe.^8

However, modern historians view this event in a broader context. Islamic expansion into Europe was not an "invasion" in the modern sense, but part of the natural political and military dynamics of that time. The Muslim forces did not intend to destroy Christianity or European civilization, but rather to expand Islamic territory as other political powers did.^1

For the Islamic world itself, the defeat at Tours did not stop the development of Islamic civilization. The Umayyad Caliphate continued to rule in Andalusia for centuries and produced extraordinary advances in science, arts, and architecture. Cordoba became one of the most important centers of learning in Europe during the Middle Ages.^2

## Wisdom and Reflection

The Battle of Tours reminds us of the principle that in Islam, warfare is not the primary objective, but a means to establish justice. Allah SWT says:

> وَلَوْلَا دَفْعُ اللَّهِ النَّاسَ بَعْضَهُم بِبَعْضٍ لَّفَسَدَتِ الْأَرْضُ ﴿٢٥١﴾
>
> *"And if it were not for Allah checking [some] people by means of others, the earth would have been corrupted."* ﴾251﴿
> — QS. Al-Baqarah (2): 251

Tours became a point of balance of power between Islam and Europe, showing how Allah establishes check-and-balance between civilizations to maintain world equilibrium.^8

This event also teaches the importance of strong leadership in difficult situations. The death of Abdul Rahman al-Ghafiqi shows how the leadership factor can influence the course of history.

**Points of wisdom:**
- **The importance of unity and solidarity** in facing challenges
- **Wise leadership** determines the success of a mission  
- **Tolerance between civilizations** must be maintained even in conflict
- **Knowledge and civilization** must not stop due to military defeat
