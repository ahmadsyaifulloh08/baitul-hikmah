# Bilal ibn Rabbah — The First Muezzin & Symbol of Islamic Equality (614 CE)

## Context

The story of **Bilal ibn Rabbah** is one of the most powerful narratives of faith-driven courage and the social revolution brought by Islam. Bilal was a dark-skinned slave of Abyssinian (Ethiopian) descent owned by **Umayyah ibn Khalaf**, one of the Quraysh nobles most fiercely opposed to Islam.^1

In the social structure of pre-Islamic Mecca, slaves occupied the lowest stratum — they were not fully human in any legal sense, but property that could be sold, beaten, or killed without legal consequence. Islam arrived with a message that radically rejected this hierarchy. The earliest Qur'anic verses affirmed that human dignity is determined by piety, not by skin color or social standing.^2 ^5

When Bilal embraced Islam — most likely in the second or third year of prophethood (around 612–613 CE) — he was not merely professing a creed. He was consciously rejecting the entire system that enslaved him, choosing to submit only to Allah. This decision triggered one of the most harrowing episodes of persecution in early Islamic history.^3

## The Events

### Bilal's Conversion

Bilal was among the *al-Sabiqun al-Awwalun* — the first generation to embrace Islam. Ibn Sa'd records that he accepted Islam through the da'wah of Abu Bakr al-Siddiq, who actively invited people from every social stratum during the secret da'wah phase.^3

For a slave, embracing Islam carried far greater risks than for a nobleman. Slaves had no tribal protection, no wealth with which to buy their safety, and were entirely at their master's mercy. Bilal knew all of this — and still believed.^2

### Torture by Umayyah ibn Khalaf

When Umayyah ibn Khalaf discovered Bilal's conversion, he was enraged. Ibn Hisham narrates that Umayyah tortured Bilal with extreme cruelty: every midday — when the Meccan sun was at its most scorching — Umayyah ordered Bilal to be laid on his back on the burning desert sand, then a massive boulder was placed upon his chest.^2

In this state, Umayyah would say to Bilal: "You will remain like this until you die, or until you renounce Muhammad and worship al-Lat and al-'Uzza!"^2

Yet Bilal, with a boulder crushing his chest and his back seared by sand, uttered only one word:

**"Ahad. Ahad."** ("The One. The One.")^2

This word — simple, resolute, saturated with faith — became one of the most iconic utterances in Islamic history. Amid extreme physical torture, Bilal refused to deny the oneness of Allah. Every cry of "Ahad" was a proclamation of tawhid that pierced through violence and oppression.

Ibn Hisham narrates that Waraqah ibn Nawfal once passed by Bilal while he was being tortured, and said: "Ahad, Ahad, by Allah, O Bilal." He then turned to Umayyah and the other torturers and said: "By Allah, if you kill him in this state, I will make his grave a place of pilgrimage."^2

### Liberation by Abu Bakr

**Abu Bakr al-Siddiq** — whose wealth was spent more than anyone else's on freeing enslaved Muslims — learned of Bilal's suffering. He went to Umayyah ibn Khalaf and offered to purchase Bilal.^1

Ibn Hisham narrates that Abu Bakr said to Umayyah: "Do you not fear Allah concerning this wretched man?" Umayyah replied sarcastically: "You are the one who corrupted him, so free him yourself." Abu Bakr purchased him at a high price and immediately freed Bilal for the sake of Allah.^2

In Sahih al-Bukhari, the Prophet SAW said of Abu Bakr:

> *"The most generous of people to me in his companionship and his wealth is Abu Bakr."*
> — Sahih al-Bukhari, no. 466, Kitab al-Salah

Abu Bakr did not free Bilal alone. Ibn Sa'd records that Abu Bakr freed seven other enslaved Muslims, including **Amir ibn Fuhayrah** and **Umm Ubays**, all with his own money.^3

### Bilal as the First Muezzin

After the Hijrah to Medina and the construction of Masjid al-Nabawi, Prophet Muhammad SAW chose Bilal as the **first muezzin** in Islam.^1 This selection was not merely a functional appointment — it was a revolutionary statement.

A former dark-skinned slave — who in Mecca had been regarded as no more than property — now stood at the highest point in Medina, calling out in a voice heard throughout the city, summoning Muslims to stand as equals before Allah in prayer.^4

Bilal's voice was described as melodious and powerful. He called the adhan every day throughout the Prophet's lifetime, and his standing in the Prophet's eyes was immensely honored.

## Analysis and Lessons

### A Revolution of Equality

Bilal's story demonstrates that Islam — from its very first days — was a movement of radical equality. Fourteen centuries before modern civil rights movements, Islam declared that skin color, social status, and wealth do not determine a person's worth before God.^2

### The Power of One Word

"Ahad" — the single word Bilal uttered under torture — carries a power that transcends all theological argument. It shows that true faith is not merely intellectual knowledge, but a conviction of the heart so firm that no physical torment can break it.^2

### Abu Bakr: A Model of Humanitarian Activism

Abu Bakr used his wealth to free human beings. His actions were not mere charity — they were concrete social activism: using privilege to liberate the oppressed. This model remains relevant today.^3

### A Voice That Changed the World

The selection of Bilal as the first muezzin was a decision laden with symbolic significance. The voice that called Muslims to worship Allah — the most important voice in the community's spiritual life — was given to a former slave. This proved that in Islam, spiritual authority is not determined by lineage but by the quality of one's faith and sacrifice.^1
