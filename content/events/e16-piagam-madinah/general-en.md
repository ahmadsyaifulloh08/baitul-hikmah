# The Charter of Madinah: The First Written Constitution in the History of Civilisation

## Context: Madinah Before the Charter

When Prophet Muhammad ﷺ arrived in Madinah in 622 CE, the city was not a unified political entity. Madinah was a complex mosaic of competing groups that frequently clashed.^1 ^2

The two major Arab tribes — **Aws** and **Khazraj** — had only recently concluded a prolonged civil war that culminated in the **Battle of Bu'ath** just a few years before the hijrah.^1 The wounds of that conflict were still fresh, and inter-clan tensions smouldered beneath the surface.

Alongside the two Arab tribes lived three major Jewish tribes: **Banu Qaynuqa'**, **Banu Nadir**, and **Banu Qurayzhah**. They controlled much of Madinah's economic activity — agriculture, trade, and metalwork — and possessed well-fortified strongholds.^1 ^3 Their relationships with the Arab tribes were transactional: they allied with one side (Aws or Khazraj) for economic advantage and mutual protection.

In addition, there were Arab polytheists in Madinah who had not yet embraced Islam, as well as the *muhajirin* — Muslims from Makkah who had just arrived, without homes, wealth, or livelihoods.

In this extraordinarily complex socio-political landscape, Prophet Muhammad ﷺ drafted a document that modern scholars have called **the first written constitution in the history of human civilisation** — predating the Magna Carta (1215 CE) by more than five centuries.

## The Event: Drafting the *al-Sahifah*

The document is known in classical sources as *al-Sahifah* (the charter/scroll) or *al-Kitab* (the written document). Ibn Hisham preserved it in full in his *Sirah Nabawiyyah*, and Abu Ubayd al-Qasim quoted it in *al-Amwal*.^1 ^3

Ibn Hisham opens the text of the charter with the words: *"Bismillahirrahmanirrahim. Hadhihi kitabun min Muhammad al-Nabiy..."* ("In the name of Allah, the Most Gracious, the Most Merciful. This is a document from Muhammad the Prophet...").^1

The charter consists of approximately **47 clauses** (according to modern scholarly numbering) governing the relationships between all groups in Madinah.^1 ^2 ^3 Its principal tenets include:

### The Inclusive Concept of *Ummah*

The first clause declares that the Muslims of Quraysh (Muhajirin) and Yathrib (Ansar), along with anyone who follows them, strives with them, and struggles alongside them, constitute **one nation (*ummah wahidah*) distinct from all others**.^1

Remarkably, the charter also incorporated the Jewish community into the same political framework. Clause 25 and those following state: *"The Jews of Banu Awf are one nation with the believers — the Jews have their religion and the Muslims have their religion — both their clients and themselves..."*^1 ^3

This means *ummah* in the context of the Charter of Madinah operated on two levels: the Muslim community as a religious body, and the political *ummah* of Madinah encompassing all residents — Muslims, Jews, and others — as a single civic entity.

### The Principle of Religious Freedom

The charter explicitly guaranteed freedom of religion. Each group was entitled to practise its own faith. There was to be no compulsion in matters of belief.^1 ^3 This was a direct implementation of the Quranic principle^4:

> لَا إِكْرَاهَ فِي الدِّينِ ﴿٢٥٦﴾
>
> *"There is no compulsion in religion."*
> — QS. Al-Baqarah (2): 256

### Collective Defence

The charter stipulated that all signatories were obligated to defend Madinah from external attack. If Madinah was attacked, all groups — Muslim and non-Muslim alike — were required to participate in its defence. No party was permitted to make a separate peace with Madinah's enemies without collective consent.^1 ^2

### Justice and Legal Protection

Each group was responsible for its own internal affairs and the payment of *diyah* (blood money) for its members. However, the principle of justice was universal: no person who committed injustice was to be shielded, and every person who was wronged was entitled to assistance.^1 ^3

### Authority for Dispute Resolution

Clause 42 specified that if any dispute arose that could lead to harm, its resolution must be referred to **Allah and Muhammad** — meaning Prophet Muhammad ﷺ served as the supreme arbiter (*hakam*) in inter-group disputes.^1 ^3 This effectively made him the head of state of Madinah.

## Analysis: Why the Charter of Madinah Was Revolutionary

**First, the supremacy of written law.** In seventh-century Arabia, law was customary and unwritten — passed down through oral tradition and enforced by tribal authority. The Charter of Madinah introduced the concept of codified law, binding all parties in writing. This was an extraordinary civilisational leap.

**Second, from tribal loyalty to citizenship.** In the traditional Arab system, a person's identity and protection were determined by their tribe. The Charter of Madinah created a new identity: citizen of Madinah. A person was protected not because of their tribe but because of their membership in the political community of Madinah.

**Third, structured pluralism.** The charter did not erase group identities — every tribe and every religion retained its internal autonomy. But all were bound within a shared political framework with clear rights and obligations. This was a model of pluralism that acknowledged difference while building unity.

**Fourth, universal justice.** The clauses protecting the wronged and denying protection to wrongdoers demonstrated that justice was not the monopoly of any single group. Even a Muslim who committed injustice was not to be shielded by fellow Muslims if the victim was a non-Muslim.

## Lessons: The Relevance of the Charter of Madinah for the Modern Age

1. **Diversity is not a threat.** The Charter of Madinah proved that a society diverse in ethnicity and religion can coexist peacefully within a single political framework, provided the rules are clear and just.

2. **Constitutionalism.** Long before the modern concept of a constitution, Prophet Muhammad ﷺ applied the principle that authority must be constrained by collectively agreed written rules.

3. **Citizenship transcends primordial identity.** The Charter of Madinah shows that Islam, from its very inception, recognised a concept of citizenship not limited to any particular religion or ethnicity.

4. **Justice as the foundation of unity.** It was not uniformity that united Madinah, but justice. As long as all parties were treated fairly, difference did not become a source of division.
