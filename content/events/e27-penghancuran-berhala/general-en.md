# The Destruction of Idols at the Ka'bah: Purification of Monotheism

## Context: A Sacred House Defiled

The Ka'bah was built by **Prophet Ibrahim** and his son **Ismail** *'alayhimas salam* as a house of worship dedicated to Allah alone.^1 Over the centuries that followed, the Arabian Peninsula underwent a prolonged spiritual decline. Generation after generation, idol worship crept into Arab tradition until the Ka'bah — the house of monotheism — was filled with 360 idols.^1 ^2

Ibn Hisham in his *Sirah Nabawiyyah* records that the first person to introduce idol worship to Makkah was **Amr ibn Luhay al-Khuza'i**, the leader of Banu Khuza'ah, who encountered idol worship during a journey to al-Sham and brought back an idol named Hubal.^1 The Prophet ﷺ said, in a hadith narrated by Imam al-Bukhari:^3

> رَأَيْتُ عَمْرَو بْنَ لُحَيٍّ يَجُرُّ قُصْبَهُ فِي النَّارِ
>
> *"I saw Amr ibn Luhay dragging his intestines in the Fire."*
> — Sahih al-Bukhari, no. 3521, Kitab Ahadith al-Anbiya

Among the chief idols worshipped by the Arabs were:
- **Hubal** — the largest idol inside the Ka'bah, made of red carnelian stone, shaped like a man
- **Al-Lat** — in Ta'if, worshipped by the Thaqif tribe
- **Al-Uzza** — in the valley of Nakhlah between Makkah and Ta'if, worshipped by Quraysh
- **Manat** — on the coast between Makkah and Madinah, worshipped by the Aws and Khazraj

The Quran mentions the last three explicitly^4:

> أَفَرَأَيْتُمُ اللَّاتَ وَالْعُزَّىٰ ﴿١٩﴾ وَمَنَاةَ الثَّالِثَةَ الْأُخْرَىٰ ﴿٢٠﴾
>
> *"Have you then considered al-Lat and al-Uzza, ﴾19﴿ and Manat, the third, the other one? ﴾20﴿"*
> — QS. An-Najm (53): 19-20

Beyond statues, the Ka'bah also contained paintings. Ibn Hisham records that among the paintings inside the Ka'bah were depictions of Prophet Ibrahim and Ismail casting lots with divination arrows — a historical distortion portraying two prophets of monotheism as though engaged in pre-Islamic practices.^1 There were also images of Maryam and Isa *'alayhimas salam*.^4

## The Event: The Day of Purification

The destruction of the idols took place on the day of Makkah's liberation, 20 Ramadan 8 AH (January 11, 630 CE). After entering Makkah peacefully, the Prophet ﷺ went directly to the Ka'bah.

Al-Bukhari records that the Prophet ﷺ performed *tawaf* (circumambulation) seven times on his camel. With each circuit, he pointed at the idols arrayed around the Ka'bah with a staff or bow in his hand.^3 Each time his staff pointed at an idol, it fell — some toppling forward on their faces, others falling on their backs.^2

As he pointed and toppled the idols, he recited two verses of the Quran:

> جَاءَ الْحَقُّ وَزَهَقَ الْبَاطِلُ إِنَّ الْبَاطِلَ كَانَ زَهُوقًا ﴿٨١﴾
>
> *"Truth has come and falsehood has vanished. Indeed, falsehood is ever bound to vanish. ﴾81﴿"*
> — QS. Al-Isra (17): 81

> قُلْ جَاءَ الْحَقُّ وَمَا يُبْدِئُ الْبَاطِلُ وَمَا يُعِيدُ ﴿٤٩﴾
>
> *"Say: Truth has come, and falsehood can neither originate anything nor restore it. ﴾49﴿"*
> — QS. Saba (34): 49

After the *tawaf*, the Prophet ﷺ ordered all 360 idols to be removed from the Ka'bah and destroyed.^1 ^3 Stone idols were smashed; wooden ones were burned. Hubal — the largest and most venerated idol of Quraysh — was broken in front of the Ka'bah's entrance.^4

Ibn Hisham records that when the Prophet ﷺ entered the Ka'bah, he found paintings on its walls. He ordered all paintings removed. According to one account by al-Azraqi in *Akhbar Makkah*, he initially shielded the paintings of Ibrahim and Maryam with his hands before ultimately ordering all images erased without exception.^1 This demonstrates that the destruction was not animosity toward art, but purification from all intermediaries in worship.

After the Ka'bah was cleansed, the Prophet ﷺ stood at its entrance and declared:^1 ^2

*"There is no god but Allah alone, with no partner. He has fulfilled His promise, aided His servant, and defeated the confederates by Himself."*

He then entered the Ka'bah and prayed two *rak'ahs* inside it. Upon emerging, he ordered **Bilal ibn Rabah** to climb atop the Ka'bah and call the *adhan* — the first *adhan* ever to sound from its roof.^3 The voice of Bilal, a former slave once tortured by Quraysh for his faith, now echoed from the most sacred site on earth. This moment became a symbol of Islam's social revolution: no superiority based on race, social status, or lineage.

## Key Dialogues and Evidence: Voices on the Day of Purification

The recently defeated Qurayshi leaders reacted in varied ways. **Abu Sufyan**, who had embraced Islam only hours earlier, watched in silence. Others were stunned.

Ibn Hisham records that when Bilal called the *adhan* from atop the Ka'bah, several Qurayshi leaders commented.^1 **Attab ibn Asid** said: *"Praise be to Allah that He honored my father by not letting him witness this day."* Yet he later embraced Islam sincerely and was appointed governor of Makkah by the Prophet ﷺ.

The destruction of idols at the Ka'bah had precedent in prophetic history. Prophet Ibrahim *'alayhis salam* had also destroyed the idols of his people, as the Quran recounts:

> فَرَاغَ إِلَىٰ آلِهَتِهِمْ فَقَالَ أَلَا تَأْكُلُونَ ﴿٩١﴾ مَا لَكُمْ لَا تَنطِقُونَ ﴿٩٢﴾ فَرَاغَ عَلَيْهِمْ ضَرْبًا بِالْيَمِينِ ﴿٩٣﴾
>
> *"Then he turned to their gods and said: 'Will you not eat? ﴾91﴿ What is the matter with you that you do not speak?' ﴾92﴿ Then he turned upon them, striking with his right hand. ﴾93﴿"*
> — QS. As-Saffat (37): 91-93

What Prophet Muhammad ﷺ did was the continuation and completion of Prophet Ibrahim's mission — restoring the Ka'bah to its original purpose: a house of worship for Allah alone.^4

## Analysis and Lessons

### Purification, Not Destruction

It is essential to understand that the destruction of idols at the Ka'bah was not vandalism or the ruin of cultural heritage — it was purification (*tathir*). The Ka'bah was built for monotheism; the idols were foreign objects that defiled it. Destroying them was restoring the Ka'bah to its original function.^1

The Quran explains the original mission of the Ka'bah's construction:

> وَإِذْ بَوَّأْنَا لِإِبْرَاهِيمَ مَكَانَ الْبَيْتِ أَن لَّا تُشْرِكْ بِي شَيْئًا وَطَهِّرْ بَيْتِيَ لِلطَّائِفِينَ وَالْقَائِمِينَ وَالرُّكَّعِ السُّجُودِ ﴿٢٦﴾
>
> *"And when We designated for Ibrahim the site of the House, saying: 'Do not associate anything with Me, and purify My House for those who circumambulate it, and those who stand, and those who bow and prostrate.' ﴾26﴿"*
> — QS. Al-Hajj (22): 26

### Symbol of an Old Order's Collapse^4

The destruction of 360 idols in a single day was a declaration that the entire pre-Islamic belief system had ended. Each idol represented an aspect of the old social order — tribal hierarchies, spiritual servitude, and injustice legitimized by "ancestral tradition." When the idols fell, the intellectual and spiritual foundations of that entire system collapsed with them.

### Bilal Atop the Ka'bah

The choice of Bilal — a Black former slave from Abyssinia — to call the *adhan* from atop the Ka'bah was no coincidence. It was a revolutionary statement: Islam recognizes no superiority based on skin color, wealth, or lineage. In the place that had for centuries symbolized Qurayshi elitism, a freed slave now stood tallest.^3

### Long-Term Impact

The destruction of idols at the Ka'bah marked a permanent turning point in the history of the Arabian Peninsula. After that day, no idol was ever erected at the Ka'bah again. Makkah, which had for centuries served as the center of Arab polytheism, became the center of monotheism that endures to this day — fourteen centuries later.

The event also inspired expeditions to destroy major idols elsewhere: **Khalid ibn al-Walid** was sent to destroy al-Uzza, **Amr ibn al-As** to destroy Suwa', and **Sa'd ibn Zayd al-Ashhali** to destroy Manat.^1 ^2 Within months, every center of idol worship in the Arabian Peninsula had vanished.
