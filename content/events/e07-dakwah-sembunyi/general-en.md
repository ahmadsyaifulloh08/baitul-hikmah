# The Secret Da'wah — The First Three Years of Islamic Propagation (610–613 CE)

## Context

After the first revelation descended in the Cave of Hira and the pause of revelation (*fatrah al-wahy*) ended, **Prophet Muhammad SAW** began his mission with extraordinary caution: secretly and limited to those closest to him.^1 This approach was far from arbitrary. Seventh-century Mecca was controlled by the Quraysh oligarchy whose economic, social, and political life depended entirely on the polytheistic religious system — idol worship at the Ka'bah generated enormous revenue from pilgrims across the Arabian Peninsula.^2

Publicly announcing monotheistic teachings in such an environment would provoke a fierce reaction capable of destroying the movement before it could take root. Therefore, for approximately three years (610–613 CE), Prophet Muhammad SAW conducted his da'wah in secret, inviting people one by one, selecting those he knew to possess clarity of heart and courage.^1

## The Events

### The First Believers

History records that the first people to embrace Islam came from the Prophet's innermost circle:

**Khadijah bint Khuwaylid** was the first person to believe. Without the slightest hesitation, she affirmed what her husband had experienced in the Cave of Hira. Ibn Hisham narrates that Khadijah was the first human being to believe in Allah and affirm the message brought by the Messenger of Allah — she strengthened him in carrying out this mission.^1

**Ali ibn Abi Talib**, then only about ten years old, became the first child to embrace Islam. He had lived in the Prophet's household since childhood because Abu Talib faced financial difficulties, and so he witnessed the Prophet's daily life and worship firsthand.^3

**Zaid ibn Harithah**, a freed slave whom the Prophet had adopted, became the first freed person (*mawla*) to embrace Islam.^1

**Abu Bakr al-Siddiq** was the most influential companion during the secret da'wah phase. He was a highly respected Quraysh merchant known for his honesty and wisdom. When Prophet Muhammad SAW conveyed the message to him, Abu Bakr believed immediately without any hesitation — he asked for no miracles or additional proof.^2

### Abu Bakr's Role in the Early Da'wah

Abu Bakr did not merely believe; he became the second *da'i* after the Prophet Muhammad SAW. Leveraging his social network and reputation among Meccan merchants, he succeeded in bringing several prominent individuals to Islam. Among those who embraced Islam through Abu Bakr's efforts were **Uthman ibn Affan**, **Zubayr ibn al-Awwam**, **Abd al-Rahman ibn Awf**, **Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas**, and **Talhah ibn Ubaydullah** — five men who would become pillars of Islamic civilization.^2

Ibn Sa'd records in *Al-Tabaqat al-Kubra* that those who embraced Islam in this early phase — known as *al-Sabiqun al-Awwalun* (the foremost pioneers) — numbered approximately forty individuals.^3

### Dar al-Arqam: The Secret Center of Da'wah

As the number of Muslims grew, a gathering place was needed to study the Qur'an and worship together. The choice fell upon the house of **Al-Arqam ibn Abi al-Arqam**, a young Qurayshi from the clan of Banu Makhzum who had embraced Islam. His house was located at the foot of Mount Safa — sufficiently secluded yet easily accessible.^1 ^4

It was here that the Prophet Muhammad SAW taught newly revealed Qur'anic verses, guided the companions in prayer, and built the spiritual foundation of the first Muslim community. Dar al-Arqam functioned simultaneously as a school, a mosque, and a coordination center — all under complete secrecy.^2

### Characteristics of the Secret Da'wah Phase

The da'wah during this phase had several important characteristics:

**First**, a personal approach (*da'wah fardiyyah*). Prophet Muhammad SAW and Abu Bakr approached people individually rather than through public declarations. They chose individuals known for their pure disposition and sound judgment.^2

**Second**, social diversity. From the very beginning, Islam made no distinction based on social class. Among the first Muslims were nobles like Uthman ibn Affan, merchants like Abu Bakr, freed slaves like Zaid ibn Harithah, women like Khadijah, and children like Ali. This diversity was a statement that Islam was a religion for all of humanity.^1

**Third**, character formation before confrontation. The three years of secret da'wah were not passive — they were a period of forging the core cadre who would become the backbone of Islamic civilization. They were nurtured with the Qur'an, taught firm faith, and trained to withstand pressure.^2

## Quotations and Evidence

The Qur'anic verses revealed during this period largely focused on three themes: strengthening faith, warnings about the Hereafter, and critiques of greed and social injustice. Among the early surahs revealed:

> وَٱلْعَصْرِ ﴿١﴾ إِنَّ ٱلْإِنسَـٰنَ لَفِى خُسْرٍ ﴿٢﴾ إِلَّا ٱلَّذِينَ ءَامَنُوا۟ وَعَمِلُوا۟ ٱلصَّـٰلِحَـٰتِ وَتَوَاصَوْا۟ بِٱلْحَقِّ وَتَوَاصَوْا۟ بِٱلصَّبْرِ ﴿٣﴾
>
> *"By time. ﴾1﴿ Indeed, mankind is in loss, ﴾2﴿ except those who believe and do righteous deeds and advise each other to truth and advise each other to patience. ﴾3﴿"*
> — QS. Al-'Asr (103): 1–3

This short surah encapsulates the four conditions of success that formed the foundation for nurturing the companions: faith, righteous deeds, mutual counsel toward truth, and mutual counsel toward patience.^4

> ٱقْرَأْ بِٱسْمِ رَبِّكَ ٱلَّذِى خَلَقَ ﴿١﴾
>
> *"Read in the name of your Lord who created. ﴾1﴿"*
> — QS. Al-'Alaq (96): 1

The first command in Islam was to read — a revolutionary message in a predominantly illiterate society, affirming that Islam is fundamentally a religion of knowledge.^4

## Analysis and Lessons

### The Strategy of Gradual Da'wah

The secret da'wah teaches an important principle: great change need not begin with a grand movement. Prophet Muhammad SAW started from the smallest circle — family and closest friends — then gradually expanded his reach. This approach ensured that the foundation of faith was solid before facing external pressure.^2

### Dar al-Arqam as an Educational Model

The house of Al-Arqam became the prototype for the first Islamic educational institution. There, no social hierarchy existed — Quraysh nobles sat alongside freed slaves, learning together from the same source. This model became the blueprint for the madrasas and universities of later Islamic civilization.^1

### The Courage of Faith Amid Uncertainty

Embracing Islam during the secret phase required extraordinary courage. The *al-Sabiqun al-Awwalun* had no guarantee of safety, no large community to protect them, and no certainty that this movement would succeed. They believed solely because of the truth they perceived in Muhammad's teachings — and this courage became the primary capital for Islam's struggle in the stages that followed.^3
