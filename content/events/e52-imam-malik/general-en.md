# Imam Malik ibn Anas: Al-Muwatta and the Maliki School (711-795 CE)

## Life in the City of the Prophet

**Malik ibn Anas ibn Malik** was born in 93 AH/711 CE in Madinah al-Munawwarah into a respected family from the Ashja' tribe^1. His grandfather, **Malik al-Akbar**, was one of the prominent successors who had met several companions of the Prophet. The religious family environment and Madinah's position as the center of Prophetic tradition deeply influenced Malik's character formation^2.

Madinah during Malik's time still preserved authentic traces of Rasulullah's ﷺ life. **The Prophet's Mosque** became the center of scholarly activities, where great scholars gathered such as **Sa'id ibn al-Musayyab** (d. 94 AH), **Urwah ibn al-Zubayr** (d. 94 AH), and **Nafi' mawla ibn Umar** (d. 117 AH)^3.

From childhood, Malik was accustomed to intensive learning environments. His mother, **Al-Aliyah bint Shurayk**, encouraged her son to study hadith and jurisprudence. She advised Malik to learn *adab* (ethics) before delving into knowledge, as adab is the foundation for a scholar^4.

## Intellectual Journey and Methodology Formation

### Learning with Madinah Scholars

**Nafi'**, the former slave of **Abdullah ibn Umar** who later became a great scholar, was Malik's main teacher in hadith. Nafi' was known for his very strong memorization of the Prophet's sayings narrated by Ibn Umar^5. From Nafi', Malik learned the importance of authentic *isnad* (chain of narrators) in hadith transmission.

**Ibn Shihab al-Zuhri** (d. 124 AH), one of the greatest hadith narrators, also became Malik's teacher when al-Zuhri visited Madinah. Al-Zuhri taught Malik about systematic methodology for collecting and verifying hadith^6.

### The Concept of 'Amal Ahl al-Madinah

Malik developed a unique concept in Islamic legal methodology: **'Amal Ahl al-Madinah** (practice of the people of Madinah). According to him, traditions practiced continuously by Madinah society since the Prophet's time have strong evidential value, sometimes even specifying solitary hadith^7.

This concept was based on understanding that Madinah was where the Prophet lived and interacted with his companions. Practices that developed and persisted there were more likely to reflect authentic sunnah compared to practices in other regions that might have experienced distortion^8.

## Al-Muwatta: The First Codification of Hadith and Jurisprudence

### Compilation Process

Around 140 AH, **Khalifah Abu Ja'far al-Mansur** asked Malik to compile a book that could serve as a standard legal reference for the entire Islamic world. Malik responded by compiling **Al-Muwatta** (The Smoothed Path), which combined hadith, companions' *athar*, and successors' opinions in one compilation^9.

Malik spent more than 40 years compiling and revising Al-Muwatta. He conducted very strict selection of hadith included, choosing only those he considered authentic and consistent with 'amal ahl al-Madinah. From approximately 10,000 hadith he collected, only about 1,700 were included in Al-Muwatta^10.

### Jurisprudential Systematization

Al-Muwatta was organized according to jurisprudential systematics (*abwab al-fiqh*), starting from purification topics, prayer, zakat, and so forth. Each chapter began with relevant hadith, then continued with companions' *athar* and Madinah scholars' opinions^11.

Al-Muwatta's uniqueness lay in its integration between hadith and living legal practice in society. Malik did not merely collect hadith but also showed how these hadith were understood and practiced by the early generations^12.

## Scholarly Assembly and Teaching Methods

### Circle at the Prophet's Mosque

Malik began teaching at **the Prophet's Mosque** at approximately age 40. His assembly became the most authoritative center for hadith and jurisprudence learning in the Islamic world at that time. Thousands of students from various corners of the world came to Madinah to learn from him^13.

**Imam al-Shafi'i**, who later established his own school, was one of Malik's most important students. Al-Shafi'i studied in Madinah for several years and greatly respected this great teacher. **Abdullah ibn Wahb** and **Ashhab ibn Abd al-Aziz** were other students who became main propagators of the Maliki school^14.

### Etiquette in Teaching

Malik paid great attention to etiquette in teaching. He always performed ablution when teaching hadith, wore his best clothes, and used perfume. When asked his reason, he replied: "I honor the hadith of Rasulullah ﷺ"^15.

This attitude reflected Malik's view that teaching hadith was not merely information transfer but worship that must be done with deep appreciation and respect^16.

## Test of Faith: The Trial of Khalq al-Quran

### Firm Stance on Allah's Attributes

During **Khalifah al-Ma'mun's** time (813-833 CE), the trial of *khalq al-Quran* (creation of the Quran) emerged, adopted by the Mu'tazilah school. The government forced scholars to acknowledge that the Quran was a creature (created), not Allah's eternal attribute^17.

Malik, along with **Ahmad ibn Hanbal** and other scholars, firmly rejected this doctrine. When asked for a statement, Malik answered decisively: "The Quran is Allah's uncreated speech"^18.

### Torture and Steadfastness

Due to his stance, Malik experienced physical torture. He was beaten until his shoulder was dislocated and never fully healed. Nevertheless, he remained firm in his position and refused to compromise with political pressure^19.

Malik's suffering became a symbol of scholars' resistance against state intervention in matters of creed. His attitude inspired generations of scholars afterward to preserve the purity of Islamic teachings from political influence^20.

## Spread and Influence of the Maliki School

### Expansion to Maghrib and Andalus

The Maliki school spread rapidly to North Africa (Maghrib) and Andalus (Islamic Spain) through Malik's students who returned to their home regions. **Sahnun** (d. 240 AH) in Tunisia and **Ibn al-Qasim** (d. 191 AH) in Egypt became main propagators of this school^21.

**The Almoravid and Almohad dynasties** in Maghrib officially adopted the Maliki school as the state's official school. This strengthened the Maliki school's position in the region until today^22.

### Adaptation to Local Cultures

The Maliki school's success in Africa and Andalus was inseparable from its flexibility in adapting to local conditions. The concept of *'urf* (local tradition) developed by Malik allowed legal adjustment to local community needs without losing the substance of sharia^23.

## Intellectual Legacy and Contributions

### Hadith Methodology

Malik made great contributions to hadith science development, especially in *sanad* and *matan* criticism aspects. The authenticity standards he established in Al-Muwatta became references for subsequent hadith scholars^24.

### Ushul Fiqih

Although he did not write specifically about *ushul fiqih*, Malik's methodology provided important contributions to this discipline's development. The concepts of *'amal ahl al-Madinah*, *istihsan*, and *maslahah mursalah* that he developed were later systematically formulated by subsequent scholars^25.

## Analysis and Lessons

**The importance of historical context in understanding hadith**: Malik showed that hadith cannot be understood separately from its practical context. 'Amal ahl al-Madinah provides perspective on how early generations understood and applied hadith.

**Integration between textual and contextual**: Malik's methodology proves that textualist and contextualist approaches in understanding sharia are not contradictory but complement each other to achieve comprehensive understanding.

**Consistency of principles in facing pressure**: Malik's attitude in facing the trial of khalq al-Quran teaches the importance of maintaining faith principles despite facing political pressure and physical suffering.
