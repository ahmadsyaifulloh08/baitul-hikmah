# The Battle of the Great Zab - End of Umayyad, Beginning of Abbasid (750 CE)

## Political Context Before the Fall of Umayyad

In the mid-8th century CE, the Umayyad Caliphate faced various serious internal challenges. **Caliph Marwan II ibn Muhammad** (744-750 CE), the last caliph of the Umayyad dynasty, inherited a caliphate already undermined by internal conflicts, regional rebellions, and dissatisfaction from various groups in society.^1

The most organized opposition movement was led by the Abbasid family, descendants of **Abbas ibn Abd al-Muttalib**, uncle of Prophet Muhammad ﷺ. They claimed that the caliphate should be in the hands of the Prophet's family (Ahl al-Bayt) and not the Umayyad dynasty which was considered to have deviated from the principles of early Islam.^1

## Abbasid Propaganda and Mobilization

**Abu al-Abbas as-Saffah** and his brother **Abu Ja'far al-Mansur** began a systematic propaganda campaign from 718 CE, especially in the Khurasan region (Afghanistan and eastern Iran). They exploited the dissatisfaction of *mawali* (non-Arabs who converted to Islam) who felt discriminated against in the Umayyad socio-political system.^2

This movement was led militarily by **Abu Muslim al-Khurasani**, a charismatic general who successfully organized multiethnic forces consisting of Persians, Turkics, and Arabs. Their slogan was "*ar-rida min al Muhammad*" (the one approved from Muhammad's family), which was deliberately made ambiguous to attract support from various opposition groups.^2

## Preparation Toward Final Confrontation

In 749 CE, Abbasid forces successfully controlled most of the Khurasan region and began moving west toward the center of the caliphate. **Qahtaba ibn Shabib al-Ta'i**, one of Abu Muslim's main generals, led the advance forces that successfully conquered Kufa and claimed the city as the Abbasid operational base.^3

Marwan II realized this serious threat and mobilized all remaining military strength. He gathered forces from Syria, Mesopotamia, and Egypt to face the Abbasid challenge. However, Umayyad troop morale was already very low due to a series of defeats and defections that had occurred in recent years.^1

## Location and Strategic Significance

The Great Zab River, a tributary of the Tigris in northern Mesopotamia (modern Iraq), was chosen as the battle location because of its strategic position. This area controlled access to Baghdad and Damascus, so whoever controlled it would have strategic advantage in controlling the center of the caliphate.^5

The two armies met in January 750 CE on the banks of this river. Umayyad forces were led directly by Marwan II with about 120,000 soldiers, while Abbasid forces under the command of **Abdullah ibn Ali**, uncle of Abu al-Abbas, had about 200,000 soldiers with high morale.^1

## The Course of Battle

The battle began on January 25, 750 CE with an Umayyad cavalry attack that tried to utilize their mobility advantage. However, Abbasid forces implemented solid defensive tactics by placing heavy infantry in the front lines and archers behind them. This formation proved effective in withstanding Umayyad cavalry attacks.^1

The turning point of the battle occurred when some Umayyad forces from the Iraq region began to switch sides (desert) to the Abbasids. This created cracks in the Umayyad ranks and gave Abbasid forces the opportunity to launch a decisive counterattack.^1

Marwan II himself was wounded in battle and had to flee with the remnants of his still loyal forces. This defeat not only marked the end of the battle, but also the end of the Umayyad dynasty that had ruled for almost a century (661-750 CE).^1

## Immediate Consequences

After victory at the Great Zab, Abbasid forces quickly moved to control the entire core territory of the caliphate. **Abu al-Abbas as-Saffah** was proclaimed as the first caliph of the Abbasid dynasty in Kufa in February 750 CE, beginning a new era in Islamic history.^2

Marwan II continued to flee to Egypt where he was eventually killed in August 750 CE in the village of Busir. His death definitively marked the end of the Umayyad dynasty in the East, although one branch of the family managed to survive and establish the Emirate of Cordoba in Andalusia.^4

## Paradigm Shift in Power

The Abbasid victory brought fundamental changes in the structure of Islamic power. While the Umayyads relied more on Arab elites and used Damascus as their center, the Abbasids built a more inclusive coalition involving Persians, Turkics, and various other ethnic groups.^6

The center of the caliphate also moved from Damascus to Baghdad, which was built by **Caliph al-Mansur** (754-775 CE). This change was not only geographical, but also symbolic: from Mediterranean-Byzantine orientation to Persian-Central Asian orientation that brought different cultural and intellectual influences.^6

## Impact on Islamic Civilization

The transition from Umayyad to Abbasid not only changed the ruling dynasty, but also brought profound civilizational transformation. The Abbasid era became the golden age of Islamic science with the establishment of Bayt al-Hikmah (House of Wisdom) in Baghdad and mass translation of Greek, Persian, and Indian works.^6

However, it should be noted that Umayyad civilizational achievements were also extraordinary, especially in architecture (Umayyad Mosque of Damascus, Dome of the Rock) and the geographical expansion of Islam which reached its peak during this period. The Battle of the Great Zab thus marked a transition between two great phases of Islamic civilization.^5

## Wisdom and Reflection

The Battle of the Great Zab reminds us of the natural law of politics: no power is eternal except Allah's power. The Quran teaches:

> تِلْكَ الدَّارُ الْآخِرَةُ نَجْعَلُهَا لِلَّذِينَ لَا يُرِيدُونَ عُلُوًّا فِي الْأَرْضِ وَلَا فَسَادًا ﴿٨٣﴾
>
> *"That home of the Hereafter We assign to those who do not desire exaltedness upon the earth or corruption."* ﴾83﴿
> — QS. Al-Qasas (28): 83

This verse provides perspective that the Umayyad dynasty fell due to prolonged arrogance and oppression — reminding that true authority belongs only to those who do not seek exaltedness and corruption on earth.^7

This event also teaches the importance of legitimacy and popular support in government, as well as how injustice and discrimination can become seeds of destruction for a dynasty.

**Lessons that can be learned:**
- **Political power** requires continuous legitimacy and support from the people
- **Social justice** is the foundation of long-term political stability
- **Dynastic changes** do not always mean civilizational decline, but can open new chapters
- **The contribution of each era** must be appreciated regardless of the end of political power
