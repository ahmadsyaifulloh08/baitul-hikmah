# Orphanhood: The Death of the Father, Mother, and Grandfather of Prophet Muhammad ﷺ

## Before the Dawn: The Death of Abdullah ibn Abd al-Muttalib

The story of Prophet Muhammad's orphanhood begins even before his birth — or shortly after it, depending on which historical account one follows. Abdullah ibn Abd al-Muttalib, the Prophet's father, died young at approximately 25 years of age, leaving his wife Aminah bint Wahb either pregnant or with a newborn son.¹ ²

Historians differ on the precise timing of Abdullah's death. Ibn Hisham, in his *Sirah Nabawiyyah*, states that Abdullah died while the Prophet was still in his mother's womb.¹ This is corroborated by Ibn Sa'd in *Tabaqat al-Kubra*, who records that Abdullah died in Madinah (then called Yathrib) on his return journey from al-Sham, and was buried in the house of a family from the Banu al-Najjar — the tribe of Abdullah's mother, Salma bint Amr.⁵

A minority view holds that Abdullah died several months after the Prophet's birth. Al-Mubarakfuri in *al-Rahiq al-Makhtum* documents both versions but leans toward the majority opinion*jumhur* that Abdullah died before Muhammad's birth.² Ibn Qayyim al-Jawziyyah in *Zad al-Ma'ad* concurs with this position.⁷

What all sources agree upon is this: Abdullah died in Madinah during a trading journey, leaving behind very little — five camels, a small flock of sheep, and a female servant named Umm Ayman (Barakah).¹ ⁵ This absence of material wealth marked the Prophet's life from its very beginning: Muhammad ﷺ was born not into luxury, but into modest circumstances.

From a theological perspective, this pre-birth orphanhood was part of the divine design. As Allah declared in Surah Ad-Dhuha:

> أَلَمْ يَجِدْكَ يَتِيمًا فَآوَىٰ ﴿٦﴾
>
> *"Did He not find you an orphan and give you refuge?"*
> — QS. Ad-Dhuha (93): 6

## In the Desert of Banu Sa'd: The Nursing Years

It was an established custom among the Arab aristocracy, particularly the Quraysh, to send their infants to Bedouin wet-nurses*murdi'ah* in the countryside. The purpose was twofold: the child would grow physically strong in the fresh desert air, and would acquire pure, eloquent Arabic*fasahah* — untainted by the foreign-influenced dialects of the trading cities.¹

Ibn Hisham and al-Bayhaqi in *Dalail al-Nubuwwah* relate the touching story of how Halimah bint Abi Dhu'ayb of the Banu Sa'd came to Makkah with a group of Bedouin women seeking infants to nurse.¹ ⁶ That year was one of drought and famine; Halimah's donkey was so emaciated it slowed the entire caravan, and her own milk had nearly dried up, leaving her own infant crying through the night.

One by one, the women of Banu Sa'd chose infants from wealthy Qurayshi families. No one wanted to take baby Muhammad — an orphan from a family that could not pay well. Halimah herself initially hesitated. But when no other infant remained, she told her husband, al-Harith ibn Abd al-Uzza: *"By Allah, I do not wish to return without an infant. Let me take that orphan child."*¹

What followed, as narrated by Halimah herself through Ibn Ishaq's transmission, was a cascade of extraordinary blessings.¹ The moment baby Muhammad was in her arms, Halimah's milk flowed abundantly, their thin camel gave plentiful milk, and their feeble donkey strode ahead of the caravan. The other women marveled: *"O Halimah, what has happened to you?"*

Muhammad ﷺ lived among the Banu Sa'd for approximately four to five years.² In these vast desert expanses, he grew — running across sand dunes, herding sheep alongside his foster-siblings, and absorbing the pure Bedouin Arabic that would later become the vessel for Quranic revelation.

### The Opening of the Chest*Shaqq al-Sadr*

During his time with the Banu Sa'd, an event occurred that holds a prominent place in Islamic tradition: the opening of the chest*shaqq al-sadr*. Imam Muslim records in his *Sahih* from Anas ibn Malik that the angel Jibril came to young Muhammad while he was playing with other children, opened his chest, extracted a clot of black substance, washed his heart with Zamzam water in a golden basin, and restored it.⁴

This event alarmed Halimah and her husband, who feared for their foster-child's safety. Despite their reluctance, they decided to return Muhammad to his mother Aminah in Makkah.¹ Ibn Hisham records that Halimah told Aminah: *"I have fulfilled my duty, and I fear something may befall him."* After Aminah pressed her, Halimah recounted what had happened. Aminah responded calmly: *"This child of mine will have a great affair."*¹

## Aminah's Final Journey: The Death of a Mother (576 CE)

After Muhammad returned to his mother's care in Makkah, Aminah bint Wahb decided to take her son to visit his paternal relatives in YathribMadinah. There, Abdullah was buried, and there lived the extended family of the Banu al-Najjar — the tribe of Muhammad's paternal grandmother.¹

Ibn Hisham relates that Aminah set out with Muhammad, who was then approximately six years old, accompanied by Umm Ayman (Barakah), the loyal servant left behind by Abdullah.¹ They stayed in Madinah for about a month. It was here that young Muhammad saw his father's grave for the first time — a father he had never known.

On the return journey to Makkah, at a place called **al-Abwa'** — a small village situated between Makkah and Madinah — Aminah fell ill and died.¹ ² ⁵ Ibn Sa'd in *Tabaqat al-Kubra* and al-Mubarakfuri in *al-Rahiq al-Makhtum* agree that she was buried at al-Abwa'.

Consider the scene: a six-year-old child standing in an arid landscape, watching his mother — the only parent he had ever known — lowered into the earth in an unfamiliar place. Muhammad was now a complete orphan. Umm Ayman, weeping, carried the boy back to Makkah, to the care of his grandfather Abd al-Muttalib.

This loss left a permanent imprint. Years later, during his journey to Hudaybiyyah, the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ passed through al-Abwa' and stopped to visit his mother's grave. His companions saw him weep so deeply that they too began to cry. Imam Muslim records that the Prophet said:⁴

> اسْتَأْذَنْتُ رَبِّي أَنْ أَسْتَغْفِرَ لِأُمِّي فَلَمْ يَأْذَنْ لِي، وَاسْتَأْذَنْتُهُ أَنْ أَزُورَ قَبْرَهَا فَأَذِنَ لِي
>
> *"I asked my Lord for permission to seek forgiveness for my mother, but He did not permit me. Then I asked for permission to visit her grave, and He permitted me."*
> — Sahih Muslim, no. 976, Kitab al-Jana'iz)

## Under the Wing of Abd al-Muttalib (576–578 CE)

After Aminah's death, custody of Muhammad fell to his grandfather, Abd al-Muttalib ibn Hashim — the great chief of Quraysh and custodian of the Ka'bah.¹ Abd al-Muttalib was one of the most revered figures in Makkah, known for his nobility, generosity, and wisdom. He was the one who had rediscovered the well of Zamzam after it had been buried for generations, and the one who had confronted Abraha when the army of elephants threatened the Ka'bah.

Ibn Hisham describes Abd al-Muttalib's affection for his grandson as extraordinary — surpassing even his love for his own sons.¹ A special mat was always spread for Abd al-Muttalib in the shade of the Ka'bah, and none of his sons dared sit upon it. But when little Muhammad came and sat on the mat, Abd al-Muttalib did not remove him. When his sons tried to pull the boy away, the patriarch said: *"Leave my grandson alone. By Allah, he shall have a great affair."*¹

Abd al-Muttalib took Muhammad everywhere — to the councils of the Quraysh, to gatherings of honor, to the precincts of the Ka'bah. He made his orphaned grandson his closest companion. In those two brief years, Muhammad received the protection and love of Makkah's most powerful leader.

But destiny tested him again. In 578 CE, when Muhammad was approximately eight years old, Abd al-Muttalib died at an advanced age.⁵ Ibn Sa'd records his age as approximately 82, though some accounts mention 120 years — a figure likely symbolic in the Arab tradition for indicating extreme longevity.

Before his death, Abd al-Muttalib entrusted Muhammad's care to his son **Abu Talib** — Abdullah's full brother (both were sons of Fatimah bint Amrof the Banu Makhzum).¹ ² This choice was logical both by kinship and sentiment: Abu Talib was the uncle most closely related to Muhammad by blood.

## In Abu Talib's Care: The Early Orphan Years

Abu Talib was not a wealthy man. He was a trader of modest means supporting a large family. Yet he honored his father's dying wish with unwavering devotion. Al-Mubarakfuri notes that Abu Talib loved Muhammad more than his own children — he would not sleep unless Muhammad was at his side, and he would not travel without Muhammad accompanying him.²

Life in Abu Talib's household shaped Muhammad's character in profound ways. He learned about simplicity, hard work, and empathy for the less fortunate. He herded sheep for the people of Makkah to help supplement his uncle's household income — a profession he would later recall with pride.

Imam al-Bukhari records in his *Sahih* that the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ said:³

> مَا بَعَثَ اللَّهُ نَبِيًّا إِلَّا رَعَى الْغَنَمَ
>
> *"There was no prophet who did not herd sheep."* The companions asked: *"Even you, O Messenger of Allah?"* He replied: *"Yes, I used to herd sheep for the people of Makkah for a few qirats."*
> — Sahih al-Bukhari, no. 2262

## The Wisdom Behind Orphanhood: Reading the Divine Design

The orphanhood of Prophet Muhammad ﷺ was not merely a biographical fact — it was a spiritual foundation designed by Allah to prepare a messenger who would transform the course of human history. The Quran itself makes this orphanhood a point of remembrance and argument:

> أَلَمْ يَجِدْكَ يَتِيمًا فَآوَىٰ ﴿٦﴾ وَوَجَدَكَ ضَالًّا فَهَدَىٰ ﴿٧﴾ وَوَجَدَكَ عَائِلًا فَأَغْنَىٰ ﴿٨﴾
>
> *"Did He not find you an orphan and give you refuge? And He found you lost and guided you. And He found you in need and made you self-sufficient."*
> — QS. Ad-Dhuha (93): 6-8

Scholars have identified several dimensions of wisdom in this divine arrangement:

**First, independence from human influence.** Without a father shaping his worldview, Muhammad grew free from the ideological biases of patriarchal authority. He did not inherit the tradition of idol worship through a paternal chain of command. His education came directly from lived experience and, ultimately, from divine revelation.

**Second, authentic empathy.** One who has experienced loss, hunger, and dependence on the kindness of others possesses an empathy that cannot be taught through books. When the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ later championed the rights of orphans, the poor, and the oppressed, it was not political rhetoric — it was the voice of someone who had stood in their place.

**Third, proof of prophethood.** An orphan without formal education, without wealth, without a power network, who went on to unite the entire Arabian Peninsula and build a civilization that has endured for over fourteen centuries — this constitutes a powerful argument that his success was not the fruit of human effort alone, but divine assistance.

**Fourth, a model for the ummah.** In every society and every era, there are orphaned children. By making His final prophet an orphan, Allah elevated the dignity of every orphan on earth. The message is clear: one's social circumstances at birth do not determine one's destiny.

## Impact on Islamic Law and Ethics

The Prophet Muhammad's experience of orphanhood had a direct impact on Islamic legislation. The Quran repeatedly emphasizes the protection of orphans:

> فَأَمَّا الْيَتِيمَ فَلَا تَقْهَرْ ﴿٩﴾
>
> *"So as for the orphan, do not oppress him."*
> — QS. Ad-Dhuha (93): 9

> وَلَا تَقْرَبُوا مَالَ الْيَتِيمِ إِلَّا بِالَّتِي هِيَ أَحْسَنُ ﴿١٥٢﴾
>
> *"And do not approach the orphan's property except in a way that is best."*
> — QS. Al-An'am (6): 152

> إِنَّ الَّذِينَ يَأْكُلُونَ أَمْوَالَ الْيَتَامَىٰ ظُلْمًا إِنَّمَا يَأْكُلُونَ فِي بُطُونِهِمْ نَارًا ﴿١٠﴾
>
> *"Indeed, those who devour the property of orphans unjustly are only consuming fire into their bellies."*
> — QS. An-Nisa (4): 10

The Prophet Muhammad ﷺ himself made care for orphans a marker of faith. Imam al-Bukhari records:³

> أَنَا وَكَافِلُ الْيَتِيمِ فِي الْجَنَّةِ هَكَذَا
>
> *"I and the one who sponsors an orphan will be in Paradise like this"* — and he indicated his index and middle fingers held close together.
> — Sahih al-Bukhari, no. 5304

## Methodological Notes

In constructing this chronology, several points of scholarly disagreement warrant mention:

1. **Timing of Abdullah's death**: The majority*jumhur* of scholars, including Ibn Ishaq, Ibn Hisham, and Ibn Sa'd, hold that Abdullah died before the Prophet's birth.¹ ⁵ A minority, including one report attributed to Ibn Hazm, suggest he died several months after. The first opinion has stronger chains of transmission*isnad* and broader support among primary sources.

2. **Duration of nursing**: There is variation between two years (the standard nursing period) and four to five years (the total time spent with the Banu Sa'd).¹ ² The two-year figure refers to active nursing, while the longer duration encompasses the entire desert sojourn.

3. **Age of Abd al-Muttalib at death**: The most commonly cited figure is 82 years,⁵ though some accounts mention up to 120 years. The former is more historically plausible.

4. **The opening of the chest**: Narrated in *Sahih Muslim* from Anas ibn Malik, giving it the status of an authentic*sahih* hadith.⁴ Some modern scholars interpret it metaphorically. This article presents it as per the original narration while noting the existence of alternative interpretations.
