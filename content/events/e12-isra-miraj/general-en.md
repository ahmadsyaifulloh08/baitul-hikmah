# Isra' Mi'raj — The Night Journey and Ascension to the Heavens and the Commandment of the Five Daily Prayers (620 CE)

## Context

The event of Isra' Mi'raj occurred on the night of 27 Rajab in the eleventh year of prophethood (around 620 CE), though scholars differ regarding the precise date.¹ This event is one of the most momentous occasions in Islamic history — an extraordinary journey that carried **Prophet Muhammad SAW** from al-Masjid al-Haram in Mecca to al-Masjid al-Aqsa in Bayt al-Maqdis (Jerusalem), then ascending through seven layers of heaven to *Sidrat al-Muntaha*, the highest boundary ever reached by any created being.²

This event did not occur in a vacuum. It came at the lowest point of the Prophet Muhammad's life — after *'Am al-Huzn* (the Year of Sorrow) which claimed **Khadijah bint Khuwaylid** and **Abu Talib**, and after the bitter rejection at Ta'if that left physical and emotional wounds.³ It was in this condition that Allah the Almighty bestowed upon His Prophet the highest spiritual experience — a heavenly consolation that simultaneously carried the most important legislation in Islam: the obligation of the five daily prayers.⁴

Theologically, Isra' Mi'raj affirms the position of Prophet Muhammad SAW as the seal of the prophets and messengers, while simultaneously connecting the Islamic message with the earlier prophetic traditions — particularly through the congregational prayer with all the prophets at Bayt al-Maqdis.⁵

## The Events

### Part One: Isra' — The Night Journey from Mecca to Bayt al-Maqdis

#### The Opening of the Chest (*Shaqq al-Sadr*)

Prior to the Isra', the chest of Prophet Muhammad SAW was opened by the Angel **Jibril (Gabriel) AS**. In the narration of Sahih Muslim, the Prophet recounted:

> فُرِجَ عَنْ سَقْفِ بَيْتِي وَأَنَا بِمَكَّةَ فَنَزَلَ جِبْرِيلُ فَفَرَجَ صَدْرِي ثُمَّ غَسَلَهُ بِمَاءِ زَمْزَمَ ثُمَّ جَاءَ بِطَسْتٍ مِنْ ذَهَبٍ مُمْتَلِئٍ حِكْمَةً وَإِيمَانًا فَأَفْرَغَهُ فِي صَدْرِي ثُمَّ أَطْبَقَهُ
>
> *"The roof of my house was opened while I was in Mecca, then Jibril descended and opened my chest, then washed it with Zamzam water. Then he brought a golden basin filled with wisdom and faith, poured it into my chest, and then closed it."*
> — Narrated by Muslim, no. 162, Kitab al-Iman

This opening of the chest was a spiritual preparation so the Prophet could receive the extraordinary experience that lay ahead.⁶

#### The Journey on the Buraq

After this preparation, Jibril AS brought a mount called the *Buraq* — a white creature, between a donkey and a mule in size, whose every stride reached as far as the eye could see.⁷

> ثُمَّ أُتِيتُ بِدَابَّةٍ دُونَ الْبَغْلِ وَفَوْقَ الْحِمَارِ أَبْيَضَ يَضَعُ خَطْوَهُ عِنْدَ أَقْصَى طَرْفِهِ
>
> *"Then I was brought a riding beast, smaller than a mule and larger than a donkey, white, which placed its step at the farthest extent of its sight."*
> — Narrated by Muslim, no. 162, Kitab al-Iman

Prophet Muhammad SAW rode the Buraq alongside Jibril AS, traversing the journey from al-Masjid al-Haram in Mecca to al-Masjid al-Aqsa in Bayt al-Maqdis — a distance that under normal conditions would require months of caravan travel.⁸

#### Stops Along the Journey

In several narrations, Prophet Muhammad SAW stopped at several important locations during the Isra', among them:⁹

1. **Medina** (*Yathrib*) — the city that would later become the place of his emigration
2. **Mount Sinai** (*Tur Sina'*) — where Prophet Musa (Moses) AS received revelation
3. **Bethlehem** (*Bayt Lahm*) — the birthplace of Prophet Isa (Jesus) AS

At each location, Jibril AS asked the Prophet to dismount and perform two units of prayer, then informed him of the historical significance of the place.¹⁰

#### Congregational Prayer at al-Masjid al-Aqsa

Upon arriving at al-Masjid al-Aqsa, Prophet Muhammad SAW was greeted by all the previous prophets and messengers. He then led them in congregational prayer — a symbolic moment affirming that Muhammad's message is a continuation and perfection of all previous prophetic messages.¹¹

Ibn Kathir noted that the presence of all the prophets in that prayer demonstrated their acknowledgment of Muhammad SAW's spiritual leadership, and that all prophetic messages ultimately originate from the same source: Allah the Almighty.¹²

### Part Two: Mi'raj — The Ascension to the Heavens

#### The Journey Through Seven Heavens

After the prayer at al-Masjid al-Aqsa, Prophet Muhammad SAW and Jibril AS ascended to the heavens. At each layer of heaven, they were greeted and asked for permission to enter by the guardian angels. At each heaven, the Prophet also met previous prophets:¹³

1. **First heaven** — **Prophet Adam AS**, the father of all humankind. He welcomed Muhammad saying: "Welcome, O righteous son and righteous prophet."¹⁴
2. **Second heaven** — **Prophet Isa (Jesus) AS** and **Prophet Yahya (John the Baptist) AS**, maternal cousins to one another.¹⁵
3. **Third heaven** — **Prophet Yusuf (Joseph) AS**, who was given half of all beauty of creation.¹⁶
4. **Fourth heaven** — **Prophet Idris (Enoch) AS**, whom Allah raised to a high station.¹⁷
5. **Fifth heaven** — **Prophet Harun (Aaron) AS**, the brother of Prophet Musa.¹⁸
6. **Sixth heaven** — **Prophet Musa (Moses) AS**, who wept when Muhammad passed him, knowing that more of Muhammad's followers would enter Paradise than his own.¹⁹
7. **Seventh heaven** — **Prophet Ibrahim (Abraham) AS**, *khalilullah* (the beloved of Allah), who was reclining against al-Bayt al-Ma'mur — the celestial Ka'bah entered by 70,000 angels every day, never to return.²⁰

The complete narration of these encounters is found in Sahih al-Bukhari:

> فَانْطَلَقَ بِي جِبْرِيلُ حَتَّى أَتَى السَّمَاءَ الدُّنْيَا فَاسْتَفْتَحَ... قِيلَ مَنْ هَذَا قَالَ جِبْرِيلُ قِيلَ وَمَنْ مَعَكَ قَالَ مُحَمَّدٌ قِيلَ وَقَدْ أُرْسِلَ إِلَيْهِ قَالَ نَعَمْ قِيلَ مَرْحَبًا بِهِ فَنِعْمَ الْمَجِيءُ جَاءَ
>
> *"Then Jibril took me until we reached the lowest heaven, and he asked for it to be opened... It was asked: 'Who is this?' Jibril said: 'Jibril.' It was asked: 'And who is with you?' He said: 'Muhammad.' It was asked: 'Has he been sent for?' He said: 'Yes.' It was said: 'Welcome to him; what an excellent arrival has come.'"*
> — Narrated by al-Bukhari, no. 3207, Kitab Bad' al-Khalq

#### Sidrat al-Muntaha

After passing beyond the seventh heaven, Prophet Muhammad SAW reached *Sidrat al-Muntaha* — the lote tree at the farthest boundary, the frontier between the realm of creation and Divine majesty. Even Jibril AS stopped at this point, saying that he could not proceed further — were he to cross that boundary, his wings would burn.²¹

Allah the Almighty describes Sidrat al-Muntaha in the Qur'an:

> وَلَقَدْ رَآهُ نَزْلَةً أُخْرَىٰ ﴿١٣﴾ عِندَ سِدْرَةِ الْمُنتَهَىٰ ﴿١٤﴾ عِندَهَا جَنَّةُ الْمَأْوَىٰ ﴿١٥﴾ إِذْ يَغْشَى السِّدْرَةَ مَا يَغْشَىٰ ﴿١٦﴾ مَا زَاغَ الْبَصَرُ وَمَا طَغَىٰ ﴿١٧﴾
>
> *"And he certainly saw him in another descent, at Sidrat al-Muntaha. Near it is the Garden of Refuge. When there covered the lote tree that which covered it, the sight did not swerve, nor did it transgress its limit."*
> — QS. Al-Najm (53): 13–17

It was at Sidrat al-Muntaha that Prophet Muhammad SAW received revelation directly from Allah the Almighty — without the intermediary of any angel — an honor not bestowed upon any prophet before him.²²

#### The Commandment of the Five Daily Prayers

The most monumental moment of the Mi'raj was the prescription of the obligatory prayer. In the lengthy and detailed narration found in Sahih al-Bukhari and Sahih Muslim, it is recounted that Allah initially prescribed fifty prayers per day and night.²³

When Prophet Muhammad SAW descended and passed by Prophet Musa AS at the sixth heaven, Musa asked about what had been commanded. Upon hearing about the fifty prayers, Musa said:

"I have had experience with people before you. I struggled greatly with the Children of Israel. Your nation will not be able to bear that. Return to your Lord and ask for a reduction."²⁴

Prophet Muhammad SAW then returned to the presence of Allah and requested a reduction. Allah reduced ten prayers. However, Musa again advised him to ask for further reduction. This process repeated several times — each time the Prophet descended, Musa advised him to return — until the number was reduced to five prayers.²⁵

When Musa once more suggested asking for further reduction, Prophet Muhammad SAW replied:

> قَدْ رَاجَعْتُ رَبِّي حَتَّى اسْتَحْيَيْتُ فَمَا أَنَا بِرَاجِعٍ
>
> *"I have returned to my Lord repeatedly until I feel ashamed. I will not go back again."*
> — Narrated by al-Bukhari, no. 3207, Kitab Bad' al-Khalq

Then a proclamation from Allah the Almighty was heard:

> أَمْضَيْتُ فَرِيضَتِي وَخَفَّفْتُ عَنْ عِبَادِي... هِيَ خَمْسٌ وَهِيَ خَمْسُونَ لَا يُبَدَّلُ الْقَوْلُ لَدَيَّ
>
> *"I have established My obligation and lightened the burden upon My servants... They are five (in practice), yet they are fifty (in reward). The decree from Me shall not be changed."*
> — Narrated by al-Bukhari, no. 3207, Kitab Bad' al-Khalq

Thus, the five daily prayers were established with the reward of fifty prayers — a mercy and generosity from Allah the Almighty.²⁶

### Other Visions

During the Mi'raj journey, Prophet Muhammad SAW was also shown various sights, among them:²⁷

1. **Rivers of Paradise** — including rivers of milk, honey, wine (non-intoxicating), and clear water
2. **Al-Bayt al-Ma'mur** — the celestial Ka'bah entered by 70,000 angels daily
3. **Depictions of punishment and reward** — those who consumed usury were shown with enormous bellies unable to stand; those who consumed the wealth of orphans with lips like the lips of camels
4. **Paradise and Hellfire** — the Prophet was shown both as a warning and glad tidings for his community

### Return to Mecca

The entire journey of Isra' Mi'raj took place in a single night. When Prophet Muhammad SAW returned to Mecca, his bed was still warm and the ablution water he had spilled before departing had not yet dried — demonstrating that the journey took place in an extraordinarily brief span of time by worldly measures.²⁸

## Dialogue and Sacred Texts

### Qur'anic Evidence of the Isra'

The event of the Isra' is immortalized in the opening of Surah al-Isra':

> سُبْحَانَ الَّذِي أَسْرَىٰ بِعَبْدِهِ لَيْلًا مِّنَ الْمَسْجِدِ الْحَرَامِ إِلَى الْمَسْجِدِ الْأَقْصَى الَّذِي بَارَكْنَا حَوْلَهُ لِنُرِيَهُ مِنْ آيَاتِنَا ۚ إِنَّهُ هُوَ السَّمِيعُ الْبَصِيرُ ﴿١﴾
>
> *"Exalted is He who took His servant by night from al-Masjid al-Haram to al-Masjid al-Aqsa, whose surroundings We have blessed, to show him of Our signs. Indeed, He is the All-Hearing, the All-Seeing."*
> — QS. Al-Isra' (17): 1

The use of the word *subhana* (Exalted) at the opening of the verse indicates the grandeur and extraordinariness of this event, while simultaneously affirming that it lies within Allah's power alone.²⁹

### The Reaction of Meccan Society

The next morning, when Prophet Muhammad SAW recounted his Isra' Mi'raj experience to the people of Mecca, the reactions were varied. The Quraysh laughed and mocked him. Some who had already embraced Islam even wavered and abandoned their faith.³⁰

However, **Abu Bakr al-Siddiq** gave a response that became the standard of faith. When the Quraysh came to him and asked: "Do you believe that your companion claims to have traveled to Bayt al-Maqdis in a single night?" Abu Bakr replied:

"If he said it, then he is truthful. Indeed, I believe him in what is greater than that — I believe him concerning the news from the heavens that comes morning and evening."³¹

It was because of this statement that Abu Bakr received the title *al-Siddiq* (the Truthful, the one who always affirms the truth).³²

To test the veracity of the Prophet's claim, the Quraysh asked him to describe al-Masjid al-Aqsa — a building he had never physically visited before. Allah then displayed al-Masjid al-Aqsa before the Prophet so that he could describe it in detail, including the number of doors and its architecture. Quraysh travelers who had been to Bayt al-Maqdis confirmed the accuracy of the description.³³

## Analysis and Lessons

### The Physical and Spiritual Nature of Isra' Mi'raj

Among scholars, there has been extensive discussion about whether the Isra' Mi'raj occurred in both body and soul, or only in soul (as a dream). The majority of Sunni scholars — including Ibn Abbas, Ibn Hisham, al-Tabari, and Ibn Kathir — maintain that the event occurred in both body and soul simultaneously while the Prophet was awake, not as a dream. The main arguments are:³⁴

1. If it were merely a dream, there would be no reason for the Quraysh to reject and mock it, as dreams are ordinary occurrences
2. The Qur'an uses the word *'abdihi* (His servant), which encompasses both body and soul
3. Authentic hadiths describe physical details of the journey (the Buraq mount, the opening of the chest)

### Prayer as a Daily Mi'raj

One of the most profound wisdoms of the Mi'raj is that prayer — the only act of worship commanded directly by Allah without an intermediary — was given at the highest place ever reached by any created being. This affirms the status of prayer as *mi'raj al-mu'min* (the mi'raj of the believer) — the means by which every Muslim "ascends" to draw closer to Allah each day.³⁵

The negotiation process between Prophet Muhammad SAW and Prophet Musa AS regarding the number of prayers also contains wisdom about Allah's mercy and the concern prophets have for one another. Musa, having had experience with the Children of Israel, knew the limitations of human beings and urged Muhammad to seek reduction — a form of prophetic solidarity that transcends the boundaries of time and community.³⁶

### The Connection of Three Sacred Mosques

Isra' Mi'raj connects the three holiest mosques in Islam: al-Masjid al-Haram (Mecca), al-Masjid al-Aqsa (Jerusalem), and al-Bayt al-Ma'mur (the heavens). This connection affirms that Islam is not a religion confined to a single geographic region, but rather a universal message linking earth and heaven, past and future, and the entire prophetic tradition from Adam to Muhammad.³⁷

### A Test of Faith

The reaction of Meccan society to the news of Isra' Mi'raj served as a test of faith that distinguished those who believed sincerely from those who believed half-heartedly. Abu Bakr's response — "If he said it, then he is truthful" — became the model of ideal faith: accepting reports of the unseen not through logic alone, but through complete trust in the truthfulness of the one who bears the news.³⁸

### Consolation After Sorrow

In the chronological context of the *sirah*, Isra' Mi'raj is heaven's answer to earthly suffering. After the loss of Khadijah and Abu Talib, after the humiliation at Ta'if, Allah raised His Prophet beyond all creation — as if to say that the world may reject, but the heavens open their gates wide. This pattern — deep sorrow followed by magnificent grace — becomes an eternal source of comfort for every believer facing trials.³⁹
