# The Death of Prophet Muhammad ﷺ — 12 Rabi al-Awwal 11 AH

## Context: Signs of Farewell

In the months preceding his death, Prophet Muhammad ﷺ gave subtle indications that his prophetic mission was nearing its end. The revelation of Surah An-Nasr during the Farewell Hajj was understood by several senior companions — particularly **Abu Bakr as-Siddiq** and Abdullah ibn Abbas — as a notification of the Prophet's approaching death.^1

Imam al-Bukhari narrates that Abdullah ibn Abbas said: Umar ibn al-Khattab once invited me to a gathering of the veterans of Badr — though some objected due to my young age. Umar asked about the meaning of Surah An-Nasr. When the others were silent, I answered: *"It is a notification of the approaching death of the Messenger of Allah ﷺ."* Umar said: *"I know nothing of its meaning except what you have stated."*^1

During Ramadan of 10 AH, the Prophet ﷺ performed *i'tikaf* (spiritual retreat) for twenty days — twice his usual ten days. He also reviewed the Quran with Jibril twice in his final year — whereas he normally reviewed it once each Ramadan. Al-Bukhari narrates that the Prophet ﷺ told his daughter **Fatimah**: *"Jibril used to review the Quran with me once every year, but this year he reviewed it twice. I can only see this as a sign that my death is near."*^1 ^4

Fatimah wept upon hearing this. But the Prophet ﷺ then whispered to her again, and this time she smiled. When **Aisha** later asked what he had said, Fatimah revealed that the second whisper was news that she would be the foremost among the women of Paradise and the first of his family to join him.^1

## The Onset of Illness: The Night at Baqi'

Imam al-Bukhari and Ibn Hisham narrate that the Prophet's illness began at the end of the month of Safar, 11 AH. One night, the Prophet ﷺ awoke his servant Abu Muwayhib and said: *"I have been commanded to seek forgiveness for the people of Baqi'."*^1 ^2

They went to the Baqi' cemetery in the darkness of night. There, the Prophet ﷺ stood among the graves of the companions who had preceded him and prayed:

> السَّلَامُ عَلَيْكُمْ يَا أَهْلَ الْمَقَابِرِ لِيَهْنِ لَكُمْ مَا أَصْبَحْتُمْ فِيهِ مِمَّا أَصْبَحَ فِيهِ النَّاسُ أَقْبَلَتِ الْفِتَنُ كَقِطَعِ اللَّيْلِ الْمُظْلِمِ
>
> *"Peace be upon you, O dwellers of the graves. Glad tidings to you for the state you are in compared to what the living face. Trials approach like patches of dark night."*
> — Narrated by Muslim, Kitab al-Jana'iz

Then he told Abu Muwayhib: *"I have been offered the keys to the treasures of this world and eternal life therein followed by Paradise, or to meet my Lord and Paradise. I have chosen to meet my Lord and Paradise."*^2 After that night, the Prophet ﷺ began to suffer severe headaches.

## Days of Illness in Aisha's Chamber

### Moving to Aisha's Room

The Prophet ﷺ maintained a rotating schedule among the rooms of his wives. As the illness grew worse, he began asking repeatedly: *"Where am I tomorrow? Where am I tomorrow?"* His wives understood that he wished to be in Aisha's room, so they all permitted him to remain there.^1

**Aisha bint Abu Bakr** narrated her experience of caring for the Prophet ﷺ during his illness. She recounted that the Prophet ﷺ would lean against her chest or rest in her lap, and she would recite the *mu'awwidhat* (protective surahs) while passing his own hand over his body — for the Prophet's hand carried more blessing than anyone else's.^1

### Severe Fever

Al-Bukhari narrates that the Prophet ﷺ suffered from extremely high fever. Abdullah ibn Mas'ud once touched the Prophet's body and exclaimed: *"O Messenger of Allah, you are suffering a very severe fever!"* The Prophet ﷺ replied:

> إِنِّي أُوعَكُ كَمَا يُوعَكُ رَجُلَانِ مِنْكُمْ
>
> *"Indeed, I suffer from fever as severely as two men among you."*
> — Narrated by al-Bukhari, no. 5648

Ibn Mas'ud asked: *"Is that because you receive a double reward?"* The Prophet ﷺ answered: *"Yes, that is so."*^1 Even in his most acute suffering, the Prophet ﷺ transformed every moment into a lesson for his community.

### The Final Address in the Mosque

Despite his severe illness, the Prophet ﷺ exerted himself to ascend the mosque pulpit a few days before his death. Al-Bukhari narrates that he said:

> إِنَّ عَبْدًا خَيَّرَهُ اللَّهُ بَيْنَ أَنْ يُؤْتِيَهُ مِنْ زَهْرَةِ الدُّنْيَا مَا شَاءَ وَبَيْنَ مَا عِنْدَهُ فَاخْتَارَ مَا عِنْدَهُ
>
> *"Indeed, there is a servant whom Allah has given the choice between the splendor of this world, as much as he wishes, and what is with Allah — and he has chosen what is with Allah."*
> — Narrated by al-Bukhari, no. 466

**Abu Bakr** immediately wept upon hearing this — he alone understood that the "servant" was the Prophet ﷺ himself, and that this meant farewell was imminent.^1 The other companions wondered at Abu Bakr's tears — why would he weep over mention of "a servant"? They would understand the meaning only later.

The Prophet ﷺ then ordered that all doors opening onto the mosque be sealed, except the door of Abu Bakr — a signal that Abu Bakr would be the one to lead the community after him.^1

### The Command for Abu Bakr to Lead the Prayer

When the Prophet ﷺ could no longer lead the prayer himself, he commanded: *"Tell Abu Bakr to lead the people in prayer."* Aisha tried to redirect this duty to Umar, knowing her father Abu Bakr would weep. But the Prophet ﷺ insisted:

> مُرُوا أَبَا بَكْرٍ فَلْيُصَلِّ بِالنَّاسِ ... إِنَّكُنَّ لَأَنْتُنَّ صَوَاحِبُ يُوسُفَ
>
> *"Tell Abu Bakr to lead the people in prayer ... You are indeed like the female companions of Yusuf."*
> — Narrated by al-Bukhari, no. 713

His meaning was that, just as the women in the story of Prophet Yusuf displayed one thing while concealing another intention, Aisha's real reason was personal — not that Abu Bakr was unworthy.^1

Abu Bakr led the prayer seventeen times during the Prophet's illness.^3 During one prayer, the Prophet ﷺ felt slightly better and came out to the mosque supported by Ali ibn Abi Talib and al-Fadl ibn Abbas. Seeing the Prophet ﷺ approach, Abu Bakr stepped back from the imam's position, but the Prophet ﷺ gestured for him to remain. The Prophet ﷺ sat beside Abu Bakr and prayed together — Abu Bakr followed the Prophet's movements, and the congregation followed Abu Bakr.^1

## The Final Day: Monday, 12 Rabi al-Awwal 11 AH

### The Last Dawn

Imam al-Bukhari narrates from Anas ibn Malik: on Monday morning, as Abu Bakr was leading the Fajr prayer, the Prophet ﷺ drew aside the curtain of Aisha's room that faced the mosque. The companions saw his face — radiant as a page of the Quran. They nearly abandoned their prayer from sheer joy, believing the Prophet ﷺ had recovered.^1

The Prophet ﷺ smiled at the sight of the neatly ordered rows, then gestured for them to continue their prayer. He let the curtain fall once more. That was the last time the companions saw the face of Prophet Muhammad ﷺ in full.

### The Final Moments

Aisha narrated the final moments of the Prophet's life in vivid detail. He was resting against her chest. Abdurrahman ibn Abu Bakr (Aisha's brother) entered carrying a *siwak* (tooth stick). The Prophet ﷺ gazed at the *siwak*, and Aisha understood that he wanted it. She took it, chewed it to soften it, then gave it to the Prophet ﷺ. He cleaned his teeth thoroughly — more thoroughly than Aisha had ever seen.^1

Then the Prophet ﷺ raised his hand — or his finger — and looked upward. Aisha heard him say:

> مَعَ الَّذِينَ أَنْعَمَ اللَّهُ عَلَيْهِمْ مِنَ النَّبِيِّينَ وَالصِّدِّيقِينَ وَالشُّهَدَاءِ وَالصَّالِحِينَ
>
> *"With those upon whom Allah has bestowed favor among the prophets, the truthful, the martyrs, and the righteous."*

Then he said:

> اللَّهُمَّ اغْفِرْ لِي وَارْحَمْنِي وَأَلْحِقْنِي بِالرَّفِيقِ الْأَعْلَى
>
> *"O Allah, forgive me, have mercy on me, and join me with al-Rafiq al-A'la (the Highest Companion)."*
> — Narrated by al-Bukhari, no. 4440

He repeated "al-Rafiq al-A'la" three times. Then his hand fell, and he departed to be with Allah. Aisha said: *"He passed away between my chin and my chest."*^1

Ibn Sa'd in *Tabaqat al-Kubra* records that it was the time of Duha (mid-morning) on Monday, 12 Rabi al-Awwal, 11 AH — corresponding to June 8, 632 CE.^3 He was sixty-three years old.

## The Companions' Reactions

### Umar ibn al-Khattab: Denial and Anguish

The news of the Prophet's death struck the companions with unimaginable force. **Umar ibn al-Khattab** — a man renowned for his strength and resolve — refused to accept reality. Al-Bukhari narrates that Umar stood before the people and swore:

*"By Allah, the Messenger of Allah has not died! He has gone to meet his Lord just as Musa went to meet his Lord and was absent from his people for forty nights. By Allah, the Messenger of Allah will return and sever the hands and feet of those who claim he has died!"*^1

Umar continued to speak passionately, threatening anyone who said the Prophet ﷺ had died. The listeners were confused — some believed him, others doubted.

### Abu Bakr: Steadfastness in Crisis

While Umar was still speaking, **Abu Bakr as-Siddiq** arrived at the mosque. He had been at his residence in Sunh (on the outskirts of Madinah) when he heard the news. He went directly to Aisha's room, uncovered the cloth from the Prophet's face, kissed his forehead, and said: *"By my father and mother, O Messenger of Allah. You are good in life and good in death."*^1

Abu Bakr then came out to the mosque. Umar was still speaking. Abu Bakr asked him to be silent, but Umar continued shouting. Abu Bakr did not force the matter — he began speaking on his own, and the people left Umar to listen to Abu Bakr.

Abu Bakr praised Allah, then spoke the words that would echo through the ages of Islamic history:

> مَنْ كَانَ مِنْكُمْ يَعْبُدُ مُحَمَّدًا فَإِنَّ مُحَمَّدًا قَدْ مَاتَ وَمَنْ كَانَ مِنْكُمْ يَعْبُدُ اللَّهَ فَإِنَّ اللَّهَ حَيٌّ لَا يَمُوتُ
>
> *"Whoever among you worshipped Muhammad — then Muhammad has died. And whoever among you worshipped Allah — then Allah is Ever-Living and shall never die."*
> — Narrated by al-Bukhari, no. 3667

Then Abu Bakr recited the Quranic verse:

> وَمَا مُحَمَّدٌ إِلَّا رَسُولٌ قَدْ خَلَتْ مِن قَبْلِهِ الرُّسُلُ ۚ أَفَإِن مَّاتَ أَوْ قُتِلَ انقَلَبْتُمْ عَلَىٰ أَعْقَابِكُمْ ﴿١٤٤﴾
>
> *"Muhammad is not but a messenger. Other messengers have passed before him. So if he were to die or be killed, would you turn back on your heels?"*
> — QS. Ali Imran (3): 144

Al-Bukhari narrates that when Abu Bakr recited this verse, the people felt as though they were hearing it for the first time — even though they had memorized it years ago. Umar collapsed to the ground — his legs could no longer support him. He said: *"By Allah, it was as though I had never heard this verse until Abu Bakr recited it."*^1

### Grief Engulfing Madinah

Ibn Sa'd in *Tabaqat al-Kubra* describes Madinah on that day as a city submerged in grief unlike anything it had known before. Anas ibn Malik said: *"The day the Messenger of Allah ﷺ came to Madinah, everything was radiant. And the day he died, everything turned dark. We had scarcely dusted off our hands from burying him before we felt our very hearts had changed."*^3

## Washing, Funeral Prayer, and Burial

### Washing the Body

Ibn Sa'd narrates that the Prophet's body was washed by Ali ibn Abi Talib, al-Abbas ibn Abd al-Muttalib (the Prophet's uncle), al-Fadl and Qutham (both sons of al-Abbas), Usamah ibn Zayd, and Shuqran (the Prophet's servant). Ali performed the actual washing while al-Abbas and his sons helped turn the body.^3

Ali remarked during the washing: *"By my father and mother, how fragrant you are — in life and in death."*^3 The Prophet ﷺ was washed without his garments being removed — a distinction unique to him.

### Funeral Prayer

What was unique about the Prophet's funeral was that **no single imam led the prayer**. The companions entered in waves — group after group — and each prayed individually over his body. Men first, then women, then children.^1 ^3

This process continued from Monday until the middle of the night between Tuesday and Wednesday.

### Burial

The companions initially disagreed about the burial site. Some proposed the mosque, others proposed Baqi'. Abu Bakr resolved the matter by conveying a hadith he had heard from the Prophet ﷺ:

> مَا قَبَضَ اللَّهُ نَبِيًّا إِلَّا فِي الْمَوْضِعِ الَّذِي يُحِبُّ أَنْ يُدْفَنَ فِيهِ
>
> *"Allah does not take the soul of a prophet except in the place where he wishes to be buried."*
> — Narrated by al-Tirmidhi

Thus the Prophet ﷺ was buried in Aisha's room — the place where he had died — beneath his bed. The grave was dug by Abu Talhah al-Ansari, who constructed a *lahd* (a lateral niche in the side of the grave, the burial style of Madinah).^3

The burial took place in the middle of the night on Wednesday. The last person to descend into the grave was Qutham ibn al-Abbas.

## Analysis: The Meaning of the Prophet's Death for Islamic Civilization

### The End of Revelation, the Beginning of Responsibility

With the death of Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, revelation from heaven was severed forever. No prophet would come after him — this is a foundational belief of Islam. Yet the end of revelation did not mean the end of guidance; the Quran and Sunnah he left behind became an eternal compass for humanity.

### The Test of Leadership

The moments following the Prophet's death constituted the most severe test of leadership in Islamic history. The contrast between Umar's reaction (emotional, in denial) and Abu Bakr's response (composed, decisive, rooted in the Quran) illustrates why Abu Bakr was destined to become the first caliph. True leadership is tested not when all is well, but when crisis strikes.

### The Humanity of the Prophet ﷺ

One of the most important lessons from the Prophet's death is the affirmation of his humanity. Prophet Muhammad ﷺ was not God — he was a human being who fell ill, suffered, and ultimately died like all others. Yet the way he faced death — with prayer, surrender to Allah, and compassion for those closest to him — made his very death a final lesson for his community.

### Eternal Love

The relationship between the Prophet ﷺ and his companions — especially Aisha who nursed him with devoted tenderness, Abu Bakr who demonstrated unwavering faith in the darkest moment, and Umar whose emotional reaction revealed the depth of his love — represents the most beautiful portrait of human bonds in Islamic history.

## Lessons: Teachings from the Darkest Day

The death of the Messenger of Allah ﷺ teaches Muslims several fundamental lessons:

**First**, that Islam does not depend on a single individual — however great that individual may be — but on the teachings he brought. Abu Bakr reminded: *"Whoever worshipped Muhammad — Muhammad has died. Whoever worshipped Allah — Allah is Ever-Living and shall never die."*

**Second**, that death is a certainty for every creature. If the Prophet ﷺ, the most beloved of Allah, died, then no one is immortal in this world.

**Third**, that how a person faces crisis defines their character. Abu Bakr became the model for managing grief — he wept, kissed the Prophet's forehead, then rose and led the community. Grief did not destroy him; he transformed his sorrow into strength.

**Fourth**, that the Prophet's final words — choosing "al-Rafiq al-A'la" (the Highest Companion) — demonstrate that for the believer, death is not an ending, but a reunion with Allah and His beloved ones.
