# The Founding of Al-Qarawiyyin University (859 CE)

## Historical Context and Regional Politics

In the mid-9th century CE, North Africa experienced significant political and intellectual transformation. The Abbasid Dynasty in Baghdad began losing control over distant territories, while local powers started consolidating their authority. In the Maghreb (western North Africa), the **Idrisid Dynasty** founded by **Idris ibn Abdillah** in 788 CE had successfully created the first independent Islamic kingdom in Morocco.^1

**Idris II** (793-828 CE), son of the dynasty's founder, moved the capital from Volubilis to the newly established **Fez**. This city quickly developed into an important commercial and cultural center, attracting merchants and scholars from throughout the Mediterranean region and sub-Saharan Africa. Fez's strategic position as a link between Andalusia, the Maghreb, and sub-Saharan Africa made it a unique civilizational melting pot.^2

Within the broader intellectual context, the 9th century represented the pinnacle of the **Islamic Golden Age**. In Baghdad, the **Baitul Hikmah** under Caliph al-Ma'mun became the world's leading center for translation and scientific research. In Cordoba, **Abd al-Rahman II** developed libraries and academies that rivaled Baghdad. Fez, with its population comprising Arabs, Berbers, Andalusians, and merchants from various lands, was ready to become the third center of learning in the great trio of Islamic civilization.^3

## An Extraordinary Figure: Fatimah bint Muhammad Al-Fihri

**Fatimah bint Muhammad Al-Fihri** was born into a family originating from **Kairouan**, an important intellectual and religious center in Tunisia.^4 Her father, **Muhammad ibn Abdillah Al-Fihri**, was a successful merchant who migrated from Kairouan to Fez in the early 9th century to take advantage of economic opportunities offered by the rapidly developing Idrisid capital.

The Al-Fihri family belonged to the intellectual elite of Kairouan, which had a strong tradition in religious learning and knowledge. Fatimah grew up in an environment that highly valued learning, where the family's private library contained an impressive collection of manuscripts on theology, Islamic law, history, and Arabic literature. Her education included not only Quranic and Hadith studies but also literature, basic mathematics, and commercial principles important for merchant families.^5

When Muhammad Al-Fihri passed away, he left considerable wealth to Fatimah and her sister **Maryam**. This wealth consisted of urban properties in Fez, agricultural lands around the city, shares in trans-Saharan trade caravans, and investments in the textile and leather craft industries that were Fez's specialties. According to modern historians' calculations, this inheritance was equivalent to hundreds of thousands of gold dinars—a fantastical sum by 9th-century standards.^1

## The Founding Process: From Vision to Realization

### Motivation and Initial Vision

Fatimah's decision to use her entire inheritance to build an educational institution was not spontaneous. She had long contemplated the fragmented state of education in Fez. Although various *halaqah* (learning circles) existed in different mosques, there was no institution providing systematic and comprehensive education on a large scale.

Fatimah was also inspired by developments in educational institutions in other centers of Islamic civilization. She heard about the progress of the **Baitul Hikmah** in Baghdad and the great mosques in Cordoba that functioned not only as places of worship but also as universities. Her vision was to create something similar in Fez—an institution that could rival learning centers in the East and provide a unique contribution to Islamic civilization.^6

In discussions with Fez's leading scholars, Fatimah outlined her vision: "Fez has the potential to become a beacon of knowledge in the Maghreb. But we need an institution that not only teaches religious sciences but also worldly sciences needed for the progress of the ummah. This is a trust that Allah has given us through abundant provisions."^2

### Physical Construction Process

Construction began in 859 CE with the selection of a strategic location in the heart of Fez. The chosen site was approximately 1,200 square meters in the area that later became known as **Fez el Bali** (Old Fez). This area was selected for its accessibility from various parts of the city and its proximity to the commercial center.^7

Fatimah adopted a very hands-on approach to the construction process. She not only provided funding but was also directly involved in architectural planning, construction material selection, and construction supervision. The building design combined traditional Maghreb architectural elements with innovations adapted for educational purposes.

The **main mosque** was built with a mihrab facing Mecca and could accommodate about 20,000 worshippers. The **library area** was designed with special ventilation systems to maintain humidity and protect manuscripts. **Learning rooms** (*majlis*) were built with good acoustics for discussions and teaching. **Dormitories** for out-of-town students were also provided, complete with shared kitchen facilities and recreational areas.^1

### Extraordinary Spiritual Dedication

The most remarkable aspect of the construction process was Fatimah's unparalleled spiritual dedication. According to records by **Abu Imran al-Fasi**, a contemporary historian, Fatimah fasted every day during the construction process that lasted nearly two years. She only broke her fast after performing the **first Maghrib prayer** in the newly completed mihrab in 860 CE.^8

This spiritual ritual demonstrated not only personal piety but also deep understanding of the **concept of waqf** in Islam. For Fatimah, building Al-Qarawiyyin was not merely a construction project but comprehensive worship involving wealth, time, energy, and spirituality.

## Curriculum Development and Methodology

### Initial Phase: Transformation from Halaqah to Institution

In the initial phase (860-900 CE), educational activities at Al-Qarawiyyin still followed the traditional *halaqah* model—informal learning circles where a sheikh taught a group of students around mosque pillars. However, gradually this system developed into something more structured with regular schedules, standard curricula, and clear evaluation methods.^9

**Qurra' al-Sab'ah** (Seven Quranic Readers) became the first core subject, where students learned various qira'at (recitation styles) of the Quran according to the tradition of seven famous qira'at imams. **Maliki Fiqh** became the foundation of Islamic law learning, considering that the Maghreb traditionally followed the Maliki school. **Nahwu and Sarf** (Arabic grammar and morphology) were taught based on the works of Sibawayh and al-Khalil ibn Ahmad.^6

### Expansion to Rational Sciences (900-1000 CE)

In the 10th century, under the leadership of several visionary scholars, Al-Qarawiyyin's curriculum was expanded to include rational sciences. **Mathematics** was taught based on the works of al-Khwarizmi and al-Battani, **astronomy** used *zij* (astronomical tables) from Ptolemaic and Indian traditions, **medicine** referred to Galen's works translated into Arabic, and **philosophy** of Aristotle in Arabic versions began to be introduced.^10

This curriculum expansion sparked heated theological debate. Conservative groups, influenced by the **Ahl al-Hadith** movement, considered rational sciences as bid'ah (innovation) that endangered doctrinal purity. Conversely, progressive groups, inspired by **Mu'tazilah** and later **Ash'ariyyah** traditions, argued that reason and knowledge were tools for understanding Allah's magnificence.

This controversy was resolved through brilliant synthesis by **Abu Hamid al-Ghazali** (1058-1111 CE), who taught at Al-Qarawiyyin during his visit to the Maghreb. Al-Ghazali demonstrated that rational sciences, when studied with proper intention and within the framework of faith, actually strengthened belief in Allah.^2

### Innovative Learning Methodologies

Al-Qarawiyyin developed several learning methodologies that later became standard in Islamic universities:

**Ijazah System**: Every student who completed learning under a sheikh received an **ijazah** (certificate) stating their competence in a particular field. This system created clear quality standards and accountability in education.^11

**Munazarah and Jadal**: Scholarly debate became an integral part of learning methodology. Every Friday, **munazarah kubra** (great debates) were held on controversial topics, where senior students and professors argued based on strong evidence.

**Rihlah 'Ilmiyyah**: Student and professor exchange programs with other institutions, such as Al-Azhar in Cairo, Zaytuna in Tunis, and even Cordoba in Andalusia, became a tradition that enriched intellectual perspectives.

## Distinguished Students and Alumni

### Classical Period (900-1200 CE)

Al-Qarawiyyin attracted students from throughout the Islamic world and even from Europe. Some great names who studied there include:

**Ibn Battuta** (1304-1368 CE), the greatest Muslim traveler in history, began his education at Al-Qarawiyyin before starting his legendary journeys. He later wrote: "Al-Qarawiyyin gave me not only knowledge, but also curiosity about the world that drove me to travel."^3

**Ibn Khaldun** (1332-1406 CE), the father of modern sociology, spent an important period of his life in Fez and taught at Al-Qarawiyyin. His masterpiece, *Muqaddimah*, was written based on his observations and reflections on civilizational dynamics he witnessed in the Maghreb.^4

**Al-Idrisi** (1100-1165 CE), the greatest Muslim geographer, studied mathematics and astronomy at Al-Qarawiyyin before moving to Sicily to serve King Roger II. His world map, *Tabula Rogeriana*, remained the most accurate until the 16th century.^12

### Non-Muslim Students

One of Al-Qarawiyyin's most interesting aspects was its openness to non-Muslim students, particularly from Europe. **Gerbert d'Aurillac** (946-1003 CE), who later became **Pope Sylvester II**, studied mathematics and astronomy at Al-Qarawiyyin. He introduced Arabic numerals (0-9) to Europe and became one of the key figures in the Medieval Renaissance.^5

**Moses Maimonides** (1135-1204 CE), the greatest Jewish philosopher of the Middle Ages, also attended philosophy and medicine lectures at Al-Qarawiyyin while his family lived in Fez. His monumental work, *Guide for the Perplexed*, shows strong influence from Islamic philosophical traditions he learned there.^6

## The Library: World's Intellectual Treasury

The **Al-Qarawiyyin Library** developed into one of the largest and most important in the Islamic world. By the 12th century, its collection had reached more than 120,000 manuscripts in various languages: Arabic, Berber, Latin, Hebrew, and even some in local African languages.^7

The cataloging system used methods developed by **Ibn al-Nadim** in *Kitab al-Fihrist*, with special modifications for Al-Qarawiyyin's needs. Manuscripts were classified by subject (ulum diniyyah, ulum aqliyyah, adab), then by author, and finally by transcription year. This system enabled efficient searches within the very large collection.

Al-Qarawiyyin's **scriptorium** produced about 500-800 new manuscripts annually, both copies of classical works and original works by professors and senior students. The quality of calligraphy and illumination (decoration) of Al-Qarawiyyin manuscripts reached very high artistic standards, rivaling productions from Baghdad and Cordoba.^1

### Preservation and Conservation Programs

Al-Qarawiyyin became a pioneer in manuscript preservation techniques. **Abu Abdillah al-Fasi** (d. 1166 CE), the brilliant head librarian, developed storage methods in cedar cabinets resistant to termites and humidity. He also created a manuscript rotation system to reduce damage from excessive use.

The **tashih** (correction) manuscript program was conducted systematically, where expert teams compared various versions of the same work to produce the most accurate edition. This process was crucial considering copying errors could drastically change text meaning.^8

## Influence on European Universities

### Organizational Model and Curriculum

Al-Qarawiyyin's influence on European university development was significant, although often unrecognized in conventional historical narratives. The **faculty system** later adopted by Oxford, Cambridge, and the Sorbonne was first developed at Al-Qarawiyyin with clear divisions between faculties of religion, law, medicine, and philosophy.

The **tiered degree system** (baccalaureus, magister, doctor) that became the European university standard also originated from Al-Qarawiyyin's ijazah system that distinguished between **ijazah amma** (general ijazah), **ijazah khassa** (special ijazah), and **ijazah iftitah** (teaching license).^13

### Knowledge Transfer

European students who studied at Al-Qarawiyyin became very important agents of knowledge transfer. They brought not only texts in Arabic but also learning methodologies, academic organizational systems, and scientific mindsets they learned in Fez.

**Daniel of Morley** (fl. 1170-1190), an English scholar who studied at Al-Qarawiyyin, later became one of the translators of Aristotelian works from Arabic to Latin. **Michael Scot** (1175-1232), who was also at Al-Qarawiyyin, translated works by Ibn Rushd and Ibn Sina that later influenced Thomas Aquinas and European scholasticism.^9

## Golden Age and Decline

### Peak Glory (1100-1350 CE)

The period between the 12th and 14th centuries became Al-Qarawiyyin's golden age. The **Almoravid** (1040-1147 CE) and later **Almohad** (1121-1269 CE) dynasties provided great financial and political support to the university. Major renovations and expansions were undertaken, including additions of dormitories, new libraries, and astronomical observatories.^10

During this period, Al-Qarawiyyin had more than 8,000 regular students and about 1,200 professors and academic staff. Students came from throughout the Islamic world: Andalusia, Maghreb, Egypt, Hejaz, Iraq, Persia, and even from Central Asia and India. Its reputation as an international center of learning reached its peak.^2

### Challenges and Adaptation (1350-1500 CE)

The 14th and 15th centuries brought major challenges. The **Black Death** (1347-1351) drastically reduced student and professor populations. The **Reconquista** in Andalusia caused waves of Muslim refugees that restructured Maghreb demographics. **Economic decline** due to shifts in world trade routes reduced financial support.

However, Al-Qarawiyyin showed extraordinary resilience. **Curriculum reforms** were implemented to focus more on society's practical needs. **Community service programs** were expanded, including free clinics, legal consultation, and literacy programs. **Funding source diversification** through partnerships with merchant guilds helped maintain operational sustainability.^11

## Contributions to Knowledge Preservation and Transmission

### Transition Period (1500-1800 CE)

When other centers of Islamic civilization experienced decline or destruction—Baghdad fell to the Mongols (1258), Cordoba to Castile (1236), Constantinople to the Ottomans (1453)—Al-Qarawiyyin became one of the last guardians of classical Islamic intellectual heritage. Thousands of manuscripts from various destroyed libraries were saved and brought to Fez.^3

**Mass copying programs** were conducted to ensure important works were not lost. Teams of scribes worked day and night to make copies of works by Ibn Sina, Ibn Rushd, al-Ghazali, Ibn Khaldun, and hundreds of other scholars. Without this effort, many classical Islamic works might have been lost forever.

### Modernization and Continuity (1800-present)

The 19th and 20th centuries brought modernization challenges. French colonial penetration in Morocco (1912) threatened Al-Qarawiyyin's existence as an independent institution. However, the intellectual and political struggles of Moroccan scholars and nationalists successfully maintained its autonomy.^4

**King Mohammed V** and later **Hassan II** provided great support for modernizing Al-Qarawiyyin without eliminating its character as a traditional Islamic university. Modern faculties were added: engineering, medicine, economics, and sciences, while traditional faculties were maintained.

## International Recognition and Modern Legacy

In 1963, **Guinness World Records** officially recognized Al-Qarawiyyin as the "world's oldest continuously operating university." **UNESCO** also provided similar recognition and included Al-Qarawiyyin's library in the **Memory of the World** program as humanity's documentary heritage.^12

Today, Al-Qarawiyyin continues to operate with about 8,000 students from 40 countries. Its curriculum combines traditional Islamic studies with modern programs in sciences, technology, and humanities. The library, now containing more than 400,000 volumes, remains an important research center for Islamic studies and Maghreb history.

## Wisdom and Contemporary Relevance

### Model of Holistic Islamic Education

Al-Qarawiyyin proved that Islamic education need not be dichotomous between religious and secular sciences. **Organic integration** between both in a coherent curriculum created graduates who not only mastered science and technology but also had strong spiritual and ethical foundations.

### Women's Role in Civilization

Fatimah Al-Fihri's figure inspires contemporary discussions about women's roles in Islam. She proved that Islam provides wide space for women to contribute to civilizational progress, not only as individuals but as leaders and visionaries.^5

### Sustainability through Waqf

Al-Qarawiyyin's financing model through the waqf system demonstrates the potential of Islamic economics to create sustainable social institutions. This system allows institutions to operate independently without depending on short-term political or economic fluctuations.

### Intellectual Pluralism

Al-Qarawiyyin's tradition of accepting students from various religious and cultural backgrounds shows that Islamic institutions can become spaces for constructive civilizational dialogue, not sources of conflict or isolation.
