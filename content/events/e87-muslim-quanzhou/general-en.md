# Muslim Community of Quanzhou - Islamic Traces in China (~1200-1300 CE)

## The Silk Road and Islamic Arrival

**Quanzhou** (泉州), known as **Zaytun** in Arabic sources, was the most important port on the southern coast of China during the **Song Dynasty** (960-1279 CE) and early **Yuan Dynasty** (1271-1368 CE).^1 This city became the main terminal of the maritime Silk Road connecting China with the Islamic World, India, and Southeast Asia.^1

Muslim traders began settling in Quanzhou since the 8th century CE, but significant communities only formed during the Southern Song period (1127-1279 CE).^2 They came mainly from **Persia**, **Arabia**, and **Central Asia**, bringing not only trade goods but also Islamic religious and cultural traditions.^2

## Song Dynasty Tolerance

**Song Emperors** implemented open trade policies (*haishang*) that benefited foreign merchants, including Muslims.^1 The Song government gave special status to Muslim merchant communities through the **Shibosi** (市舶司) system, a kind of port authority that regulated maritime trade and provided legal protection.^1

This tolerance was not only economically pragmatic, but also philosophical.^1 Song **Confucianism** emphasized social harmony and acceptance of "cultured barbarians" (*wen yi*), a category given to Muslims because they were considered to have sophisticated moral and intellectual systems.^1

## Muslim Community Structure

The Quanzhou Muslim community organized itself within a **clan system** that adapted Chinese social structure with Islamic values.^3 They had a **Grand Imam** (大伊玛, *da yiman*) who not only led prayers but also functioned as a mediator with Chinese authorities.^3

**Qingjing Mosque** (清净寺, Pure Mosque) built in 1009 CE became the center of religious and social activities.^3 The mosque's architecture combined classical Islamic elements with local adaptations, reflecting a unique cultural synthesis between Islam and China.^3

## Economic Contribution

Quanzhou Muslims controlled **spice, perfume, and textile trade** which became the backbone of the city's economy.^1 They had commercial networks extending to **Samarkand**, **Bukhara**, **Baghdad**, **Basra**, and **Hormuz**.^1

**Ibn Battuta**, the famous Muslim traveler, visited Quanzhou in 1346 CE and noted that this city was "one of the largest ports in the world" with a prosperous and influential Muslim population.^2 He also noted the existence of Muslim judges (*qadi*) who handled internal community legal cases.^2

## Cultural Adaptation

The Quanzhou Muslim community developed **syncretic traditions** that combined Islamic practices with Chinese culture.^3 They adopted Chinese names, learned classical Chinese language and literature, and participated in **imperial examinations** (*keju*).^3

**Ding Zhufan** (丁注凡), a Muslim of Arab descent who became a high official during the Yuan period, wrote works on harmonization between Islam and Confucianism.^1 He argued that the concept of **tawhid** in Islam aligned with the concept of **tianli** (heavenly principle) in Neo-Confucianism.^1

## Yuan Dynasty and Mongol Era

When the **Mongols** established the Yuan Dynasty, the position of Quanzhou Muslims actually strengthened because the Mongols implemented the **semu** (色目) system that gave special status to "colored-eye people" including Muslims.^1 **Seyyed Ajall Shams al-Din Omar**, a Muslim from Central Asia, even became governor of Yunnan and facilitated the spread of Islam in that region.^1

The Yuan Dynasty also opened wider access for Muslims to **careers in bureaucracy**.^1 Many Quanzhou Muslims became high officials, engineers, astronomers, and translators in the imperial administration.^1

## Libraries and Intellectuals

The Quanzhou Muslim community had **libraries and learning centers** that stored manuscripts in Arabic, Persian, and Chinese.^3 They translated Islamic philosophical and scientific works into Chinese, contributing to cross-cultural knowledge transfer.^3

**Ma Duanlin** (馬端臨), a Muslim-Chinese historiographer, wrote "*Wenxian Tongkao*" (文獻通考), an institutional encyclopedia that became an important reference for Chinese history.^3 This work demonstrated the intellectual sophistication of the Chinese Muslim community.^3

## Wisdom and Lessons

The success of the Quanzhou Muslim community in **navigating cultural plurality** teaches the importance of adaptability without compromising core values.^2 They showed that Islam as *rahmatan lil 'alamin* can be universally accepted when presented with wisdom and respect for local context.^2

This story also shows that **economic integration** can become a bridge for acceptance and mutual understanding between communities.^1 Significant economic contributions proved the value-add of diversity in society.^1
