# The Sixth Crusade - Frederick II's Diplomacy

## 🧑 Who was the main figure?

Frederick II was a very unique and controversial Holy Roman Emperor. He was the grandson of Frederick Barbarossa who participated in the Third Crusade. Unlike his grandfather who failed, Frederick II succeeded in getting Jerusalem without fighting!

Interestingly, Frederick was quarreling with Pope Gregory IX over political issues in Italy. The Pope even excommunicated Frederick from the church, but he still led the crusade. Imagine, someone cursed by the church actually succeeded in capturing the holy city!

## ⭐ What did he do?

Frederick didn't use swords like previous crusaders. He used his brain and diplomacy! He knew that Sultan al-Kamil of Egypt was fighting with his brothers who ruled Damascus and Jerusalem.

Frederick offered friendship to al-Kamil. In return, he asked for Jerusalem to be returned without bloodshed. Al-Kamil agreed because he needed support to fight against his brothers.

In February 1229, they signed the Treaty of Jaffa. Frederick got Jerusalem, Bethlehem, and Nazareth. But al-Kamil kept control of the Dome of the Rock and al-Aqsa Mosque. Very clever!

## 💡 Why was it extraordinary?

Frederick succeeded in getting what previous crusades couldn't achieve - Jerusalem! And more amazingly, he did it without killing anyone. This shows that brains are sharper than swords.

On March 18, 1229, Frederick crowned himself as King of Jerusalem in the Church of the Holy Sepulchre. Since no bishop would crown him (because he was excommunicated by the Pope), he took the crown himself and put it on!

Their treaty also arranged for 10 years of peace and religious freedom for both communities. Muslims could pilgrimage to Jerusalem, Christians could visit their holy sites. A win-win solution!

## 📖 Guidance & Wisdom

The success of Frederick and al-Kamil teaches that mutual respect can overcome religious and cultural differences. Both were equally intelligent and understood that war only harms the people.

Al-Kamil proved that a Muslim leader could be wise in negotiations. Although criticized for "surrendering" Jerusalem, he was actually clever because he saved lives and kept control of the most important holy sites.
