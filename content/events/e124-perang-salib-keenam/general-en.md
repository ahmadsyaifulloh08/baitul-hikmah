# The Sixth Crusade - Frederick II's Diplomacy (1228-1229 CE)

## Complex Political Background

**Frederick II**, Holy Roman Emperor, was the most controversial figure in Crusade history.^1 As grandson of Emperor Frederick Barbarossa, he had an obligation to fulfill his grandfather's vow to undertake an expedition to the Holy Land. However, his relationship with Pope Gregory IX was very poor due to political conflicts in Italy.

Frederick had promised to undertake an expedition since his coronation in 1215 CE, but kept delaying it for various political and military reasons.^2 This delay led to his excommunication by the Pope in 1227 CE, creating a paradoxical situation: an emperor cursed by the church leading a war in the name of Christianity.

## Diplomatic, Not Military Strategy

Unlike previous crusades that relied on military force, Frederick II chose a diplomatic approach.^1 He understood that Sultan **al-Kamil** of Egypt was facing internal conflicts with his brothers **al-Ashraf** of Damascus and **al-Mu'azzam** of Jerusalem.

Frederick exploited this political tension by offering an alliance to al-Kamil.^3 In exchange for diplomatic support for al-Kamil's position, Frederick requested the return of Jerusalem without bloodshed. This approach was highly controversial among Christians who expected traditional "holy war."

## Negotiations with al-Kamil

The correspondence between Frederick and al-Kamil showed a level of mutual respect rarely seen in medieval Christian-Muslim relations.^2 Both leaders were equally learned, interested in philosophy, and understood complex political realities.

Al-Kamil faced a dilemma: giving Jerusalem to Frederick would strengthen his position against his brothers, but would also anger the Muslim ummah.^1 Frederick promised that Christian control over Jerusalem would be symbolic and would not disturb Muslim religious activities.

## Treaty of Jaffa (1229 CE)

In February 1229 CE, both parties signed the **Treaty of Jaffa** which ended the Sixth Crusade without major battles.^3 Under this treaty, Frederick gained Jerusalem, Bethlehem, and Nazareth, while al-Kamil retained control over the Dome of the Rock and al-Aqsa.

The treaty also established *a 10-year truce* and guaranteed religious freedom for both communities.^1 Muslims could make pilgrimages to Jerusalem, while Christians gained access to their holy sites. This was a pragmatic solution that benefited both sides.

## Crown Without Blessing

On March 18, 1229 CE, Frederick II crowned himself as **King of Jerusalem** in the Church of the Holy Sepulchre.^2 No bishop would crown him because of his excommunication status, so he took the crown from the altar and wore it himself.

This ceremony became a symbol of the ambiguity of his entire expedition: politically successful but spiritually failed.^3 Frederick achieved what previous crusades had not accomplished, but without church legitimacy and genuine Christian support.

## Controversial Reactions

In Europe, reactions to Frederick's success were deeply divided.^1 Pope Gregory IX actually attacked Frederick's territories in Southern Italy while the emperor was in the Middle East, showing that political conflict was more important than religious achievement.

Traditional Crusader knights were also dissatisfied because they came to fight, not to negotiate.^2 Many felt that victory without bloodshed lacked the same spiritual value as "martyrdom" in battle.

## Islamic World's Reaction

Among Muslims, reactions were also mixed. **Al-Kamil** was heavily criticized by scholars and people for "surrendering" Jerusalem.^3 However, political leaders understood that al-Kamil's decision was actually clever because it avoided destruction and maintained control over the most important holy sites.

The biggest critic was **an-Nasir Dawud** of Kerak who accused al-Kamil of being a traitor.^1 However, history proved that al-Kamil's diplomatic approach was more effective than prolonged military confrontation.

## Strategic Analysis

The Sixth Crusade showed that **diplomacy can be more effective than war** in achieving political goals.^2 Frederick and al-Kamil both realized that prolonged conflict harmed both sides and benefited their enemies.

This success also showed the importance of **timing in international politics**.^3 Frederick exploited the moment when the Islamic World was divided, while al-Kamil took advantage of Frederick's desire to strengthen his imperial legitimacy.

## Long-term Impact

Despite being controversial, the Sixth Crusade created an important precedent for **peaceful coexistence** between Christians and Muslims in Jerusalem.^1 This model showed that both communities could share access to holy sites without destroying each other.

Unfortunately, the truce only lasted until 1244 CE when the **Khwarezmians** recaptured Jerusalem at the request of **as-Salih Ayyub**.^2 However, Frederick and al-Kamil's diplomatic model continued to influence future international relations.

## Wisdom and Lessons

This story teaches that **political wisdom is sometimes nobler than military heroism**.^3 Frederick and al-Kamil showed that true leaders are those who prioritize people's welfare over personal prestige.

The Treaty of Jaffa also proved that **mutual respect** can overcome religious and cultural differences.^1 When both sides respect each other's interests, win-win solutions become possible.^5
