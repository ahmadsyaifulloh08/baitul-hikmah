# The First Revelation (Iqra') at the Cave of Hira — The Beginning of Muhammad's Prophethood (610 CE)

## Context

The descent of the first revelation in the month of Ramadan in 610 CE is the most decisive moment in Islamic history — indeed, it may be considered one of the most consequential moments in the history of human civilization. On that night, in a small cave at the summit of Jabal al-Nur (the Mountain of Light), approximately six kilometers from the Ka'bah, **Prophet Muhammad SAW**, then forty years old, received a visit from **Jibril (Gabriel) AS** and the first revelation from Allah in the form of the opening verses of Surah al-'Alaq.¹

To appreciate the depth of this event, the context of Muhammad's life leading up to this moment of prophethood must be traced.

At the age of forty, Muhammad ibn Abdullah had lived a life rich with experience yet full of hardship. He had grown up as an orphan, been raised by his grandfather and uncle, worked as a shepherd and trader, married Khadijah, and built a family. In the eyes of Meccan society, he was known as *al-Amin* (the Trustworthy) and *al-Sadiq* (the Truthful) — titles bestowed spontaneously by the community based on decades of interaction with him.³

But beneath this outwardly tranquil life, Muhammad harbored a deep spiritual restlessness. He lived among a society steeped in idol worship, oppression of the weak, the practice of burying infant girls alive, and many other forms of injustice. The *jahiliyyah* (age of ignorance) traditions rampant across the Arabian Peninsula clashed with his innately pure sense of humanity.⁴

It was this restlessness that drove Muhammad to increasingly frequent retreats of *tahannuth* (worship and contemplation) at the Cave of Hira. Ibn Hisham narrates in the chapter on the beginning of revelation that Muhammad would bring provisions to the cave and stay there for several nights — sometimes ten nights, sometimes more — engaging in *tafakkur* (deep reflection on truth). When his provisions ran out, he would descend to Khadijah's home to resupply, then return to the cave.³

This tradition of *tahannuth* at the Cave of Hira continued for several years before the descent of the first revelation. Al-Mubarakfuri in *Al-Rahiq al-Makhtum*, in the chapter on the beginning of revelation, notes that this habit of seclusion was part of divine preparation — Allah was preparing Muhammad's soul to bear the immense weight of revelation.⁴

The Cave of Hira itself is not a large cavern — it is merely a small niche at the mountain's summit, large enough for only one person to sit or recline. From the cave's mouth, the city of Mecca and the Ka'bah are visible in the distance. This quiet, remote place became the silent witness to the greatest moment in human history.³

### Ru'ya al-Sadiqah: True Dreams

Before revelation came in waking form, Muhammad experienced a preliminary phase known as *ru'ya al-sadiqah* — true dreams. In an authentic hadith:

> أَوَّلُ مَا بُدِئَ بِهِ رَسُولُ اللَّهِ صلى الله عليه وسلم مِنَ الْوَحْيِ الرُّؤْيَا الصَّالِحَةُ فِي النَّوْمِ، فَكَانَ لاَ يَرَى رُؤْيَا إِلاَّ جَاءَتْ مِثْلَ فَلَقِ الصُّبْحِ
>
> *"The beginning of revelation for the Messenger of Allah SAW was true dreams during sleep. He never saw a dream except that it came true like the breaking of dawn."*
> — Narrated by al-Bukhari, no. 3, Kitab Bad' al-Wahy

Ibn Hajar al-Asqalani in *Fath al-Bari*, in his commentary on this hadith, explains that this phase of true dreams lasted for six months before the first revelation descended in the waking state. These dreams served as a spiritual "warm-up" — accustoming Muhammad's soul to the experience of receiving information from the unseen realm before he would face the far more intense experience of meeting the angel Jibril directly.⁵

## The Events

### The Night of the First Revelation

On the 17th night of Ramadan (according to the majority opinion, though there is some disagreement) in 610 CE, Muhammad was at the Cave of Hira as was his custom. That night, something that had never happened before came upon him.

Jibril appeared in physical form. The authentic hadith narrated by Aisha through the chain of Urwah ibn al-Zubayr describes this event in remarkable detail:¹

Jibril said to Muhammad: *"Iqra'!"* (Read!). Muhammad replied:

"I cannot read."

Then Jibril embraced Muhammad with tremendous force — so tightly that Muhammad felt he could barely endure it — then released him and said again: *"Iqra'!"* Muhammad replied once more: "I cannot read." Jibril embraced him again with the same force, released him, and said: *"Iqra'!"* Muhammad replied: "I cannot read." Then Jibril embraced him a third time, released him, and said:¹

> اقْرَأْ بِاسْمِ رَبِّكَ الَّذِي خَلَقَ ﴿١﴾ خَلَقَ الْإِنسَانَ مِنْ عَلَقٍ ﴿٢﴾ اقْرَأْ وَرَبُّكَ الْأَكْرَمُ ﴿٣﴾ الَّذِي عَلَّمَ بِالْقَلَمِ ﴿٤﴾ عَلَّمَ الْإِنسَانَ مَا لَمْ يَعْلَمْ ﴿٥﴾
>
> *"Read in the name of your Lord who created. Created man from a clinging substance. Read, and your Lord is the Most Generous. Who taught by the pen. Taught man that which he knew not."*
> — QS. Al-'Alaq (96): 1–5

These five verses — the very first revelation of the entire Quran — carry a profoundly deep message. Allah's first command to humanity was not about prayer, charity, or fasting, but *iqra'* — read, seek knowledge, employ the intellect that Allah has bestowed.

### Descent from the Cave of Hira

After Jibril departed, Muhammad descended from the Cave of Hira trembling violently. Ibn Hisham narrates in the chapter on the first revelation that Muhammad was deeply frightened — he had just experienced something entirely beyond any human experience.³

An authentic hadith describes Muhammad's condition:

> فَرَجَعَ بِهَا رَسُولُ اللَّهِ صلى الله عليه وسلم يَرْجُفُ فُؤَادُهُ
>
> *"The Messenger of Allah SAW returned with those verses, his heart trembling violently."*
> — Narrated by al-Bukhari, no. 3, Kitab Bad' al-Wahy

Muhammad rushed home to Khadijah. Upon arriving, he said to his wife: *"Zammiluuni, zammiluuni!"* (Cover me, cover me!). Khadijah immediately wrapped him in blankets until his fear subsided.¹

### Khadijah's Role: The First Woman to Believe

After Muhammad calmed down, he recounted what had happened at the Cave of Hira to Khadijah. He expressed his fear: "I am afraid something has happened to me."¹

Khadijah's response at this moment is one of the most important moments in Islamic history. With complete conviction and tenderness, Khadijah said:

"Never! By Allah, Allah will never humiliate you. Indeed, you maintain the ties of kinship, bear the burdens of others, provide for the destitute, honor your guests, and assist those afflicted by calamity."¹

Khadijah's words were not empty comfort — they were a logical analysis from a wise woman. Khadijah argued that someone of Muhammad's noble character could not possibly be subjected to harm by Allah. Goodness attracts goodness, not evil.⁴

### Waraqah ibn Nawfal: Confirmation from a Scholar of Scripture

Khadijah then took Muhammad to meet **Waraqah ibn Nawfal**, Khadijah's cousin who had embraced Christianity and was deeply versed in the Gospel scriptures. Waraqah was an elderly, blind man.¹

After hearing Muhammad's account, Waraqah said:

"This is *al-Namus* (the angel of revelation) whom Allah sent to Moses. How I wish I were young at that time! How I wish I were alive when your people drive you out!"

Muhammad asked in surprise: "Will they drive me out?"

Waraqah replied: "Yes. No one has ever brought what you have brought without being opposed. And if I live to see that day, I will support you with all my strength."¹

Waraqah's statement is significant for several reasons. First, it provided confirmation from another religious tradition that Muhammad's experience was not demonic possession or madness, but an authentic prophetic experience. Second, it warned that the path of prophethood would be fraught with rejection and expulsion — a prediction that proved entirely correct.⁵

Not long after this encounter, Waraqah passed away. Revelation also ceased for a period — known as *fatrat al-wahy* (the pause of revelation) — before resuming gradually.²

## Dialogue and Sacred Texts

### The Meaning of Iqra' and Its Significance

The command *"Iqra'"* as the first revelation carries profoundly deep meaning. The word *iqra'* derives from the root *q-r-a* which does not only mean "to read" in the sense of letters and writing, but also "to gather," "to examine," and "to contemplate." This command was given to someone who was *ummi* (unlettered) — indicating that "reading" here transcends the literacy of letters; it encompasses reading the signs of Allah's greatness throughout the universe.⁵

These first five verses also mention *al-qalam* (the pen) as an instrument of teaching — an extraordinary tribute to the tradition of writing and knowledge. Allah began His revelation not with threats or punishment, but with an invitation to learn and to know. This established the foundation that Islam, at its core, is a religion of knowledge.⁴

### The Three Embraces: Symbolism and Wisdom

Scholars have interpreted Jibril's three embraces (*ghat*) as a spiritual process that prepared Muhammad's soul to receive revelation. Ibn Hajar in *Fath al-Bari*, in his commentary on the hadith of the first revelation, explains that the first embrace tested Muhammad's readiness, the second steadied him, and the third strengthened him so that he could receive the words of Allah.⁵

Al-Mubarakfuri adds that these embraces also signify that receiving revelation was not a light or easy process — it required extraordinary physical and spiritual strength. Revelation was not mere literary inspiration or poetic impulse; it was a direct communication from the Creator of the universe.⁴

### Muhammad's Fear: The Humanity of a Prophet

Muhammad's reaction of trembling fear after receiving the first revelation reveals a deeply authentic human side. A fraudster claiming false prophethood would surely have prepared a grand and confident narrative. But Muhammad was instead frightened, confused, and in need of his wife's support. For Islamic historians and theologians, this reaction is itself proof of the authenticity of his experience.⁴

Sahih Muslim narrates that Muhammad's fear was so great that he briefly contemplated throwing himself from the mountaintop. But each time he approached the edge, Jibril appeared and said: "O Muhammad, indeed you are truly the Messenger of Allah." He would then find peace and turn back.²

## Analysis and Lessons

### An Intellectual Revolution

The first revelation in the form of a command to read constituted a declaration of intellectual revolution. In a society that was largely illiterate, Allah began His final message with a tribute to knowledge. The Islamic civilization that would later produce scientists, philosophers, and mathematicians — from al-Khwarizmi to Ibn Sina, from Ibn Rushd to al-Biruni — is rooted in these first five verses.⁴

### Khadijah: The First Pillar of Islam

Without Khadijah's response of conviction and support, the moment of the first revelation might have become a paralyzing crisis. Khadijah did not merely comfort Muhammad — she provided a logical framework for understanding his experience. She was not simply an obedient wife, but an intellectual and spiritual partner capable of analyzing the situation with clarity.⁴

Khadijah then became the first person to believe in Muhammad's message — *al-sabiqat al-awwalun* (the very first believer). Her faith was not blind faith; it was grounded in deep knowledge of Muhammad's character over twenty-five years of living together.

### The Gradualness of Revelation

The event of the first revelation also marked the beginning of a process of revelation that would span twenty-three years — from 610 to 632 CE. The Quran did not descend all at once, but gradually in accordance with context and need. This gradualness carries a deeply pedagogical wisdom: it allowed Muslims to understand, internalize, and practice each verse step by step.⁴

### The Cave of Hira and the Tradition of Contemplation

Muhammad's practice of *tahannuth* at the Cave of Hira teaches the importance of providing quiet space for reflection and introspection. Amid the bustle of life, human beings need moments of silence to encounter truth. The tradition of *khalwah* (seclusion for worship) that later became a part of Islamic spirituality is rooted in Muhammad's experience at the Cave of Hira.³

### The Beginning of World Transformation

The five verses that descended at the Cave of Hira on that night launched a movement that would permanently reshape the face of the world. From a small cave atop a silent mountain, a civilization was born that would stretch from Spain to Indonesia, from science to art, from law to architecture. The first revelation was not merely the beginning of Muhammad's prophethood — it was the beginning of a new chapter in the history of humankind.⁴
