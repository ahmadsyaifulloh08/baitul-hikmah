# Umrah al-Qada: The Peaceful Return to Makkah

## Context: A Promise Fulfilled

One year had passed since the Treaty of Hudaybiyyah — the agreement that brought many companions to tears as they had to return home without performing umrah. One of the treaty's clauses stipulated that the Muslims could return to Makkah the following year to perform umrah and stay for three days.^1 ^2

In the month of Dhul-Qa'dah of the 7th year of Hijrah (March 629 CE), Prophet Muhammad ﷺ prepared for the journey to fulfill that promise. Approximately 2,000 Muslims joined — a larger number than the Hudaybiyyah caravan. They brought sacrificial animals and donned the garments of *ihram*.^1

What distinguished this umrah from an ordinary journey was its psychological and political dimension. This was the first time since the hijrah — seven years — that the Muslims returned to their birthplace officially and peacefully. For many emigrants, this was a homecoming.

## The Event: Three Days in Makkah

### Departure and Preparation

Prophet Muhammad ﷺ prepared the caravan meticulously. He brought full arms for the journey — swords, spears, and bows — but in accordance with the treaty, these weapons had to be stored outside the city limits of Makkah. Only sheathed swords could be carried in, as was customary for travelers.^1 ^2

The Quraysh honored the treaty. They vacated the city of Makkah and withdrew to the surrounding hills for three days. From their vantage points, they watched the Muslims enter the city.^2 The scene was rich with irony: the Quraysh, who once held absolute power over Makkah, now sat watching the very people they had persecuted and driven out walking into the sacred city.

### Entering Makkah

The Muslim caravan entered Makkah from the north. Prophet Muhammad ﷺ rode his camel, while **Abdullah ibn Rawahah** held the reins and recited poems glorifying Islam.^1

Upon entering the Sacred Mosque, the Prophet wore his *ihram* with the right shoulder exposed (*idhtiba'*) and performed *tawaf* — circling the Ka'bah seven times. He walked with quick, vigorous strides (*raml*) during the first three circuits.^1 ^2 This was a deliberate strategy: the Quraysh had spread rumors that the Muslims were weak and sickly from the fever of Yathrib (Madinah). By walking briskly with bared shoulders, the Prophet demonstrated that the Muslims were healthy and strong.

The companions followed the Prophet's example. The sight of 2,000 healthy, strong Muslims performing *tawaf* with full vigor demolished the Quraysh narrative of Muslim weakness. The Quraysh who peered from the hillsides witnessed firsthand the strength and discipline of a community they had once dismissed.^2

### Bilal's Adhan from the Ka'bah

One of the most historic moments during those three days was when Prophet Muhammad ﷺ ordered **Bilal ibn Rabah** to climb atop the Ka'bah and call the adhan.^1 ^2 Bilal — a former slave from Abyssinia who had been tortured by Umayyah ibn Khalaf on the scorching sands of Makkah for his faith — now stood atop the most sacred structure in Makkah, proclaiming the oneness of Allah with his melodious voice echoing across the entire city.

For the Muslims, this was an extraordinary moment of spiritual triumph. For the Quraysh, it was a stark challenge to their pride. Several Quraysh leaders reportedly felt deeply humiliated seeing someone they deemed lowly — a former black slave — standing atop what they considered the most noble place.^2

Yet that was precisely Islam's message: a person's honor is determined by piety, not by lineage, skin color, or social status.

### The Prophet's Marriage to Maymunah

During the stay in Makkah, Prophet Muhammad ﷺ married **Maymunah bint al-Harith**, the aunt of Khalid ibn al-Walid and Abdullah ibn Abbas. This marriage carried a strategic dimension — Maymunah came from an influential family among the Quraysh and allied tribes, strengthening the Prophet's network of alliances.^1 ^2

The Prophet intended to hold a *walimah* (wedding feast) in Makkah and invite the Quraysh, as a gesture of peace and reconciliation. However, on the third day, Quraysh envoys came and asked the Muslims to leave Makkah as per the treaty. The Prophet requested an extension to complete the feast, but the Quraysh refused. He complied with the agreement and departed Makkah on time.^1

The marriage to Maymunah was ultimately celebrated at **Sarif**, outside the boundaries of Makkah.^2

### Departure

After exactly three days, the Muslims left Makkah in an orderly fashion. There were no incidents, no violations of the treaty from either side. This proved that a peace agreement could be upheld with integrity by both parties.^1

## Analysis: The Meaning Behind Three Days

### Symbolic Victory

Umrah al-Qada was a symbolic victory whose impact far exceeded the three physical days in Makkah. It demonstrated several important things:

**First, Islam returned to its roots.** The Ka'bah was built by Ibrahim and Ismail as a house of worship for Allah the One. With the return of the Muslims and the adhan called from atop the Ka'bah, the sacred structure served its original purpose once more — even if only for three days.

**Second, Muslim self-confidence.** Many emigrants who had left Makkah pursued and persecuted now returned with heads held high, worshipping openly in the same place where they had once hidden their faith. This transformation provided an extraordinary morale boost.

**Third, the impression on the Quraysh.** Witnessing firsthand the strength, discipline, and spirit of the Muslims changed the perception of many Quraysh. Not a few began to seriously consider Islam after observing this umrah. **Khalid ibn al-Walid** and **Amr ibn al-As** — two of the most talented military commanders in Arabia — embraced Islam shortly after Umrah al-Qada, partly due to what they had witnessed.^2

### Upholding the Treaty

Umrah al-Qada also demonstrated Prophet Muhammad's commitment to agreements. He arrived on time, brought exactly what was permitted, stayed precisely three days, and departed when asked — even when he could have argued for an extension for the wedding feast. This integrity built the Prophet's reputation as a trustworthy treaty partner.

## Lessons from Umrah al-Qada

**Patience bears sweet fruit.** A year earlier, the companions returned heartbroken without performing umrah. Now they came back and completed the delayed worship with multiplied joy. Their patience was not in vain.

**Integrity in agreements.** The Prophet demonstrated that keeping one's word — even an agreement that initially felt burdensome — is a non-negotiable principle. A believer must be true to their word.

**True honor.** Bilal atop the Ka'bah was a symbol that Islam dismantles hierarchies based on race and social status. The most honored in Allah's sight is the most pious, not the wealthiest or most noble by birth.

**Dawah through example.** Without a single word of direct preaching, the presence of 2,000 strong and disciplined Muslims in Makkah was the most effective dawah. Khalid ibn al-Walid and Amr ibn al-As were moved not by sermons, but by what they saw.^3
