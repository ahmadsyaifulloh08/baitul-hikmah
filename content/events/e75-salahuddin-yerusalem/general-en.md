# Saladin Recaptures Jerusalem (1187 CE)

## Context

In the late twelfth century, the Islamic world faced one of its most profound challenges since the time of Prophet Muhammad ﷺ. For 88 years, **Jerusalem** (al-Quds), Islam's third holiest city, had remained under Crusader occupation following the devastating conquest of 1099 CE. This period witnessed the establishment of four Crusader states across the Levant: the Kingdom of Jerusalem, the Principality of Antioch, the County of Tripoli, and the County of Edessa. The initial conquest had been marked by one of the most horrific massacres in medieval history, with contemporary chroniclers estimating that between 40,000 and 70,000 Muslim and Jewish inhabitants were slaughtered.^1

The Muslim world during this period was fragmented and unable to mount an effective, coordinated response. The Fatimid Caliphate in Egypt was in decline, the Abbasid Caliphate in Baghdad held only symbolic authority, and the various Turkish emirates in Syria and Mesopotamia were often at odds with one another. The Crusader success was largely due to this disunity, which allowed smaller Christian armies to defeat larger but divided Muslim forces.^1

However, the seeds of eventual Muslim victory were planted in the mid-twelfth century with the rise of **Imad ad-Din Zangi**, who recaptured Edessa in 1144 CE, followed by his son **Nur ad-Din Mahmud**, who began the systematic unification of Syria and Egypt. Nur ad-Din's vision was clear: only a united Muslim front could successfully challenge the Crusader presence. His greatest achievement was not military, but organizational — he created the ideological and institutional framework for Muslim resistance.^1

It was in this context that **Salah ad-Din Yusuf ibn Ayyub** — known to the West as Saladin — emerged as the leader who would fulfill Nur ad-Din's vision and liberate Jerusalem.

## The Rise of Saladin

### Early Life and Formation

Saladin was born in 1137 CE in Tikrit, in modern-day Iraq, to a Kurdish family of modest origins. His father, **Ayyub ibn Shadhi**, was a military commander who served under Nur ad-Din. The family moved to Ba'labakk and later to Damascus, where young Saladin received his education in Islamic theology, Arabic literature, and military arts. Contemporary biographers describe him as a serious student who was particularly drawn to the study of theology and law.^2

His military career began under the mentorship of his uncle **Shirkuh**, one of Nur ad-Din's most trusted generals. Shirkuh recognized his nephew's potential and involved him in the complex military campaigns in Egypt during the 1160s. These campaigns were part of a larger struggle between the dying Fatimid Caliphate, the rising Nur ad-Din, and the ambitious Crusader Kingdom of Jerusalem for control over Egypt's immense wealth.^3

### The Path to Leadership

When Shirkuh died suddenly in 1169 CE, the young Saladin, then only 32, found himself unexpectedly appointed as vizier of Egypt under the nominal Fatimid Caliph al-Adid. This appointment was controversial — many of Shirkuh's older and more experienced officers felt they deserved the position. However, Saladin quickly proved his worth through a combination of military skill, diplomatic acumen, and genuine religious piety.^1

In 1171 CE, Saladin took the momentous step of formally ending the Fatimid Caliphate and restoring Egypt's allegiance to the Abbasid Caliph in Baghdad. This act was not merely political; it was a powerful symbol of Sunni Muslim unity. The khutbah (Friday sermon) in Cairo once again included prayers for the Abbasid Caliph, marking the end of nearly two centuries of Fatimid rule.^1

### The Vision of Unity

After Nur ad-Din's death in 1174 CE, Saladin gradually expanded his authority beyond Egypt. He faced a critical choice: remain content as the de facto ruler of the richest province in the Middle East, or pursue the dangerous path of unification against the Crusaders. Saladin chose the latter, driven by what contemporary sources describe as a genuine sense of religious obligation.^4

His method was masterful. Rather than relying on force alone, Saladin built legitimacy through justice, generosity, and personal piety. He distributed much of Egypt's wealth to his soldiers and to religious institutions. Ibn Shaddad, his personal secretary and biographer, records numerous instances of Saladin giving away gold and precious objects to the point where his treasury was often empty. When reminded that he should save for the future, Saladin reportedly said, "I do not hoard gold and silver; my treasury is the contentment of my people."^5

## The Campaign for Unity

### Consolidating Syria

Between 1174 and 1186 CE, Saladin systematically brought the various Syrian emirates under his control. This was not a simple matter of conquest — he had to overcome the legitimate claims of Nur ad-Din's heirs, navigate the complex tribal politics of the region, and maintain his forces while under constant pressure from the Crusader states.^6

The process required numerous compromises and strategic marriages. Saladin married Nur ad-Din's widow to legitimize his position, granted autonomy to local rulers who submitted voluntarily, and demonstrated remarkable patience in dealing with those who initially resisted his authority. His approach was fundamentally different from typical medieval conquest — he sought to build consensus, not merely impose his will.^1

### Diplomatic and Military Preparation

During this period, Saladin also engaged in complex diplomatic relations with the Crusader states. The situation was far from a simple Christian-Muslim divide. Individual Crusader nobles often allied with Muslim leaders against other Christians, while Muslim emirs sometimes sought Crusader support against rivals. Saladin navigated this labyrinth with skill, gradually isolating the most aggressive Crusader leaders while maintaining working relationships with others.^1

The breaking point came with **Raynald de Châtillon**, the Lord of Kerak. Raynald was notorious for his violation of truces, his raids on Muslim pilgrimage caravans, and most provocatively, his threats to attack Mecca and Medina. In 1186 CE, Raynald's latest violation of a truce gave Saladin the perfect justification for war.^1

## The Battle of Hattin

### Strategic Brilliance

The **Battle of Hattin** (4 July 1187 CE) represents one of the most brilliant military campaigns in medieval history. Saladin's strategy was based on intimate knowledge of both the terrain and his enemy's psychology. He began by besieging Tiberias, forcing the Crusader leadership into an agonizing choice: allow the city to fall, or march across the waterless plateau in the height of summer.^7

King **Guy de Lusignan** of Jerusalem faced intense pressure from his nobles, particularly from Raynald de Châtillon, to march to the relief of Tiberias. Against the advice of more experienced commanders like **Raymond III of Tripoli**, Guy ordered the advance. The Crusader army — consisting of about 20,000 men, including 1,200 knights — left their base at Saffuriya on July 3rd.^1

### The Trap Closes

Saladin's forces harassed the Christian army throughout their march, preventing them from reaching water sources. As the Crusaders struggled across the arid plateau, they were continuously attacked by Muslim light cavalry who would charge in, fire arrows, and retreat before the heavy cavalry could respond. By evening of July 3rd, the Christians were exhausted, dehydrated, and demoralized.^8

That night, Saladin's forces lit fires around the Christian camp, filling the air with smoke and denying the Crusaders rest. The psychological effect was devastating — the Christians could see they were completely surrounded, and the smoke made their thirst even more unbearable.^9

### The Final Assault

On the morning of July 4th, the Battle of Hattin began in earnest. The Crusader army, weakened by thirst and exhaustion, was unable to maintain its usual disciplined formations. The infantry broke first, fleeing toward the twin hills called the Horns of Hattin. Without infantry support, the famous Crusader heavy cavalry became isolated and vulnerable.^10

The battle ended with the complete destruction of the Crusader field army. King Guy was captured, along with almost all the major nobles and knights of the Kingdom of Jerusalem. Most significantly, the **True Cross** — the most sacred Christian relic in the Crusader states — fell into Muslim hands.^1

### The Character of Victory

Saladin's conduct after victory revealed his character and established patterns that would define his later treatment of Jerusalem. He treated King Guy with honor, personally serving him water and observing the Arab tradition of hospitality even toward prisoners. However, when Raynald de Châtillon was brought before him, Saladin executed him personally, fulfilling a vow he had made after Raynald's violations of sacred truces.^11

## The Liberation of Jerusalem

### The March to the Holy City

Following Hattin, the Crusader fortresses fell like dominoes. With their field army destroyed and their leadership captured, the remaining Crusader garrisons could offer only token resistance. Saladin systematically reduced the coastal cities and inland fortresses, following a deliberate strategy that isolated Jerusalem by cutting its lines of supply and communication.^12

Saladin arrived at the gates of Jerusalem in late September 1187 CE. The city's defenses were commanded by **Balian of Ibelin**, one of the few noble survivors of Hattin. The garrison consisted mainly of citizens and refugees from other fallen cities, with perhaps a few hundred actual soldiers.^13

### The Siege and Negotiations

Initial negotiations failed when Saladin demanded unconditional surrender while Balian insisted on terms. The siege began in earnest on September 20th, with Saladin's engineers targeting the northern wall where the city's defenses were weakest. Within days, a breach had been opened, making the city's fall inevitable.^14

At this critical moment, Balian requested a final meeting with Saladin. The Crusader commander made a desperate threat: if no terms were offered, the Christians would destroy all the Muslim holy sites in the city, kill their Muslim prisoners, and fight to the death. More importantly, he reminded Saladin that a massacre would make every remaining Crusader fortress fight to the last man, prolonging the war indefinitely.^1

### The Terms of Surrender

Saladin agreed to terms that were extraordinarily generous by the standards of medieval warfare. The basic agreement was that the city would surrender peacefully in exchange for the safe passage of all inhabitants who could pay a ransom: ten gold dinars for men, five for women, and one for children. Those unable to pay would be given forty days to raise the money or arrange for someone to pay for them.^15

## The Contrast with 1099

### Islamic Justice in Practice

The liberation of Jerusalem on **October 2, 1187 CE** (27 Rajab 583 AH) became one of history's most powerful demonstrations of Islamic principles of justice and mercy in warfare. The contrast with the Crusader conquest of 1099 could not have been starker:

**No Massacre**: Despite having every legal and emotional justification for revenge, not a single civilian was killed during or after the city's surrender. This restraint was particularly remarkable given that many of Saladin's soldiers had family members killed in the 1099 massacre.^1

**Humane Implementation**: Saladin went far beyond the agreed terms. When it became clear that many inhabitants could not afford the ransom, he freed thousands at his own expense. His brother **al-Adil** requested and was granted one thousand captives to free as a gift. Saladin's own secretary records that the Sultan was moved to tears by the plight of families being separated.^2

**Religious Tolerance**: Christian churches and holy sites were protected. The Church of the Holy Sepulchre remained under Christian control, with Saladin personally guaranteeing the safety of Christian pilgrims. This was particularly significant since Eastern Orthodox Christians, who had been oppressed under Latin Crusader rule, were welcomed back to the city.^1

**Symbolic Restraint**: The golden cross atop the Dome of the Rock was removed and replaced with a crescent, but this was done peacefully and without damaging the structure. Saladin ordered that the building be cleansed with rose water before being restored to Muslim worship.^1

### International Recognition

Even European chroniclers, though they were Saladin's enemies, recorded his noble conduct with admiration. **Ernoul**, a contemporary Crusader historian, wrote: "Never did a man show greater courtesy to vanquished enemies than Saladin showed to the Christians of Jerusalem."^3 During the European Enlightenment, Saladin became a symbol of the ideal knight, with **Dante** placing him in Limbo rather than Hell in the *Divine Comedy*, and **Voltaire** using him as an example of natural nobility.^4

## Analysis and Lessons

### The Prophetic Model

Saladin consciously modeled his conduct on the **Conquest of Mecca** (Fath Makkah) by Prophet Muhammad ﷺ. Like the Prophet's bloodless conquest of Mecca, Saladin's liberation of Jerusalem was marked by magnanimity toward former enemies and protection of religious sites. The timing was also significant — the conquest occurred on 27 Rajab, coinciding with the anniversary of **Isra and Mi'raj**, adding profound spiritual resonance to the victory.^1

### Islamic Laws of War

Saladin's conduct exemplified the Islamic principles of warfare established in the Qur'an and Sunnah:

> "Fight them until there is no more persecution and religion belongs to God. If they cease, then there is to be no aggression except against the oppressors." — Qur'an 2:193^1

> "When you meet your enemies in battle, behave nobly. Do not mutilate, do not kill children, old people, or women. Do not cut down trees or destroy crops." — Hadith from Abu Dawud^1

The Prophet's instructions to his commanders consistently emphasized protection of non-combatants, respect for places of worship, and humane treatment of prisoners. Saladin's implementation of these principles in Jerusalem became a model for subsequent Muslim military leaders.^1

### The Power of Unity

Perhaps most importantly, Saladin demonstrated that Muslim unity was not merely a political necessity but a moral imperative. His success came not from superior numbers or technology, but from his ability to unite disparate Muslim populations under a common vision of justice and liberation. This unity was built through moral leadership rather than mere coercion.^1

## Civilizational Impact

### Immediate Consequences

The recapture of Jerusalem triggered the **Third Crusade** (1189-1192 CE), which brought the legendary confrontation between Saladin and **Richard the Lionheart**. Paradoxically, this conflict increased cultural exchange between East and West, as extended periods of warfare were interspersed with truces during which diplomatic, commercial, and intellectual contacts flourished.^1

### Jerusalem as an Open City

Under Saladin and his Ayyubid successors, Jerusalem became genuinely cosmopolitan for the first time since the Arab conquest of 638 CE. Christians, Muslims, and Jews could all worship freely in the city. The Ayyubids actively encouraged Christian pilgrimage, seeing it as both religiously meritorious and economically beneficial. This openness contrasted sharply with the exclusivist policies of the Crusader period.^5

### Standards of Warfare

Saladin's conduct in Jerusalem established humanitarian standards that influenced Islamic jurisprudence for centuries. Legal scholars such as **Ibn Taymiyyah** and **al-Mawardi** cited his example when developing principles for the treatment of conquered populations and protection of religious minorities. These principles later influenced the development of international humanitarian law.^6

### Cultural Memory

Saladin became a universal symbol transcending religious and cultural boundaries. In Islamic tradition, he represents the ideal of the just ruler who combines military prowess with moral integrity. In European tradition, he became the model of chivalric honor, admired even by his enemies. This cross-cultural admiration helped foster the later development of international diplomatic norms based on mutual respect.^7

## The Living Legacy

### Modern Relevance

In contemporary discussions of conflict resolution, Saladin's Jerusalem model offers valuable insights. His approach combined military decisiveness with post-victory restraint, immediate tactical gains with long-term strategic thinking, and religious conviction with pragmatic accommodation. Modern peace negotiators often cite his example as proof that military victory need not preclude magnanimous settlement.^8

### Lessons for Leadership

Saladin's path to leadership offers enduring lessons for political and military leaders. His rise was based on competence and moral authority rather than inheritance or force. He built coalitions through inclusion rather than intimidation, and he maintained loyalty through justice and generosity. His treasury was often empty because he gave freely to his supporters and to the poor, yet this generosity strengthened rather than weakened his position.^1

### The Unity Imperative

Perhaps most relevant for the contemporary Muslim world is Saladin's demonstration that unity is both achievable and essential. He showed that sectarian and ethnic differences need not prevent cooperation when there is common purpose and just leadership. His model of unity through moral authority rather than hegemonic dominance remains relevant for modern efforts at Muslim cooperation.^1

## Theological Dimensions

### Divine Assistance and Human Effort

Islamic sources consistently interpret Saladin's success as an example of divine assistance (*nusrah*) granted to those who strive in Allah's path. However, they equally emphasize that this assistance came only after extraordinary human effort — years of patient coalition-building, careful military preparation, and moral purification. The Qur'anic principle "And that there is nothing for man except what he strives for" (53:39) is frequently cited in this context.^1

### The Jerusalem Connection

The liberation of Jerusalem was seen as fulfilling the Qur'anic promise of the **Isra and Mi'raj**:

> "Glory be to Him who took His servant for a journey by night from al-Masjid al-Haram to al-Masjid al-Aqsa, whose surroundings We have blessed, that We might show him some of Our signs." — Qur'an 17:1^1

Muslim scholars interpreted Saladin's victory as proof that Allah's blessings remained on Jerusalem and that righteous effort would be rewarded with the restoration of the holy sites to Islamic control.^1

## Conclusion

The recapture of Jerusalem by Saladin in 1187 CE represents one of history's most remarkable examples of how military victory can be combined with moral triumph. Saladin's achievement was not merely tactical — it was civilizational. He proved that Islamic principles of justice and mercy were not mere ideals but practical guidelines that could be implemented even in the midst of warfare.

The contrast between Saladin's magnanimous victory and the Crusaders' brutal conquest 88 years earlier helped establish Islam's moral authority in the medieval world. European chroniclers, despite their religious antagonism, were compelled to acknowledge the superiority of Saladin's conduct. This acknowledgment contributed to the gradual development of more humane standards of warfare in Europe itself.

Saladin's success demonstrated that Muslim unity, when based on justice and moral leadership rather than mere political expedience, could overcome seemingly impossible odds. His model of leadership — combining religious conviction with pragmatic accommodation, strategic patience with tactical boldness, and personal austerity with political generosity — remains relevant for contemporary Muslim societies facing their own challenges of unity and renewal.

Most importantly, Saladin's Jerusalem showed the world what Islamic civilization at its best could accomplish: a society that was simultaneously devout and tolerant, powerful and just, victorious and merciful. In an age when religious conflict often seems intractable, the example of a Muslim leader who could be both a faithful believer and a magnanimous victor offers hope that faith and humanity need not be in conflict.

The golden crescent that replaced the cross atop the Dome of the Rock was not merely a symbol of conquest — it was a symbol of restoration, of justice achieved and mercy extended, of the ancient promise that righteousness would ultimately triumph over oppression. In this sense, Saladin's greatest victory was not military but moral, and its lessons echo across the centuries to illuminate the path for all who seek to combine strength with justice, faith with mercy, and victory with honor.
