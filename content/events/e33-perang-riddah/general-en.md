# The Riddah Wars: The Apostasy Rebellion After the Death of the Prophet ﷺ

## Context: The Arabian Peninsula on the Brink of Fragmentation

When the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ died on 12 Rabi al-Awwal 11 AH (632 CE), the Arabian Peninsula stood on fragile ground. The entire region had only recently been unified under the banner of Islam — many tribes had embraced the faith in the final years of the Prophet's life, particularly after *Fath Makkah* (the liberation of Makkah) in 8 AH and during the *'Am al-Wufud* (Year of Delegations) in 9 AH. For some of these tribes, their allegiance to Islam was more political than theological.^1

Al-Tabari in *Tarikh al-Rusul wa al-Muluk* describes how news of the Prophet's death spread like wildfire across the peninsula.^1 Tribe after tribe declared apostasy or refused to pay *zakat*. In their view, the political covenant they had forged was with Muhammad personally — not with the state of Madinah or any successor. With his passing, they considered the agreement void.

Ibn al-Athir in *Al-Kamil fi al-Tarikh* classifies the rebellious groups into several categories:^2 First, those who genuinely reverted to the idol worship of their ancestors. Second, those who followed false prophets — Musaylimah al-Kadhdhab in Yamamah, Sajah bint al-Harith in Mesopotamia, Tulayha ibn Khuwaylid among the Banu Asad, and al-Aswad al-Ansi in Yemen. Third, those who still claimed to be Muslim but refused to pay *zakat*, arguing that the obligation applied only during the Prophet's lifetime.

In practical terms, the entire Arabian Peninsula was in revolt except for Makkah, Madinah, and Taif.^1 ^2 Even in Madinah itself, tension was palpable. Umar ibn al-Khattab, usually firm and courageous, was so shaken by the Prophet's death that he stood in the mosque declaring the Prophet could not possibly have died.^1

## The Events: Abu Bakr's Resolve and the Decision to Fight

### Critical Moments in Madinah

**Abu Bakr al-Siddiq**, newly appointed as the first caliph through consultation at Saqifah Banu Sa'idah, faced the gravest crisis in early Islamic history. He had to simultaneously manage three challenges: internal consolidation in Madinah, the mass apostasy threat, and the deployment of Usamah ibn Zayd's army — a mission the Prophet had ordered before his death.

Al-Baladhuri in *Futuh al-Buldan* records that senior companions urged Abu Bakr to delay Usamah's expedition, given that Madinah itself was threatened by surrounding rebel tribes.^3 Abu Bakr refused categorically. He declared:

*"By Allah, even if dogs were to drag the feet of the Prophet's wives, I would not withhold an army that the Messenger of Allah ﷺ had dispatched."*^1

This decision, seemingly reckless to many, proved to be a stroke of strategic brilliance. The departure of Usamah's army projected strength — it suggested that Madinah was so powerful it could launch an expedition even while under threat. Tribes wavering in their loyalty were deterred.

### The Refusal to Compromise on Zakat

The most pivotal challenge came from tribes that refused to pay *zakat*. They sent delegations to Madinah offering a compromise: they would continue to pray, but would not pay *zakat*. Several companions, including Umar ibn al-Khattab, were inclined to accept this bargain to avoid bloodshed.

Al-Tabari records the historic exchange between Abu Bakr and Umar:^1

Umar said: *"How can you fight people who testify that there is no god but Allah?"*

Abu Bakr responded with what became one of the most famous statements in Islamic history:

*"By Allah, I will fight anyone who separates prayer from zakat. Verily, zakat is the right due upon wealth. By Allah, if they were to refuse me even a hobbling cord that they used to pay to the Messenger of Allah ﷺ, I would fight them for withholding it."*^1 ^2

This declaration established a crucial precedent in Islamic political thought: that Islam is an integrated system — prayer, *zakat*, and all other pillars of the *shariah* cannot be selectively observed. Abu Bakr affirmed that the authority of the Islamic state did not depend on the Prophet's person but on the *shariah* he had brought.

### Eleven War Banners

After Usamah's army returned safely — indeed, with a victory — Abu Bakr organized a massive military campaign against the rebellion. Al-Tabari and al-Baladhuri record that Abu Bakr formed **eleven brigades**, each led by a companion-commander, and dispatched them in eleven different directions across the Arabian Peninsula.^1 ^3

Among the key commanders and their assignments:

1. **Khalid ibn al-Walid** — against Tulayha ibn Khuwaylid of the Banu Asad, then proceeding to Yamamah against Musaylimah
2. **Ikrimah ibn Abi Jahl** — first wave toward Yamamah
3. **Shurahbil ibn Hasanah** — reserve for Yamamah
4. **Amr ibn al-As** — against rebel tribes in the north (Quda'ah)
5. **Muhajir ibn Abi Umayyah** — toward Yemen and Hadhramaut
6. **Khalid ibn Sa'id** — toward the Syrian frontier

Abu Bakr gave clear instructions to every commander: before engaging in battle, offer Islam once more to those who had apostatized. If they accept, welcome them back. If they refuse, fight them.^3

## Voices from the Battlefield

### Tulayha ibn Khuwaylid — The False Prophet of Banu Asad

Tulayha ibn Khuwaylid claimed prophethood and rallied followers from the Banu Asad, Banu Fazarah, and parts of the Banu Tayy. He claimed to receive revelation from an angel named Dhu al-Nun. Khalid ibn al-Walid moved swiftly against him.

At the Battle of Buzakha, Khalid's forces decisively defeated Tulayha's army. Al-Tabari records that Tulayha himself fled the battlefield.^1 Remarkably, Tulayha later repented, re-embraced Islam, and participated in the conquests of Persia — a story that demonstrates that the door of repentance remained open even for a false prophet.

### Malik ibn Nuwayrah — The Tragedy at Butah

One of the most controversial episodes of the Riddah Wars was the killing of **Malik ibn Nuwayrah**, leader of the Banu Yarbu' clan of Banu Tamim. Malik had refused to pay *zakat* after the Prophet's death, though his status as a Muslim remains debated among historians.

Al-Tabari records that Khalid ibn al-Walid captured Malik and ordered his execution, despite Malik claiming to still be Muslim.^1 This incident sparked considerable controversy. Abu Qatadah al-Ansari, a companion who participated in the campaign, was so outraged that he left Khalid's army and went directly to Abu Bakr in Madinah to lodge a complaint.

Umar ibn al-Khattab demanded that Khalid be punished. However, Abu Bakr decided not to punish Khalid, reasoning that the commander had acted based on his field judgment — a decision that reflected Abu Bakr's wisdom in balancing justice with military stability during wartime.^1 ^2

### Al-Aswad al-Ansi — The Rebellion in Yemen

**Al-Aswad al-Ansi** had claimed prophethood in Yemen even before the Prophet ﷺ died. He managed to seize Sana'a and killed the Muslim governor there. However, his rebellion ended relatively quickly — he was assassinated through an internal conspiracy on the night before news of the Prophet's death reached Yemen. Al-Tabari records that his own wife played a role in the assassination.^1

### Sajah bint al-Harith — The Prophetess of Banu Tamim

**Sajah bint al-Harith** was a woman of the Banu Tamim who claimed to be a prophetess. She had a Christian background and claimed to receive revelation. What makes her story unique is her meeting with Musaylimah al-Kadhdhab — the two eventually married in an extraordinary political alliance. Al-Tabari records that after Musaylimah's defeat at Yamamah, Sajah repented and returned to Islam.^1

## Analysis and Lessons

### Abu Bakr's Leadership: Resolve Amid the Storm

The Riddah Wars revealed the extraordinary leadership qualities of Abu Bakr. A man known for his gentleness and who often wept while reciting the Quran demonstrated iron resolve when the foundations of Islam were threatened. There was no compromise on principle, even when nearly everyone around him counseled a middle path.^4

Abu Bakr's decision to fight those who refused *zakat* established a vital precedent: that the pillars of Islam cannot be separated, and that the Islamic state bears the obligation to uphold the *shariah* in its entirety. This principle became foundational in *fiqh siyasah* (Islamic constitutional law) throughout history.

### Khalid ibn al-Walid: The Drawn Sword of Allah

**Khalid ibn al-Walid**, given the title *Sayfullah al-Maslul* (The Drawn Sword of Allah) by the Prophet ﷺ himself, displayed extraordinary military genius throughout the Riddah campaigns. His speed of movement from one theater to another — from Buzakha to Butah, then onward to Yamamah — demonstrated a capacity for strategic maneuver rarely seen in world military history.

### The Reunification of Arabia

Historically, the Riddah Wars were a pivotal event that determined the future of Islam and the Arabian Peninsula. Had Abu Bakr failed, Islam would very likely have shrunk back into a regional faith confined to the Hijaz, and the great expansions into Persia and the Byzantine East would never have materialized. Victory in the Riddah Wars reunited Arabia and laid the foundation for the great conquests (*futuhat*) that followed.

### Lessons on Apostasy and Context

The Riddah Wars are often discussed in the context of religious freedom in Islam. It is essential to understand the historical context: those who were fought were not simply individuals who had privately changed their beliefs, but tribes that had militarily and politically rebelled against the state of Madinah, threatening the security and stability of the entire Muslim community. Al-Baladhuri emphasizes that the military and political dimensions of this rebellion cannot be separated from its religious dimension.^3

### The Quran on Steadfastness of Faith

The events of the Riddah had in fact been foreshadowed by the Quran:

> يَا أَيُّهَا الَّذِينَ آمَنُوا مَن يَرْتَدَّ مِنكُمْ عَن دِينِهِ فَسَوْفَ يَأْتِي اللَّهُ بِقَوْمٍ يُحِبُّهُمْ وَيُحِبُّونَهُ أَذِلَّةٍ عَلَى الْمُؤْمِنِينَ أَعِزَّةٍ عَلَى الْكَافِرِينَ ﴿٥٤﴾
>
> *"O you who believe, whoever among you turns back from his religion, Allah will bring forth a people whom He loves and who love Him — humble toward the believers, stern toward the disbelievers. ﴾54﴿"*
> — QS. Al-Ma'idah (5): 54

Commentators, including Ibn Kathir, interpret "a people whom He loves " in this verse as referring to Abu Bakr and the companions who fought against the apostasy.^2
