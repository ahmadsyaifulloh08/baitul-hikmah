# Umar ibn Abd al-Aziz — The Reformist Caliph (717–720 CE)

## Historical Context: The Umayyad Dynasty at Its Zenith and Its Problems

At the beginning of the 8th century CE, the Umayyad Dynasty was at the height of its territorial power — the caliphate stretched from Spain in the west to the borders of China in the east. Yet behind this grandeur lay serious problems threatening stability: unjust taxation of non-Arab Muslims (*mawali*), official corruption, Umayyad family nepotism, and persistent sectarian tensions.^1

It was in this context that **Umar ibn Abd al-Aziz** ascended the throne as the eighth caliph of the Umayyad Dynasty in 99 AH (717 CE). He succeeded Sulayman ibn Abd al-Malik, who had designated him as successor — a surprising choice since Umar was not Sulayman's son but his cousin.^2

Umar ibn Abd al-Aziz was a great-grandson of Umar ibn al-Khattab through his mother — **Umm Asim bint Asim ibn Umar ibn al-Khattab**. This kinship with Caliph Umar al-Faruq was more than genealogy; it appears to have profoundly shaped Umar ibn Abd al-Aziz's vision and character of leadership.^1 ^3

## The Events: Reforms That Shook the Empire

### Day One: Rejecting Luxury

Ibn Abd al-Hakam in *Sirat Umar ibn Abd al-Aziz* describes a dramatic inauguration scene. When Umar was pledged allegiance as caliph, he ascended the pulpit with a pale face and tears in his eyes. He told his people:^1

*"O people, I have been tested with this office without seeking it and without consulting the Muslims. I release the pledge of allegiance upon my neck; choose for yourselves."*

The people cried out: "We have chosen you, O Commander of the Faithful!"

His first action immediately sent shockwaves: he **returned all personal wealth and property** that had been given to him by previous caliphs to the state treasury (*bayt al-mal*). Prize horses, servants, properties, and jewels — all returned.^1 ^2

He also ordered the entire Banu Umayyah family to return assets they had obtained unlawfully over decades. This decision naturally provoked intense anger among his own relatives.

### Tax Reform: Justice for the Mawali

Umar ibn Abd al-Aziz's most consequential reform was in taxation. Under previous caliphs, non-Arab Muslims (*mawali*) — particularly recent converts in Persia, Egypt, and other regions — continued to pay the **jizyah** (non-Muslim tax) despite having already embraced Islam.^2 ^3 This policy was profoundly unjust and contrary to the principle of equality in Islam.

Umar ordered the abolition of the jizyah for all Muslims regardless of ethnicity. Al-Tabari records that when his governors protested that this policy would drastically reduce state revenue, Umar replied:^2

*"By Allah, I wish that all people would embrace Islam so that you and I would have to earn our living with our own hands. "*

### Administrative Reforms

Umar also carried out comprehensive administrative reforms:^1 ^2 ^3

- **Ending the cursing of Ali ibn Abi Talib** — Previous Umayyad caliphs had maintained the reprehensible tradition of cursing Ali from mosque pulpits. Umar abolished this practice and replaced it with the recitation of a Quranic verse:

> إِنَّ اللَّهَ يَأْمُرُ بِالْعَدْلِ وَالْإِحْسَانِ وَإِيتَاءِ ذِي الْقُرْبَىٰ ﴿٩٠﴾
>
> *"Indeed, Allah commands justice, benevolence, and giving to relatives. ﴾90﴿"*
> — QS. An-Nahl (16): 90

- **Dismissal of corrupt officials** — Governors known for oppression were removed and replaced.
- **Limiting police powers** — Umar prohibited torture and imprisonment without due legal process.
- **Infrastructure improvement** — Road construction, well-digging, and hospital building across various provinces.
- **Peaceful *da'wah*** — Umar sent letters of invitation to non-Muslim rulers, calling them to Islam through diplomacy rather than force.

### An Ascetic Lifestyle

Ibn al-Jawzi in *Sifat al-Safwa* paints a picture of Umar's personal life that stood in stark contrast to the luxury of other Umayyad caliphs:^3

- He refused to live in the palace, choosing a simple house instead.
- His salary as caliph was only **two dirhams per day** — far below the standard.
- His wife, **Fatimah bint Abd al-Malik** (daughter of a previous caliph), was asked to return all her jewelry to the state treasury. When asked if she minded, Fatimah replied: *"I would rather be with you than with all the jewelry in the world."*^1
- He often ate only bread and olive oil, and his clothes were full of patches.

## Scriptural Foundations

Umar ibn Abd al-Aziz frequently cited the Prophet's hadith in explaining his policies. Among his most-cited:

> كُلُّكُمْ رَاعٍ وَكُلُّكُمْ مَسْئُولٌ عَنْ رَعِيَّتِهِ
>
> *"Each of you is a shepherd, and each of you will be asked about his flock."*
> — Narrated by al-Bukhari, no. 7138; Muslim, no. 1829

## Analysis and Lessons

### "The Second Umar"

Classical Muslim historians gave Umar ibn Abd al-Aziz the title *"Umar al-Thani"* (the Second Umar) — equating him with his great-grandfather, Umar ibn al-Khattab. Al-Tabari and Ibn Kathir record that many scholars considered him one of the *khulafa' al-rashidun* (rightly-guided caliphs), despite his belonging to the Umayyad Dynasty.^2

Prophet Muhammad ﷺ himself had foreshadowed the return of justice after a period of injustice:

> تَكُونُ النُّبُوَّةُ فِيكُمْ مَا شَاءَ اللَّهُ ... ثُمَّ تَكُونُ خِلَافَةً عَلَىٰ مِنْهَاجِ النُّبُوَّةِ
>
> *"Prophethood will remain among you as long as Allah wills... then there will be a caliphate upon the path of prophethood."*
> — Narrated by Ahmad, no. 18406

Many scholars interpret this final phase as a reference to the leadership of Umar ibn Abd al-Aziz.

### An Early Death and an Enduring Legacy

Umar ibn Abd al-Aziz died in Rajab 101 AH (February 720 CE) at approximately 39 years of age, after ruling for only **two years and five months**. Many accounts state that he was poisoned by members of the Umayyad family who could not accept his reforms.^1 ^3

Despite his extremely brief reign, the impact of his reforms was immense. He proved that an honest and just leader can change the direction of a nation in a short time.

### Justice Above All

The story of Umar ibn Abd al-Aziz teaches that justice is not merely a slogan — it demands real sacrifice. Umar sacrificed personal comfort, family relationships, and even his safety for the sake of justice. This represents the highest standard of leadership in the Islamic tradition.

### Reform from Within

What makes Umar unique is that he reformed the system from within. He was not an outside rebel — he was a member of the ruling family who chose to change a corrupt system from a position of power. This demonstrates that genuine change does not always require revolution; sometimes reform from within is more effective and meaningful.
