# Abu Bakr as-Siddiq — The First Caliph

## Context: Crisis After the Death of the Prophet ﷺ

The death of Prophet Muhammad ﷺ on 12 Rabi al-Awwal 11 AH (632 CE) left a leadership vacuum without precedent in Islamic history. The Prophet had not explicitly designated a successor — though there were strong indications pointing toward **Abu Bakr as-Siddiq**, such as the command for him to lead prayers during the Prophet's illness and the order to seal all doors to the mosque except Abu Bakr's.^1

The situation on the day of the Prophet's death was critical. His body had not yet been buried, while the question of leadership demanded immediate resolution. The Ansar (the native people of Madinah) gathered at the **Saqifah of Banu Sa'idah** — a meeting hall — to discuss the selection of a leader. They intended to pledge allegiance to Sa'd ibn Ubadah, chief of the Khazraj tribe, as caliph.^1 ^2

## The Event at Saqifah Bani Sa'idah

### The Decisive Meeting

Al-Tabari in *Tarikh al-Rusul wa al-Muluk* narrates the events at Saqifah in considerable detail. When Abu Bakr and Umar learned that the Ansar had assembled at the Saqifah, they hurried there together with Abu Ubaidah ibn al-Jarrah.^1

At the Saqifah, the Ansar had already begun their deliberations. Sa'd ibn Ubadah — who was ill at the time — delivered a speech emphasizing the contributions of the Ansar: they were the ones who sheltered the Prophet ﷺ, they provided refuge for the Muhajirin, and they formed the military backbone of Islam.^1

When Abu Bakr arrived, he listened carefully. Then he stood and spoke calmly yet decisively. Ibn Kathir in *al-Bidayah wa al-Nihayah* records that Abu Bakr acknowledged every contribution of the Ansar without reservation. However, he then presented the key argument: that the leadership of Islam must rest with the Quraysh, because all the Arab tribes would only accept authority from this tribe, and the Prophet ﷺ himself was from the Quraysh.^2

Abu Bakr then offered: *"I am content to pledge allegiance to either of these two men: Umar ibn al-Khattab or Abu Ubaidah ibn al-Jarrah."*^1 Both immediately refused. Umar said: *"By Allah, we will not accept this leadership while you are alive. You are the most worthy among the Muhajirin. You were the Prophet's companion in the cave. You led the prayer when he was ill."*

### The Pledge to Abu Bakr

Umar immediately extended his hand and pledged allegiance to Abu Bakr. Abu Ubaidah followed. Then the Ansar — save for a handful — pledged as well. Al-Tabari records that the process, though filled with debate, concluded swiftly.^1

The following day, Abu Bakr ascended the pulpit of the Prophet's Mosque and received the general pledge of allegiance from all the people of Madinah. It was then that he delivered his famous inaugural address:

*"O people, I have been appointed as your leader, yet I am not the best among you. If I do well, support me. If I err, correct me. Truthfulness is a trust, and lying is a betrayal. The weak among you is strong in my sight until I restore his right to him. And the strong among you is weak in my sight until I take from him the rights of others. Obey me so long as I obey Allah and His Messenger. But if I disobey Allah and His Messenger, then you owe me no obedience."*^1 ^2

This speech — brief yet monumental — established several revolutionary principles of governance: the accountability of the ruler, the supremacy of law, the people's right to correct their leader, and conditional obedience contingent upon the leader's adherence to divine command.

## Dispatching the Army of Usamah

One of Abu Bakr's first and most controversial decisions as caliph was dispatching the **army of Usamah ibn Zayd**. The Prophet ﷺ had ordered a military expedition to the Roman frontier under the command of Usamah — a young man of approximately 18-20 years — before falling ill. The army had already encamped outside Madinah when the Prophet ﷺ died.^1

Many senior companions urged Abu Bakr to cancel or postpone the expedition. Their reasoning was sound: Madinah was in crisis, tribes were beginning to apostatize, and sending a large force northward would weaken Madinah's defenses. Some even objected to Usamah's youthful command.

Abu Bakr, however, was resolute:

*"By Allah, in Whose hand is my soul, even if I were certain that wild beasts would drag me away, I would still dispatch the army of Usamah as the Messenger of Allah ﷺ commanded. I will not untie a knot that the Messenger of Allah ﷺ has tied."*^1 ^2

This decision demonstrated Abu Bakr's unwavering principle: the Prophet's commands must be carried out without negotiation. Strategically, the decision proved brilliant — Usamah's successful expedition signaled to the Arab tribes that Madinah remained formidable despite the Prophet's death.

## The Riddah Wars: Defending the Integrity of Islam

### The Wave of Apostasy

The greatest crisis Abu Bakr faced was the wave of *riddah* (apostasy) that swept across much of the Arabian Peninsula. Al-Tabari records that the apostasy took several forms:^1

1. **Complete apostasy** — tribes that returned to idol worship and abandoned Islam entirely
2. **False prophets** — individuals who claimed prophethood, most notably **Musaylimah al-Kadhdhab** in Yamamah, Sajah bint al-Harith, and Tulayhah ibn Khuwaylid
3. **Zakat refusers** — tribes that still professed Islam but refused to pay the *zakat* tax to the central government in Madinah

### Abu Bakr's Resolve

The zakat refusers sparked heated debate. Umar and several other companions argued that they should not be fought because they still pronounced the *shahadah*. Abu Bakr, however, refused to compromise:

*"By Allah, if they withhold even the rope used to tie a camel that they used to give to the Messenger of Allah ﷺ, I will fight them for withholding it."*^1

Ibn Kathir in *al-Bidayah wa al-Nihayah* notes that Abu Bakr's argument rested on the principle that *zakat* is an obligation inseparable from prayer — both are mentioned together in dozens of Quranic verses. To refuse *zakat* was to reject a fundamental pillar of Islam.^2

### The Battle of Yamamah and the Defeat of Musaylimah

The fiercest battle of the Riddah Wars was the **Battle of Yamamah** against Musaylimah al-Kadhdhab and his forces from the Banu Hanifah. Al-Tabari narrates that Musaylimah commanded a very large army and held easily defensible terrain.^1

Abu Bakr dispatched **Khalid ibn al-Walid** to lead the campaign against Musaylimah. The battle was ferocious — many *huffazh* (memorizers of the Quran) fell as martyrs. Ibn Kathir records that approximately seventy memorizers of the Quran were killed at Yamamah — a staggering number given the limited total of memorizers at the time.^2

Ultimately, Wahshi ibn Harb — the same man who had killed Hamzah ibn Abd al-Muttalib at the Battle of Uhud — hurled his spear and struck Musaylimah. The battle ended in a decisive victory for the Muslim forces.

The Riddah Wars as a whole succeeded in restoring the entire Arabian Peninsula to Islamic authority in less than a year — a remarkable military and political achievement.

## The Compilation of the Quran: An Eternal Project

### Background

The large number of Quran memorizers killed at Yamamah raised serious alarm. **Umar ibn al-Khattab** approached Abu Bakr and proposed that the Quran be compiled into a single written manuscript — for if memorizers continued to fall, portions of the Quran could be lost.^1 ^2

Abu Bakr was initially hesitant: *"How can I do something that the Messenger of Allah ﷺ did not do?"* But Umar persisted until Abu Bakr agreed — and he entrusted **Zayd ibn Thabit**, the Prophet's scribe of revelation, with leading the compilation project.

### The Methodology of Compilation

Zayd ibn Thabit employed an extraordinarily rigorous methodology: every verse had to be verified by at least two witnesses who had heard it directly from the Prophet ﷺ, in addition to being cross-checked against the memorization of the *huffazh*. Al-Tabari records that Zayd said: *"By Allah, had they tasked me with moving a mountain, it would not have been heavier than what they commanded me to do in compiling the Quran."*^1

The resulting manuscript was kept by Abu Bakr, then passed to Umar, then to Hafsah bint Umar — until it eventually served as the basis for the standardized copies produced during the caliphate of Uthman ibn Affan.

## The Death of Abu Bakr (634 CE)

Abu Bakr served as caliph for only two years and three months. In the month of Jumada al-Akhirah, 13 AH (634 CE), he fell ill. Ibn al-Jawzi in *Sifat al-Safwa* describes how Abu Bakr faced his illness with the same composure and surrender that the Prophet ﷺ had demonstrated.^3

Before his death, Abu Bakr consulted with the senior companions and designated **Umar ibn al-Khattab** as his successor — a decision accepted by the majority of Muslims.

Abu Bakr requested that he be buried beside the Messenger of Allah ﷺ. He also requested that his shroud be his old, worn garments — not new cloth — because *"the living need new cloth more than the dead."*^3

Abu Bakr died on the night of Tuesday, 22 Jumada al-Akhirah, 13 AH, at the age of sixty-three — the same age as the Messenger of Allah ﷺ at his death. He was buried beside the Prophet ﷺ in Aisha's room.

## Analysis: Abu Bakr's Legacy for Islamic Civilization

### Preserving Unity in Times of Division

Abu Bakr's greatest achievement was preserving the unity of the Muslim community at its most critical juncture. When tribes apostatized, when false prophets arose, and when the foundations of the Islamic state trembled, Abu Bakr stood firm. Without his decisiveness in the Riddah Wars, Islam might well have contracted back into a local movement in Madinah.

### A Model of Servant Leadership

Abu Bakr's inaugural address established a standard of leadership unprecedented in the Arab world — and perhaps anywhere in the world at that time. A ruler who openly declares that he is not the best, that the people have the right to correct him, and that obedience to him is conditional — this was an early form of public accountability that remains remarkably modern.

### Preserving Revelation

Abu Bakr's decision to compile the Quran into a single manuscript was one of the most consequential decisions in Islamic history — and in human history. Without this project, the integrity of the Quranic text we read today might have been jeopardized. Abu Bakr understood that preserving revelation was more important than any military conquest.

### The Title "As-Siddiq" — The Truthful

Ibn al-Jawzi in *Sifat al-Safwa* explains why Abu Bakr received the title *as-Siddiq* (the Supremely Truthful). He earned it because he was the first to affirm the Prophet ﷺ during the Night Journey*Isra' wa Mi'raj* — when all of Makkah mocked the Prophet's claim of traveling to Jerusalem in a single night. Abu Bakr said: *"If he said it, then he is truthful."* Without question, without hesitation.^3

This truthfulness was not mere verbal affirmation — it manifested in every action Abu Bakr took: he affirmed the Prophet ﷺ with his wealth, his family, his life, and ultimately with his brief yet monumental leadership.

## Lessons from the First Caliph

**First**, that true leadership is tested in crisis. Abu Bakr did not become caliph during peacetime — he assumed office at the most critical juncture and successfully navigated a storm that threatened total destruction.

**Second**, that principles must not be compromised. Abu Bakr's refusal to compromise on the matter of *zakat* and the Prophet's commands demonstrates that certain things are more important than political convenience.

**Third**, that good leaders prepare their successors. By designating Umar, Abu Bakr ensured a stable continuity of leadership — rather than leaving the community in a vacuum.

**Fourth**, that simplicity and integrity are a leader's true crown. Abu Bakr did not accumulate wealth during his tenure, did not build palaces, and even requested burial in his old garments. Power, for him, was a trust — not a privilege.
