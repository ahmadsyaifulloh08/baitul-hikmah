# The Battle of Ain Jalut — Mamluks Stop the Mongols (1260 CE)

## Context of Mongol Invasion: Tsunami from the East

In the mid-13th century CE, the Islamic world faced an existential threat unprecedented in its history. The Mongols, who had swept away civilizations from China to Eastern Europe, now turned their gaze to the heart of the Islamic world. The destruction they left behind was not merely military, but total annihilation: cities razed, libraries burned, irrigation systems destroyed, and millions of lives extinguished.^1

**Hulagu Khan**, grandson of Genghis Khan and ruler of western Mongolia, received a special mandate from his brother **Möngke Khan** (Great Khan) to conquer the entire Middle East up to Egypt. This expedition that began in 1253 CE was not merely an ordinary military campaign — it was a mission of systematic extermination against Islamic political power in the region.^1

The destruction began with **the fall of Alamut** (1256 CE), the final fortress of the Assassin sect considered impregnable. Hulagu used advanced Chinese siege technology — giant catapults and Chinese fire cannons (*huo pao*) — to destroy stone fortresses that had stood for hundreds of years. The massacre that followed became a portent of what would happen to other Muslim cities.^2

**Baghdad, capital of the Islamic world for 500 years, fell on February 10, 1258 CE** after a brief siege. Ibn al-Athir described the destruction that occurred: *"Since Allah created Adam until today, there has never been such a calamity befalling Islam."*^2 Al-Musta'sim Billah, the last Abbasid caliph, was executed by being wrapped in carpet and trampled by horses — a Mongol method of killing to avoid "spilling noble blood." An estimated 200,000-1,000,000 Baghdad residents were massacred in a week of systematic slaughter.^2

The world's largest library at that time, Baitul Hikmah of Baghdad, was completely destroyed. Books were thrown into the Tigris River until the water turned black with ink for days. Ibn Kathir recorded: *"Knowledge that had been accumulated for centuries vanished in a matter of days. Civilization that took five centuries to build was destroyed in five days."*^2

## Westward Movement: Damascus and Aleppo

After Baghdad, Mongol forces continued moving west with unstoppable momentum. **Aleppo fell in January 1260 CE** after a six-day siege. Al-Nasir Yusuf, the last Ayyubid ruler in Syria, fled without resistance, leaving his people to face the same fate as Baghdad.^3

**Damascus, the pearl of the Levant, surrendered without resistance on March 1, 1260 CE.** The city's inhabitants, having heard the fate of Baghdad and Aleppo, opened the city gates to avoid massacre. Hulagu showed "mercy" by not conducting mass slaughter, but the city was thoroughly plundered and many scholars and intellectuals were executed.^3

Terrifyingly, Hulagu was not moving alone. He had surprising local allies: **Bohemond VI of Antioch and Hetum I of Lesser Armenia**, Christian rulers who saw the Mongol invasion as an opportunity to revenge their defeats in the Crusades. They provided troops and local guides to the Mongols, creating a Christian-Mongol alliance that threatened Islam's existence in the region.^3

## Mamluk Situation: The Only Remaining Islamic Power

While the eastern Islamic world was completely destroyed, in the west only one organized power remained: **the Mamluk Sultanate in Egypt**. The land of the Nile became a refuge for thousands of Muslim scholars, merchants, and craftsmen fleeing the destruction in Baghdad and Syria. They brought horrific stories of Mongol brutality that made all of Egypt shudder with terror.^2

**Sultan al-Mudzaffar Qutuz** ascended the Mamluk throne in 1259 CE in a very difficult situation. Born with the original name Mahmud ibn Mamdud, he was actually a prince of Khwarizm who was sold as a slave after his kingdom was destroyed by the Mongols. The irony of fate made him now the only Muslim leader capable of facing the conqueror of his homeland.^2

Qutuz faced dual challenges: besides external threats from the Mongols, he also faced internal instability. Mamluk amirs were split between those who wanted to negotiate with the Mongols and those who insisted on war. Some amirs even suggested retreating to Nubia or Sudan and letting the Mongols occupy Egypt.^2

## Qutuz and Baibars: Unity in Crisis

In this crisis situation, Qutuz made a very controversial but brilliant decision: recalling **Baibars al-Bunduqdari** from exile. Baibars was one of the most talented Mamluk commanders, but he had been exiled for his involvement in the murder of Sultan Turanshah (Qutuz's predecessor) and internal political conflicts.^1

**Baibars al-Bunduqdari** (full name al-Malik adh-Dhahir Rukn ad-Din Baibars) was a Kipchak Turk purchased as a slave from the Central Asian steppes. He had extensive combat experience against the Crusaders and a reputation as the finest tactical strategist among the Mamluks. Most importantly, he was the only Mamluk commander who had directly faced Mongol forces on a small scale and understood their tactics.^1

The meeting between Qutuz and Baibars in Cairo in early 1260 CE was filled with tension. Ibn Taghribirdi recorded their dialogue: Qutuz said, *"O Baibars, this threat exceeds our disputes. Islam is in danger."* Baibars replied, *"Sultan, I am willing to die for Islam, though not for you."* Though their personal relationship remained cold, both agreed that Islam's interests were greater than personal ambitions.^1

## Hulagu's Ultimatum: Threat of Total Annihilation

In mid-1260 CE, Hulagu sent a special envoy to Cairo carrying a very insulting ultimatum letter. The contents of the letter, as recorded by Al-Maqrizi, showed arrogance and systematic threats:^3

*"From the King of Kings, God on earth, to Qutuz who claims to be Sultan of Egypt:*

*We are the army of God on His earth. He created us from the fire of His wrath to punish those who rebel against His commands. Look at what we have done to countries that oppose us: Baghdad, capital of Islam, we have razed. Its caliph we have killed. Its scholars we have bathed in their own blood.*

*If you surrender and acknowledge our sovereignty, you will be safe. But if you resist, heaven will not accept your souls, and you will suffer the same fate as Baghdad.*

*We have warned you. The responsibility for spilled blood is in your hands."*^3

This letter was not only a military ultimatum, but also psychological warfare designed to paralyze the spirit of resistance. Hulagu deliberately mentioned details of Baghdad's destruction to instill fear and demoralization.

Reactions in the Mamluk palace were very diverse. Some amirs led by **Faris al-Din Aqtay al-Mustaqrib** proposed negotiating or at least sending tribute to placate Hulagu. They argued that the Mamluks could not possibly defeat a power that had destroyed an empire as large as the Abbasid Caliphate.^2

## Historic Decision: "If You Don't March, I'll Go Myself!"

Facing this division, Sultan Qutuz made a decision that would change world history. In an emergency meeting of amirs at the Cairo Citadel, he rose and with a voice trembling with emotion said:^2

*"O amirs! You consume the wealth of Baitul Mal and refuse to wage jihad in Allah's path! You live from the taxes of Muslim people and now when Islam is threatened, you want to flee? By Allah, there is no god but He, if you do not march to war, I myself will go with this sword in my hand!"*

Then, in a dramatic moment, Qutuz removed his sultan's crown and placed it on the floor. *"If any of you is braver to lead this jihad, please take this crown. I don't need a throne if it must be bought with betrayal of Islam."*^2

This speech shook the amirs. Baibars was the first to rise and say: *"The Sultan has spoken with the heart of a believer. We are with you until death."* One by one, the other amirs followed, until the decision for war was agreed upon unanimously.^2

To finance this very expensive war, Qutuz took a step never before taken by previous Mamluk rulers: he auctioned all his personal property and palace jewelry. Even Qutuz's queen contributed all her personal gold and gems to buy weapons and armor.^2

## Mobilization and War Strategy

Mamluk mobilization to face the Mongols was an extraordinary logistical achievement. Within weeks, Qutuz managed to assemble about 20,000 elite Mamluk soldiers — the finest force ever assembled by the sultanate. This army consisted of:^4

- **12,000 heavy Mamluk cavalry** (*Faris*) — the core strength where each person was a professional soldier with years of training
- **5,000 light cavalry** (*Turcoman*) from Arab and Turkmen tribes who joined the jihad  
- **3,000 infantry** including Nubian archers and sword fighters from southern Egypt

What distinguished this army from other Muslim armies defeated by the Mongols was **standardization of equipment and training**. Each Mamluk was equipped with identical lamellar armor, high-quality composite bows, and at least three war horses. They were also trained in tight formations and coordination tactics similar to Mongol discipline itself.^4

Baibars developed a clever strategy based on his analysis of Mongol tactical weaknesses. He realized that Mongols relied on **pincer maneuvers** and **feigned retreats** to draw enemies out of formation. Therefore, he designed counter-tactics: Mamluks would use the same tactics, but with tighter coordination and higher flexibility.^1

## Situation Development: Death of Möngke Khan

While Qutuz was preparing his forces, an event occurred that changed the strategic situation: **Möngke Khan, the Great Khan of the Mongols, died in China in August 1259 CE**. This death triggered a succession crisis among the Mongols that forced many senior commanders to return to Karakorum to elect a new khan. Hulagu was forced to send most of his forces east, leaving only about 10,000-15,000 soldiers in Syria under the command of **Kitbogha Noyan**.^1

Kitbogha Noyan was a Nestorian Christian Mongol who had additional motivation to destroy Islamic power. He felt confident that with the support of local Christian allies (Antioch and Armenia), the remaining force was sufficient to conquer Egypt. This decision became a fatal mistake that would determine the course of history.^1

## Movement to Gaza: Diplomacy and Espionage

In summer 1260 CE, Mamluk forces began moving out of Egypt toward Gaza in neat formation. This was a critical moment determining the future of the Islamic world. If they lost, there would be no power left to stop the Mongols from reaching Mecca and Medina.

Qutuz sent secret letters to the inhabitants of Damascus and Aleppo, informing them of the coming liberating army and asking them to prepare to rebel against Mongol garrisons. He also negotiated with **Prince Julian of Sidon**, one of the Crusader lords, to get safe passage through their territory to Galilee.^3

Surprisingly, some Crusader lords actually provided logistical assistance to the Mamluks. They realized that if the Mongols controlled the entire region, their position as an independent power would end. Better to face Muslims they already knew than Mongols they had never understood.^3

## September 3, 1260 CE: The Battle That Changed History

On **September 3, 1260 CE (25 Ramadan 658 AH)**, both armies met at the plains of **Ain Jalut** (Spring of Goliath), near Nazareth, Palestine. This location was carefully chosen by Baibars because it was relatively open terrain but had small hills that could be used to hide reserve forces.^3

That morning, thick fog enveloped the plains, making visibility very limited. Kitbogha Noyan, with 12,000 troops consisting of core Mongols, Turkish auxiliaries, and Armenian-Georgian Christian forces, moved in standard Mongol formation: strong center with cavalry archers on both flanks.^3

Baibars began the battle with **feigned frontal assault** tactics. He led 3,000 light cavalry attacking the Mongol center with thunderous takbir cries, then retreated in an "orderly" manner as if beaten back. This tactic aimed to lure the Mongols into pursuing into a trap.^1

Kitbogha, confident of easily defeating the "Muslim barbarians" as had always happened before, ordered aggressive pursuit. Mongol cavalry chased Baibars into a narrow valley between two small hills. This was what the Mamluks had been waiting for.^1

## Critical Moment: "Wa Islamah!"

At the planned moment, Baibars gave the signal with a yellow flag. Suddenly, from both sides of the hills, **8,000 heavy Mamluk cavalry** who had been hiding since dawn appeared and struck the right and left flanks of the Mongols who were in pursuit formation. This pincer attack was devastating — the Mongols were trapped and could not maneuver.^1

In the midst of fierce fighting when both sides were pressing each other in close combat, a moment occurred that became legend. Mamluk forces seemed to be getting divided and there were signs of possible retreat. Seeing this critical situation, **Sultan Qutuz descended directly to the battlefield**.^2

Qutuz removed his helmet so his face could be clearly seen by all troops, then with a thunderous voice shouted: **"Wa Islamah! Wa Islamah!"** (O Islam! O Islam!) while raising his sword high. Then he continued: *"I am the Sultan! I will die or win! No retreat!"*^2

This cry had an electric effect on all Mamluk forces. Those who had begun to waver suddenly gained new spirit. With cries of "*La ilaha illa Allah! Muhammad Rasulullah!*" that resounded, all Mamluk forces charged with fury that shook the earth.^2

## Mongol Destruction and Kitbogha's Death

This final Mamluk assault destroyed the resistance of the already surrounded Mongols. **Kitbogha Noyan** himself was killed in personal duel with one of the Mamluk amirs after fighting with admirable courage until the last second. Al-Maqrizi recorded that Kitbogha's body was found surrounded by 15 Mamluk corpses — showing how fierce his final resistance was.^3

The auxiliary Armenian and Georgian forces, seeing their commander dead and the Mongol core destroyed, fled in panic. Only about 2,000 Mongol soldiers managed to escape the massacre, while the rest — nearly 10,000 men — died or were captured.^3

Surprisingly, Qutuz gave orders to treat prisoners well according to Islamic teachings, different from Mongol practice which usually slaughtered all defeated soldiers. Some Mongol prisoners who converted to Islam later joined Mamluk forces.^2

## Liberation of Damascus and Domino Effect

News of the Ain Jalut victory spread like lightning throughout the region. **Damascus, which had been under Mongol control for almost a year, immediately experienced popular rebellion**. The remaining Mongol garrison, without support from the main army that had been destroyed, could not defend the city and fled north.^3

One by one, Syrian cities under Mongol control were liberated: **Aleppo, Hama, Homs**. Within weeks, all Mongols were expelled from Syria and retreated to their territories in northern Mesopotamia and Persia. Hulagu Khan, who was in Maragheh (Iran), received news of the defeat with extraordinary shock.^1

More importantly, this victory had tremendous psychological effects. **The myth of Mongol invincibility** — which had lasted for 50 years and destroyed morale throughout the world from Europe to Asia — finally collapsed. Muslims throughout the world, from Al-Andalus to India, gained new spirit that invaders could be defeated.^1

## Global Impact: Saving Civilization

The significance of the Battle of Ain Jalut went beyond mere military victory. This was a **turning point in world history** that saved Islamic civilization from total extinction and changed the direction of global history:

**1. Saving Mecca and Medina**: If the Mamluks had lost, there would be no power left to prevent the Mongols from reaching the Haramain. The destruction of Mecca and Medina would be an irreparable psychological trauma for Muslims worldwide.^1

**2. Maintaining trade routes**: Mongol control over the entire silk route from China to the Mediterranean would give them total economic monopoly. Mamluk victory ensured east-west trade routes remained diversified.^4

**3. Protecting Africa**: Without resistance in Egypt, the Mongols would likely continue expansion into North Africa and possibly sub-Saharan Africa. This would fundamentally change African history.^4

**4. Inspiring global resistance**: News of Ain Jalut victory reached Europe, India, and Southeast Asia, inspiring local resistance against Mongol expansion in various regions.^1

## Qutuz vs Baibars: Tragedy Amid Victory

Ironically, this brilliant victory was followed by a shocking personal tragedy. On the return journey from Syria to Egypt, open conflict occurred between Qutuz and Baibars regarding the division of liberated territories.^2

Qutuz insisted that all of Syria should be directly integrated into the Mamluk Sultanate with governors appointed from Cairo. Baibars, feeling his services in victory were not properly appreciated, demanded autonomous governorship over part of Syria as compensation.^2

This tension culminated on **October 23, 1260 CE** near Gaza when Baibars and several other senior amirs killed Qutuz in a sudden attack. Ibn Taghribirdi recorded Qutuz's last words: *"Baibars, is this the reward for my service to you?"*^2

Thus, the hero who had saved the Islamic world from total destruction died at the hands of his own ally only 50 days after his historic victory. Baibars then ascended the throne as Sultan and continued the war against Mongols and Crusader States.^2

## Analysis and Wisdom

The Battle of Ain Jalut teaches several eternal lessons:^1 ^2

**Unity in facing crisis**: Qutuz and Baibars set aside personal disputes for the greater good.^1 **Brave leadership**: Qutuz did not merely command from behind but descended directly to the battlefield.^2 **Faith and spirit**: In a nearly desperate situation, faith and the spirit of jihad became the force that overcame the enemy's numerical superiority.^2

Most importantly, this battle proved that **revival begins with the decision to rise**, not from ideal conditions. The Mamluks did not wait until they had forces as large as the Mongols or more advanced weaponry. They fought with what they had, but with unshakeable determination.

Ain Jalut also shows the importance of **strategic timing**. This victory was not coincidence, but the result of precise calculation: attacking when the enemy was divided, using advantageous terrain, and utilizing spiritual motivation to overcome material weaknesses.

The Battle of Ain Jalut marked a turning point in Islamic world history, saving civilization from total destruction and opening the way for the revival of Islamic power in the Middle East.^1 ^3 ^5 This is proof that strong faith, solid unity, and brave leadership can overcome any force, however great.

## Preparation and Strategy

Qutuz took the revolutionary step of seeking help from his former enemy, Prince **Baibars al-Bunduqdari** who had previously been exiled.^1 Baibars was the finest military strategist among the Mamluks and had experience fighting the Mongols.^1 Despite personal tensions, both united against the common threat.^1

The Mamluk army consisted of about 20,000 soldiers, far smaller than the estimated 40,000 Mongol forces.^4 However, they had advantages: high discipline, superior cavalry weapons, and most importantly, a burning spirit of jihad.^4

## The Battle at Ain Jalut

On September 3, 1260 CE (25 Ramadan 658 AH), both armies met at the plains of Ain Jalut (Spring of Goliath) near Nazareth, Palestine.^3 Kitdhuka Noyan, the Mongol commander, led forces consisting of Mongols, Turks, and Georgian and Armenian Christian troops.^3

Baibars began the battle by attacking the Mongol left flank using classic Mamluk hit-and-run tactics.^1 When the Mongol forces pursued, Baibars retreated according to plan to draw the enemy into a trap.^1 In the midst of fierce fighting, Sultan Qutuz himself entered the battlefield shouting: "Wa Islamah!" (O Islam!), inspiring his troops.^2

At the critical moment when Mamluk forces seemed pressed, Qutuz removed his helmet to be recognized by his troops and shouted loudly: "I am the Sultan! I will die or win!"^2 This spirit spread and the Mamluk army rose with a devastating counterattack.^2

## Victory and Its Impact

The Mongol army was ultimately decisively defeated.^1 Kitdhuka Noyan was killed in battle, and the remaining Mongol forces fled.^1 For the first time in history, Mongol forces suffered a crushing defeat in open battle on flat terrain.^1

News of this victory spread rapidly throughout the Islamic world.^3 Damascus, previously under Mongol control, was quickly liberated.^3 The Mongol threat to Egypt and North Africa ended, while Hulagu Khan was forced to retreat to Persia.^3
