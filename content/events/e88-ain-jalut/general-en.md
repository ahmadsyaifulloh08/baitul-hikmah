# The Battle of Ain Jalut — Mamluks Stop the Mongols

## Background

In the mid-13th century CE, the Islamic world faced its greatest threat since the Crusades.^1 The Mongols under Hulagu Khan, grandson of Genghis Khan, had destroyed Baghdad in 1258 CE and ended the Abbasid Caliphate.^1 The Mongol army continued westward, conquering Damascus and threatening to completely annihilate Islamic civilization in the Middle East.^1

Amidst this destruction, only the Mamluk Sultanate in Egypt remained as an organized Islamic power.^2 **Sultan Qutuz** (al-Malik al-Mudzaffar Saif ad-Din Qutuz) ascended the Mamluk throne in 1259 CE during this crisis.^2 He faced a terrible dilemma: surrender to the Mongols or prepare to face an army that had never been defeated in open battle.^2

## Mongol Threat and Hulagu's Ultimatum

Hulagu Khan sent an ultimatum to Sultan Qutuz containing threats of total annihilation if Egypt did not surrender unconditionally.^3 The ultimatum mockingly read: "We are the army of God on His earth. He created us from the fire of His wrath... If you submit, you will be safe, but if you resist, what happened to Baghdad will happen to you."^3

Facing this threat, Sultan Qutuz gathered the Mamluk amirs for consultation.^2 Many advised negotiation or retreat to the south.^2 However, Qutuz firmly declared: "O amirs, you consume the wealth of Baitul Mal but refuse to wage jihad in Allah's path. By Allah, if you do not march, I will go alone!"^2

## Preparation and Strategy

Qutuz took the revolutionary step of seeking help from his former enemy, Prince **Baibars al-Bunduqdari** who had previously been exiled.^1 Baibars was the finest military strategist among the Mamluks and had experience fighting the Mongols.^1 Despite personal tensions, both united against the common threat.^1

The Mamluk army consisted of about 20,000 soldiers, far smaller than the estimated 40,000 Mongol forces.^4 However, they had advantages: high discipline, superior cavalry weapons, and most importantly, a burning spirit of jihad.^4

## The Battle at Ain Jalut

On September 3, 1260 CE (25 Ramadan 658 AH), both armies met at the plains of Ain Jalut (Spring of Goliath) near Nazareth, Palestine.^3 Kitdhuka Noyan, the Mongol commander, led forces consisting of Mongols, Turks, and Georgian and Armenian Christian troops.^3

Baibars began the battle by attacking the Mongol left flank using classic Mamluk hit-and-run tactics.^1 When the Mongol forces pursued, Baibars retreated according to plan to draw the enemy into a trap.^1 In the midst of fierce fighting, Sultan Qutuz himself entered the battlefield shouting: "Wa Islamah!" (O Islam!), inspiring his troops.^2

At the critical moment when Mamluk forces seemed pressed, Qutuz removed his helmet to be recognized by his troops and shouted loudly: "I am the Sultan! I will die or win!"^2 This spirit spread and the Mamluk army rose with a devastating counterattack.^2

## Victory and Its Impact

The Mongol army was ultimately decisively defeated.^1 Kitdhuka Noyan was killed in battle, and the remaining Mongol forces fled.^1 For the first time in history, Mongol forces suffered a crushing defeat in open battle on flat terrain.^1

News of this victory spread rapidly throughout the Islamic world.^3 Damascus, previously under Mongol control, was quickly liberated.^3 The Mongol threat to Egypt and North Africa ended, while Hulagu Khan was forced to retreat to Persia.^3

## Lessons and Wisdom

The Battle of Ain Jalut teaches several important lessons:^1 ^2

**Unity in facing crisis**: Qutuz and Baibars set aside personal disputes for the greater good.^1 **Brave leadership**: Qutuz did not merely command from behind but entered the battlefield directly.^2 **Faith and spirit**: In a nearly desperate situation, faith and the spirit of jihad became the force that overcame the enemy's numerical superiority.^2
