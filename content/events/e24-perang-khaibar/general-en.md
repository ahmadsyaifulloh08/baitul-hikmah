# The Battle of Khaybar

## Context: The Threat from the North

Khaybar was a large oasis situated approximately 150 kilometers north of Madinah, consisting of seven formidable fortresses built atop volcanic hills. Its inhabitants were a Jewish community powerful both economically and militarily — they controlled the most fertile agricultural land in the Hijaz and possessed abundant weaponry.^1 ^2

After the expulsion of the Banu Nadir from Madinah, their leaders such as **Huyayy ibn Akhtab** and **Sallam ibn Abi al-Huqayq** made Khaybar their new headquarters. From there, they actively forged anti-Muslim alliances. They were the chief architects of the Battle of the Trench (Khandaq), inciting Arab tribes and financing a massive coalition to besiege Madinah.^1 ^2 ^3

Even after the failure of the Trench siege, Khaybar remained a center of conspiracy. They cultivated ties with the Ghatafan tribe, promising half of Khaybar's harvest if Ghatafan would help attack Madinah.^2 This threat could not be tolerated indefinitely.

After the Treaty of Hudaybiyyah secured the southern front (the Quraysh), Prophet Muhammad ﷺ could turn his attention to the northern threat. In the month of Muharram of the 7th year of Hijrah (May 629 CE), he led approximately 1,600 troops toward Khaybar.^1

## The Event: Fortress by Fortress

### Preparation and March

Prophet Muhammad ﷺ departed from Madinah with a force composed of Hudaybiyyah veterans — those who had pledged beneath the tree. The march lasted several days through rocky desert terrain. He deliberately chose a route that cut the road between Khaybar and their ally Ghatafan, preventing reinforcements.^2

When the caravan arrived near Khaybar at dawn, they found Khaybar's farmers emerging with hoes and baskets heading to their fields — unaware of the Muslim army's arrival. Seeing the large force, they screamed in panic and fled into their fortresses. Prophet Muhammad ﷺ declared:^1

> اللَّهُ أَكْبَرُ، خَرِبَتْ خَيْبَرُ، إِنَّا إِذَا نَزَلْنَا بِسَاحَةِ قَوْمٍ فَسَاءَ صَبَاحُ الْمُنْذَرِينَ
>
> *"Allahu Akbar! Khaybar is ruined! When we descend upon a people's courtyard, evil is the morning of those who have been warned."*
> — Sahih al-Bukhari, no. 4197, Kitab al-Maghazi

### The Fortresses of Na'im and al-Sa'b

The fighting began at the fortresses in the **al-Natah** district. The first to fall were the fortresses of Na'im and al-Sa'b. The battles were fierce — Khaybar's defenders were formidable warriors entrenched behind thick stone walls on hilltops, raining down rocks and arrows from elevated positions.^2 ^3

During the first several days, the Muslim forces struggled to breach the defenses. Abu Bakr led an assault on one fortress but could not penetrate it. Then Umar led the next attack, with the same result.^1 ^2

### Ali and the Fall of Fortress Qamus

At this point occurred one of the most celebrated moments in Islamic history. Prophet Muhammad ﷺ declared at night:^1

> لَأُعْطِيَنَّ الرَّايَةَ غَدًا رَجُلًا يَفْتَحُ اللَّهُ عَلَى يَدَيْهِ، يُحِبُّ اللَّهَ وَرَسُولَهُ وَيُحِبُّهُ اللَّهُ وَرَسُولُهُ
>
> *"I shall give the banner tomorrow to a man through whom Allah will grant victory — he loves Allah and His Messenger, and Allah and His Messenger love him."*
> — Sahih al-Bukhari, no. 4210, Kitab al-Maghazi

Every companion hoped to be that man. The next morning, the Prophet asked: "Where is Ali ibn Abi Talib?" He was told that **Ali** was suffering from a severe eye ailment (*ramad*). The Prophet summoned him, applied his saliva to Ali's eyes — and Ali's eyes healed instantly as though they had never been afflicted. The Prophet then handed him the banner.^1

Ali advanced toward Fortress Qamus, the most impregnable stronghold in Khaybar, commanded by **Marhab**, Khaybar's most feared warrior. Marhab emerged to challenge Ali to single combat, reciting verses boasting of his prowess. Ali accepted the challenge, and in the duel, Ali defeated Marhab.^1 ^2

It is narrated that during the battle, Ali's shield shattered. He seized the fortress gate and used it as a shield. After the battle, seven or eight men tried to lift the gate but could not.^2 ^3 While the historicity of this detail is debated among scholars, it captures the heroic scale of the battle in the collective memory of the Muslim community.

Fortress Qamus fell, and the remaining fortresses surrendered one after another: al-Watih, al-Sulalim, and others. All of Khaybar was subdued within approximately ten days.^2

### The Poisoning Incident

After the conquest, a Jewish woman named **Zaynab bint al-Harith** (Marhab's wife) served the Prophet a roasted lamb that had been laced with poison. The Prophet took one bite, then spat it out and said: *"This bone tells me it is poisoned."* However, **Bishr ibn al-Bara'**, a companion who ate several bites, later died from the poison.^1

When asked why she did it, Zaynab replied: *"If you are truly a prophet, the poison will not harm you. And if you are merely a king, I would have freed my people from you."* According to some narrations, the Prophet pardoned her, though others report she was punished for Bishr's death.^1 ^2

## Analysis: Military and Economic Dimensions

### Military Strategy

The conquest of Khaybar demonstrated several sophisticated military principles:

**Enemy isolation.** The Prophet cut Ghatafan's supply route before attacking Khaybar. When Ghatafan prepared to send reinforcements, they received reports (possibly deliberate misinformation) that Madinah was under attack, so they turned back. By the time they arrived at Madinah and found no attack, Khaybar had already fallen.^2

**Siege warfare.** This was one of the first major siege operations in Islamic history. The Prophet showed tactical understanding of how to conquer fortified positions — avoiding suicidal frontal assaults, using encirclement to weaken defenses, and leveraging individual prowess (such as Ali's) to breach key points.

### Economic Dimension

The most significant aspect of Khaybar was the post-war settlement model. Rather than expelling Khaybar's inhabitants, Prophet Muhammad ﷺ offered an agreement: they could remain and work the land on the condition that they surrender half of the harvest to the Muslims. Khaybar's people accepted because they were irreplaceable agricultural experts.^1 ^2

This revenue-sharing system (*musaqah* and *muzara'ah*) became an important precedent in Islamic economic law. It demonstrated pragmatism: instead of destroying productive assets, Islam chose to integrate and share the yields fairly. This model endured until the caliphate of Umar ibn al-Khattab, who eventually relocated Khaybar's inhabitants to al-Sham.

### Strengthening of the Muslim Position

With the fall of Khaybar, the Muslims gained a major economic resource that strengthened their position. The spoils (*ghanimah*) from Khaybar were substantial, including weaponry, food supplies, and access to productive farmland.^2 This significantly transformed the economic condition of the Muslims — from a community often in want to a financially self-sustaining entity.

## Lessons from Khaybar

**Merit-based leadership.** The Prophet did not give the banner to Abu Bakr or Umar — the two most senior companions — but to Ali, who was even ill at the time. This decision was based on divine guidance and capability, not seniority. It teaches that responsibilities should be assigned to the most suitable person.

**Courage and faith.** Ali's story illustrates the connection between physical courage and spiritual strength. His miraculous healing and battlefield valor affirm that Allah's aid comes to those who place their trust in Him.

**Pragmatism in victory.** The revenue-sharing model with Khaybar's inhabitants demonstrates that Islam does not teach total destruction of a defeated party. Productivity and expertise are valued, even from a former adversary.

**Vigilance.** The poisoning incident is a reminder that military victory does not eliminate all threats. A leader must remain vigilant even after the battle is over.
