# Shaykh Abdul Qadir al-Jilani — Founder of the Qadiriyyah Order

## Historical Context

The 11th century CE marked a crucial period in the development of Islamic Sufism. Following the intellectual golden age led by figures such as al-Ghazali, the Islamic world experienced profound spiritual transformation. Amidst complex political and social dynamics, Sufi masters emerged who made significant contributions to the development of mystical orders.^1

Baghdad, as the center of Islamic civilization at that time, became a flourishing ground for various streams of religious thought. The community's need for spiritual guidance encouraged the emergence of scholars who mastered not only Islamic law but also possessed extraordinary spiritual depth.^2

**Abdul Qadir al-Jilani** (1077-1166 CE) was born in Gilan, a region south of the Caspian Sea now part of Iran. His full name was Abu Muhammad Muhyi al-Din Abdul Qadir ibn Abi Salih Musa ibn Abdullah al-Jili. He descended from a family with lineage tracing back to Imam Hasan ibn Ali ibn Abi Talib.^1

## Journey to Baghdad

At age 18, Abdul Qadir left his hometown in Gilan to pursue knowledge in Baghdad. This journey was not an easy decision, considering Baghdad was a highly competitive center of learning that required mature mental and spiritual preparation.^3

In Baghdad, Abdul Qadir studied under various renowned scholars. He learned Hanbali jurisprudence from Abu Sa'id al-Mukharrimi, hadith from Abu Ghalib ibn al-Baqillani, and Sufism from Abu al-Khair Hammad ibn Muslim al-Dabbas. The diversity of his teachers demonstrated his strong desire to master various disciplines of religious knowledge.^1

During his studies, Abdul Qadir was known as a highly diligent and modest student. He lived in a madrasah and led a very simple life, often fasting and performing night prayers. His discipline and seriousness in seeking knowledge made him well-known among Baghdad's scholars.^2

## Establishment of Madrasah and Da'wah

After completing his education, Abdul Qadir began teaching at the madrasah founded by his teacher, Abu Sa'id al-Mukharrimi. In 1127 CE, he was formally appointed as the head of this madrasah. Under his leadership, the institution flourished and became one of the most influential educational centers in Baghdad.^3

Abdul Qadir's teaching methods attracted significant attention. He not only taught Islamic law but also emphasized the importance of spiritual purification and noble character. This holistic approach made his madrasah sought after by thousands of students from across the Islamic world.^1

In one of his sermons, Abdul Qadir explained the importance of balance between knowledge and practice:

> يَا أَيَّتُهَا النَّفْسُ الْمُطْمَئِنَّةُ ﴿٢٧﴾ ارْجِعِي إِلَىٰ رَبِّكِ رَاضِيَةً مَّرْضِيَّةً ﴿٢٨﴾ فَادْخُلِي فِي عِبَادِي ﴿٢٩﴾ وَادْخُلِي جَنَّتِي ﴿٣٠﴾
>
> *"O peaceful soul, return to your Lord, well-pleased and pleasing [to Him]. And enter among My [righteous] servants, and enter My Paradise."* ﴾27-30﴿
> — QS. Al-Fajr (89): 27-30^4

This verse became the core of the Sufi teachings that Abdul Qadir taught — the attainment of the peaceful soul (*nafs al-mutma'innah*) as the highest goal of the spiritual journey.^4

## The Qadiriyyah Order and Its Teachings

The Qadiriyyah order founded by Abdul Qadir al-Jilani became one of the most influential Sufi orders in Islamic mystical history. This order emphasized balance between *shariah* and *haqiqah*, between worldly and spiritual life.^1

The core teachings of the Qadiriyyah include three main aspects: *thariqah* (spiritual path), *haqiqah* (spiritual reality), and *ma'rifah* (knowledge of Allah). Abdul Qadir taught that a *salik* (spiritual traveler) must first master the *shariah* before entering higher spiritual dimensions.^2

In his treatise "Al-Ghunya li Thalib Tariq al-Haqq," Abdul Qadir explained various *maqamat* (spiritual stations) that a Sufi must traverse. He emphasized the importance of *zuhud* (asceticism), *tawadhu'* (humility), and *mahabbah* (love for Allah) as fundamental pillars of the spiritual journey.^3

The litanies and remembrance practices taught in the Qadiriyyah reflect profound understanding of Islamic esotericism. One famous dhikr is "La hawla wa la quwwata illa billah," practiced in specific ways and particular quantities.^1

## Works and Intellectual Legacy

Abdul Qadir al-Jilani left invaluable intellectual heritage. His most famous work is "Al-Ghunya li Thalib Tariq al-Haqq" (Sufficiency for the Seeker of the Path of Truth), a comprehensive encyclopedia of Sufism.^2

In this work, he discussed various aspects of spiritual life, from social etiquette and worship practices to the highest stations in Sufism. His approach was highly systematic, combining evidence from the Qur'an, hadith, and spiritual experiences of previous Sufis.^3

Another important work is "Futuh al-Ghaib" (Revelations of the Unseen), a collection of his sermons containing spiritual counsel. In this work, Abdul Qadir demonstrated his profound understanding of the Qur'an and his ability to translate verses into spiritual life contexts.^1

## Wisdom and Spiritual Legacy

Abdul Qadir al-Jilani's influence on Islamic Sufism development was enormous and continues to this day. The Qadiriyyah order he founded spread to various parts of the world, from India to West Africa, from Turkey to Southeast Asia.^2

In the Archipelago, the Qadiriyyah entered through scholars who had studied in the Middle East. Its spread began in the 16th century and flourished in the 17th and 18th centuries. Figures like Shaykh Abdul Rauf as-Singkili in Aceh and Shaykh Yusuf al-Makassari in Sulawesi became principal disseminators of this order in Indonesia.^3

Abdul Qadir's methodology in combining academic and spiritual approaches provided inspiration for generations of scholars after him. He successfully demonstrated that profound spiritual life did not conflict with active involvement in social and educational life.^1

The death of Abdul Qadir al-Jilani on 21 Rabi'ul Akhir 561 AH/1166 CE in Baghdad marked the end of an era, yet his teachings continued to live and develop through his students scattered throughout the Islamic world.^2
