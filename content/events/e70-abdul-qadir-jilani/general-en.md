# Shaykh Abdul Qadir al-Jilani — Founder of the Qadiriyyah Order

## Historical Context and Background

The 11th and 12th centuries CE marked a pivotal period in the development of Islamic Sufism. Following the intellectual golden age led by figures such as al-Ghazali and al-Junayd al-Baghdadi, the Islamic world experienced profound spiritual transformation. Amidst complex political dynamics, including the ongoing Crusades and internal divisions within the Islamic world, emerged Sufi masters who made significant contributions to the development of mystical orders.^1

Baghdad, as the center of Islamic civilization at that time, became a flourishing ground for various streams of religious thought. The social-political pressures and the community's need for spiritual guidance encouraged the emergence of scholars who mastered not only Islamic law but also possessed extraordinary spiritual depth. In this dynamic intellectual atmosphere, Sufism developed as an important dimension of Muslim religious life.^2

**Abu Muhammad Muhyi al-Din Abdul Qadir ibn Abi Salih Musa ibn Abdullah al-Jilani** was born in the year 470 AH/1077 CE in Jilan (Gilan), a region south of the Caspian Sea that is now part of Iran. Jilan at that time was known for its fertility and was the birthplace of many great scholars. Abdul Qadir came from a noble family with lineage tracing back to **Imam Hasan ibn Ali ibn Abi Talib**, the grandson of the Prophet ﷺ from his mother's side, Fatimah bint Abdullah al-Sawma'i.^3

Abdul Qadir's father, Abu Salih Musa, was a righteous and respected man in his community. His mother, Umm al-Khayr Fatimah, was known as an extremely pious woman who possessed *karamah* (spiritual gifts). This religious family environment provided a strong spiritual foundation for Abdul Qadir from childhood. He grew up in an atmosphere filled with Islamic teachings and profound spiritual practices.

## Early Education and Spiritual Calling

Abdul Qadir's early education began in his hometown of Jilan. He demonstrated extraordinary intelligence in memorizing the Qur'an and studying basic religious sciences. His local teachers recognized his great potential and suggested that he continue his education in larger centers of learning.^1

At the age of 18, Abdul Qadir made an important decision to leave his hometown in Jilan to seek knowledge in Baghdad. This decision was not easy, considering Baghdad was a highly competitive center of learning that required mature mental and spiritual preparation. Before departing, his mother gave him advice and prayers that would become his life's guidance: "My son, never speak falsehood, even under the most threatening circumstances."^2

The journey to Baghdad was not easy. Abdul Qadir had to travel a long distance under uncomfortable conditions. However, his strong determination to seek knowledge enabled him to endure all challenges. Upon arriving in Baghdad, he immediately sought simple lodging and began looking for teachers who could guide him in various disciplines of knowledge.^3

## Intellectual Journey in Baghdad

In Baghdad, Abdul Qadir studied under various renowned scholars from different academic disciplines. In jurisprudence, he learned the Hanbali school from **Abu Sa'id al-Mukharrimi** (d. 513 AH), a prominent jurist known for his deep understanding of Islamic legal methodology. This choice of the Hanbali school reflected Abdul Qadir's inclination toward a more conservative approach closely aligned with the primary sources (Qur'an and hadith).

In hadith studies, he learned from **Abu Ghalib ibn al-Baqillani**, a renowned traditionist with reliable chains of transmission. From this teacher, Abdul Qadir learned not only hadith collections but also the methodology of hadith criticism and ways to understand the meanings contained in the Prophet's sayings ﷺ. This profound mastery of hadith later became one of his primary strengths in teaching.^1

Most importantly in Abdul Qadir's intellectual journey was his study of Sufism under **Abu al-Khair Hammad ibn Muslim al-Dabbas** (d. 525 AH). Al-Dabbas was one of the prominent Sufis of his time with a reliable spiritual lineage tracing back to Imam al-Junayd al-Baghdadi. From this teacher, Abdul Qadir learned the foundations of the mystical path, *maqamat* (spiritual stations), and matters related to *suluk* (spiritual journey).^2

The diversity of his teachers demonstrated Abdul Qadir's strong desire to master various disciplines of religious knowledge comprehensively. He did not want to become merely a jurist or hadith scholar, but sought to integrate all these sciences into a holistic understanding of Islam.^3

## Period of Seclusion and Character Formation

During his studies, Abdul Qadir was known as a highly diligent and modest student. He lived in a madrasah and led a very simple life, often fasting and performing night prayers (*qiyam al-lail*). His discipline and seriousness in seeking knowledge made him well-known and respected among Baghdad's scholars.

After completing his formal education, Abdul Qadir underwent a period of *uzlah* (seclusion) to deepen his spiritual experience. For 25 years, from ages 25 to 50, he lived an extremely simple life focused on worship and contemplation. This period was crucial in forming his character as a true Sufi.^1

During his seclusion, Abdul Qadir often performed *khalwat* (spiritual isolation) in various places, including in the deserts around Baghdad. He underwent strict spiritual training, including prolonged fasting, night prayers, and intensive remembrance of Allah. The profound spiritual experiences during this period became the foundation for his later teachings.^2

## Beginning His Teaching and Da'wah Career

After completing his period of seclusion, in 1127 CE, Abdul Qadir began to emerge publicly as a scholar and spiritual teacher. He began teaching at the madrasah previously led by his teacher, Abu Sa'id al-Mukharrimi. When his teacher passed away, Abdul Qadir was formally appointed as the head of that madrasah.^3

Under Abdul Qadir's leadership, this madrasah flourished rapidly and became one of the most influential educational centers in Baghdad. What distinguished this madrasah from other educational institutions was the holistic approach applied by Abdul Qadir. He not only taught Islamic law in the narrow sense but also emphasized the importance of spiritual purification, noble character, and the spiritual dimension of Islamic teachings.

Abdul Qadir's teaching methods were highly attractive and innovative. He used an approach that combined deep scientific explanations with spiritual advice that touched the heart. His sermons contained not only explanations of jurisprudential rulings but also profound reflections on the meaning of life, the purpose of human creation, and ways to achieve closeness to Allah.^1

## Sufi Teachings and the Qadiriyyah Order

The Qadiriyyah order founded by Abdul Qadir al-Jilani became one of the most influential orders in the history of Islamic Sufism. What distinguished this order from other orders was its emphasis on balance between *shariah* and *haqiqah*, between worldly and spiritual life. Abdul Qadir taught that true Sufism is Sufism that does not abandon Islamic law.^2

The core teachings of the Qadiriyyah include three main interconnected aspects: **thariqah** (spiritual path), **haqiqah** (spiritual reality), and **ma'rifah** (direct knowledge of Allah). Abdul Qadir taught that a *salik* (spiritual traveler) must first master *shariah* well before entering higher spiritual dimensions. Without a strong foundation in Islamic law, the spiritual journey would become misleading.^3

In his teachings, Abdul Qadir emphasized the importance of **fana fi'llah** (annihilation in Allah) as the highest goal of the spiritual journey, but he also taught about **baqa billah** (subsistence with Allah), where a Sufi returns to the world with new spiritual consciousness to serve the community. This concept shows that Sufism is not an escape from the world, but a way to live in the world with high spiritual awareness.

The litanies and remembrance practices taught in the Qadiriyyah order reflect profound understanding of the esoteric dimension of Islam. One famous *dhikr* is **"La hawla wa la quwwata illa billah"** which is practiced in specific ways and particular quantities. This *dhikr* is not only uttered with the tongue but also absorbed by the heart until it reaches the level of *dhikr qalbi* (remembrance of the heart) and *dhikr sirri* (secret remembrance).^1

## Intellectual and Spiritual Works

Abdul Qadir al-Jilani left an invaluable and widely influential intellectual legacy. His most famous and comprehensive work is **"Al-Ghunya li Thalib Tariq al-Haqq"** (Sufficiency for the Seeker of the Path of Truth), a Sufi encyclopedia covering almost all aspects of Muslim spiritual life. This work consists of two major parts: the first part discusses the *usul* (foundations) of religion and the second part discusses *furu'* (branches) and proper etiquette.^2

In **"Al-Ghunya,"** Abdul Qadir systematically discusses various aspects of spiritual life, from daily social etiquette, proper worship practices, to the highest stations in Sufism. The approach used is highly academic yet practical, combining evidence from the Qur'an and hadith with the spiritual experiences of previous Sufis and personal insights gained through profound spiritual experience.^3

Another important work is **"Futuh al-Ghaib"** (Revelations of the Unseen), which is a collection of 78 sermons delivered by Abdul Qadir at his madrasah. In this work, Abdul Qadir demonstrates his profound understanding of the Qur'an and his ability to translate verses into spiritual and practical life contexts. Each sermon in this book is a profound reflection on various aspects of Muslim life.

The third equally important work is **"Al-Fath al-Rabbani wa al-Fayd al-Rahmani"** (The Divine Opening and the Merciful Outpouring), containing 62 *majlis* (teaching sessions) delivered on Sundays after the Asr prayer. This work has different characteristics from the two previous works, as it focuses more on practical aspects of spiritual life and advice for daily living.^1

## Teaching and Da'wah Methodology

Abdul Qadir's teaching methodology was unique and effective. He used an approach he called **"al-jam' bayna al-shari'ah wa al-haqiqah"** (combining Islamic law and spiritual reality). In every teaching, he always began with evidence from the Qur'an and hadith, then explained the esoteric and spiritual dimensions of those teachings.^2

Abdul Qadir was also known for his ability to speak to various audiences. He could deliver profound teachings to scholars and intellectuals, but could also convey simple advice understandable to ordinary people. This flexibility in communication methods made him very popular and respected by various levels of society.^3

In one of his famous sermons, Abdul Qadir explained about the peaceful soul by quoting a Quranic verse:

> يَا أَيَّتُهَا النَّفْسُ الْمُطْمَئِنَّةُ ﴿٢٧﴾ ارْجِعِي إِلَىٰ رَبِّكِ رَاضِيَةً مَّرْضِيَّةً ﴿٢٨﴾ فَادْخُلِي فِي عِبَادِي ﴿٢٩﴾ وَادْخُلِي جَنَّتِي ﴿٣٠﴾
>
> *"O peaceful soul! ﴾27﴿ Return to your Lord, well-pleased and pleasing [to Him]. ﴾28﴿ And enter among My [righteous] servants, ﴾29﴿ and enter My Paradise. ﴾30﴿"*
> — QS. Al-Fajr (89): 27-30

This verse became the core of the Sufi teachings that Abdul Qadir taught — the attainment of **nafs al-mutma'innah** (the peaceful soul) as the highest goal of the spiritual journey. According to Abdul Qadir, the peaceful soul is one that has achieved perfect peace with Allah, with oneself, and with the universe.^1

## Spread of the Qadiriyyah Order Worldwide

The influence of Abdul Qadir al-Jilani was not limited to his time alone but continued to develop and spread throughout the Islamic world. Abdul Qadir's students, who came from various countries, returned to their respective homelands and spread the teachings of the Qadiriyyah Order. In a relatively short time, this order became one of the most widespread orders in Islamic history.^1

In **the Maghreb and North Africa**, the Qadiriyyah Order was spread by students who came from those regions to study in Baghdad. This order flourished in Morocco, Algeria, Tunisia, and Libya. In these regions, the Qadiriyyah Order adapted to local cultures while maintaining the core teachings taught by Abdul Qadir.^2

In **West Africa and Sudan**, the Qadiriyyah Order became one of the main forces in spreading Islam. Muslim scholars and traders affiliated with this order played important roles in Islamic da'wah in regions not yet fully Islamized. Cities like Timbuktu and Gao became important centers of the Qadiriyyah Order in West Africa.^3

In **the Indian Subcontinent**, the Qadiriyyah Order entered through Sufis who came from Central Asia and Persia. In India, this order developed with unique characteristics, integrating Sufi teachings with local spiritual traditions. Figures like Khwaja Mu'in al-Din Chishti, though founding the Chishti Order, also had spiritual connections with Abdul Qadir al-Jilani.

## The Entry of the Qadiriyyah Order to the Archipelago

In **the Archipelago**, the Qadiriyyah Order entered through scholars who had studied in the Middle East, particularly in Mecca and Medina during their pilgrimage. Its spread began in the 16th century and flourished in the 17th and 18th centuries. This process was inseparable from the network of Archipelago scholars who had intellectual and spiritual connections with Islamic learning centers in the Middle East.^1

**Shaykh Abdul Rauf as-Singkili** (1615-1693 CE) in Aceh was one of the main disseminators of the Qadiriyyah Order in the Archipelago. He studied directly in the Middle East and received *ijazah* (spiritual license) from scholars with lineage tracing back to Abdul Qadir al-Jilani. After returning to Aceh, he taught this order and wrote works in Malay that facilitated the spread of Sufi teachings among local communities.^2

**Shaykh Yusuf al-Makassari** (1626-1699 CE) in Sulawesi also played an important role in spreading the Qadiriyyah Order. He not only spread this order in Sulawesi but also brought it to various other regions in the Archipelago. Many of his works in Sufism referenced the teachings of Abdul Qadir al-Jilani.^3

In **Java**, the Qadiriyyah Order was developed by various scholars and later underwent interesting development through synthesis with other orders, such as in the Qadiriyyah wa Naqshabandiyyah Order founded by Shaykh Ahmad Khatib Sambas in the 19th century. This synthesis demonstrated the flexibility of Abdul Qadir's teachings that allowed adaptation to local conditions and needs.

## Moral Teachings and Spiritual Ethics

One of the most important aspects of Abdul Qadir al-Jilani's teachings was his emphasis on morals and spiritual ethics. In his view, true Sufism must be reflected in daily behavior of noble character. He often said that the sign of a true Sufi is not in the *karamah* (supernatural abilities) possessed, but in noble character and obedience to Islamic law.^1

Abdul Qadir taught the concept of **"al-akhlaq al-ilahiyyah"** (divine character), which is the effort to adorn oneself with Allah's attributes that can be possessed by humans, such as ar-Rahman (Most Compassionate), ar-Rahim (Most Merciful), as-Sabur (Most Patient), and al-Halim (Most Forbearing). According to Abdul Qadir, the more someone adorns themselves with Allah's attributes, the closer they become to Allah.^2

In relationships with fellow humans, Abdul Qadir taught the principle of **"khidmat al-khalq"** (serving Allah's creatures). He emphasized that a Sufi must not isolate themselves and abandon their social responsibilities. Instead, a Sufi must be active in serving society, helping those in need, and contributing positively to the common good.^3

## Influence on the Development of Sufism

Abdul Qadir al-Jilani's influence on the development of Islamic Sufism was enormous and multidimensional. He successfully built a harmonious synthesis between the exoteric (zahir) and esoteric (batin) dimensions of Islamic teachings. This approach became a model for many Sufis after him and contributed to the development of what later became known as moderate Sufism or Sunni Sufism.

Abdul Qadir's methodology in combining academic and spiritual approaches provided inspiration for generations of scholars after him. He successfully demonstrated that profound spiritual life did not conflict with active involvement in social life, education, and da'wah. The scholar-Sufi model developed by Abdul Qadir later became an ideal for many scholars throughout the Islamic world.^1

In the field of spiritual literature, Abdul Qadir's works became important references for Sufi writers after him. His writing style that combined spiritual depth with intellectual clarity influenced the tradition of writing Sufi books until modern times.^2

## Legacy and Contemporary Relevance

The death of Abdul Qadir al-Jilani on 21 Rabi'ul Akhir 561 AH/1166 CE in Baghdad marked the end of an era, yet his teachings continued to live and develop through his students scattered throughout the Islamic world. His tomb in Baghdad became an important pilgrimage site for Muslims from around the world.^3

In the modern context, Abdul Qadir's teachings about balance between spiritual and worldly life are highly relevant. Amidst the challenges of modernization and secularization, Abdul Qadir's approach that integrates spirituality with social engagement offers a model of religious life that is not alienated from contemporary reality.

The principles of spiritual ethics taught by Abdul Qadir, such as honesty, justice, compassion, and service to others, remain relevant as foundations for building a better society. In the context of globalization and pluralism, Abdul Qadir's teachings on tolerance and interfaith dialogue also make positive contributions.^1

The Qadiriyyah Order to this day remains active in various parts of the world, with millions of followers spread from West Africa to Southeast Asia. The continuity of this order for more than 800 years demonstrates the vitality and relevance of Abdul Qadir al-Jilani's teachings that continue to provide spiritual meaning for Muslims across different eras and places.^2 ^3
