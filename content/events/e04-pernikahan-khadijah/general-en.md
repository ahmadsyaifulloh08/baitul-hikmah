# The Marriage to Khadijah bint Khuwaylid (595 CE)

## Context

In the year 595 CE, **Prophet Muhammad SAW**, then approximately twenty-five years old, married **Khadijah bint Khuwaylid**, a noblewoman of the Quraysh aged about forty, renowned as the most successful businesswoman in Mecca. This marriage was not merely a personal event — it was one of the most decisive moments in Islamic history, for from this union emerged the foundation that would sustain the prophetic mission for over two decades.^1

To understand the context of this marriage, the backgrounds of both parties must first be understood.

Muhammad ibn Abdullah grew up as an orphan — his father, Abdullah, died before he was born, and his mother, Aminah, passed away when he was only six years old. He was raised successively by his grandfather Abd al-Muttalib and then by his uncle Abu Talib. Although he came from the respected Banu Hashim family, Muhammad possessed no significant wealth. He worked as a shepherd in his youth, later becoming involved in trade.^3

Khadijah bint Khuwaylid, on the other hand, was one of the most prominent women in Mecca. She belonged to the Banu Asad clan — a distinguished family among the Quraysh. Before marrying Muhammad, Khadijah had been widowed twice. But what defined her most was her success in trade. She managed a large-scale commercial enterprise that dispatched caravans to Syria and Yemen, and was known in Mecca by the titles *al-Tahirah* (the Pure Woman) and *Sayyidatu Nisa' Quraysh* (Lady of the Women of Quraysh).^2

## The Events

### Muhammad Works for Khadijah

The story of this marriage began as a professional relationship. Khadijah, who was constantly seeking trustworthy people to manage her trade affairs, heard about Muhammad's reputation as a young man known for his honesty and trustworthiness — the title *al-Amin* was already attached to him among the people of Mecca.^3

Khadijah then offered Muhammad the opportunity to lead her trade caravan to Syria. Ibn Hisham narrates in the chapter on Muhammad's youth that Khadijah told Muhammad she was drawn by his honesty, the nobility of his character, and the truthfulness of his speech, which were well known among the people.^1

Muhammad accepted the offer and departed for Syria accompanied by Maysarah, Khadijah's trusted servant. This trading venture proved extraordinarily successful — yielding profit reportedly twice what was normally expected.^3

### Maysarah's Report

Upon returning from Syria, Maysarah reported to Khadijah not only the commercial success but also Muhammad's extraordinary character during the journey. Maysarah described how Muhammad traded with impeccable honesty, how noble his conduct was in dealing with people, and various remarkable things he had witnessed along the way.^1

Maysarah's report reinforced what Khadijah already knew about Muhammad. She realized that Muhammad was not merely a competent trader — he was a human being of exceptionally rare quality.^2

### The Proposal and Marriage

According to Ibn Sa'd's narration in *Al-Tabaqat al-Kubra*, in the chapter on the Prophet's wives, it was Khadijah who took the initiative to express her desire to marry Muhammad. Khadijah conveyed her intention through her friend, Nafisah bint Munabbih, who approached Muhammad and asked why he had not yet married. When Nafisah mentioned Khadijah's name, Muhammad expressed his interest.^2

Muhammad then conveyed his intentions to his uncles. Abu Talib, along with several members of Banu Hashim, came to formally propose to Khadijah at her family's home. Ibn Hisham narrates the sermon delivered by Abu Talib on this occasion, in which he praised his nephew as a young man unmatched among the Quraysh in terms of nobility, character, and virtue.^1

The marriage was solemnized with a dowry of twenty young she-camels (*bakrah*), as narrated by most historians. The wedding was witnessed by the leaders of Banu Hashim and Banu Asad.^1

### Married Life

The marriage of Muhammad and Khadijah stands as one of the most harmonious domestic stories ever recorded in history. Throughout their twenty-five years of marriage — until Khadijah's death in 619 CE — Muhammad did not marry another woman. This is a highly significant fact given the deeply ingrained tradition of polygamy in Arab society at the time.^3

From this marriage, six children were born: al-Qasim, Zaynab, Ruqayyah, Umm Kulthum, Fatimah, and Abdullah (also known as al-Tayyib or al-Tahir). Their sons — al-Qasim and Abdullah — died in infancy, while all four daughters lived to adulthood.^2

Khadijah was far more than a wife in the conventional sense. She was a life partner in the fullest meaning of the word — advisor, supporter, and benefactor. Her wealth was devoted entirely to the family and later to the Islamic mission. Her status as a successful entrepreneur meant that Muhammad had the economic freedom to spend time in *tafakkur* (contemplation) at the Cave of Hira, which ultimately led to the descent of the first revelation.^3

## Dialogue and Sacred Texts

The marriage of Muhammad and Khadijah carried a profoundly spiritual dimension. Throughout his life, Prophet Muhammad SAW always spoke of Khadijah with love and reverence. In an authentic hadith narrated by Aisha:

> مَا غِرْتُ عَلَى أَحَدٍ مِنْ نِسَاءِ النَّبِيِّ صلى الله عليه وسلم مَا غِرْتُ عَلَى خَدِيجَةَ وَمَا رَأَيْتُهَا وَلَكِنْ كَانَ النَّبِيُّ صلى الله عليه وسلم يُكْثِرُ ذِكْرَهَا
>
> *"I was never jealous of any of the Prophet's wives as I was jealous of Khadijah, even though I never saw her. But the Prophet SAW would frequently mention her."*
> — Narrated by al-Bukhari, no. 3818, Kitab Fada'il al-Sahabah

More remarkably still, Allah the Almighty Himself sent greetings to Khadijah through the Angel Jibril:

> أَنَّ جِبْرِيلَ قَالَ لِلنَّبِيِّ صلى الله عليه وسلم: هَذِهِ خَدِيجَةُ قَدْ أَتَتْ مَعَهَا إِنَاءٌ فِيهِ إِدَامٌ أَوْ طَعَامٌ أَوْ شَرَابٌ، فَإِذَا هِيَ أَتَتْكَ فَاقْرَأْ عَلَيْهَا السَّلَامَ مِنْ رَبِّهَا وَبَشِّرْهَا بِبَيْتٍ فِي الْجَنَّةِ مِنْ قَصَبٍ لَا صَخَبَ فِيهِ وَلَا نَصَبَ
>
> *"Jibril said to the Prophet SAW: 'Here is Khadijah coming with a vessel containing condiment, food, or drink. When she comes to you, convey to her greetings of peace from her Lord, and give her glad tidings of a house in Paradise made of pearl, in which there is no noise nor fatigue.'"*
> — Narrated by al-Bukhari, no. 3820, Kitab Fada'il al-Sahabah

The Prophet also said regarding Khadijah's rank:

"The best women among the inhabitants of Paradise are Khadijah bint Khuwaylid, Fatimah bint Muhammad, Maryam bint Imran, and Asiyah bint Muzahim the wife of Pharaoh."^4

These statements demonstrate that the relationship between Muhammad and Khadijah was not merely a worldly harmonious marriage, but a bond acknowledged and blessed by Allah the Almighty Himself.

## Analysis and Lessons

### A Woman as Initiator

One of the most compelling aspects of this story is that Khadijah took the initiative in the marriage process. In the midst of sixth-century patriarchal Arab society, Khadijah — a forty-year-old widow — proposed to a twenty-five-year-old young man. This fact challenges the stereotype that women in Islamic tradition were always passive in matters of marriage.^2

Khadijah recognized Muhammad's qualities not for his wealth or power — for Muhammad possessed neither — but for his honesty, the nobility of his character, and his integrity. Khadijah's choice reflects a profound wisdom that places character above material wealth.

### Equality Within Difference

The fifteen-year age difference between Khadijah and Muhammad, Khadijah's status as a wealthy widow and Muhammad's as a modest orphaned young man — none of these became obstacles to a marriage that would become one of the happiest in all of history. This marriage demonstrates that compatibility of soul and character is far more important than equality of social or economic status.^3

### The Foundation of the Mission

Without Khadijah, the early landscape of Islamic preaching would have been profoundly different. Her economic support freed Muhammad from the pressures of earning a livelihood, allowing him to contemplate at the Cave of Hira. Her emotional support became his anchor when the first revelation descended and Muhammad felt afraid. Her faith as the first person to embrace Islam (*al-sabiqat al-awwalun*) gave legitimacy and moral strength to the newborn message.^3

### A Model Household

Twenty-five years of monogamy in a society that viewed polygamy as the norm was a powerful statement about Muhammad's character and the depth of his love for Khadijah. This marriage stands as a model that fidelity, mutual respect, and genuine love are the foundations of a strong household.^1
