# The Hijrah to Madinah — The Beginning of the Islamic Calendar (622 CE)

## Historical Context

### An Increasingly Hostile Makkah

After 12 years of preaching in Makkah (610-622 CE), Prophet Muhammad ﷺ and his followers faced intensifying persecution. The deaths of his two greatest protectors — his wife Khadijah and uncle Abu Talib — in 619 CE ('Am al-Huzn / Year of Sorrow) stripped away the clan protection that had shielded him. The three-year economic and social boycott (616-619 CE) in the Valley of Abu Talib had weakened the Muslim community physically and economically.

The Quraysh even plotted to assassinate the Prophet through a conspiracy involving one representative from each tribe — so that his blood would be distributed across all clans, preventing the Banu Hashim from seeking retribution against any single tribe. This plot is recorded in the Qur'an:

> *"And [remember] when those who disbelieved plotted against you to restrain you or kill you or evict you. But they plan, and Allah plans. And Allah is the best of planners."*
> — Qur'an, Al-Anfal [8]: 30

### Yathrib: A City Ready

Meanwhile, the city of Yathrib (later known as Madinah al-Munawwarah) had shown openness to Islam. The First Pledge of Aqabah (621 CE) and Second Pledge of Aqabah (622 CE) demonstrated that the people of Yathrib — particularly the Aws and Khazraj tribes — were willing to accept, protect, and provide sanctuary for the Prophet ﷺ and the Muslims.

The two major tribes of Yathrib had long been trapped in civil war (the Battle of Bu'ath). They needed a neutral, respected leader — and Prophet Muhammad ﷺ was the answer.

## The Journey of Hijrah

### Preparation and Departure

After receiving divine permission to emigrate, the Prophet ﷺ prepared with meticulous care. He asked Ali ibn Abi Talib to sleep in his bed that night — wearing his green cloak — as a decoy for the Quraysh who had surrounded his house. Ali, then approximately 23 years old, accepted this dangerous mission without hesitation.

The Prophet ﷺ left his house at night, passing through the besiegers while reciting the opening verses of Surah Yasin, and Allah obscured their vision so none could see him.

He then met Abu Bakr al-Siddiq, and the two departed southward — the opposite direction from Madinah — to mislead pursuers. They hid in **Cave Thawr** (غار ثور) for three nights.

### Cave Thawr: A Moment of Trust

While hiding in Cave Thawr, the Quraysh sent their best trackers to find the Prophet. The pursuers reached the cave's entrance, but Allah sent miracles: a spider spun its web across the opening, and a pair of doves nested there — convincing the pursuers that no one was inside.

Abu Bakr, deeply anxious, whispered: *"O Messenger of Allah, if one of them looks down at his feet, he will surely see us."* The Prophet ﷺ replied with extraordinary calm:

> *"What do you think of two when Allah is their third?"*
> — Sahih al-Bukhari no. 3653, Sahih Muslim no. 2381

This moment is immortalized in the Qur'an:

> *"...when they were in the cave and he said to his companion, 'Do not grieve; indeed Allah is with us.'"*
> — Qur'an, Al-Tawbah [9]: 40

### The Journey to Madinah

After three nights, the Prophet ﷺ and Abu Bakr continued their journey guided by Abdullah ibn Urayqith, a polytheist trusted as their guide. They took an unusual coastal route to avoid pursuit, a journey of approximately 8 days.

Suraqah ibn Malik, an expert horseman lured by the 100-camel bounty, tracked them down and nearly caught them. However, his horse's legs kept sinking miraculously into the sand. Suraqah ultimately requested a guarantee of safety and concealed their tracks. In a prophetic moment, the Prophet ﷺ even promised Suraqah the bracelets of Kisra (the Persian Emperor) — a promise fulfilled during the caliphate of Umar ibn al-Khattab.

### Arrival at Quba and Madinah

The Prophet ﷺ arrived at Quba (on Madinah's outskirts) on Monday, 12 Rabi al-Awwal of the first year of Hijrah (~24 September 622 CE). There, he established **Masjid Quba** — the first mosque founded upon piety (Qur'an 9:108).

He then entered Madinah, welcomed by its inhabitants singing:

> *"Tala'a al-badru 'alayna, min thaniyyat al-wada'..."*
> *"The full moon has risen upon us, from the farewell hills..."*

To determine his residence, the Prophet ﷺ released his camel, declaring he would settle wherever it stopped. The camel halted on land belonging to two orphans — which became the site of **Masjid al-Nabawi**.

## Analysis of Significance

### 1. The Establishment of the First Islamic State

The Hijrah was not merely a physical relocation — it was the founding of Islam's first political entity. In Madinah, the Prophet ﷺ established:
- **The Charter of Madinah** — a written constitution governing relations between Muslims, Jews, and other groups
- **The Muhajirin-Ansar Brotherhood** — new social bonds transcending tribal affiliations
- **An economic system** — fair markets, prohibition of usury, zakat as a redistribution instrument

### 2. The Beginning of the Hijri Calendar

Caliph Umar ibn al-Khattab later designated the Hijrah as the starting point of the Islamic calendar. This was no arbitrary decision — it reflected the understanding that the Hijrah, not the Prophet's birth or the first revelation, was the most transformative moment: when Islam transitioned from a phase of preaching to a phase of civilization-building.

### 3. A Model of Crisis Management and Leadership

The Hijrah demonstrates the Prophet's extraordinary strategic intelligence:
- **Detailed planning** — appointing Ali as a decoy, choosing nighttime, alternative routes
- **Task delegation** — Abdullah ibn Urayqith (guide), Asma bint Abu Bakr (logistics), Amir ibn Fuhayrah (erasing tracks)
- **Tawakkul** — after all human effort, complete reliance on Allah

### 4. The Paradigm of "Hijrah" in Islam

The concept of hijrah evolved into a broader principle: migration from sin toward obedience, from ignorance toward knowledge, from laziness toward productivity. The Prophet ﷺ said:

> *"The true muhajir (emigrant) is one who abandons what Allah has forbidden."*
> — Sahih al-Bukhari no. 10, Sahih Muslim no. 40

## Civilizational Impact

The Hijrah transformed Islam from a spiritual movement into a civilization. Within less than a decade:
- Madinah became an organized city-state
- The Charter of Madinah became a model for pluralism and constitutionalism
- The Islamic economy began forming on principles of justice
- Masjid al-Nabawi served simultaneously as a center of governance, education, and justice
