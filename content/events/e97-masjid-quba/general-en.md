# Quba Mosque — The First Mosque in Islam

## Historical Context

In 622 CE, when **Prophet Muhammad** ﷺ performed the *hijrah* from Makkah to Yathrib (later renamed Madinah), he did not proceed directly to the city center. The Prophet ﷺ and **Abu Bakr As-Siddiq** first stopped at a suburb called Quba^1. This arrival was not coincidental, but part of a carefully planned *da'wah* strategy following the *Bai'atul Aqabah* that involved delegations from the Khazraj and Aws tribes^2.

Quba was located approximately 3 kilometers south of the center of Yathrib. This area was inhabited by several families from the Aws tribe, including the Banu Amr ibn Auf clan who had already embraced Islam before the *hijrah*^1. **Kultsum ibn al-Hidam**, a leader from Banu Amr ibn Auf, became the host who welcomed the Prophet's ﷺ arrival with great honor.

During four days in Quba (12-16 Rabi'ul Awwal 1 AH), the Prophet ﷺ did not merely rest from the long journey. He utilized this time to lay the foundation of the first Muslim community outside Makkah by building a permanent place of worship — a mosque^3.

## The Construction Event

### Site Selection and Preparation

**The Prophet** ﷺ chose a plot of land belonging to **Sahl and Suhail**, two orphans from Banu Amr ibn Auf^1. Although the landowners wanted to donate the land, the Prophet ﷺ insisted on purchasing it at a fair price, demonstrating the principle of justice in transactions^2.

This land, measuring approximately 70 x 60 cubits (roughly 35 x 30 meters), was selected for its strategic location: close enough to residential areas for easy access, yet spacious enough to accommodate large congregations^3. The location also had an orientation that allowed the *qiblah* to face Baitul Maqdis (Jerusalem), which at that time was still the direction of prayer for Muslims.

### Construction Process

The construction of Quba Mosque involved direct participation from the Muhajirun companions and the Quba residents from the Ansar^1. **The Prophet** ﷺ himself participated in the construction process, carrying stones and building materials. **Abu Bakr**, **Umar ibn al-Khattab** (who arrived later), and other companions worked together in mutual cooperation.

The mosque's construction used local materials: stones from the surrounding area as foundation, date palm trunks as supporting pillars, and palm fronds as roofing^2. The walls were built with sun-dried clay bricks. The floor was left as compacted earth, in accordance with the simplicity exemplified by the Prophet ﷺ.

### Architectural Features and Functions

Quba Mosque was built with a simple yet functional concept. The main building was rectangular with one open space for congregational prayer^3. At the front was a portico (*suffah*) where companions gathered to listen to the Prophet's ﷺ teachings.

Remarkably, this mosque was equipped with sanitary facilities — an innovation in worship architecture at that time^2. The Prophet ﷺ ordered the construction of ablution areas and bathrooms separate from the prayer space, emphasizing the importance of cleanliness in worship.

The building's orientation faced Baitul Maqdis, but after the revelation of verses about the change of qiblah in the second year of Hijriyyah, this orientation was adjusted to face the Kabah^1.

## Quranic Dialogue and Evidence

The special status of Quba Mosque is immortalized in the Quran, Surah At-Taubah:^4

> لَّمَسْجِدٌ أُسِّسَ عَلَى التَّقْوَى مِنْ أَوَّلِ يَوْمٍ أَحَقُّ أَن تَقُومَ فِيهِ ۚ فِيهِ رِجَالٌ يُحِبُّونَ أَن يَتَطَهَّرُوا ۚ وَاللَّهُ يُحِبُّ الْمُطَّهِّرِينَ ﴿١٠٨﴾
>
> *"A mosque whose foundation was laid from the first day on piety (Quba Mosque) is more worthy of your standing for prayer therein. In it are men who love to purify themselves; and Allah loves those who purify themselves."*
> — QS. At-Taubah (9): 108

This verse was revealed as confirmation that Quba Mosque holds a special position because it was built on the foundation of *taqwa* from the very first day^3. Scholars interpret "taqwa from the first day" as sincere intention to worship Allah, without any embellishment of grandeur or showing off^1^4.

**Ibn Abbas** narrated that the Prophet ﷺ said:

> مَنْ تَطَهَّرَ فِي بَيْتِهِ ثُمَّ أَتَى مَسْجِدَ قُبَاء فَصَلَّى فِيهِ صَلاةً كَانَ لَهُ كَأَجْرِ عُمْرَةٍ
>
> *"Whoever purifies himself in his house, then comes to Quba Mosque and prays therein, will have the reward equivalent to an umrah."*
> — HR. Ahmad, no. 3221, Musnad Ahmad

This hadith demonstrates that the virtue of Quba Mosque lies not only in its historical aspect as the first mosque, but also in its continuing spiritual value^2.

## Analysis and Wisdom

### Significance in Islamic Architectural Development

Quba Mosque became the prototype for subsequent mosques in Islamic civilization^1. The simplicity of its design conveyed the message that places of worship need not be luxurious to bring one closer to Allah. The principle of functionality over grandeur — a value later adopted in mosque architectural traditions across the world.

The presence of ablution and sanitary facilities within the mosque complex became a standard that continues to this day^2. This innovation shows that Islam not only regulates spiritual aspects, but also considers health and cleanliness as integral parts of worship.

### Social and Community Dimensions

The construction of Quba Mosque involved cooperation between Muhajirun and Ansar, symbolizing the unity of the *ummah* that transcends tribal and regional boundaries^3. **The Prophet** ﷺ who worked directly showed that leadership in Islam is participatory, not feudal.

During four days in Quba, this mosque became the center of religious and social activities. Here the Prophet ﷺ led the first congregational prayer on Madinah soil, delivered sermons to local residents, and received delegations from various tribes who wanted to declare their Islam^1.

### Institutional Legacy

Quba Mosque laid the foundation for the concept of mosque as a multi-functional institution in Islamic civilization^2. Besides being a place of prayer, mosques also function as educational centers, places of consultation, and socio-economic bases for Muslim communities. This model was later adopted in the construction of the Prophet's Mosque and mosques in cities conquered by Muslims.

The tradition of visiting (*ziyarah*) Quba Mosque that the Prophet ﷺ performed regularly every Saturday also gave birth to the concept of mosque as a spiritual destination^3. This developed into the tradition of *ziyarah* to various historical mosques in the Islamic world.

The special reward for prayer at Quba Mosque, equivalent to umrah, shows that the spiritual value of a place is not measured by its physical grandeur, but by the level of piety and sincerity that underlies the construction and activities within it^1.
