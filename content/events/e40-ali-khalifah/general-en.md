# Ali ibn Abi Talib — The Fourth Caliph of Islam (656-661 CE)

## Context: Crisis After the Assassination of Uthman

The assassination of Caliph Uthman ibn Affan on 18 Dhul Hijjah 35 AH (June 656 CE) plunged the Muslim world into its worst political crisis since the death of Prophet Muhammad ﷺ. Madinah was controlled by rebel groups from Egypt, Kufa, and Basra. The city that had served as the seat of the caliphate was left without a leader for several days.¹ ²

Al-Tabari in *Tarikh al-Rusul wa al-Muluk* relates that the surviving senior companions — including Talhah ibn Ubaydillah, al-Zubayr ibn al-Awwam, and Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas — agreed that only one person possessed the capacity and standing to lead the ummah through this crisis: **Ali ibn Abi Talib**.¹

Yet Ali himself was initially reluctant to accept the caliphate under such turbulent conditions. Ibn Kathir records that Ali said:²

*"Leave me and seek someone else. We are facing a matter that has many facets."*

Despite this, pressure continued from all sides. The companions held that no one was more worthy than Ali — he was the Prophet's ﷺ cousin and son-in-law, one of the first to embrace Islam, a courageous warrior in every battle, and renowned for his knowledge and justice. Ultimately, Ali accepted the public pledge of allegiance at the Prophet's Mosque.¹ ²

## The Event: The Caliphate of Ali ibn Abi Talib

### Profile of the Caliph

**Ali ibn Abi Talib** ibn Abd al-Muttalib ibn Hashim was the Prophet Muhammad's ﷺ cousin and the husband of his daughter, **Fatimah al-Zahra**. He embraced Islam in his childhood — according to the majority of scholars, he was the first child to believe in the Prophet ﷺ.¹ ² He grew up under the direct care of the Prophet ﷺ from a young age, when Abu Talib faced financial hardship and the Prophet ﷺ took Ali into his household.

Ali bore many honorifics: *Abu al-Hasan*, *Abu Turab* (given by the Prophet ﷺ), *Asadullah* (Lion of God), and *Karramallahu Wajhah* (may God ennoble his face). Al-Tabari and Ibn Kathir document his extraordinary bravery at the battles of Badr, Uhud, Khandaq, and Khaybar.¹ ²

Prophet Muhammad ﷺ said of Ali in a well-known hadith:

> أَنْتَ مِنِّي بِمَنْزِلَةِ هَارُونَ مِنْ مُوسَى إِلَّا أَنَّهُ لَا نَبِيَّ بَعْدِي
>
> *"Your position to me is like that of Aaron to Moses, except that there is no prophet after me."*
> — Sahih al-Bukhari, no. 3706; Sahih Muslim, no. 2404

### Policies and Reforms

Upon assuming office, Ali made several major decisions reflecting his commitment to justice — decisions that also triggered political resistance:¹ ²

**Dismissal of Uthman-era governors.** Ali replaced nearly all governors appointed by Uthman, including those from the Banu Umayyah. He viewed this as a necessary reform to address popular grievances. However, this policy also meant he lost the support of the dismissed officials.

**Transfer of the capital to Kufa.** After the Battle of the Camel, Ali moved the seat of government from Madinah to **Kufa** in Iraq. This was a strategic decision — Kufa was closer to the conflict zones and served as a strong base of support for Ali. Yet this transfer also marked the permanent end of Madinah's role as the caliphate's capital.¹

**Egalitarian distribution of treasury funds.** Ali returned to the policy of distributing *bayt al-mal* funds equally, without differentiation based on seniority or status — an approach that differed from the policies of Umar and Uthman before him. He said, as recorded in *Nahj al-Balaghah*:³

*"By God, even if I found that such wealth had been used to marry women or buy slaves, I would return it. For indeed, in justice there is ample room."*³

### The Battle of the Camel (656 CE)

Ali's first great trial as caliph came in the form of the **Battle of the Camel** (*Jamal*), which took place near Basra in Jumada al-Thani 36 AH (December 656 CE).¹ ²

The battle pitted Ali's forces against those led by Talhah ibn Ubaydillah, al-Zubayr ibn al-Awwam, and Aisha bint Abu Bakr (Mother of the Believers). Talhah and al-Zubayr demanded that Ali first punish Uthman's killers before addressing other matters. Ali maintained that stability had to be established first before legal proceedings could be carried out.¹ ²

Al-Tabari relates that before the battle, Ali strove vigorously for a diplomatic resolution, sending envoys to negotiate and nearly reaching a peace agreement. However, provocation by parties who desired chaos ultimately ignited the fighting.¹

The battle ended with the defeat of Talhah and al-Zubayr's side. Both were killed — Talhah struck by an arrow and al-Zubayr slain while withdrawing from the battlefield after abandoning his intention to fight. Ali grieved deeply over their deaths. He treated Aisha with full honor and escorted her back to Madinah with a dignified guard.¹ ²

### The Conflict with Mu'awiyah and the Battle of Siffin

Ali's greatest conflict was with **Mu'awiyah ibn Abi Sufyan**, the governor of Syria, who refused to pledge allegiance to Ali and demanded the surrender of Uthman's killers. This conflict culminated in the Battle of Siffin in 657 CE — an event discussed separately due to its significance.

### The Khawarij Schism

After the *tahkim* (arbitration) at Siffin, a portion of Ali's troops who were displeased broke away and formed the group known as the **Khawarij** (those who departed). They considered Ali to have committed a grave sin by accepting arbitration, proclaiming the slogan: *"La hukma illa lillah"* (There is no judgment except God's).¹ ²

Ali responded with a sharp rebuttal. *Nahj al-Balaghah* records his words:³

*"Kalimatun haqqun yurad biha batil"* — *"A word of truth intended for a false purpose."*

Ali attempted dialogue with the Khawarij and managed to bring back the majority. But the hardline core gathered at Nahrawan and began committing violence against other Muslims. Ali was ultimately forced to confront them at the **Battle of Nahrawan** (658 CE), where the Khawarij were militarily defeated — though their ideology survived.¹

### The Martyrdom of Ali

On 19 Ramadan 40 AH (27 January 661 CE), as Ali prepared to lead the Fajr prayer at the Great Mosque of Kufa, a Khawarij member named **Abd al-Rahman ibn Muljam** struck him with a poisoned sword.¹ ² Ali survived for two days before passing away on 21 Ramadan. He was approximately 63 years old.

Ibn Kathir relates that before his death, Ali instructed his sons:²

*"I counsel you to fear God. Do not desire the world even if the world desires you. Do not grieve over anything of the world that you miss."*

Regarding his assassin, Ali said:

*"If I survive, I shall decide his fate. And if I die, then do not kill anyone other than him. Do not mutilate the man, for I heard the Messenger of God ﷺ say: 'Do not mutilate, even a biting dog.'"*¹ ²

## Key Texts and Prophetic Testimony

Ali's intellectual wealth is preserved in *Nahj al-Balaghah*, a compilation of his sermons, letters, and aphorisms assembled by **al-Sharif al-Radi** in the 4th century AH.³ Among his most celebrated sayings:

On justice:
*"Injustice toward the weak is the worst form of oppression."*³

On knowledge:
*"Knowledge is better than wealth. Knowledge guards you, while you guard wealth. Wealth diminishes when spent, while knowledge increases when shared."*³

On leadership:
*"The one most worthy of leadership is the strongest among them in bearing responsibility and the most knowledgeable of God's commands regarding governance."*³

## Analysis and Lessons

### The Tragedy of Just Leadership in Unjust Times

Ali's caliphate stands as a tragic example of just leadership exercised in unjust times. Ali had a clear vision of justice, simplicity, and equality — yet he led at a moment when the ummah was already fractured by a *fitnah* that had begun before he came to power.

### Principle Over Pragmatism

Ali consistently chose principle over political compromise. His decision to replace governors all at once, while ethically sound, gave the opposition grounds to resist him. Historians note that Ali's idealistic approach collided with political realities that demanded gradualism.¹ ²

### Intellectual and Spiritual Legacy

Ali left behind an extraordinarily rich intellectual legacy. He is considered one of the greatest orators and thinkers in the Arabic language. His sayings in *Nahj al-Balaghah* continue to be studied and quoted by scholars of all schools of thought to this day.³ The Prophet Muhammad ﷺ himself said:

> أَنَا مَدِينَةُ الْعِلْمِ وَعَلِيٌّ بَابُهَا
>
> *"I am the city of knowledge and Ali is its gate."*
> — Al-Tirmidhi, no. 3723; Al-Hakim in Al-Mustadrak

With Ali's martyrdom, the era of the Khulafa al-Rashidun — the four rightly guided caliphs — came to an end, and a new chapter in Islamic history began.
