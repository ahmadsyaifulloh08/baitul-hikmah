## 🧑 Who Was He?

Abu al-Qasim ibn Muhammad al-Junayd was born around 830 CE in Baghdad. His father was a pious glass merchant, which is why al-Junayd was often called al-Qawwariri (the glass-maker).

Al-Junayd learned Sufism from his uncle, al-Sari al-Saqati, who was a student of Ma'ruf al-Karkhi. He also studied from al-Harith al-Muhasibi about muhasabah (self-examination) and muraqabah (spiritual observation).

Al-Junayd grew up during Baghdad's golden age as the center of Islamic civilization. The city became a place where various schools of Sufism and Islamic spirituality flourished.

## ⭐ What Did He Do?

Al-Junayd developed the doctrine of fana (self-annihilation) and baqa (subsistence in Allah) which became the foundation of Sunni Sufism. Fana doesn't mean the disappearance of human existence, but the dissolution of self-consciousness in consciousness of Allah.

He created a system of maqamat (spiritual stations) and ahwal (spiritual states). Maqamat are achieved through effort, while ahwal are gifts from Allah that cannot be forced.

Al-Junayd emphasized that every spiritual experience must conform to the Quran and Sunnah. His principle: "Every spiritual experience not witnessed by the Book and Sunnah is false."

## 💡 Why Was He Extraordinary?

Al-Junayd successfully kept Sufism within the corridor of Islamic law. He created a synthesis between the exoteric and esoteric dimensions of Islam that became known as Sunni Sufism.

The spiritual methodology he developed was very systematic and provided a clear roadmap for spiritual seekers. This prevented deviation and excess in Sufi practice.

Al-Ghazali later adopted al-Junayd's theoretical framework and gave strong philosophical legitimacy to Sufism. His influence spread throughout the Islamic world through various Sufi orders.
