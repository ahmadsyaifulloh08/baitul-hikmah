# Wa'ad al-Mau'udah — Female Infanticide in Pre-Islamic Arabia

## The Jahiliyyah Context

The term *Jahiliyyah* — the Age of Ignorance — denotes the period preceding the advent of Islam in the Arabian Peninsula. Pre-Islamic Arab society was organized along rigidly patriarchal tribal lines, where a clan's honor, military strength, and economic survival depended overwhelmingly on its male members. Within this framework, daughters were frequently perceived not as blessings but as liabilities: mouths to feed who would eventually leave for another tribe upon marriage, contributing nothing to the household's defense or material wealth. ^1 This deeply entrenched patriarchal worldview created the social conditions under which the practice of *wa'ad* — the live burial of infant girls — could emerge and persist.

Several interrelated factors drove the practice. First, there was the pervasive anxiety over family honor (*'ird*). In a society where inter-tribal raids and warfare were endemic, the capture of women represented the gravest humiliation a clan could suffer. A daughter taken captive could be enslaved or forced into concubinage, staining the tribe's collective honor irreparably. For some fathers, killing a newborn daughter was considered a preemptive measure against such future disgrace. ^2 Second, economic hardship played a decisive role. The harsh desert environment of the Hijaz and Najd rendered subsistence precarious, and in times of drought or famine, an additional daughter was seen as an unsustainable burden. The Quran itself alludes to this motivation, condemning those who killed their children *min imlaq* — out of poverty. ^3

Third, certain superstitious beliefs associated daughters with ill fortune. Some tribes regarded the birth of a girl as an ominous sign, a mark of divine disfavor upon the household. The convergence of economic desperation, tribal honor culture, and superstition produced a moral environment in which infanticide, though never universally accepted, could be practiced without consistent social censure. It is crucial to note, however, that *wa'ad* was not a pan-Arabian institution. Many tribes — particularly those with stronger commercial economies or sedentary lifestyles, such as parts of the Quraysh — did not practice it, and individual Arabs across the peninsula actively opposed and condemned the custom even before Islam. ^4

## The Practice of Wa'ad

Classical Arabic sources describe the act of *wa'ad* with restrained but unmistakable clarity. The most commonly reported method involved the father taking the newborn — or in some accounts, a girl of several years — to a remote location outside the settlement, digging a pit in the earth, and burying the child alive. Some reports indicate that the mother was sometimes complicit, though more frequently she was powerless to intervene against the father's patriarchal authority. Al-Tabari and Ibn al-Athir record that in certain cases, the father would wait until the child was old enough to walk and speak before carrying out the act, making the cruelty all the more harrowing in its deliberateness. ^5

The practice was not uniform across the Arabian Peninsula, either in prevalence or in method. Historical evidence suggests that *wa'ad* was concentrated among specific tribes — most notably the Tamim, Qays, Asad, Hudhayl, and certain Bakr ibn Wa'il clans — rather than being a universal Arabian custom. ^6 Tribes engaged in long-distance trade, such as the Quraysh of Mecca, appear to have practiced it far less frequently, likely because their commercial prosperity reduced the economic pressures that drove infanticide elsewhere. Furthermore, several prominent pre-Islamic figures are recorded as having actively opposed the practice, indicating that moral objection to *wa'ad* existed within Arab society itself, even in the absence of a codified religious prohibition.

## Recorded Accounts

Among the most poignant accounts preserved in the *hadith* literature is that of Qays ibn 'Asim al-Tamimi, a tribal chief who embraced Islam during the Prophet Muhammad's lifetime. Upon his conversion, Qays reportedly confessed to the Prophet ﷺ that he had buried several of his daughters alive during the Jahiliyyah. As he recounted his deeds, he wept bitterly, and those present wept with him. The Prophet ﷺ is said to have responded with grief, declaring that whoever had committed such acts in the Age of Ignorance and then repented sincerely would be forgiven by God. ^7 Qays's account became emblematic in Islamic literature of the moral transformation that Islam demanded and achieved — the conversion of a man who had killed his own children into one who recognized the enormity of his crime.

A more contested account involves 'Umar ibn al-Khattab, the second Caliph, who is sometimes cited as having buried a daughter before Islam. This report, however, has been the subject of considerable scholarly debate. Many classical and modern scholars regard the attribution as weak (*da'if*) or fabricated, noting that 'Umar's known biography does not support it and that the report lacks a reliable chain of transmission (*isnad*). ^8 Ibn Hajar al-'Asqalani and other *hadith* critics have expressed reservations about the account, and it is more likely a conflation with the general cultural milieu of the Jahiliyyah than a verified biographical detail.

In contrast to these narratives of participation and repentance stands the figure of Zayd ibn 'Amr ibn Nufayl, a pre-Islamic monotheist (*hanif*) of the Quraysh who rejected both the polytheism and the moral corruption of his society. Zayd is recorded as having actively intervened to prevent acts of *wa'ad*, purchasing infant girls from fathers who intended to bury them and raising the children himself or returning them to their families once the crisis had passed. ^9 His example is significant because it demonstrates that moral opposition to infanticide did not require the formal revelation of Islam — conscience and monotheistic conviction alone could produce ethical action. The Prophet Muhammad ﷺ himself spoke of Zayd with respect, saying that he would be raised on the Day of Judgment as "a community unto himself." ^10

## Quranic Condemnation

The Quran addresses the practice of *wa'ad* with unambiguous moral force, framing it as one of the most grievous sins of the Jahiliyyah. The most direct and rhetorically powerful condemnation appears in Surah al-Takwir, where the buried infant girl is given voice on the Day of Judgment:

> وَإِذَا ٱلْمَوْءُۥدَةُ سُئِلَتْ ﴿٨﴾ بِأَىِّ ذَنۢبٍ قُتِلَتْ ﴿٩﴾
>
> "And when the girl-child who was buried alive is asked — for what sin was she killed?"
> — QS. At-Takwir: 8–9

The rhetorical strategy of these verses is remarkable: rather than addressing the perpetrators directly, God grants the voiceless victim a hearing in the cosmic court. The buried girl is not questioned about her own deeds but is asked to testify — a devastating inversion that places the guilt squarely upon her killers. ^11

Surah al-An'am explicitly prohibits the killing of children on grounds of poverty, dismantling the primary economic justification for *wa'ad*:

> وَلَا تَقْتُلُوٓا۟ أَوْلَـٰدَكُم مِّنْ إِمْلَـٰقٍ ۖ نَّحْنُ نَرْزُقُكُمْ وَإِيَّاهُمْ
>
> "And do not kill your children out of poverty; We provide for you and for them."
> — QS. Al-An'am: 151

The verse reframes the issue theologically: sustenance (*rizq*) belongs to God, not to human calculation. A father who kills his child for fear of want has, in Quranic terms, committed both murder and an act of disbelief in divine providence.

Surah an-Nahl captures the psychological moment of a father receiving news of a daughter's birth, exposing the toxic shame that undergirded the practice:

> وَإِذَا بُشِّرَ أَحَدُهُم بِٱلْأُنثَىٰ ظَلَّ وَجْهُهُۥ مُسْوَدًّا وَهُوَ كَظِيمٌ ﴿٥٨﴾ يَتَوَٰرَىٰ مِنَ ٱلْقَوْمِ مِن سُوٓءِ مَا بُشِّرَ بِهِۦ ۚ أَيُمْسِكُهُۥ عَلَىٰ هُونٍ أَمْ يَدُسُّهُۥ فِى ٱلتُّرَابِ ۗ أَلَا سَآءَ مَا يَحْكُمُونَ ﴿٥٩﴾
>
> "And when one of them is given the news of a female child, his face turns dark and he is filled with grief. He hides himself from the people because of the evil of that which he has been informed. Should he keep it in humiliation, or bury it in the ground? Unquestionably, evil is what they decide."
> — QS. An-Nahl: 58–59

The Quran does not merely prohibit *wa'ad*; it dismantles the entire moral and psychological architecture that sustained it — the shame, the economic rationalization, and the dehumanization of female life. ^12

## The Islamic Revolution

The abolition of female infanticide was not an isolated legal reform but part of a comprehensive restructuring of gender relations in early Islamic society. The Prophet Muhammad ﷺ consistently elevated the status of daughters through both his teachings and his personal example. In a well-known hadith, he declared: *"Whoever raises two daughters and treats them well, he and I will be like these two on the Day of Judgment"* — and he held up his index and middle fingers together. ^13 In a society that had buried daughters alive, the Prophet's promise that raising daughters would earn proximity to him in Paradise represented a total inversion of Jahiliyyah values.

The Prophet's own relationship with his daughter Fatimah bint Muhammad exemplified this new ethic. He would stand when she entered a room, kiss her forehead, and seat her beside him — acts of public honor that were extraordinary in the patriarchal context of seventh-century Arabia. ^14 Fatimah's status as *Sayyidat Nisa' al-'Alamin* (Leader of the Women of the Worlds) in Islamic tradition cemented the theological principle that female worth was intrinsic and divinely ordained.

Beyond personal example, Islam introduced concrete legal protections for women that had no precedent in Arabian custom. Women were granted the right to inherit property (QS. An-Nisa: 7–12), the right to own and manage their wealth independently, and the right to *mahr* — a mandatory marriage gift paid to the bride herself, not to her father. ^15 The cumulative effect of these reforms was to transform daughters from expendable liabilities into rights-bearing individuals whose dignity was guaranteed by divine law.

## Civilizational Impact

The trajectory from *wa'ad* to the emergence of some of history's most influential women represents one of the most dramatic civilizational transformations in the human record. Within a single generation of the Prophet's mission, Arab society produced women of extraordinary accomplishment. Khadijah bint Khuwaylid, the Prophet's first wife, was a successful merchant who employed men and managed her own commercial enterprise — a figure virtually unthinkable in a society that buried its daughters. 'A'ishah bint Abi Bakr became one of the most prolific transmitters of *hadith* and a recognized legal authority whose opinions were sought by the most senior Companions. ^16

In subsequent centuries, the Islamic civilization's commitment to female education produced figures such as Fatimah al-Fihri, who founded the University of al-Qarawiyyin in Fez in 859 CE — recognized by UNESCO and the Guinness World Records as the oldest continuously operating degree-granting institution in the world. ^17 That the world's first university was founded by a Muslim woman is a fact of profound symbolic weight when set against the backdrop of pre-Islamic infanticide.

The contrast with contemporary civilizations is instructive. In Rome, the *patria potestas* granted fathers absolute authority over their children's lives, and the exposure of unwanted infants — particularly girls — was legally sanctioned well into the Christian era. ^18 In medieval Europe, women would not gain independent property rights comparable to those granted by Islamic law until the nineteenth century. The Islamic revolution in gender ethics, initiated in the seventh century as a direct response to practices like *wa'ad*, thus anticipated by more than a millennium many of the legal protections that the modern world considers fundamental.
