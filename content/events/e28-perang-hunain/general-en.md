# The Battle of Hunayn and the Siege of Ta'if

## Context: Threat from the South

The liberation of Makkah in Ramadan 8 AH (630 CE) fundamentally altered the balance of power in the Arabian Peninsula. But not all tribes accepted this new reality. Two major tribal confederations south and southeast of Makkah — **Hawazin** and **Thaqif** — saw the rise of Islam as a direct threat to their power and way of life.^1

**Malik ibn Awf al-Nasri**, a young leader of approximately thirty years who commanded Hawazin, made a bold decision: he mobilized the full strength of Hawazin and their allies to attack the Muslims before they could consolidate their power after Fath Makkah.^1 ^2 Malik employed an unusual psychological tactic — he ordered his entire army to bring their families, wealth, and livestock to the battlefield. His logic: if the warriors saw their families behind them, they would fight to the death and none would dare flee.

This decision was harshly criticized by **Duraid ibn al-Simmah**, an ancient and vastly experienced Hawazin war commander. Duraid — carried to the battlefield on a litter due to his age exceeding a hundred years — told Malik:^1

*"By Allah, you are merely a herder of camels and sheep! Can anything stop a man who is fleeing? If your army wins, only men with swords and spears matter. But if you lose, you will have shamed your families and surrendered your wealth."*

Malik rejected Duraid's counsel — a decision that would prove catastrophic.

## The Event: Ambush in the Valley of Hunayn

The Prophet ﷺ departed from Makkah with approximately 12,000 men — the 10,000 who had participated in Fath Makkah plus 2,000 Makkans who had recently embraced Islam.^1 ^2 This was the largest army the Prophet had ever led. The sheer numbers bred overconfidence among some. Someone even declared: *"We will not be defeated today due to our small numbers."*^2

The Quran recorded this attitude and its consequences^4:

> لَقَدْ نَصَرَكُمُ اللَّهُ فِي مَوَاطِنَ كَثِيرَةٍ ۙ وَيَوْمَ حُنَيْنٍ ۙ إِذْ أَعْجَبَتْكُمْ كَثْرَتُكُمْ فَلَمْ تُغْنِ عَنكُمْ شَيْئًا وَضَاقَتْ عَلَيْكُمُ الْأَرْضُ بِمَا رَحُبَتْ ثُمَّ وَلَّيْتُم مُّدْبِرِينَ ﴿٢٥﴾
>
> *"Allah has already given you victory in many regions and on the day of Hunayn, when your great numbers pleased you, but they did not avail you at all, and the earth was confining for you despite its vastness; then you turned back, fleeing. ﴾25﴿"*
> — QS. At-Tawbah (9): 25

Malik ibn Awf had prepared a cunning trap. He positioned his forces in the ravines and slopes of the Hunayn Valley — a narrow valley surrounded by steep hills. When the Muslim forces entered the valley at the still-dark hour of dawn, arrows suddenly rained upon them from every direction, followed by brutal cavalry charges from the flanks.^4

The Muslims, unprepared for the ambush, panicked and scattered.^1 ^2 Within minutes, the great army of 12,000 broke apart. Only a handful of companions remained at the Prophet's side — among them **Abu Bakr**, **Umar**, **Ali ibn Abi Talib**, **al-Abbas ibn Abd al-Muttalib**, and **Abu Sufyan ibn al-Harith** (the Prophet's cousin).

In this critical moment, the Prophet ﷺ displayed extraordinary courage. Rather than retreat, he spurred his mule toward the enemy. **Al-Abbas**, known for his thunderous voice, was ordered to rally the fleeing troops:^1 ^2

*"O Ansar! O companions of the Samurah tree (the Pledge of Ridwan)!"*

The call echoed across the entire valley. The companions who heard it immediately turned and rushed back to the Prophet's side, answering: *"Labbayk! Labbayk!"* (Here we are! Here we are!) Imam Muslim records that the battle then reversed — Hawazin, who had initially dominated, were now driven back.^3

The Quran describes the divine aid at this moment:

> ثُمَّ أَنزَلَ اللَّهُ سَكِينَتَهُ عَلَىٰ رَسُولِهِ وَعَلَى الْمُؤْمِنِينَ وَأَنزَلَ جُنُودًا لَّمْ تَرَوْهَا وَعَذَّبَ الَّذِينَ كَفَرُوا ﴿٢٦﴾
>
> *"Then Allah sent down His tranquility upon His Messenger and upon the believers, and sent down soldiers you did not see, and punished those who disbelieved."*
> — QS. At-Tawbah (9): 26

Hawazin suffered a decisive defeat. Their warriors fled, abandoning their families, wealth, and livestock — exactly as Duraid ibn al-Simmah had feared. The Muslims captured 6,000 prisoners and seized 24,000 camels, 40,000 sheep, and a large quantity of silver. ^4

## The Siege of Ta'if

The remnants of Hawazin and Thaqif retreated to the city of Ta'if — a fortress city surrounded by high walls in the Hijaz highlands.^1 The Prophet ﷺ besieged Ta'if for approximately twenty days. The Muslims employed a *dabbabah* (a leather-covered siege vehicle to protect soldiers) and a *manjaniq* (a large catapult) — the first time such siege technology was used by the Muslim army.^2

However, Ta'if's fortifications proved too formidable, and its inhabitants had prepared supplies for a prolonged siege. After consulting with his companions, the Prophet ﷺ decided to lift the siege and withdraw.^1

When **Umar ibn al-Khattab** announced the withdrawal, some companions protested. The Prophet ﷺ said: *"Then fight tomorrow."* The next day, many were wounded, and the Prophet ﷺ again said: *"We shall return tomorrow, God willing."* This time they accepted.^2 The Prophet then prayed for the people of Ta'if: *"O Allah, guide Thaqif and bring them to us."*

This prayer was answered — months later, a delegation from Thaqif came to Madinah and declared their acceptance of Islam without any fighting.^2

## Key Dialogues and Evidence: The Distribution of Spoils

One of the most compelling episodes after Hunayn was the distribution of war spoils at Ji'ranah. The Prophet ﷺ gave exceptionally large shares to the recently converted Qurayshi leaders — such as Abu Sufyan, his son Mu'awiyah, and Safwan ibn Umayyah — to soften their hearts toward Islam (*ta'lif al-qulub*).^1 ^2

This decision disappointed some of the Ansar. They felt sidelined — those who had stood with the Prophet from the earliest days received less than those who had only just embraced Islam. When word of this reached the Prophet ﷺ, he gathered the Ansar and delivered a deeply moving address:^1 ^2

*"O Ansar, did I not come to you when you were astray, and Allah guided you through me? Were you not poor, and Allah enriched you? Were you not divided, and Allah united your hearts?"*

They answered: *"Allah and His Messenger are the most gracious."*

The Prophet ﷺ continued:

*"O Ansar, could you not reply — and you would be truthful — that you came to us rejected, and we believed you; that you came abandoned, and we helped you; that you came expelled, and we sheltered you?"*

Then he spoke in a voice that shook their hearts:

*"Are you not content that people go home with sheep and camels, while you go home with the Messenger of Allah? By Him in Whose hand is Muhammad's soul, were it not for the Hijrah, I would be one of the Ansar. If mankind took one valley and the Ansar took another, I would take the valley of the Ansar."*^1 ^2

The Ansar wept until their beards were soaked, and they said: *"We are content with the Messenger of Allah as our share."*

## Analysis and Lessons

### The Lesson of the Initial Defeat

Hunayn teaches a vital lesson: numbers and material strength do not guarantee victory. When the Muslims took pride in their 12,000 warriors, they nearly lost. Victory came not from numbers, but from divine aid and steadfast faith.

### Courage in Leadership

At the most critical moment — when nearly the entire army had fled — the Prophet ﷺ advanced toward the front line. This is the highest principle of leadership: the leader stands at the front in times of danger, not at the rear. His courage inspired the troops to return and reverse the tide of battle.

### Diplomacy of the Heart

The unequal distribution of spoils was not injustice — it was a strategic investment. By softening the hearts of recently converted Qurayshi leaders, the Prophet ﷺ secured their long-term loyalty. Meanwhile, the Ansar — whose faith was already firm — received something far more precious: closeness to the Prophet of Allah.

### The Wisdom of Lifting the Siege

The decision to withdraw from Ta'if demonstrates mature military wisdom. The Prophet ﷺ was not trapped by ego or prestige — when the situation was unfavorable, he withdrew with honor. And the result proved superior: Thaqif ultimately embraced Islam voluntarily, without resentment or coercion.
